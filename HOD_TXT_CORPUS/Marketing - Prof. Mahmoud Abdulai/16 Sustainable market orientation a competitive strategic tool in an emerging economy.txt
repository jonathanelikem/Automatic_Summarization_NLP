The purpose of this research is to test the effect of sustainable market orientation
(SMO) as a strategic tool on banks’ sustainable balanced ﬁrm performance (SBFP)
in an emerging economy. The research data was collected via the survey approach
through self-administered questionnaires to 445 bank employees. Exploratory Factor
Analysis was employed to establish the dimensionality of the research constructs,
whereas multiple linear regressions were utilised to test the research hypotheses.
Results suggest that SMO is positively and signiﬁcantly related to sustainable balanced performance of banks. The study conﬁrms the SMO concept promulgated in
prior studies. Employees’ opinions on bank Managers incorporating sustainability
management variables into market orientation as a strategy are encouraged by scholars to engender SBFP. Strategic approaches of this kind leads to balance performance of organisations and the larger society which is central to all stakeholders.
This study may be the initial quantitative investigation into SMO and SBFP in the
strategic marketing literature.
Keywords: sustainable marketing orientation; market orientation; sustainable
balanced ﬁrm performance; marketing strategy; emerging markets

Introduction
The integration of sustainability principles to corporate management strategy for a balanced and sustained economic and social performance has been effective in the marketing literature (Claudy, Peteeson, & O’Driscoll, 2013; Crittenden, Crittenden, Ferrell,
Ferrell, & Pinney, 2011; Wooliscroft, Ganglmair-Wooliscroft, & Noone, 2013). Sustainable management (SM) and marketing impact on ﬁrms’ long-term performance have
been conceptualized differently and empirically tested in marketing literature (Brik,
Rettab, & Mellahi, 2011; Crittenden et al., 2011; Maignan, Ferrell, & Ferrel, 2005;
Mitchell, Wooliscroft, & Higham, 2010). One of such conceptualizations for which this
study intends to build on is the interest of marketers to combine SM with market orientation (MO) for effective, efﬁcient and balance performance of ﬁrms (Crittenden et al.,
2011; Mitchell, Wooliscroft, & Higham, 2013; Mitchell et al., 2010). Scholars are of
the view that deﬁciencies in corporate governance, exploitative social and environmental tendencies, and an emphasis on maximizing short-term proﬁtability at the expense
of long-term stakeholder interest are necessitating the re-conceptualisation of the MO
construct (Mitchell et al., 2010, 2013; Pantouvakis, 2014). These failures have been
attributed to corporate management regimes and leadership style that are not active. In

Organisations that adopt SMO are expected to achieve balanced sustainable performance (SP) (Crittenden et al., 2011; Mitchell et al., 2013). This means that such ﬁrms
are able to perform well from economic and social perspectives. SMO will stimulate
corporate performance and organisations would be able to elicit positive evaluations

opportunities for marketing research, for which this study intends to contribute into the

the model in an emerging economy context which will provide the means for corporate
managers to examine and ﬁne-tune corporate marketing strategies by applying an
empirically proven mix of long-term economic, social, and environmental performance
indicators (Mitchell et al., 2010). More so, SMO emphasis to proﬁtably satisfying market demand places adequate obligations on corporations to meet social and environmental expectations of stakeholders (Crittenden et al., 2011). This is beneﬁcial to the ﬁrms’
long-term corporate marketing strategy, hence the SMO construct (Mitchell et al.,
2013).
Ghana is among emerging economies and one of the fastest growing and most
politically stable in sub-Saharan Africa (Ward, 2013). Ghana’s banking industry is the
empirical context of this research. The industry is governed by the central bank known
as Bank of Ghana (BoG) (Narteh, 2013a, 2013b). The mandate of the BoG is to ensure
sustainable growth in output and employment through creating the right conditions for
all sectors of the Ghanaian economy, particularly, the banking industry (Bank of Ghana,
2014). The BoG achieves this through price stability, low inﬂation and supporting government economic objectives to ensure sound macroeconomic stability for the banking
industry and other industries to thrive. Currently, the banking sector is the fastest growing industry in Ghana, the emergence of new banks and their impact on the Ghanaian
banking industry has been much discussed (Hinson, Owusu-Frimpong, & Dasah, 2009;
Mahmoud, Kastner, & Akyea, 2011; Narteh, 2013a, 2013b). In relation to sustainable
operations, banks in Ghana are reported to be practicing SMO in diverse areas, which
is in line with marketers’ observation (Bradshaw & Tadajewski, 2011; Lewin, Strutton,
& Paswan, 2011; McDonagh & Prothero, 2014). Banks have sustainability policies to
which they commit themselves (UT Bank, 2014). For instance, they are committed to
be responsible and maintaining, integrity, respect and high ethical standards in Ghana.
Banks also hold themselves accountable to generate economic values for their customers. In addition, they commit themselves to respect the environment and also partner with other key stakeholders to ensure they improve on the environmental proﬁle of
their products and services in Ghana. Banks are also claiming to be making positive
impact on communities where they operate (UT Bank, 2014).
Banks in Ghana are reported to be operating sustainably with long-term goals in
mind rather than short-term proﬁtability (Hinson et al., 2009). They want to ensure they
experience market share growth of their customers and clients and hence sustainable

Literature review and conceptual framework
Market orientation
MO has been the cornerstone of corporate marketing strategy for the past six decades
(Jaworski & Kohli, 1993; Kohli & Jaworski, 1990; McKitterick, 1957; Narver & Slater,
1990; Pantouvakis, 2014). Hunt and Morgan (1995), using Resource-Advantage Theory, determined that a market-oriented ﬁrm can achieve a position of a competitive
advantage and superior long-run performance. MO has been conceptually and empirically linked to superior business performance in the extant literature (Gebhardt,
Carpenter, & Sherry, 2006; Hunt, 2012; Hunt & Lambe, 2000; Kirca, Jayachandran, &
Bearden, 2005). MO provides ﬁrms with a source of competitive advantage (Morgan,
Vorhies, & Mason, 2009). Empirical studies on the topic have so far shown that ﬁrms
that embrace MO concepts develop increased market-sensing and customer-linking
capabilities that lead to superior business performance (Doyle & Armenakyan, 2014;
Hawkins, 2014; Jaworski & Kholi, 1993; Kirca et al., 2005; Kohli & Jaworski, 1990;
Kuada & Buatsi, 2005; Kumar, Jones, Venkatesan, & Leone, 2011; Lings & Greenley,
2009; Pantouvakis, 2014). Over the period, a number of revisions and suggestions
regarding the MO construct have emerged (Blankson et al., 2013; Homburg & Pﬂesser,
2000; Lado, Maydeu-Olivares, & Rivera, 1998; Mitchell et al., 2010; Morgan &
Strong, 1998; Pantouvakis, 2014).
Traditionally, the studies on MO have been based on two perspectives. The ﬁrst
perspective argues that MO is ‘the organisational culture that most effectively and

efﬁciently creates necessary behaviours for the creation of superior value for buyers
and, thus, continuous superior performance for business’ (Narver & Slater, 1990, p 21).
Narver and Slater (1990) argued that a market-oriented ﬁrm will have a long-term focus
and emphasis on proﬁtable operations. They suggest that a MO is relevant in every
market environment and, as such, must be the foundation for a business’s competitive
advantage strategy. In line with this argument, MO is found to be an important determinant of ﬁrm performance Jaworski and Kohli (1993), Deshpande, Farley, & Webster,
(1993), and Homburg and Pﬂesser (2000). Kohli and Jaworski (1990) identiﬁed three
core themes of a MO: (1) customer focus, (2) coordinated marketing, and (3) proﬁtability. The behavioral perspective, depicted by Kohli and Jaworski (1990), relates to speciﬁc actions or behaviors necessary to achieve a MO. They view MO as follows:
organisation-wide generation of market intelligence pertaining to current and future
needs of the customer, thus dissemination of intelligence within the organisation and
responsiveness to existing and potential customers.
Crittenden et al. (2011) in reviewing the literature on MO were of the view that
while a MO has evolved over time to be almost synonymous with a customer (as end
consumer) and competitor focus, researchers have called for the broadening of ‘market’
so as to include a larger constituency base. Accordingly, Slater and Narver (1995) refer
to key stakeholders and call for the inclusion of suppliers, businesses in different industries, consultants, universities, and government agencies in the MO construct. Matsuno
and Mentzer (2000, p. 5) recommend an extended domain pertaining to ‘relevant industry market participants (i.e. competitors, suppliers, and buyers) and inﬂuencing factors
(i.e. social, cultural, regulatory, and macroeconomic factors).’ From an internal perspective, Schonberger (1990) suggests that functional areas in a ﬁrm form a continuous
chain of customers that extends from a product/service concept to the end user (consumer) of the product. This broader stakeholder perspective (both internally and externally) is consistent with the contemporary stakeholder perspective that has historically
been the dominant paradigm in sustainability research (Crittenden et al., 2011). The
stakeholder perspective takes into account not just one stakeholder group, such as consumers, but all groups to whom the business is responsible (Crittenden et al., 2011).
Marketers assuming this larger stakeholder perspective as related to sustainability will
shift the ﬁrm’s MO (both behavioral and cultural) from an end-consumer focus to a
broader set of stakeholders (Maignan and Ferrell 2004; Crittenden et al., 2011). Signiﬁcantly, scholars also suggest that a MO that encompasses the broad base of stakeholders provides an avenue to stronger competitive advantage (Crittenden et al., 2011;
Ferrell, Gonzalez-Padron, Hult, & Maignan, 2010). In recent times, there is evidence
that MO has substantial conceptual shortcomings warranting a proposition of SMO as a
more comprehensive management framework (Crittenden et al., 2011; Mitchell et al.,
2010).
Sustainable market orientation
Given the measurable impact of possessing a MO in conjunction with the competitive
advantage offered by a marketing strategy that incorporates sustainability, it stands to
reason that a market-oriented approach to sustainability would serve as a resource
advantage for the ﬁrm (Crittenden et al., 2011). Moreover, researchers posit that integrating bodies of knowledge from one or more substantive areas can initiate theory
development in marketing (Yadav, 2010; Crittenden et al., 2011). Our aim here is to
follow these researchers suggestion of using interrelations to advocate a SMO

framework that identiﬁes the underlying constructs of SMO and sustainable balance performance. To succeed in this endeavour, this research draws from a variety of business
and non-business disciplines to understand the phenomenon. Thus, the SMO is derived
from integrating theory across a marketing management (Kohli & Jaworski, 1990; Narver
& Slater, 1990) and sustainability management (Kuosmanen and Kuosmanen (2009). An
SMO approach to sustainability is predicated on the understanding that MO is a type of
organizational behavior (Kohli & Jaworski, 1990) and more than just a focus on the customer and the competitor. Rather, a culture of a MO encourages behaviors that affect
organizational learning, which can instill sustainability into the fabric of a ﬁrm, yielding a
resource advantage (Slater & Narver, 1995).
In respect to sustainability, Kuosmanen and Kuosmanen (2009, p. 235) state that,
‘Sustainability is nowadays generally accepted as one of the key success factors in the
long term business strategy of the ﬁrm.’ SMO is a strategic orientation that comprises
balanced and equitable intergenerational management of environmental, social and economic resources (Mitchell et al., 2013). SMO presents a framework that combines the
macro-marketing principles of sustainability and micro-marketing of intelligence generation, dissemination and responsiveness to create proﬁtable products and services for
the target market and society as a whole (Bradshaw & Tadajewski, 2011; Jaworski &
Kohli, 1993; Lings & Greenley, 2009; Meunier-FitzHugh & Lane, 2009; Mitchell
et al., 2013; Mittelstaedt, Shultz, Kilbourne, & Peterson, 2014). A corporate commitment to SMO will reﬂect an understanding of latent market and community expectations that is also likely to foster trust-based market, societal, and governmental
relationships and minimise the risk of government intervention and regulation (Mitchell
et al., 2010). The proponents of SMO identiﬁed MO, macromarketing, corporate stakeholder responsibility and sustainable development management as the major building
blocks of the concept (Crittenden et al., 2011; Mitchell et al., 2010, 2013).
SMO dimensions include broadly MO and SM. Firms related strategic decisions
that enhance or impede the implementation of the MO and SM would have an inﬂuence on their SMO culture. SM issues are important contributors to consumer decisionmaking (Cummins, Reilly, Carlson, Grove, & Dorsch, 2014). SM comprises ecological
orientation, societal orientation (SO) and economic orientation (EconoO) (Mitchell
et al., 2013). Mitchell et al. (2010) in their exploratory study developed a theoretical
framework of the relationship between the SMO concept and its predictors in which
they suggested that societal, ecological and EconoOs are predictors or antecedents to
SMO. However, other studies have postulated that societal, economic and environmental orientations (EnviroOs) are dimensions of sustainable development (Choi & Ng,
2011; Mitchell et al., 2013). MO and SM are conceptualised as components of SMO
(Choi & Ng, 2011; Mitchell et al., 2013).
SOs involve businesses recognizing the importance of conformity with social norms
(Varman & Costa, 2008). Corporate marketing managers should proactively manage
social factors in order to attain SMO. Focusing on micromarketing alone to the neglect
of macromarketing factors such as social interventions would not aid businesses in
attaining SP. Firms’ ecological orientation is suggested to be dependent on the target
consumers (Ling-Yee, 1997). In markets where consumers are ecological conscious and
embark on green-product-related information search, and purchase green products often
will compel ﬁrms targeting such markets to be ecologically conscious in the operations.
Ecological orientation refers to the degree of emotional, the amount of factual knowledge, the level of willingness, as well as the extent of actual behaviour on pollution-environment issues (Ling-Yee, 1997). Mitchell et al. (2013) found that ecological

Environmental responsible marketing of well-branded products and services by

Conceptual framework
As a guide to this study, a framework is developed based on previous literature on MO
and SM (Choi & Ng, 2011; Crittenden et al., 2011; Mitchell et al., 2010). The framework presented (see Figure 1) suggests that ﬁrms with a high commitment to SMO are
likely to be engaged in SM and MO. The consequences of this will be sustainable
balanced social and economic performance. This is expected to ensure marketing of
well-branded products by ﬁrms that address signiﬁcant stakeholder needs. Firms will
continue to exhibit high commitment to SMO even in a highly competitive marketplace
(Crittenden et al., 2011; Mitchell et al., 2010). The framework has three main
components made of (1) SMO; (2) SBFP and (3) control variables (Figure 1).

SMO has two main dimensions in this framework, namely: SM and MO. The
dimensions of SM are SO, EnviroO and EconoO. The dimensions for the MO construct
was not decomposed into its various dimensions in this study, because these components have been extensively examined in prior literature (Jaworski & Kohli, 1993;
Kohli & Jaworski, 1990; Kuada & Buatsi, 2005; Mahmoud, Kastner, & Yeboah, 2010;
Narver & Slater, 1990). SBFP has brand performance (BP) and SP as its dimensions.
The BP reﬂects economic performance, whereas SP in this sense represents the social
performance in this conceptualisation. Social performance means non-ﬁnancial measures of sustainability progress in the overall assessment of a company’s SP (Crittenden
et al., 2011). For instance, companies may target reducing their greenhouse emissions
effect by 2% in 2016. Economic performance on the other hand is ﬁnancial measures
of sustainability progress in the overall assessment of a company’s SP. Grifﬁn and
Mahon (1997) suggest the need for multiple sources of performance and note that performance should be observed from both ﬁnancial (i.e. corporate ﬁnancial performance)
and non-ﬁnancial (i.e. corporate social performance) metrics.
Hypotheses
To investigate the consequences of SMO of banks in Ghana, the following hypotheses
were developed and tested in this study.
H1: There is a signiﬁcant positive relationship between sustainable market orientation
(SMO) and sustainable balanced ﬁrm performance (SBFP).
H2: There is a signiﬁcant positive relationship between societal orientations (SO) and sustainable balanced ﬁrm performance (SBFP).
H3: There is a signiﬁcant positive relationship between environmental orientation (EvironO) and sustainable balanced ﬁrm performance (SBFP).
H4: There is a signiﬁcant positive relationship between economic orientation (EconoO) and
sustainable balanced ﬁrm performance (SBFP).
H5: There is a signiﬁcant positive relationship between market orientation (MO) and sustainable balanced ﬁrm performance (SBFP).

Research methodology
Data collection
Primary data was collected for testing the hypotheses. The sample consisted of 445
employees of all the commercial banks in Ghana. This is in line with good practices as
precedents of the use of bank employees are available in prior studies (Mahmoud et al.,
2011; Opoku & Essien, 2011). To be speciﬁc, the sample drawn were from senior-level
personnel of commercial banks in Ghana through a purposive sampling technique.
Moreover, these categories of the population are educated and are presumed to possess
the appropriate knowledge in understanding sustainability and marketing issues.
Nonetheless, the researcher explained to the participants what MO and sustainable
development represent in the study, before the questionnaires were administered.
Customers were not included in this study to avert the serious drawback of subjective
performance measures (Haugland, Myrtveit, & Nygaard, 2007; Noble, Sinha, & Kumar,
2002).

The ﬁrst assessment for the qualiﬁcations of the respondents was to determine their

and returned questionnaire increased to 445. All questionnaires were found to be usable
probably due to the self-administered survey approach adopted for this study. The analysis of the two-wave of questionnaires received did not show signiﬁcant differences
between early and late respondents. Therefore, non-response bias was not a problem
for this research.
Measurement
Measurement in this study included independent variables, dependent variables and
control variables.
Independent variables
SMO, SO, EnviroO, MO and EconoO were the independent variables. These were
measured with scales developed from existing literature (Jaworski & Kohli, 1993;
Mitchell et al., 2010, 2013). All items were scored on a ﬁve-point likert scale in which
1 equals ‘strongly disagree’ and 5 equals ‘strongly agree’.
Dependent variables
Over-reliance on ﬁnancial performance has been identiﬁed as one of the key limitations
of extant marketing literature (Brik et al., 2011). This current study measured SBFP
subjectively, based on the initial empirical research (Mitchell et al., 2013). SBFP was
categorised into BP which was an indication of economic well-beings of the banks and
long-term performance reﬂect social or sustainability performance of the banks. Overall,
12 items were developed (see appendix) from the qualitative study of Mitchell et al.
(2013) to measure SBFP. Seven items measure BP, whereas 5 items measure long-term
performance.
Control variables
The study controlled for the size of the banks based on its assets, the number of years
employees have worked with the bank and age of the employees with single item measure. There are precedents of the use of single item measure in marketing research
(Morgan et al., 2009). Past research provides evidence that the size of ﬁrms have an
impact on their performance (Brik et al., 2011; Morgan et al., 2009). Likewise, the loyalty and age of the respondents to the brand can inﬂuence their responses and as such
were controlled in this study. Banks size were measured based on Ghana banking survey ranking of banks in Ghana. In Ghana, banks are ranked based on their performance. The ﬁrst top four banks are referred to as ‘quartile one’ banks and as such were
ranked in that order (Mahmoud et al., 2011). Any other listed as respondent bank was

Construct validation and reliability
In order to validate the multidimensional scales of SMO and SBFP, a factor analysis
was performed (see Table 2). The Exploratory Factor Analysis (EFA) (applying principal component analysis), based on the suggested rule of eigenvalue > 1, indicate all
items loaded appropriately onto their respective factors. The factor analysis of SMO
produces four factors: SO, EnviroO, EconoO and MO accounting for 47.979% of the
total variance, with composite reliability of .904. The loadings of these four factors of
SMO range from .501 to .812. Cronbach’s alpha values are .850, .867, .545 and .775,
respectively, for SO, EnviroO, EconoO and MO. The EFA results in two factors to
measure the construct of SBFP. There are BP and SP. These two factors account for
60.486% of total variance for SBFP. The composite reliability is .905, which is high.
The loadings for the two factors of SBFP range between .524 and .844. A cut-off of .5
for Cronbach alpha (Nunnally, 1978) was adopted for the constructs. In line with
Blankson and Stokes (2002), the accepted benchmark for item-to-total correlation was
set above .3.
The validity of the scales was tested through content, convergent and discriminant
validity (Abdulai Mahmoud & Yusif, 2012; Blankson & Stokes, 2002; O’Cass & Ngo,
2007). The content validity checks were performed on the constructs taking into
account both the literature and opinions received during the pre-test with academics
and practitioners. This was to ensure that the scale items would be meaningful to the
sample and capture the issues which were intended to be measured (Abdulai Mahmoud

Discussions and implications
The main ﬁndings of this research show a signiﬁcant and positive relationship between
SMO and SBFP. This means that SMO generates both economic and social performance for emerging economies banking sector and must be encouraged. In the ﬁrst
place, the ﬁnding of the study conﬁrms the conceptualisation that SMO results in sustainable balanced performance of ﬁrms, at least in the banking sector in Ghana. Moreover, the ﬁndings provide support to earlier exploratory research conducted on the
subject (Mitchell et al., 2013). Managers must sustain their interest in both short-term
and long-term strategic performance of banks, rather than on short-term economic
gains. The results of this study also support the argument that societal, environmental
and EconoOs are dimensions of banks SMO. This shows that banks whose strategies
are not only internally focus but are externally oriented would be relatively positioned
to be more sustainable market-oriented, which consequently will result in long-term

economic and social performance for such banks. Employees are argued to identify
with corporate institutions that are perceived to be involved in sustainability management that have direct impact with them (Choi & Ng, 2011).
The ﬁndings of this study is re-echoing the argument by strategic marketing scholars to add sustainable marketing into conventional focus on marketing management on
customers and market dimensions (Brik et al., 2011; Mitchell et al., 2010, 2013). The
research ﬁndings should spur corporate managers to examine and transform marketing
strategies by applying an empirically proven mix of long-term economic, social, ecological and environmental performance indicators for corporate and societal beneﬁts.
Corporate managers must view the adoption of SMO as an opportunity for growth and
not threat to the success of their operations. This assertion is supported by social movements among consumers (including internal consumers) demonstrating a growing concern with the environment as ﬁrms pursue their economic agenda (Cummins et al.,
2014).
Furthermore, the ﬁndings on the dimensions of SMO on SBFP also indicate that
the employees value all of these variables as they make tactical and strategic decisions.
This is in line with a study by Choi and Ng (2011) which concluded that employees
respond to multiple dimensions of sustainability. The implications to managers are that
equal attentions are needed to all the SMO dimensions. Generally, management focus
on both the micromarketing and macromarketing constructs are likely to generate
favourable response from target market than either of them. Scholars postulate that
ﬁrms that are environmental sustainable are seeking competitive advantage (Lewin
et al., 2011; Mitchell et al., 2013; Wooliscroft et al., 2013). This implies SMO has the
potential of deriving competitive advantage for banks investigated.
The positive and signiﬁcant inﬂuence of EnviroO and SBFP demonstrates that
banks are concerned about the external marketing environment. In relation to concern
for cost-oriented values and beneﬁt-oriented values (Lewin et al., 2011) on ecological
orientation, the ﬁndings of this study suggest that banks are most likely to be concerned on the cost and beneﬁts of their operations to the environment. A previous study
has established that beneﬁt-oriented values have a negative relation with concerns,
whereas cost-oriented values are positively associated with safety and regulatory concerns (Lewin et al., 2011).
The ﬁndings of this study also suggest that managers who are not yet incorporating
SM in their corporate strategy must begin to do so. The advantages for adopting SMO
far outweigh its cost in the long-term (Lewin et al., 2011). Lagging behind in terms of
SMO adoption could be costly. As some marketing scholars are of the view managers
are incorporating sustainability initiatives other than those purely under academic
umbrella (Cummins et al., 2014). This implies that aside academic researchers’ interest
in sustainable issues, managers in industry or practice are also incorporating sustainability matters in their work. Apart from the adoption of SMO beneﬁt that inure to the
banks, managers have the responsibility to protect the environment. In this regard,
banks have the pivotal role in environmental and social friendly operations at their own
level and other partner ﬁrms operations. For instance, banks can ensure loans are
approved and disbursed to sectors that are perceived to be environmental friendly in
their operations.
Environmental marketing suggest that outside factors such as economic downturns
can and do play an inﬂuencing role in the degree to such trends become enhanced in
the corporate structures (Cummins et al., 2014). At the corporate level, SMO might not
be given attention due to faltering economic conditions as an external variable

Conclusions and directions for future research
This study contributes to the emerging economies strategic marketing literature. This is
achieved by applying SMO construct to the understanding of sustainable operations of
banks and its effects on their economic and social performance. The assessment of the
banking industry environmental operations and the synergistic effect are not easily
attainable due to the difﬁculty in visualizing and understanding the impact of banking
sector compared to manufacturing and mining. Nonetheless, the study empirically
shows evidence of beneﬁts of banks adoption of SMO, which leads to better and balanced performance of banks relative to focusing on micromarketing at an emerging
economy context. Thus, SMO is a competitive strategic tool among banks in an emerging economy. Several reasons account for this ﬁnding, including and not limited to:
environmental education, competition, and increase in employee knowledge about the
environment and the integration of internal and external banks strategies.
The main underlying premise of SMO is that banks’ use of MO and SM principles
when making strategic decisions for sustainability balanced economic and social performance (Bradshaw & Tadajewski, 2011; Claudy et al., 2013; Wooliscroft et al., 2013).
This ensures that banks are protected at normal or economic boom periods and at difﬁcult times. Banks’ products and services may suffer diffusions among employees if they
perceive them to be unsustainable as a result of poor environmental, social and economic management.
Assessment of SMO in the banking industry is an evaluation of corporate relationships with key economic, social, and other environmental stakeholders. This calls for
corporate management to apply SMO principles to achieve improved balanced economic, social long-term organizational performance. Moreover, the investigating of the
application of SMO in banks in an emerging economy, particularly, in a sub-Saharan
African context adds to existing empirical studies of SMO beyond the studies conducted in developed countries. Prior studies in SMO recommended to researchers,
establishing the potential of SMO in different industry sectors, for which this study has
accomplished. Further, this investigation adds value to the SMO concept as recommended by previous scholars (Mitchell et al., 2010). Empirical research on SMO is at
the embryonic stage, thus, this paper stimulate further research into the concept. Moreover, to the best of the researcher’s knowledge, this may be the ﬁrst quantitative study
to empirically examine the SMO construct in an emerging economy.
The test of the hypotheses using banks employees’ perceptions of SMO and its
relation to performance without other stakeholders’ views serves as short-comings to
this research. In this regard, it is important to consider customers, shareholders and
other constituents’ views in subsequent SMOs studies. However, it is believed that the
use of perceptual data provides real-time facts than using documentary evidence.
