Abstract
Purpose – The aim of this study is to examine how market orientation, innovation, and corporate social
responsibility (CSR) jointly impact business performance.
Design/methodology/approach – This study adopts a quantitative research design. Research
evidence was collected via a questionnaire-based survey of marketing managers and executives of
telecommunication companies in Ghana. The hypotheses developed following a review of scholarship
on marketing, strategy and corporate citizenship were tested through regression analysis.
Findings – The results indicate that firms’ degree of market orientation and CSR have significant impact
on innovation, which then influences business performance. Furthermore, market orientation has direct
significant effect on CSR, which tends to mediate the influence of market orientation on business
performance.
Research limitations/implications – The use of longitudinal research that combines managers’
evaluation with a survey of consumers, employees, and other relevant stakeholders is suggested to
confirm the results of this study.
Practical implications – This study suggests that innovation and CSR are two key ingredients for
transforming market orientation into a successful business strategy. Managers are to note that even
‘‘doing good’’ (a CSR initiative) requires a sense of innovation for it to trigger any significant financial
performance for the business.
Originality/value – This study introduces a model in which the relationship between CSR, market
orientation, and performance is mediated by innovation. The empirical evidence enhances the existing
literature on marketing, corporate citizenship, and strategy.
Keywords Market orientation, Innovation, Corporate social responsibility, Telecommunication, Ghana

There is a growing body of literature on the market orientation construct (see Kohli and
Jaworski, 1990; Narver and Slater, 1990; Ruekert, 1992; Greenley, 1995; Pitt et al., 1996;
Appiah-Adu and Ranchhod, 1998; Harris, 2000; Esteban et al., 2001; Guo, 2002; Blankson
and Stokes, 2002; Perry and Shao, 2002; Green et al., 2005; Keskin, 2006; Ozer et al., 2006;
Low et al., 2007). Studies have analyzed the construct in terms of its conceptualization (Kohli
and Jaworski, 1990; Narver and Slater, 1990; Ruekert, 1992) and factors (both internal and
external) influencing the degree of market orientation in organizations (see Harris, 2000;
Ozer et al., 2006). Additionally, other scholars have linked the construct to business
performance (Ruekert, 1992; Appiah-Adu and Ranchhod, 1998). In relation to the market
orientation-business performance relationship, it has been shown that organizations that
exhibit higher levels of market orientation experienced superior performance in terms of new
product success (Atuahene-Gima, 1996), return on assets (Narver and Slater, 1990), growth
in market share (Appiah-Adu and Ranchhod, 1998), customer satisfaction, service quality,
and employee satisfaction (Agarwal et al., 2003).

Analogous to proponents of innovation-performance and market orientation-performance
relationships, a considerable stream of scholarly research has emerged in the management
literature suggesting that corporate social responsibility (CSR) orientation is the key to
stimulating long-term stability, growth, and sustainable performance in a dynamic and
changing environment (Balabanis et al., 1998; Luo and Homburg, 2007; Gyves and
O’Higgins, 2008; Prado-Lorenzo et al., 2008). In fact, some studies even reported that CSR
has synergy with market orientation (Narver and Slater, 1990; Maignan et al., 1999; Qu,
2007, 2009). Qu (2009), for instance, accounted for the joint effects of market orientation and
CSR on firm performance in his survey of star-rated hotels in China by reporting that:
[. . .] although both market orientation and CSR could enhance performance, once the effects of
CSR are accounted for, the direct effects of market orientation on performance diminish
considerable to almost non-existent.

It would seem that a market orientation may not be the only determining factor of firm
performance (Han et al., 1998; Baker and Sinkula, 1999; Agarwal et al., 2003). This study
extends Qu’s (2009) work by testing for the nomological web of relations among market
orientation, CSR and innovation. Innovation in managerial and organizational practices
suggests new ways of enhancing corporate responsibility through rebuilding the
relationship between organizations and the wider community and developing an
understanding of the social capital that exists in the communities which an organization
serves (Bartlett, 2009). Although evidence exists to suggest that both innovation and CSR
should be considered as important determinants of business performance, studies
empirically addressing their joint effects on performance are far reaching. In this paper, we
propose and test a model of the relationship between market orientation, innovation, CSR
and business performance in a sample comprising marketing managers and executives of
all telecommunication companies operating in Ghana.
The Ghanaian telecommunication industry has been deregulated, and companies operating in
the sector now have to serve customers under competitive forces. Market orientation would
seem to be very important to these companies (Kurtinaitiene, 2005), because with increasing
global competition and changes in consumer needs, companies must organize their activities
with a strong focus on their markets, in order to survive (Mahmoud et al., 2010).
The telecommunication sector is of particular interest, as it retails intangible products in
which service quality and customer orientation are crucial elements. In service sectors, such as
telecommunication, a firm can hardly differentiate itself on the basis its products alone
thereby making the acquisition of competitive advantage extremely difficult. It seems that
developing and planning effective innovative strategies is a critical requirement for service
organizations. Additionally, CSR activities could be crucial for improving corporate image in
order to stimulate market performance (Luo and Bhattacharya, 2006; Prado-Lorenzo et al.,
2008). To date, no Ghanaian study has addressed the issues of market orientation, innovation
and CSR in the telecom sector. This study is designed around two research questions: how
does innovation function in the relationship between market orientation and business
performance? And how does CSR affect innovative performance when complemented with
market orientation?

it is combined with a learning culture. The authors stress that market orientation and learning

Given the complex nature of the market orientation-performance relationship, firms must rely
on some competences in order to transform market orientation into a successful business
strategy, with CSR and innovation as the two key competences in this regard. Drawing on
several theoretical bases, we argue further that managers must marry CSR with innovation in
order to achieve some business gains for their businesses.
This paper is set out as follows: the introduction deals with a discussion of market orientation,
innovation, and CSR and why these issues are being examined in the context of Ghana’s
telecommunications sector. The second section of the paper introduces Ghana’s
telecommunication industry, whilst the third and forth parts of the paper address the
conceptual framework and hypotheses as well as the study research design. The fifth section
of the paper dwelt on the analysis and results whilst the sixth section takes on the discussions
and implications. The paper concludes with limitations and indications for future research.

Ghana’s telecommunication sector
Historically, Ghana’s telecommunication sector can be broken into two distinct phases
following independence in 1957 from British colonial rule. In the first phase, the sector was
heavily regulated with Ghana Post and Telecommunications (GP&T) as the sole provider of
telecom services for both fixed and mobile networks. During this era, the sector was
characterized by deficient telecommunications infrastructure and operational inefficiencies
on the part of P&T (Frempong and Henten, 2004). Services provided by GP&T were very
poor with low penetration rates. This continued up until 1994 when government lunched the
‘‘Accelerated Development Program 1994-2000 (ADP 2000)’’ to deregulate the industry,
which marked the beginning of a new era in the sector. As part of the restructuring plan,
Ghana Telecom was incorporated to succeed GP&T. Subsequently, in 1996, the National
Communication Authority (NCA) was established by Parliamentary Act to regulate the
sector, promote a stable operating environment for all participants, and promote fair
competition and efficiency. The deregulation encouraged new entrants into the industry
such as Millicom Ghana ltd and Kassapa Telecom. Today, the sector is fast growing with
multinational companies like MTN, Zain, and Vodafone Plc. (who have acquired majority
interest in Ghana Telecom) all featuring prominently in the sector. Nigerian operator
Globacom, has also recently been licensed to operate in Ghana.
Unlike the monopoly era, innovation in the sector has been driven by market liberalization as
several services including international roaming, mobile money services, internet facilities,
and teleconferencing facilities have been introduced and customers now have a range of
choices apart from merely making and receiving calls. Besides introducing innovative
products onto the market, the telecom operators also separately invest substantial amounts
into high-impact CSR activities, particularly in education, health, environment, sports, music,
arts and entertainment (see Ofori and Hinson, 2007, for more on CSR activities of leading
Ghanaian companies). The market leader (see Table I for facts on market share), MTN for
instance, invests 1 percent of pre-tax profits every year into CSR through its autonomous
CSR wing, MTN Ghana Foundation. The Vodafone Group also recently earmarked $1 million
US dollars for the training of entrepreneurs in Africa to leverage the world wide web as a

Conceptual framework and hypotheses
Market orientation
Market orientation has been defined as the extent to which an organization is inclined to
implement the marketing concept (Kohli and Jaworski, 1990). Scholars (see Homburg and
Pflesser, 2000) have examined the concept from both behavioral and cultural perspectives. In
the behavioral approach, market orientation has been viewed as a set of processes relating to
the philosophy of an organization, and in that regard is referred to as the organization-wide
generation of market intelligence or information pertaining to current and future customer needs,
dissemination of the information across departments, and organization-wide responsiveness to
this information (Kohli and Jaworski, 1990). This perspective urges firms to know the customer
needs and preferences and to analyze exogenous factors that influence those needs and
preferences. There is also the need for organization’s departments to coordinate and react
appropriately to the market needs and to take actions in response to intelligence that is
generated and disseminated (Kohli and Jaworski, 1990).
From the cultural perspective, Narver and Slater (1990) defined market orientation as ‘‘the
organizational culture that most effectively and efficiently creates the necessary behaviors for
the creation of superior value for buyers and, thus, continuous superior performance for the
business’’ (p. 21). This approach emphasizes three components of market orientation –namely
customer orientation, competitor orientation, and inter-functional coordination. Customer
orientation is a set of beliefs that puts the customer’s interest first and requires a sufficient
understanding to create products or services of superior value (Deshpande et al., 1993; Narver
and Slater, 1990). Competitor orientation requires firms to understand the strengths,
weaknesses, capabilities and strategies of competitors and actively engage in information
acquisition on existing and potential competitor (Deshpande et al., 1993; Narver and Slater,
1990). Inter-functional coordination is defined as ‘‘the coordinated utilization of company
resources in creating superior value for target customers’’ (Narver and Slater, 1990, p. 22).
Marketing scholars have long described market orientation as a desirable strategy for
companies to pursue and they have described the practice as ‘‘customer-led’’. According to
Low et al. (2007, p. 879), it entails such things as looking for unmet customer needs, matching
these with firm competencies and then obtaining feedback from customers on the desirability
of these new offerings. Several studies have found a consistent positive relationship between

H1.

Market orientation would positively relate to the business performance of Ghanaian
telecommunication companies.

Innovation

An innovation can be a new product or service, a new production process technology, a new
structure or administrative system, or a new plan or program pertaining to organizational
members:
Organizations innovate because of pressure from the external environment, such as competition,
deregulation, isomorphism, resource scarcity, and customer demands, or because of an internal
organizational choice, such as gaining distinctive competencies, reaching a higher level of
aspiration, and increasing the extent and quality of services [. . .] Either way, the adoption of
innovation is intended to ensure adaptive behavior, changing the organization to maintain or
improve its performance (Damanpour et al., 2009, p. 635).

Therefore, the adoption of innovation is generally intended to contribute to the performance
or effectiveness of the adopting organization (Han et al., 1998; Hurley and Hult, 1998;
Calantone et al., 2002; Agarwal et al., 2003; Lee and Tsai, 2005; Keskin, 2006).
In the services sector, it is established that firms facing environmental challenges and
uncertainty can achieve superior performance through incorporating technical and
managerial innovations in their organizational structure (Han et al., 1998). Calantone et al.
(2002, p. 517) note that ‘‘innovation capability is the most important determinant of firm
performance [. . .]’’, and citing Li and Calantone (1998) they further implied that ‘‘[. . .] firms
must be innovative to gain a competitive edge in order to survive [. . .]’’ (Calantone et al., 2002,
p. 517). Therefore, when viewed as a responsive adaptation to the changing environment or as
a pre-emptive move to influence the environment, innovation can achieve some sort of
competitive advantages, and thereby business performance (Lee and Tsai, 2005). Keskin
(2006), in his study of 157 managers of Turkish SMEs, demonstrates that firm innovativeness
has a positive impact on business performance and concludes that when firms frequently try
new ideas, seek out new ways to do things, develop new product/services, and try to be
creative in their methods of operations, they become more profitable, get higher market share,
and growth rates. Several authors have found strong positive relationships between firm
innovativeness and firm performance: see for example, Zahra and Covin (1993), Yamin et al.
(1999), Calantone et al. (2002), and Low et al. (2007). The implication of these findings is that
by adopting innovation as a strategic response to adapt to the changing environment or as a
pre-emptive move to influence the environment, telecommunication companies could improve
their business performance and competitive positions. Therefore we hypothesize that:
H2.

Innovation would positively influence the business performance of Ghanaian
telecommunication companies.

Corporate social responsibility
CSR emerged from the idea that corporate bodies are an integral part of society and for that
matter the attainment of the goals and aspirations of the society must be their preoccupation,
just as the attainment of their corporate goals are. It involves those policies, activities, or
behaviors undertaken by a firm that goes beyond the traditional economic and legal
obligations that the firm has with its target internal and external stakeholders (Bowen, 1953;

j

j

VOL. 8 NO. 3 2012 SOCIAL RESPONSIBILITY JOURNAL PAGE 331

Davis, 1960; McGuire, 1963; Carroll, 1979). In line with this, the World Bank (2004) defines
CSR as:
[. . .] the commitment of businesses to contribute to sustainable economic development-working
with employees, their families, the local community, and society at large to improve the quality of
life, in ways that are good for business and good for development.

For Carroll (1979), organizations have four responsibilities or obligations to the society in
which they operate. These are: economic, legal, ethical, and discretionary responsibilities.
The economic responsibilities are the obligations for businesses to maintain economic
wealth whilst meeting consumption needs. Legal responsibilities suggest that businesses
must fulfill their economic mission within the framework of legal requirements. Ethical
responsibilities require that businesses abide by the moral rules defining appropriate
behaviors in society. Discretionary responsibilities reflect society’s desire to see businesses
contributing to its development. Whilst the economic and legal responsibilities are
obligations that have always existed for organizations and obligations that will continue to
exist in the future regardless of further changes in the mindset of consumers, ethical and
discretionary responsibilities are not always undertaken by organizations (Drucker, 1984).

Studies in strategic management have proposed a direct association between CSR
initiatives and firm performance; however, the empirical evidence has been rather
conflicting. Fombrun and Shanley (1990), Waddock and Graves (1997), Maignan et al.
(1999), and Luo and Bhattacharya (2006), for example, found the consequences of CSR on
firm performance to be positive in their studies; however, negative consequences were
reported in earlier studies such as Vance (1975), Aupperle et al. (1985), and McGuire et al.
(1988). Qu (2009, p. 572) argues that lack of coherence between the earlier and recent
studies may be attributed to the fact that ‘‘business environments are in a state of flux and
the current business environments are becoming more favorable towards businesses that
place an emphasis on CSR’’. In a more recent study of CSR practice and motivations in
Spain, Prado-Lorenzo et al. (2008), after controlling for size of the companies, found that
disclosure of information on CSR leads to an improved corporate image which eventually
increases the rate of sales growth. They state that firms which have disclosed a greater
volume of information on CSR have experienced greater sales growth, as opposed to those
organizations which have not disclosed information publicly (Prado-Lorenzo et al., 2008,
p. 1259). Moreover, scholars also suggest that CSR activities stimulate firms’ ability to attract
more talented and committed individuals who seek to merge their work and non-work lives
(Maignan et al., 1999) and boost internal employee morale and commitment within the firm
(McGuire et al., 1990). Thus, on the basis of the foregoing discussions, we hypothesize that:
H3.

CSR would have a positive impact on the business performance of Ghanaian
telecommunication companies.

Market orientation, innovation, and CSR
Market orientation basically implies doing something new or different as a response
to market conditions and this may be perceived as a kind of innovative behavior.
Consistent with the latter assertion, Slater and Narver (1995) also state that those
firms that are market-oriented enhance the level of innovation and therefore enjoy greater
success when marketing new products. Empirically it has been demonstrated that both
innovation and market orientation have significant effects on business performance albeit
much of the variations in performance are accounted for by the mediating effect of
innovation on the market orientation-business performance relationship (Agarwal et al.,
2003). In essence, it is difficult to reduce innovativeness to market orientation, or vice versa
(Carmen and José, 2008). Thus, market-orientation is a source of ideas for new products
and services and that it should therefore positively affect the degree of innovation in firms
and that market-oriented firm’s greater understanding of its market environment should also
reduce the incidence of new product failures (Atuahene-Gima, 1996; Carmen and José,
2008). Agarwal et al. (2003) support this viewpoint when he stress that firms that are less
market-oriented are less likely to consider innovation and noted that unless protected from
competition such firms are likely to face declining performance. Therefore, firms who display

H4.

The more market-oriented Ghanaian telecommunication companies are, the higher
their innovativeness. Innovation mediates the relationship between market

In respect of relationship between market orientation and CSR, Maignan et al. (1999)
proposed a mediating role of CSR in the market orientation model. According to Maignan
et al. (1999), companies’ degree of market orientation could have some positive impacts on
the level of CSR they display, suggesting that a market orientation is an antecedent to CSR
orientation. Narver and Slater (1990) contend that since a market oriented culture focuses on
being external oriented, companies would not only be sensitive to their external publics but
also be anticipatory of their concerns. Therefore, taking a preemptive approach to address
issues that are of customers concerns should make a company stand out from its
competitors and to become a more favorable choice as suppliers of products/services to
customers (Narver and Slater, 1990). Qu (2007) undertook a study of the determinants of
CSR in China. His survey of 600 hotels resulted in 143 returned responses from top
managers. The findings of the study suggest that market orientation is the most significant
predicator of CSR. In a more recent study, Qu (2009) argues that in a business environment
where external publics such as consumers have high expectations of firms’ CSR activities,
market-oriented companies will be among the first to realize the importance of CSR and
accordingly, implement CSR initiatives in their attempt to outperform competitors. Consistent
with Qu (2009), we hypothesize that:
H5.

The more market-oriented Ghanaian telecommunication companies are, the higher
their CSR activities. CSR would mediate the relationship between market
orientation and business performance in Ghanaian telecommunication companies.

Furthermore, CSR may require firms to align the interests of external stakeholders with
changes in the behaviors of internal organizational actors and this process can entail radical
organizational transformations and major changes to business models (see Zadek, 2004;
Holmes and Moir, 2007). In this connection, CSR activities will offer firms access to intense
networks distinct from their own corporate sphere, as well as providing a different view of the
marketplace and giving early warning about shifts in public tastes and values (Yaziji, 2004).
Since external environments are sources of information, being open to the new ideas that
these knowledge flows provide is a key predictor of firm innovative performance (Fey and
Birkinshaw, 2005). Thus, it is suggested that firms that open up engagements with nonprofit
stakeholders can assimilate the information received (Clarke and Roome, 1999), and can
gain competitive advantage from the acquisition and utilization of new knowledge they have
accessed through the engagement (Sharma and Vredenburg, 1998). Since CSR disclosures
lead to improved corporate image which enhances sales (Prado-Lorenzo et al., 2008),
companies will necessarily increase innovation in response to service users’ needs viewed
as the key to enhancing sales performance. In this regard, ‘‘[. . .] CSR is understood to arise
from an increased awareness that change outside the company requires management
attention’’ (D’ Amato and Roome, 2009, p. 423). This means that a CSR initiative will results to
innovations in product/services that generates maximum social benefits (Gyves and
O’Higgens, 2008; Bartlett, 2009) which ultimately translate into improved financial
performance for the firm. We therefore test the hypothesize that:
H6.

CSR would positively affect innovation in the Ghanaian telecommunication market.
Innovation would mediate the relationship between CSR and business
performance in Ghanaian telecommunication companies.

Construct measurement
Whilst the measurement instruments of Narver and Slater (1990) and Kohli and Jaworski
(1990) appear the most used instruments for determining a company’s degree of market
orientation, Bigne et al. (2003) submit that there is no one generally accepted research
instrument to measure the incidence of market orientation in companies. Depending on the
sector, different measures and components of the market orientation construct can emerge
(Bigne et al., 2003). This indicates that market orientation measures for services firms may
differ from those of manufacturing firms devised by both Narver and Slater (1990) and Kohli
and Jaworski (1990). In this vein, this study adopted the measures developed by Kolar
(2006) for his study of market orientation in a service industry in Slovenia. The instruments
are refined versions of the MARKOR, the criteria proposed by Kohli et al. (1993). Kolar (2006)
viewed market orientation as a function of intelligence generation, intelligence
dissemination, intelligence responsiveness, and marketing culture, arguing that marketing
culture is an informal counterpart of formal managerial dimensions of market orientation and
should therefore be included in its conceptualization. The current interest is on overall
market orientation, which was derived from the composite score of the four component
measures. As contended by Keskin (2006):

Although the market orientation construct has been conceptualized into three distinctive
behavioral and cultural components in the literature, the primary emphasis has been on the
combined effect of the components in actual practice (p. 407).

The operationalization of the CSR construct is based on Maignan et al.’s (1999) approach. This
scale captures all four dimensions of CSR proposed by Carroll (1979) – namely economic,
legal, ethical and discretionary responsibilities. The present study is not interested in how the
components of CSR relate to innovation and market orientation – only overall CSR tendency of
the firms was considered. Previous studies examining CSR’s relationship to market orientation
have looked at CSR in its entirety other than its dimensions (see for example; Maignan et al.,
1999). This study is inspired by a similar approach. We arrived at the overall CSR after taking a
composite measure of the four individual factors. In measuring the extent to which the
telecommunication companies were innovative, use is made of Calantone et al.’s (2002) scale.
Calantone et al. (2002) underscore that firm innovativeness involves openness to new ideas as
an aspect of a firm’s culture by a willingness to try out new ideas, seek out new ways to do
things, be creative in its methods of operation and rate of product introduction. All questions
measuring innovation, CSR, and market orientation were presented in the form of affirmative
statements asking managers of the sampled telecommunication companies to score their
level of agreement with the statements on a five-point Likert scale ranging from 1 ¼ ‘‘strongly
disagree’’ to 5 ¼ ‘‘strongly agree’’. One approach to measuring the degree to which
companies practice various strategic orientations is to rely on the perceptions of managers.
Indeed, there is precedent for the use of this particular approach in both theory generation
(e.g. Narver and Slater, 1990; Kohli and Jaworski, 1990; Kohli et al., 1993) and theory testing
(see for example, Maignan et al., 1999; Calantone et al., 2002; Keskin, 2006; Qu, 2009)
research studies found in the management literature. Thus, the present research did not
detract from normal practice in the scientific literature. Besides, in developing countries such
as Ghana, it is often difficult to obtain objective documentary information; in most cases, such
information is deemed by companies as classified information, not open to outsiders (Ofori
and Hinson, 2007).
Following the latter submission, in operationalizing business performance, a five-item scale
covering service quality, customer satisfaction, employee satisfaction, gross profit margin,
and market share was adopted from Agarwal et al. (2003). Agarwal et al. (2003, p. 74)
underscore that ‘‘[. . .] judgmental measures of performance that include customer
satisfaction, employee satisfaction, and service quality, are important prerequisite for
profitability or objective measures of performance [. . .]’’. Managers were asked to assessed
the items on a five point Likert scale of 1 ¼ ‘‘much worse than competitors’’ to 5 ¼ ‘‘much
better than competitors’’. We later dropped the items of service quality, customer
satisfaction, and employee satisfaction from the measure of business performance due to

Sampling and data collection
Data was drawn from a quantitative survey of marketing managers and executives of all
telecommunication companies operating in Ghana. The sample used for this study was a
convenience sample – the use of convenience samples has been found relevant in previous
studies (see Hall and Lockshin, 2000; Blankson and Stokes, 2002; Osuagwu, 2006;
Mahmoud et al., 2010). Managers were considered because it is believed that their position
in their organizations gives them much control over the issues under investigation (see
Narver and Slater, 1990; Maignan et al., 1999; Calantone et al., 2002; Keskin, 2006).
Following a limited pilot study in which the questionnaire used for data collection was
pre-tested on a group within the research population in order to establish the questionnaire’s
content validity, few adjustments were made on the questionnaire before final administration.
In addressing issues related to the questionnaire’s content validity, best practice criterion
suggested by (Churchill, 1995) was applied. This included conducting among other things a
series of personal interviews involving relevant academics and professionals in marketing.
The main research data were obtained through a questionnaire administered to the
convenience sample of 200 managers from marketing related departments (marketing,
sales, customer service, innovation, business development, corporate services and social
responsibilities) of the telecommunication companies operating in the national capital
Accra. The scope was limited to Accra because all the companies located their head offices
in the capital. With the help of some research assistants from the University of Ghana
Business School, questionnaires with introductory letters were hand-delivered to
respondents. Respondents were guaranteed confidentiality, and promised an incentive
(by making the research paper available to them if they so desire) for participating in the
research. In Sub-Saharan Africa, past studies (e.g. Osuagwu, 2006) found the use of
incentives to be an excellent method for encouraging managers to participate in research.
Of the 200 copies of questionnaire administered, 115 copies were found useable, resulting
in an effective response rate of 57.5 percent percent. In line with Gounaris and Avlonitis
(2001) and Osuagwu (2006), a higher response rate might not have been possible because
of the confidential nature of some of the information requested. Nevertheless, response rates
ranging from 12 to 20 percent are considered acceptable in cross-sectional studies of this
kind (Churchill, 1995). In fact, not all the completed questionnaires were collected in one tell
swoop. There were some late respondents. In view of this, we followed Armstrong and
Overton’s (1977) guidelines and conducted independent sample t-tests for differences
between early and late respondents to check for non-response bias. All t-tests indicated an
absence of significant differences between the means of the two groups at the 0.01 level,
suggesting that the sample for this research is free from response bias. Biographic data
collected are presented in Table II.

Discussions and implications
This study has extended the Qu (2009) study that examined the joint effects of market
orientation and CSR on firm performance by integrating innovation in the model. How does
innovation function in the relationship between market orientation and business
performance? And how does CSR affect innovative performance when complemented
with market orientation? These were the two research questions that distinguished between
the present study and Qu’s (2009) work. In answering these research questions this study
developed six hypotheses from an extensive review of prior scholarships, which were later
tested in a sample of telecommunication companies in Ghana. The choice of the
telecommunication industry as a reference point is due to its growing significance in the
Ghanaian economy, the active engagement of companies in this sector in social activities
which positioned them as socially responsible citizens, and the dearth of literature on the
telecommunication sector globally as regard the incidence of market orientation, CSR, and
their impact on innovative performance.
Consistent with some prior studies in their respective areas, the present research confirms
that market orientation (e.g. Selnes et al., 1996; Kurtinaitiene, 2005; Mahmoud et al., 2010),
CSR (e.g. Fombrun and Shanley, 1990; Waddock and Graves, 1997; Maignan et al., 1999;
Luo and Bhattacharya, 2006), and innovation (e.g. Zahra and Covin, 1993; Yamin et al.,
1999; Calantone et al., 2002; Low et al., 2007) individually have significant positive impacts
on business performance. But, as shown in their respective R 2 values, we conjecture that
innovation is the most important determinant of firm performance explaining up to 18 percent

authors who seem to argue that the market orientation-business performance relationship is

CSR (Maignan et al., 1999; Qu, 2007, 2009). On this basis, we can conveniently assert that
both innovation and CSR are key mediators in the market orientation-business performance
relationship.
As underscored by Maignan et al. (1999), market-oriented cultures are conducive for
proactive corporate citizenship. This assertion is reinforced by the present study’s findings
which suggest that market orientation facilitates the practice of CSR. This may be explained
by the fact that ‘‘the implication of a given magnitude of market orientation is that a business
is, to some extent, sensitive and responsive to any stakeholder or issue that may affect its
long-term performance’’ (Narver and Slater, 1990, p. 34). In addition, according to the
results, both CSR and market orientation are antecedents of organizational innovativeness.
By implication, innovative firms are invariably market-oriented (Maydeu-Olivares and Lado,
2003; Carmen and José, 2008), and are more likely to be responsible corporate citizens (see
Bartlett, 2009, for a discussion on CSR and innovation).
With regard to the study’s theoretical implications, the results indicate that market orientation
does not operate in isolation from other sources of competitive advantage. In other words,
though a market-oriented culture is important to stimulate business performance, this study
emphasizes that the co-adoption of market orientation strategy and other strategic
orientations, such as innovation and CSR initiatives, has far more positive implication to
business performance. In this connection, we agree perfectly with Slater and Narver’s (1995,
p. 63) proposition that creating a market orientation should only be the starting point. Again,
in contrast to existing marketing research that stress the direct effect of CSR on business
performance (Balabanis et al., 1998; Luo and Homburg, 2007; Prado-Lorenzo et al., 2008),
our research indicates that CSR may be more useful in firms that are more innovative in
nature. Overall, this chained relationship from CSR to innovation to a firm’s business
performance that includes market share and profitability suggests that innovativeness
represents one of the underlying pathways through which the financial potential of CSR is
realized and capitalized. The notion that the extent to which CSR is beneficial to the firm is
determined by how much CSR drives innovation points further research in a more precise
direction when evaluating the ultimate impact of CSR.
Practically, our findings on the role of innovation in this study may improve managers
understanding of why innovation matters. Specifically, this study affirms that the
interrelationship between market orientation and innovation has been recognized as one
of the key factors in performance (see Maydeu-Olivares and Lado, 2003; Agarwal et al.,
2003; Carmen and José, 2008 for a discussion on innovation and market orientation). The
present study provides some support that innovation facilitates the conversion of
market-oriented business philosophy into superior corporate performance. Integrating an
innovation strategy into a firm’s market orientation strategy should provide a more coherent
and comprehensive market development road map for the organization to follow. In line with
Carmen and José (2008), the concept of market orientation, by which firms aim to develop
an offer which creates greater value for their customers, does not restrict innovation strategy,
but rather acts as a spur, since an effort is made to become aware of and anticipate the
needs of customers in order to adapt innovations to their expectations. This research
suggests that the predominant sources of ideas for new products/services in services sector
are the marketing activities, which implies that the magnitude and the effectiveness of the

Furthermore, Brown and Dacin (1997, p. 68) cited a marketer who pondered that ‘‘we do all
good things [. . .] but we don’t know if we get anything out of it.’’ Apparently, the decision to
undertake CSR activities is an important issue with strong managerial implications since
prudent managers are confronted with tough choices in allocating their limited resources and
in prioritizing different strategic initiatives. Indeed, according to our results, marketers that
focus on ‘‘doing good’’ (CSR) will position their organizations to achieve a superior business
performance. Though this may not be guaranteed, innovation is particularly necessary to
transfer both market-oriented corporate culture and CSR activities into superior performance
outcomes. Our findings suggest that, beyond customer satisfaction (Luo and Bhattacharya,
2006) and improved corporate image (Prado-Lorenzo et al., 2008), CSR would stimulate
company’s business performance especially in terms of market share and gross profit margin
with innovation as the core competence for which CSR activities would drive greater
performance. These findings are hardly surprising in that past studies have shown that ‘‘[. . .]
without proper support of corporate abilities, such as innovativeness, CSR can be harmful to
firm performance’’ (Luo and Bhattacharya, 2006, p. 16). In this conception, it is imperative that
managers consider CSR initiatives in the light of the firm’s corporate abilities. When managers
fail to align their CSR initiatives with internal factors, they put their organization at risk which
could potentially lead to poor market performance (Luo and Bhattacharya, 2006). It is
therefore reasonable that marketing managers carefully scrutinize the organizational context
in totality before implementing CSR initiatives (Holmes and Moir, 2007).

Conclusions, limitations and future research
This research concludes that the market orientation-performance relationship can be more
complex than expected with firms having to rely on some competences in order to turn the
former into a successful business strategy. According to this study, two of such key
competences are CSR and innovation. Our findings seem to indicate that even ‘‘doing
good’’ requires a sense of innovation for it to trigger any significant financial performance for
the business. The findings of the present study supported all six hypotheses relating to the
research model, thereby, confirming the significance of innovation for both CSR and market
orientation strategies. Moreover, the uncovered mediated results provide marketing
practitioners with better understanding of the market orientation’s pathway to business
performance. The methodological limitations encountered whilst completing the study are
carefully discussed below.
The study relied solely on information provided by managers and executives working in
marketing related positions. In essence, the data gathered are reflective of managers’
evaluation of market orientation, CSR, innovation and business performance as measured in
terms of market share and profitability. Scholars argued that organizational activities are
better depicted when multi-informant research design is used (Maignan et al., 1999).
Therefore, this caveat may be addressed by future research that combines managers’
evaluation with a survey of consumers, employees, and other relevant stakeholders,
comparing non-intuitive measures of customers, for instance, with managers’ evaluation to
establish the true facts. This will be the focus of our next line of research.
It is further suggested that future research in the area use longitudinal design and objective
performance measures. In so doing, the financial statements of companies, for instance,
may be studied over three or more years in order to detect the direction of changes in the
level of key financial performance indicants. Again, this may be compared with managers’
evaluation to ascertain similarities and differences in results. It is worthy of note that some
market orientation studies, for instance, reported positive effects of market orientation on
subjective performance measures, yet when objective performance indicators were used,
the findings have been mixed (Maydeu-Olivares and Lado, 2003; Osuagwu, 2006).
In addition, there is need for an examination of the themes covered by CSR in the telecom
sector and the extent to which the CSR activities of telecom companies in Ghana address

relevant themes (i.e. social and environmental problems associated with telecom). The
present study’s replication of the existing CSR scale did not allowed us to identify these
themes, and so we propose to undertake this in our future study. Finally, a comparative
analysis between industries or countries in different geographical and cultural areas could
generate an interesting line of future research. The obtainment of results similar to those in
this study in other industries or countries would allow their generalization.
