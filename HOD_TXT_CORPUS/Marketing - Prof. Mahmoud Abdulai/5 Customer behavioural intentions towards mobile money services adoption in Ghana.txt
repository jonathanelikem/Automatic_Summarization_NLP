ABSTRACT

ARTICLE HISTORY

This study examined the determinants of mobile money service
usage intentions and assessed the effect of social influence (SI) on
mobile money services adoption and behavioural intentions (BIs).
The sample of the study comprised 300 mobile money service
users in Ghana. Guided by the conceptual framework and two
theories identified to have an effect on technology adoption and
consumer behaviour, eight hypotheses were developed and
tested using Structural Equation Modelling Techniques. It is
discovered that perceived ease of use, perceived usefulness,
perceived trust and perceived cost of use have a strong influence
on mobile money service usage. The study found SI to have a
significant effect on the adoption and BI. Providers ensure that
their mobile application services are simple to operate, fulfil
specific consumers’ needs, protect consumers’ accounts to ensure
trust and are affordable, hence positively influencing consumers’
adoption of services.

Introduction
Mobile phone coverage and adoption in sub-Saharan Africa have increased substantially
over the past decade (PWC, 2016). Until 1999, less than 1% of the populace had access to
mobile phones and this has increased to over 55% (GSMA, 2009). In Ghana in particular,
mobile phone adoption and usage have increased from 150,000 mobile phone subscribers
in 2000 to approximately 11 million subscribers in 2009 (Wireless Intelligence, 2012). This
number keeps growing due to the services of telecommunication operators across the
continent. One of such new services is the mobile money, which, according to Aker and
Mbiti (2010), is a product that allows clients to use text messages to store value in an
account accessible by the handset, convert cash in and out of the stored value account,
and transfer value between users. Due to technological development, mobile phones
are used to make payments, send money to family members and store monetary value
safely in Ghana.
Seven years after the launch of the first mobile money solution in Ghana, the service can
be said to be a game changer in the country’s financial services industry (PWC, 2016). From
a modest start in 2009, mobile money solutions now account for cash flows that compare

from GH¢11.2b in 2014. Clearly, this shows a rise that evidences the acceptance of the

The Ghana mobile telecommunication industry (GMTI) is one of the keenly competitive
markets in sub-Sahara Africa with six major international players (Yeboah-Asiamah,

years. The introduction of mobile money services in Ghana has transformed how business
operates (Tobbin & Kowornu, 2011). For example, most service providers and retail outlets
encourage mobile money subscribers to pay directly for products and services from their
mobile money wallet in order to avoid the use of physical cash payment. Consumers have
quick access to their monies when it is on their phone than going through the rigid requirements of opening bank accounts and queuing at banks to effect minor cash transactions. It
will be of interest to academicians and practitioners to understand factors that influence
mobile money service adoption so as to develop policies that will enhance and sustain
the use of mobile money services, especially in developing economies such as Ghana.
Interestingly, researchers have investigated and reported some factors that may influence mobile money adoption (Aker & Mbiti, 2010; Dermish, Kneiding, Leishman, & Mas,
2011; Gencer, 2011; Kleijnen, Lee, & Wetzels, 2009; Kusimba, Chaggar, Gross, & Kunyu,
2013; Mehrtens, Cragg, & Mills, 2011; Osei-Assibey, 2014). Mehrtens et al. (2011) indicate
that the adoption of mobile money services by consumers is influenced by factors such
as perceived benefits, readiness, external pressures, changes in structure, skills and openness to external sources of information. Consumers may resist a product uptake when the
adoption or use of the product requires significant alterations in the consumers’ value
systems, as well as their established behavioural patterns, norms, habits and traditions
(Kleijnen et al., 2009). In the Ghanaian context, Osei-Assibey (2014) studied the drivers
of behavioural intention (BI) of mobile money adoption among indigenous money collectors (called susu). The study found perceived risk (PR), education level, relative advantage
and the age of the collector to be statistically significant in influencing the BI of mobile
money services adoption.
According to Aker and Wilson (2013), deciding to use mobile money services often
depends on the usage by the members of one’s social network (which is particularly an
issue for mobile money transfers). Individual customers live in a sociocultural environment
and, as a result, some social influence (SI) might affect either positively or otherwise their
decisions towards the usage of a particular payment system (Kuhlmeier & Knight, 2005;
Mohd Suki, 2013; Tian, Shi, & Yang, 2009). SI is defined as the degree to which an individual’s friends, family members and other close relatives perceive the importance to use a
system (Ajzen & Fishbein, 1980; Choi, Choi, Kim, & Yu, 2003; Venkatesh, Morris, Davis, &
Davis, 2003). Mohd Suki (2013) argue that certain customers are mindful of what they purchase or use and, thus, whether their friends would like the product or the product would
fit in with their social group.

Considering the performance of the Mobile Money (MM) business in Ghana and the
varying factors put forward by scholars (Kusimba et al., 2013; Osei-Assibey, 2014), it will
be prudent to empirically examine these factors. This will provide practitioners and academics contemporary evidence on why consumers are patronizing the MM business.
Thus, the aim of this research is to examine factors influencing customer BIs towards
mobile money service usage and the moderating role of SI. Having established the justification and aim of this study, the paper is organized as follows: The theoretical foundations and research hypotheses are presented next. This is followed by the research
methodology. Data analysis and findings as well as the discussions and implications are
presented in that order. The final section discusses the conclusions, limitations and direction for future studies.

Theoretical foundation and research hypotheses
Two models have been linked to mobile money research and provide a broad theoretical
guideline for this study. They are:
1. The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)
2. Diffusion of Innovation Model (DIM)

Technology acceptance model
The TAM and the DIM are used in examining subscriber behaviour towards the mobile
money services in this study. The TAM was proposed by Davis (1989) to predict users’
acceptance and use of any information technology. The TAM has been specifically recommended to model the acceptance of Information Technologies and Information
Systems (Davis, 1989; Davis, Bagozzi, & Warshaw, 1989) due to its sound influence on
the acceptance of behaviour (Lymperopoulos & Chaniotakis, 2005). The theory again
has also been argued to have an empirical advantage over some theories such as
Theory of Planned Behaviour (Mathieson, 1991).
Some areas that the TAM has been used in the acceptance of Information Technologies
and Information Systems include the acceptance of commercial mobile services (Dahlberg,
Mallat, & Oorni, 2003; Jiang & Deng, 2011; Wei, Marthandan, Chong, Ooi, & Arumugam,
2009; Wei, Xinyan, & Yue, 2011), online shopping (Gefen, Karahanna, & Straub, 2003)
and e-commerce (Pavlou, 2003). According to Davis (1989), a person’s BIs to use a particular technology are based on two beliefs, which are the perceived ease of use (PEOU) and
perceived usefulness (PU). These two beliefs will be investigated in the next subsections to
establish how they influence the BIs of people in using a particular technology (i.e. mobile
money services).

Perceived ease of use (PEOU)
Perceived ease of use is the extent to which an individual believes that a system would be
easy to use and that the system should not be complex in order to encourage its adoption
(Carter & Belanger, 2004; Rogers, 1995). This means to use a particular form of technology,
little effort should be needed by the user (Liu & Li, 2010). This effort can be physical and
mental (Davis, 1993; Taylor & Todd, 1995). Extensive researches have documented

evidence on the significant effect of PEOU on user BIs (see Adam, Nelson, & Todd, 1992;
Davis, 1989; Carter & Belanger, 2004; Guriting & Ndubisi, 2006; Ramayah, Jantan, &
Aafaqi, 2003; Ramayah, Siron, Dahlan, & Mohamad, 2002). In the mobile money services,
PEOU includes how easy the registration procedure is, ease of use of the payment
method, easy access to customer services, minimal steps required to make a payment,
availability of mobile money transfer agents and how accessible the service is on
mobile phones with the basic features and software. With these easiness, the study
hypothesized that:
H1: Perceived ease of use of the mobile money system will influence customer BIs in Ghana.

Perceived usefulness (PU)
Lopez-Nicolas, Molina-Castillo, and Bouwman (2008) argued that the system should be
able to assist the consumer to carry out a job easier, quicker and in better quality. The literature suggests that PU is positively linked with the adoption of mobile payment (Kim,
Mirusmonov, & Lee, 2010; Padashetty & Krishna, 2013; Zhou, 2011). PU has been seen
to positively influence the adoption of the mobile payment system across countries
such as Finland, the USA, Japan and Germany (Guhr, Loi, Wiegard, & Breitner, 2013).
According to Tobbin and Kowornu (2011), the intention to use mobile money services
will increase if the belief in its usefulness also increases (Luarn & Lin, 2005). The ultimate
reason people exploit the use of mobile money services is that they find them useful.
Some other services introduced into the mobile services include paying of utility bills
and other fees, which is likely to increase the benefit of the service to customers. It can
be hypothesized that:
H2: Perceived usefulness of the mobile money services will influence customer BIs in Ghana.

Diffusion of innovation model
The DIM propounded by Rogers (2003) advances the communication and adoption of new
ideas and technologies. Diffusion is the process by which an innovation is communicated
through certain channels over time and among the members of a social system (Lee, 2007;
Lee, Hsieh, & Hsu, 2011; Rogers, 1983, 2003; Shin, 2007). The diffusion of innovation assesses
how, why and at what rate new ideas and technology are communicated and adopted by
consumers. The diffusion process for all innovations consists of individuals talking to one
another about the new idea, thus decreasing the perceived uncertainty of the innovation
so as to encourage its adoption and usage. Rogers (1995, 2003) identified five intrinsic
characteristics/factors that strongly influence whether or not someone will adopt an innovation. These factors are relative advantage, complexity, compatibility, trialability and
observability. Three of these factors are employed in this study based on contextual
reasons. These three factors were chosen based on the fact that they were found to be
important in consumer decision behaviour as to whether to adopt a particular technology
or not. Also, comparison could easily be made to other prior studies (Osei-Assibey, 2014).

Relative advantage
The degree to which an innovation is perceived as a better alternative to other products
and services is termed relative advantage (Puschel, Mazzon, & Hernandez, 2010).

According to Pihlajamäki (2004), relative advantage can also be examined from the value
and the cost of the service to the user. This means that using mobile money services
should be better than any traditional mode of payment and that the value gained in
using mobile money services should outweigh the cost of the service to the consumer.
Studies have consistently found relative advantage to affect positively users’ intention
to use systems across different participants (Lee, 2007; Lee et al., 2011; Shin, 2007). To
Lee et al. (2011), users are more prone to adopt a new technology (mobile money services)
when they perceive that the system offers them a relative advantage over an existing one,
such as bank or other money transfer services.
H3: Relative advantage of the mobile money services will influence customer BIs to adopt it.

Perceived trust
Perceived trust is the customers’ feeling of security, confidence and willingness to rely on a
system, product or service that it will not fail them (Chung & Kwon, 2009; Kim, Chung, &
Lee, 2011). Thus, the perceived confidence and assurance customers have in the mobile
money service that the service is free from hackers and fraudsters will have a significant
influence on the adoption rate of the service. The use of the mobile money service
system should be able to overcome users’ distrust (Siau & Shen, 2003). This means the
user should be provided with some assurance that the system will be provided with
minimum possible hindrance from hackers and fraudsters using the mobile money services. Potential adopters of the mobile money services need to have a belief that the
network is reliable and that they can trust the system at least more than their existing practices of saving and transferring money.
Trust in mobile money services is likely to influence the BI of consumers to accept/use
the services. Previous studies have found perceived trust as a significant determinant influencing users’ BIs to use any technology (Jarvenpaa & Staples, 2000; Mallat, 2007; Myers,
1997; Siau & Shen, 2003; Tobbin, 2010; Zhou, 2011). The idea that the mobile money
system is run on an SMS alert where users are notified of any transaction they perform
through the SMS alert, the use of Pincodes and the idea that users do not lose the
money when they lose their mobile device is likely to influence users’ intention to
adopt the services.
H4: Perceived trust will influence customer BIs to adopt mobile money services in Ghana.

Perceived complexity
Complexity is the end users’ perceived level of difficulty in understanding innovations and
their ease of use (Antovski & Ribarski, 2009). This is the extent to which customers perceive
the innovative idea, product or service as easy to understand and use. Thus, customers will
readily adopt the mobile money service if they perceive that the service is flexible and has
easily understandable steps which do not require the help of third parties. The ease with
which people can transfer and withdraw money is crucial to the acceptance of the service.
Empirical studies have found that the more complex the end users perceived a new technology, the lower the users’ intention to use the system (Lee, 2007; Tobbin, 2011).
Antovski and Ribarski (2009, p. 20) argue that a system which is ‘intuitive, flexible, easy
to install and has few steps or clicks will contribute to the ease of use’ as a result of it not

being complex. The notion that users can easily transfer money to friends by following
simple steps on their mobile devices reduces the complexity of the system. Also, that
users can easily withdraw money from an agent by providing secret codes and confirming
to the withdrawal makes the mobile money system less complex.
H5: Perceived complexity (PC) will influence customer BIs to adopt mobile money services in
Ghana.

Perceived risks
Perceived risks (PRs) are the beliefs of the uncertainty regarding possible negative consequences (dangers) in using a physical good or service (Featherman & Pavlou, 2003). The
perception of the possible risk exposure of a user is when his/her private information is
known to others (Cox & Rich, 1964; Featherman & Pavlou, 2003; Nyshadham, 2000).
When consumers perceive any possible losses that could be created due to the uncertainties of using mobile money services, their perception of risk will increase. The losses may
include any unfavourable consequences to consumers, such as financial loss, the violation
of privacy, dissatisfaction with performance, psychological anxiety or discomfort, time
waiting and long queues.
Perceived risk is the doubts, uncertainties or perceived dangers consumers have
regarding the outcome of their purchase decision (Arslan, Geçti, & Zengin, 2013). Given
the unique characteristics of a service, its PR is usually higher than that of a product
(Carter, Weerakkody, Phillips, & Dwivedi, 2016; de Kerviler, Demoulin, & Zidda, 2016).
The perception of possible risk exposure of a mobile money client is a potential hindrance
to the adoption of the service. The PR associated with the mobile money service could be
financial, social, functional (performance risk) or psychological risk (Arslan et al., 2013; de
Kerviler et al., 2016; Kim, Jang, & Yang, 2016; Paluch & Wünderlich, 2016). Financial risk is
the possibility of a client losing money due to unauthorized persons having access to the
client’s mobile money account. Social risk is the influence or perception of society regarding the service. For instance, the perception that the mobile money service is for the
working-class people who do not have a bank account could be a potential barrier to
the adoption of the service, since an individual may feel loss of image. Functional risk is
the inability of the service or the product to perform to the satisfaction of the customer;
for example, fear of not having access to one’s money at some point in time due to
system or network failure.
H6: Perceived risks will influence customer BIs to adopt mobile money services in Ghana.

Perceived cost of use
The rate of acceptance and adoption of mobile money service among the unbanked
population is the cost of making transactions associated with the service (Tobbin, 2012).
According to Luarn and Lin (2005) and Tobbin (2012), individuals are likely to adopt
mobile money services if they perceived that the cost is acceptable compared to other
existing alternatives of the service. If individuals perceive the cost of mobile phones to
be high, the price of SIM cards to be high, and the registration fees and other transaction
costs to be higher, their intention to adopt will be affected.
H7: Perceived cost of use will influence customer BIs to adopt mobile money services in Ghana.

Moderating role of SI
Social influence is defined as the beliefs an individual holds regarding the expectations of
others (concerning mobile money services) as well as the individual’s motivation to
comply with these expectations (Dunn, Mohr, Wilson, & Wittert, 2011). It is an individual’s
belief regarding whether significant others (friends and family) think he or she should have
a mobile money account and the extent to which the individual is willing to comply these
beliefs would have a strong influence on the adoption of the mobile money service. It is
the degree to which an individual’s friends/colleagues, family members and other close
relatives perceive the importance to use the new system (Choi et al., 2003; Venkatesh
et al., 2003). SI represents the social pressures on individuals to perform or not perform
a particular behaviour (engage in mobile money services). The perception that mobile
money services are for the unbanked population can negatively influence an individual’s
adoption of the service. Notwithstanding that, families and friends who are using mobile
money can influence others to use the system. Thus, people’s thought, feelings and behaviours are not just influenced by their individual characters, but through SI, by what relevant other people think, feel or do, as well.
As noted by Tan and Teo (2000), relevant references such as the adopter’s friends, family
and colleagues/peers may all influence the adoption decision. The reason being that a user
may be reluctant to participate in a certain behaviour at first, but when taking into consideration the importance of what the referents (i.e. friends/peers, family, etc.) think, the
user may agree to the behaviour (Venkatesh & Morris, 2000). SI can be driven by many
factors (Smith & Mackie, 2015). These include the desire to hold correct opinions (and
the assumption that what others think is likely correct and accurate, typically called informational influence); the desire to seek social rewards by agreeing with others (typically
called normative influence) and the desire to maintain and express an important identity
(typically called referent informational or social identity-based influence).
According to Aker and Wilson (2013), deciding to use mobile money services often
depends on the usage by the members of one’s social network (which is particularly an
issue for mobile money transfers). The unbanked populace may be forced to use
mobile money as it can be used to perform those services as saving, transferring and
receiving money (Aker & Wilson, 2013). Notwithstanding that, families and friends who
are using mobile money can influence others to use the system. As noted by Tan and
Teo (2000), relevant references such as the adopter’s friends, family and colleagues/
peers may all influence the adoption decision.
A study conducted by Thomas, Jewell, and Wiggins Johnson (2015) on SI dubbed
‘Hidden consumption behaviour: an alternative response to social group influence’
found out that when there is severe non-conformity of certain consumption to a particular
social group, individuals in such a group are likely to hide their consumption behaviour.
Similarly, Mohd Suki (2003) revealed that SI and the brand name of the product have
an effect on the increasing demands for smartphones. A recent study conducted by
Persaud and Schillo (2017) found that social identity and SI affect individuals’ purchase
intentions of organic food among Canadians. Linking the findings of previous studies to
the current study implies that users of mobile money may be reluctant to participate
based on the severe non-conformity of the social group, or appreciate the use of

Methodology
The focus of this study is to establish the relationship between various constructs (adoption and BIs) of mobile money services. Mobile Telecommunication Network (MTN) subscribers constituted the study population. MTN is the leading market cellular mobile
phone operator, connecting subscribers in 22 countries in Africa, Asia and the Middle
East. Also, the company is the leading mobile telecommunications industry in Ghana
with over 12 million subscribers and offering its valued subscribers a range of exciting products and services under postpaid and prepaid subscriptions. MTN’s network covers all 10
regional capitals, major cities and many rural and remote areas. The service is offered by
MTN in partnership with 10 banks. Subscribers can use MTN Mobile Money to send and
receive money, top-up MTN airtime, pay bills (DStv, ECG Postpaid, MTN Postpaid, School
fees, etc.), buy and pay for insurance, pay employee salaries, and pay for airline tickets
and other goods and services. Currently, more than 2 million Ghanaians are registered
as active users. Respondents were selected from all the 10 regional capitals (Kumasi,
Accra, Cape Coast, Takoradi, Sunyani, Tamale, Wa, Koforidua, Ho and Bolgatanga).
A purposive sampling method was used to select the various study areas and simple
random sampling was used to sample the subscribers. The purposive sampling method
was used to sample the regional business units because majority of users of mobile
money services reside in these units. The simple random method was used to give an
equal chance to the users of the service. This was done by randomly selecting users,
who were physically present at each of the business/service centre until specified
members or units were selected. Three hundred and fifty respondents were selected;

however, 300 subscribers’ responses were found to be useable after the initial screening
(Malhotra & Birks, 2007).
A Likert scale which ranged from 1 = strongly disagree to 5 = strongly agree was used.
The questions were adapted from the researchers who either used the questions in a
mobile payment or a mobile banking environment (Table 1).
Perceived usefulness (PU) and PEOU were adapted from Davis (1989), Davis et al. (1989)
and Moore and Benbasat (1991). Social influence (SI) was adapted from Ajzen (1991).
Future behavioural intention (BI) was adapted from Davis (1989) and Davis et al. (1989).
On the other hand, relative advantage (RA) and perceived complexity (PC) were measured
by items adapted from Moore and Benbasat’s (1991) study. Perceived risk (PR) was
adapted from Ndubisi and Sinti (2006) and Cruz, Laukkanen, and Muñoz (2009). Perceived
cost of use (PCOU) was measured by adapting the instrument from Luarn and Lin (2005)
and perceived trust was adapted from Pavlou (2003). In all, 30 items were used to measure
nine constructs. Three items each were used to measure PCOU, PU, SI, BI, PRs, PC, and four
items each were used to measure RA, perceived trust (PT) and PCOU.
The questionnaire was in three (3) sections. The first (section A) part measured the
demographic characteristics of the respondents, the second (section B) and the third
(section C) part measured both factors of adoption and the BIs of mobile money service
subscribers. The collection of the data lasted for three weeks. The data collected from
this study were analysed with the help of SPSS version 20 and SmartPLS 3.2.4. The data
were coded and screened for outliers or any other variation in the data set. Descriptive
analysis was used for demographic variables and hypotheses were tested using structural
equation modelling (SEM).
Validations were performed through face, content and construct validations. A pilot test
was used to assess face validity. The Ghana business school students use MM for various
financial services on campus, similar to that of the targeted population. Hence, the pilot
test was done at the University of Ghana Business School, where 30 students were
sampled. The aim of the test was to identify items that were ambiguous for modification;
hence, unclear statements were correctly worded. Colleagues and teaching assistants in
the university used their expert opinions to examine the understandability and suitability
of the questions.
Confirmatory factor analysis was used to purify the measures, assess the unidimensionality of the scale items and assess discriminant validity (whether scores on the measure do
not correlate with variations that should differ, or are expected by theory to not correlate
with the construct) among these constructs.

Results and findings

the adequacy of the measurement model of the principal constructs and the predictive
relevance of the conceptual model, and also to test the hypothesized relationships (see
Figure 2). PLS-SEM was suitable as the main objective of the study was concerned with
maximizing the prediction of respective constructs to predict the contribution of
various antecedents on intention to use mobile money services.

Additionally, PLS-SEM was selected because of its distribution-free assumption, which
was appropriate for the purpose of this study. The PLS algorithm permits each indicator
to vary in how much it contributes to the composite score of the latent variable,
instead of assuming equal weight for all indicators of a scale (Chin, Marcolin, &
Newsted, 2003). For sample size considerations in PLS-SEM, according to Hair, Ringle,
and Sarstedt (2011, p. 144), as a common rule of thumb for appropriate sample size for
testing PLS-SEM models is the rule of 10, which suggests 10 times the largest number
of structural paths directed at a particular latent construct in the structural model. In
this study, the highest number of structural paths (seven independent variables) directed
at a latent construct (intentions to use mobile money services) at a time was seven. Therefore, 7 multiplied by 10 gives 70 cases; thus, our sample of 300 respondents could be
described as satisfactory.
Although PLS-SEM estimates both the measurement model and the structural model
concurrently, the assessment of the estimated full model followed Hair et al.’s (2011,
p. 144) two-step approach: the examination of the measurement (outer) model
before the estimation of the structural (inner) model. While the measurement model
allows us to examine whether the constructs are measured with satisfactory accuracy,
the structural model helps us to assess the explanatory power of the model (see
Cool, Dierickx, & Jemison, 1989). The former is, however, a prerequisite for the latter
as it only makes sense to evaluate the structural model when the measurement
model shows evidence of reliability and validity (Henseler, Ringle, & Sinkovics, 2009).

Assessment of the measurement model
All the constructs in the study are reflective and not formative; hence, in order to validate
the model fitness, there is the need to examine the composite reliabilities (CRs), average
variance extracted (AVE), item loadings’ size and significance, and discriminant validity
(see Liang, Saraf, Hu, & Xue, 2007). Cronbach’s alpha and the CR test revealed that all constructs showed a value above the threshold (0.7 for both Cronbach’s alpha and CR,
adopted from Bagozzi & Yi, 2012) (see Table 4).

Convergent validity
To test for convergent validity, CR factor loading and AVE were examined. It is acceptable if
an individual item factor loading is greater than 0.7, CR exceeds 0.7 and AVE exceeds 0.5
(Gefen, Straub, & Boudreau, 2000) (see Table 5). All loadings for the reflective constructs
exceeded 0.7 and were shown to be significant at Bootstrap t-statistics (a. 0:01), while satisfying the CR and AVE criteria.

Assessment of the structural model
Having examined the measurement (inner) model to be suitable (i.e. reliable and valid), the
next stage is to assess the structural model. To do this, it is important to first examine the
multiple squared correlation coefficients (R 2) for each dependent (endogenous) variable in
the structural part (Chin, 1998), showing the amount of variance in the dependent variable
explained by its corresponding model. In PLS-SEM, the interpretation of the R 2 is not different from the traditional MLR (Chin, 1998), and obviously, there are no definite guidelines
for the evaluation of a model as ‘good or bad’ only based upon the magnitude of R 2 as this
will be dependent on several factors.

Results of the structural model
In PLS-SEM, structural models’ validity is assessed through the strength of the regression
weights, t-values, p-values for significance of t-statistics, as well as effect sizes of the independent variables on the dependent variables (Chin, 2010; Hair et al., 2011). The results of
the hypotheses testing are presented in Table 6.
Hypothesis 1 (PEOU of the mobile money system will influence customer BIs) was
supported in the study as indicated in Table 6. Here, the hypothesis examines the
relationship between PEOU and BIs towards mobile money use. As shown in Table 6,
the observed regression weights, t-value and p-value (t = 2.740, p < .005) indicated that
the relationship is found to be positive and significant. The results confirm that the
level of consumers’ adoption of mobile money services is dependent on the ease of use
of the services. Extensive research have documented evidence on the significant effect
of PEOU on user BIs (see Adam et al., 1992; Davis, 1989; Guriting & Ndubisi, 2006;
Ramayah et al., 2002; Ramayah et al., 2003). This study’s findings add to the existing
body of evidence on the relationship between PEOU and customer BI towards the adoption of mobile money services.
Hypothesis 2 (PU of the mobile money services will influence customer BIs towards mobile
money services adoption) considers the relationship between PU and customer BIs towards
mobile money adoption. As shown in Table 6, PU is positively and significantly related to
customer BI towards mobile money adoption. This supports hypothesis 2. Significant and
positive effects of PU on BI were observed as shown by the regression coefficient (t-value
= 2.718, p < .005). The findings suggest the extent to which an individual believes that PU
of mobile money services will determine whether they will choose to adopt and use the
service or not. The result is consistent with Tobbin and Kowornu’s (2011) findings that the
degree to which a person believes that using a particular system would enhance his or her

line with some scholarly works in the extant literature (Kim et al., 2010; Padashetty &
Krishna, 2013; Zhou, 2011). The findings also corroborate other countries’ studies such
as Finland, the USA, Japan and Germany (Guhr et al., 2013).
Hypothesis 3 tested the relationship between RA and BI towards the adoption of mobile
money services. The relationship between RA and BI was positive but not significant
(t-value = 1.092, p < .275). The results indicate that RA positively predicts customers’ BIs
in the mobile money services. However, the relationship between RA and BI was not significant. This could be attributed to the service-oriented nature of the mobile money services. Differentiating one service provider from others is usually difficult since the services
are normally the same. This might have accounted for the insignificant relationship
between RA and BI.
Hypothesis 4 tested the relationship between perceived trust and customer BI towards
the adoption of the mobile money services. The results revealed a positive and significant
(t-value = 3.009, p < .003) relationship between PT and BI towards mobile money services
among the data sampled. This shows that trust is a key factor in determining the customer
BI towards the adoption of mobile money services among the sample investigated. This
finding resonates with previous studies that have found perceived trust as a significant
determinant influencing users’ BIs to use any technology (Jarvenpaa & Staples, 2000;
Mallat, 2007; Siau & Shen, 2003; Tobbin, 2010; Zhou, 2011). This finding supports the argument that the entire financial system is based on trust.
Hypothesis 5 examined the effect of PC and behavioural BIs in the adoption and usage
of mobile money services. The regression path between PC and BI was significant (t-value
= 1.116, p < .265). This finding gives support to other empirical studies in the academic literature. For instance, Lee (2007) and Tobbin (2011) have found that the more complex the
end users perceived a new technology to be, the lower the users’ intention to use the
system.
The confidence a user has in a mobile device to perform an expected activity without
taking advantage of the result is an indication that trust is a very significant ingredient to
the customer’s intent to act favourably towards mobile money. Hypothesis 5, which
addresses the statement that PC will influence customer BI towards mobile money
adoption, was therefore supported.
Hypothesis 6 was tested to investigate the effect of PR on BIs towards the adoption of
mobile money services. The regression coefficient and t-value from PR and BIs were not
significant (t-value = 0.603, p < .547). The relationship between PR and BIs was found
not to be significant, given the results of the t-value and p-values. Given the unique characteristics of a service, its PR is usually higher than its physical good (Carter et al., 2016; de
Kerviler et al., 2016). Surprisingly, the participants in this study did not consider PR to be
relevant in making a decision with respect to their BIs towards the adoption of mobile
money services.

a significant and positive relationship between PCOU and BI. Hence hypothesis 7 was supported. This finding indicates that the cost inovlved in patronising mobile money services
by consumers is important to them. This corroborates what exists in the extant literature

Discussions and implications
This study was undertaken to assess the influence of eight exogenous variables on the BIs of
users of mobile money services in Ghana. Specifically, the study sought to identify the key
drivers of mobile money adoption and usage. The result shows that there is a positive
relationship between five of the constructs and the dependent variable, while three independent variables were not significant. The details are explained below. The result shows
that mobile money services are used mostly for buying airtime, and receiving and transferring money. Other uses such as paying bills and managing savings were relatively used by
fewer respondents. It can be implied that users of the service do not trust or perceived some
risks with the use of mobile money service for paying bills or managing savings. Using
mobile money for savings is popular among the unbanked. The use of mobile money services according to Gutierrez and Choi (2014) is for sending and receiving money. Looking

THE SERVICE INDUSTRIES JOURNAL

17

at the respondents’ age and level of education, their access to the banking system is high;
therefore, they may not see the need to use mobile money for savings.
The findings suggest that even though mobile money services are designed to provide
greater convenience to the everyday life of users, their usage has been limited to few
service types. Users are not tapping into the full opportunities mobile money services
offer, such as convenience of paying for goods and services, payment of utility bills and
broader financial inclusion to the unbanked through savings on the mobile money
wallet. The study revealed that the factors that significantly lead to the adoption of
mobile money services were perceived use, PEOU, perceived trust, PCOU and SI. The
result is consistent with the existing literature (Osei-Assibey, 2014; Tobbin & Kowornu,
2011). By implication, among other things, the respondents are more likely to adopt
and use the mobile money platform when it is seen to be user-friendly; when they see
the service to be useful, reliable and relatively cheaper and when it can be counted on
to deliver as expected.
The overall model is able to strongly predict customers’ BI towards mobile money adoption and usage. The five drivers of PEOU, PU, PT, PCOU and SI predict BI by 64.7% (R 2 of
0.647), in the research context. This means that about 65% of factors that will influence
customers to engage in mobile money services are explained by the exogenous variables
tested in the study. The study revealed that there were significant differences between low
and high SI on PEOU and PU of mobile money services. Nonetheless, SI was not found to
be significant with PRs, perceived trust and RA towards the intention to use mobile money.
The demographic characteristics of the respondents from the field indicated that majority
of the respondents were educated and they were in their youthful ages, which makes
them technology savvy. Although personal characteristic (demographic) variables were
not consistent across studies, the extant literature has demonstrated that social-demographic characteristics have a significant influence on behaviour and suggests that
younger, higher income and better educated consumers tend to accept market innovations more quickly (Osei-Assibey, 2014).

Conclusion, limitations and directions for future research
The study employed two theories, the TAM and the DIM, to examine the drivers of mobile
money adoption in Ghana. The two theories have explained to a large extent the drivers of
mobile money adoption in Ghana. The study identified and empirically examined the key
factors necessary to drive customers’ BI towards the adoption of mobile money services. It
is evident from the study that the PEOU, perceived trust, PU and PCOU are significant to
customers. Also, the level of influence from social groups – friends and family – is another
salient contributor to the adoption of mobile money services. It is, therefore, recommended that mobile money service providers streamline their strategies to make the
services not only easy to use, but also less expensive, useful and secure to instil trust in
their services. The study has demonstrated that at least there are certain key factors
that drive mobile money services’ adoption in a developing economy perspective.
PEOU was found to have a significant influence on consumer behavioural intentions.
The study, therefore, recommends that mobile money service providers must ensure
that the mobile application is very simple to use, users have quick access to their electronic
account at their own convenient time and also network challenges are addressed quickly

as soon as they are detected. PU, trust and cost were found to have a strong influence on
consumer behavioural intentions. This implies that consumers would appreciate products
or services that are beneficial and fulfil their specific needs. Mobile money service providers must make good use of the customer database captured on their systems so as to
deliver services to meet the needs of their customers. Consumer trust in the service provider is built over time. Customers’ data and transactions should be protected from
hackers and third parties who might contact customers for commercial purposes.
Service providers should resolve customer complaints quickly. There will be the need
for service providers to charge their customers fairly for the services delivered, and the
various options customers could consider in order to avoid extra charges for the services
should be provided. Finally, SI was found to be significant between adoption and BI. The
use of both affluent and low-income earners, young and old people in the promotion of
mobile money service usage would encourage more people to see themselves as potential
users of the services.
Although the study makes a significant contribution to knowledge and as is the case
with most research works, this current study carefully points out certain limitations, indicating some directions for further research. Foremost, the relationships among the constructs were tested using customers from a single country. Moreover, the study showed
that the confounding effects of SI vary among the constructs. It will, therefore, be instructive to carry out similar investigations across different contexts in order to strengthen the
nuances of such constructs in the mobile technology adoption literature. Relevantly,
testing how SI moderates the direct relationship between the aggregated constructs
and BIs would provide further enlightenment. Additionally, the effects tested in this
study are that of relationships and not causalities, and on the basis that a cross-sectional
research design was used, the study acknowledges that some dynamism in the tested
relationships may have been ignored.
