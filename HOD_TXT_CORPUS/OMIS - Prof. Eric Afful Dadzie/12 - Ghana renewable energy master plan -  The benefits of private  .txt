This paper assesses the beneﬁts of private sector participation in the Ghana Renewal Energy Master Plan (REMP) using a stylized Generation Expansion Planning model and concludes with a discussion on a ﬁnancial incentive that could be offered to private investors
to encourage their participation in the REMP. Using cost of electricity provision and level
of demand unmet, the paper argues that there is signiﬁcant beneﬁt in incentivizing the
private sector to participate in the REMP especially when the government faces budgetary
constraint and unable to meet all electricity demands. Based on its 2016 feed-in-tariff for
renewable energy generators and going by its annual investment levels in new generation
capacity, Ghana stands to save on average, as much as US$265 million per year in cost of
electricity provision along with a reduction in unmet demand by an average of 6.5% if it
could attract private investors to participate in the REMP at desired levels. This potential
cost savings can serve as the basis for a ﬁnancial incentive scheme aimed at attracting
private sector participation. This research has the tendency to unlock the conundrum of
attracting private ﬁnancing towards energy transition in similar developing economies.

Introduction
The world has ﬁnally woken up to the realization of the importance to transition from fossil fuel-based energy to renewable and clean energy. Indeed, efforts are already underway. The 2019 UN Environment Programme (UNEP) report on
trends in renewable energy investment indicates that over US$2.6 trillion were invested in renewable energy initiatives
worldwide during the period from 2010–2019 [5]. However, much of this investment came from richer countries, primarily
China, USA, Japan, Brazil, and those in the European Union such as Germany, UK, and Denmark [16,17], with virtually no
investment coming from emerging and developing economies. Afful-Dadzie [2] paints a stark picture, noting that even the
marginal progress in renewable energy investment in developing economies appears to come from few countries. In Africa
for instance, just four countries, South Africa, Egypt, Morocco and Tunisia, accounted for 80% of Africa’s share of 1.27% of
the global solar installation in 2019 [2].

well as unattractive market structures [25], the lacklustre pace in energy transition in developing economies can in some
sense be justiﬁed. Aside energy transition, developing economies also face the equally important challenge of increasing
energy access to the citizenry and meeting increasing energy demand for those already having access [3]. For example only
18% of people in Sub-Saharan Africa had access to clean fuels and technology for cooking, while less than 50% had access to
electricity in 2020 [28]. Indeed, both energy transition and energy access are very important, but both demand substantial
amount of capital, and developing economies cannot afford both. It is tempting to think that energy access could be better
achieved through energy transition since energy from renewable technologies is now seen to be competitive and, in some
jurisdictions, even cheaper than energy from conventional generators [14]. However, not having the necessary ﬁnancial resource pushes developing economies to consider energy generation technologies with better capacity factor, and of which
the initial capital outlay leads to more available generation capacity. In fact, current capacity factor and initial capital cost
of energy generators [13], compel developing economies to favour non-renewable energy generation technologies, thereby
hurting energy transition.
A case in point is Ghana which could not generate even 0.5% of its energy needs from renewable energy sources (excluding large hydro) having targeted at least 10% by 2020 [7], and which is struggling to make meaningful progress on its
new renewable energy target set for 2030. Fig. 1 shows a time series plot of the total ﬁnal energy consumption of Ghana
from the year 20 0 0 to 2021 and why Ghana is seeking to raise the percentage of renewable energy in its energy mix. Oil
and biomass (in the form of wood for charcoal and ﬁrewood) dominated Ghana’s sources of energy until 2015 when usage
of natural gas (primarily for electricity generation) started increasing. Though, Ghana has a respectable size of energy from
hydro sources, it is not counted as part of the country’s renewable energy portfolio as it comes from large scale hydro generators considered of legacy status. Unable to meet its 2020 renewable energy target, and still desirous to improve the content
of renewable energy in its energy mix, Ghana drafted a new modest renewable energy target, the REMP, to be achieved by
2030. The government of Ghana expects at least 80% of the ﬁnancing of the REMP to be provided by the private sector [7].
Sadly however, the level of private sector participation in the REMP to date is far below expectation. This is despite Ghana
having enacted a renewable energy act that provides a conducive environment for attracting renewable energy investment,
including an obligation for utility companies to purchase speciﬁc percentage of electricity from renewable energy generators
and a competitive feed-in-tariff [12].The progress of the REMP implementation so far suggest it will meet the same fate as
that of the ﬁrst renewable energy target if nothing is done to attract more private investors. Of the roughly 3.5% of the
REMP achieved so far (which is far less than the 14% expected), more than 90% is from the private sector which suggest the
crucial role of the private sector to the attainment of the REMP.
Ghana’s experience suggests that without the private sector providing the needed ﬁnancing, energy transition will be
diﬃcult to achieve in developing economies. One clear beneﬁt of private investment in the energy sector is that it allows
governments the freedom to channel their constrained ﬁnancial resources to other troubled areas of the economy and help
transfer the risks associated with ﬁnancing and operation of energy projects.
Despite the clear demand for energy, and the potential for making proﬁt, private investors have not been ﬂocking to
developing economies. Several issues account for this lukewarm attitude of the private sector, including lack of a liberalized

market, currency instability, weak banking and capital markets, creditworthiness of counterparties, and non-availability of
commercial arrangement that supports predictable revenue. In the presence of these issues, a private investor will have
to be motivated to consider investing in renewable energy projects. Many studies have waded into the debate of offering
incentives for attracting private ﬁnancing in the energy sector in developing economies (see, for instance Lam and Law [21],
Keeley and Matsumoto [19], Michalena et al. [23]). However, these have largely been advisory and do not provide a means
to quantify the incentive to offer.
The main objective of this paper is to underlie the importance of the private sector to the achievement of energy transition in Ghana and for that matter, similar developing economies. A second objective is to argue for a ﬁnancial incentive that
could be offered private investors to attract investment in energy transition in Ghana and thus similar developing economies.
Speciﬁcally, this paper offers a mechanism for governments in developing economies to determine the beneﬁt of involving
the private sector in energy transition as compared to doing it alone. Such beneﬁt, if any, could form the basis upon which
to incentivize and attract the private sector into investing in the energy transition effort of the country.
These objectives are achieved from two viewpoints. The ﬁrst looks at how much it will cost for a government to undertake its energy transition target without private sector participation especially when facing ﬁnancial constraint. The second
objective explores how much it will cost if the energy transition agenda of the government is supported by the private
sector. From these two viewpoints, the paper then determines a so-called Value Of Private Participation (VOPP), which can
serve as a basis to provide an incentive to entice private investors to support a developing economy’s energy transition
drive. Thus, we assume throughout the analysis that a private investor can only engage in renewable energy projects. The
methodology for achieving the two objectives of the paper are derived based on the construction of the Renewable Energy
Master Plan (REMP) target of the government of Ghana which bares resemblance to the renewable energy targets of most
developing economies. Using a Generation Expansion Planning (GEP) model over a planning period from 2021 to 2030, the
paper examines the total cost of electricity provision in Ghana, the level of unmet electricity demand, and the level of the
REMP achieved when the government of Ghana attempts to achieve the REMP target with and without the private sector.
A comparison of the two scenarios of with and without private sector participation in the REMP then helps to address the
two objectives of the paper. Since except biomass to cookstove, all the renewable energy generators considered in the REMP
will generate electricity, the REMP problem is analyzed from the point of view of electricity generation planning.
In the following sections, we present a brief overview of the Ghana electricity sector, as well as the REMP, followed by
the model, and the parameters used for carrying out the analysis with and without private sector involvement in the Ghana
REMP. This is followed by a discussion of the analysis, and a conclusion.
Overview of the Ghana electricity sector
The Ghana electricity sector is made up of four major institutions. They are the Ministry of Energy which the remaining
three are answerable to; the Energy Commission which enforces the sector regulations and undertakes energy planning for
Ghana; electricity generation authorities - Volta River Authority (VRA), Bui Power Authority (BPA), and Independent Power
Producers (IPPs); the transmission authority - Ghana Grid Company Limited (GRIDCo); and the distribution authorities Electricity Company of Ghana (ECG) in charge of the southern part of Ghana, and Northern Electricity Distribution Company
(NEDCo) in charge of the northern part of Ghana. There is also the Public Utility Regulatory Commission in charge of setting
tariffs for both the electricity generation and the distribution entities.
As at the end of 2021, the country had an installed and dependable generation capacity totaling 5488.82 MW and
4975.25 MW, respectively [11]. The installed capacity as at the end of 2021 comprised of 3753 MW thermal, 1584 MW
hydroelectric, and 144 MW from other renewable energy sources (that excludes large hydro). Fig. 2 shows the annual electricity generation of Ghana. It can be observed that the sector is dominated by hydro and thermal plants. The share of the
annual electricity generation from thermal overtook that of hydro in 2016. The share of the electricity generation in 2021
stood at 65% thermal, 34% hydro, and 0.55 renewable [11]. Though in truth Ghana generates more than 30% of its electricity needs from large hydro which should count towards the share of renewables in the annual electricity generation, the
country discounts the existing large hydro plants as legacy projects and does not include them in its energy transition drive.
To promote the development of renewable energy in Ghana, the government enacted the Renewable Energy Act 2011,
which among other things guarantees renewable energy generators, the uptake of the renewable energy generated, an import tax exemption for solar PV system, as well as competitive feed-in-tariff rate [12]. The act was in large part, to support
the drive to achieve at least 10% of the country’s energy needs from renewables by 2020 which was a precursor to the
REMP. The next section highlights the Ghana REMP.

the aim of increasing the renewable energy capacity of the country from 42.5 MW in 2019 to about 1390 MW by 2030 [7].
The REMP if undertaken is estimated to cost US$5.6 billion and focuses on investment in solar, wind, biomass, and small
hydro technologies. A cumulative summary of the Ghana REMP in the form of the generator types considered in this study

One way to do this is to provide incentives to the private sector. Often, the offering of an incentive receives public

Estimating the beneﬁts of the Ghana Renewable Energy Master Plan (REMP)
The model for assessing the beneﬁts of private sector participation in the Ghana REMP is derived from the viewpoint of
electricity Generation Expansion Planning (GEP). This is justiﬁed since electricity demand planning requires the selection of
relevant generation technologies in a manner that minimizes generation cost of meeting current and future demand. As such,
this research employs a stylized GEP model for the analysis. The GEP model is cast in the form of a Mixed Integer Linear
Program (MILP) to capture both the integer and non-integer variables of the problem. In addition, the model is formulated
to account for uncertainty in future electricity demand. The proposed model is thus referred to as a Stochastic MILP-GEP
model. Stochastic optimization models have been found to have superior performance compared to other techniques when
uncertainties in model parameters exist [6,15]. Most importantly, the model is constructed to take into consideration the
pertinent issues involving electricity generation in Ghana, and for that matter, developing economies.
Since in the presence of insuﬃcient funds, there will be a trade-off between achieving a renewable energy target and
meeting energy demand, the GEP model is formulated to account for the cost of electricity demanded but not supplied. The
proposed Stochastic MILP-GEP model is similar to that of Afful-Dadzie et al. [3]. However, unlike in Afful-Dadzie et al. [3],

The proposed model has two types of decision variables namely, ﬁrst stage decision variables and second stage decision
variables. The ﬁrst stage decision variables are the deterministic (investment) component, and second stage is the stochastic
(operational) component. The ﬁrst stage decision variables are, respectively, Gt,i , Gt,g , and Gt,k representing number of conventional generator type i, government renewable generator type g, and privately owned renewable generator type k to be
built in period t; Zt,i , Zt,g , and Zt,k representing newly installed capacity of conventional generator type i, government renewable generator type g, and privately owned renewable generator type k to be built in period t; Wt,i , Wt,g , and Wt,k , representing available capacity of conventional generator type i, government renewable generator type g, and privately owned
renewable generator type k to be built in period t; and Wq,g Wq,k representing available capacity of government renewable
generator type g and privately owned renewable generator type k in target year q.
The main second stage decision variables are Yt,s,i , Yt,s,g , and Yt,s,k which are the electricity generated from conventional
generators, government owned renewable energy generators, and privately owned renewable energy generators, respectively.
The rest are EIMt,s , the amount of electricity imported, and EUt,s , the amount of electricity demanded but not met.
The model seeks to minimize Eq. (1), the present value of the expected cost of providing electricity over a T planning
period, subject to constraints (2)–(18). The expected cost of Eq. (1) comprise of ﬁxed operating and maintenance cost, variable operating cost of the generators owned by the government, cost of renewable electricity generated by the private sector
(through feed-in-tariff), cost of unmet electricity demand, and cost of electricity imported. The Eq. (2) models the relationship between supply and demand. Any demand not met by supply is tracked through the unmet demand variable EUt,s . The
Eqs. (3)–(5) ensure that electricity generated by a particular generator is within its dependable capacity. The Eqs. (6)–(8)
put a limit on the number of a particular generator that can be constructed in a year. This is done to ensure that a realistic
number of generators are prescribed to be constructed in a particular year. The Eqs. (9)–(11) track the new capacity of a
generator accumulated to date since the start of the planning period, whereas Eqs. (12)–(14) track the total accumulated
capacity of a generator to date (including installed capacity before the start of the planning period). The REMP target is
modeled through Eq. (15) and ensures that the targeted total renewable energy generation capacity in a particular period
is met. Of greater importance is Eq. (16) which ensures that investment in new generators from the government side in a
particular period does not exceed the fund available. This fund is made up of allocated budget for the current period and
the unused allocated budget from the previous period. The Eq. (17) tracks the amount of unused budget in a period, while
Eq. (18) puts a limit on the level of electricity that could be imported from neighbouring countries. The Eq. (19) is the nonnegativity constraints of the model. The model was coded and solved using the General Algebraic Modeling System (GAMS)
version 24.2.1 with the ILOG CPLEX solver.
Model parameters
The sub-sections that follow present the data related to the electricity sector in Ghana and the government’s REMP. Data
on costs, such as ﬁxed and maintenance cost, and variable operating cost are taken from international sources such as the
Energy Information Administration of the U.S. government due to unavailability of local cost data.
Generator types
The study considers both conventional (non-renewable) and renewable generators. The conventional generators comprise
of Open Cycle Gas Turbine (OCGT), and Combined Cycle Gas Turbine (CCGT1, and CCGT2). The renewable generators include
Small Hydro, Solar PV, On-shore Wind, and Biomass. We assume the private sector is allowed to invest in only renewable
generators. In addition, only the government is allowed to invest in hydro generators.
Table 2 shows information on the generator types used in the case study. Aside the distinction based on ownership
(government or private sector), the generators were separated into different types based on capacity size. Hence, there are
two types of generators each for CCGT, Solar and Wind (i.e. CCGT1, CCGT2, SOLAR1, SOALR2, WIND1, WIND2). Each of these
generators have different capacities. With regards to duration of use in a period, the study assumes a total of 8760 h (i.e.
24 h for 365 days) of use in a year. The analysis ignores emissions cost as it is generally not seen as a major factor in most
developing economies. Following Afful-Dadzie et al. [4], the analysis also assumes a penalty cost of US$225 /MWh for unmet
demand. Note also that the capacity factor values are annual averages just as demand is also taken to be in an annual form.

Demand estimation
Electricity demand is stochastic and generally increases each year in most developing economies due to several reasons
including population increase and economic growth. To model this behaviour, Eq. (20) as used in Afful-Dadzie et al. [4] is
employed, where Dt,s represents electricity demand scenario s in period t. Electricity demand is assumed to increase each
year, with ω representing the percentage increase in demand between the current period and the next period. The variable
ω is random and assumed to follow a triangular distribution of the form, Triangular (0.01, 0.07, 0.14) representing a minimum
increase of 1%, an average increase of 7% and a maximum increase of 14% over a period. This was estimated using past data
on electricity supplied in Ghana in ECG [10]. The demand used for 2020 is 19716.59 GWh which is the actual total energy
consumed (including losses) in Ghana in 2020 as per the 2021 Ghana Electricity Supply Plan. The consumption in 2020 was
a 10.23% increase over that of 2019 and can be regarded as largely unaffected by covid-19 since it is within the average
annual increment of roughly 11% recorded since 2015.

The simulated demand forecast for the planning period was compared to the national demand forecast of Ghana from
the 2020 Ghana Electricity Supply Plan [8]. This comparison yielded a mean absolute percentage error (MAPE) of 4.5% which

simulated and used in the model. By its design, the model uses all 20 demand scenarios to determine an optimal solution

perform better compared to the single national forecast [6].
Budget allocation
It is assumed that Ghana allocates a portion of its yearly Gross Domestic Product (GDP) to ﬁnance new generation capacity additions, and that the GDP increases by 4% per year (based on average GDP growth from 1973–2019). Three budget
allocations will be considered for the purposes of analysing the impact on electricity cost and unmet demand, and as well to
understand the government’s approach to electricity provision when faced with ﬁnancial constraints on investment in generation capacity. These allocations are 0.50% of GDP, 0.75% of GDP, and 1.0% of GDP. The budget allocations were estimated
using the 2019 GDP of Ghana which was US$68.34 Billion [27]. In general, annual investment towards new generation capacity in Ghana is estimated to be roughly 0.75% of Ghana’s GDP based on reports from the annual electricity supply plans
of Ghana [3]. The model also assumes that if any budget allocated is not exhausted, the leftover is invested at an interest
rate of 12% per year and added to the next period’s budget for the ﬁnancing of new generation capacity. Also, all cash ﬂows
are discounted at a rate of 12% per year in accordance with the discount rate used in the Ghana generation master plan.

capacity by the government. In each of the cases, the REMP capacity achieved, the level of demand unmet if any, and the

Renewable generation capacity achieved
The renewable generation capacity achieved with and without private sector participation, and when the Government of
Ghana (GOG) allocates 0.5%, 0.75%, and 1% of its annual GDP for investment in new generation capacity (for both renewable
and non-renewable generators) is shown in Fig. 3.
It can be seen from Fig. 3, that at the lower budget of 0.5% of GDP, a bit more renewable energy capacity is attained
when the private sector participates in the REMP than when the government does it alone. The reason for this is because,
at lower budget allocation, the government looks to the private sector to provide capacity even more than is required in the
REMP to meet as much demand as possible. Indeed, it can be noticed that when there is no private sector participation at
0.5% of GDP, the renewable generation capacity achieved is barely above the required limit.
On the other hand, the government only uses the private sector to achieve the REMP target when it has bigger budget
at 0.75% and 1% of GDP and concentrate on using the available budget to procure more conventional capacity to ensure
as much demand is met. Note that investing in conventional generators allows the government to acquire more generation capacity due to their cheaper capital cost which helps reduce levels of unmet demand and the associated cost to the
economy.
In general, very little attempt is made to obtain more renewable generation capacity above the REMP target even when
there is substantial amount of demand left to be served. This is especially true at higher budget allocations of 0.75% and 1%
of GDP. This is an indication that, at current cost rates, the government sees renewable generators to be expensive and is
only willing to meet the target it has set for itself. Thus, the government only uses the private sector to either achieve the
target it has set for itself or to help drive down unmet demand when it is seriously constrained ﬁnancially. In other words,
the government is rational and chooses the best option at any point in time. This agrees with ﬁndings in Afful-Dadzie et al.
[3]. It is therefore not surprising that up to date, little progress is made in achieving the Ghana REMP.
A breakdown in terms of the renewable energy generator types when there is private sector participation in the REMP
is also presented in Table 3. The result shows that much of the renewable energy capacity is provided by the private sector,
which accounts for more than 90% of the capacity, at the lower budget allocation of 0.5% of GDP. (Fig. 3)
The level of private sector participation however decreases as government budget increases, since now the government
has more money and would seek to cut down cost by increasing its share in the REMP. Note that due to the need to make
proﬁt, the feed-in-tariff paid to private investors is generally higher than the levelized cost of electricity of the renewable
energy generators. The result in Table 3 can also serve as an implementation plan for the REMP by ensuring that the right
level of participation of the private sector is followed. For example, at budget allocation of 1% of GDP, the optimal participation level of the private sector should be 63.29% and 50.01% in 2025 and 2030, respectively, in order to minimize cost of
electricity provision. An expanded form of Table 3 with the result for all generator types (both renewable and non-renewable

Level of unmet electricity demand
Ghana currently does not track levels of electricity demanded but not met, although blackouts and load shedding occur
throughout the year. The level of electricity supply reliability in households is estimated to be around 42% [24] which gives
an indication that the level of electricity demand not met is signiﬁcant. A power reliability assessment in 2010 revealed
that Ghana losses between 2 and 6% of its annual GDP to inadequate and unreliable power supply [26]. It is estimated that
Ghana lost about $2.1 million per day during a 2014/2015 electricity crisis [20]. These statistics give credence to the fact that
a signiﬁcant level of electricity demand annually is not met in Ghana. It is likely that achieving the 10% renewable energy
target could increase demand not met, since it could lead to a reduction in investment in conventional energy generators. It
is therefore important that expected levels of unmet demand due to the enforcement of the REMP is analyzed.

Cost of electricity provision
The total cost of electricity provision under the two cases of with and without private sector participation in the
REMP is presented in Fig. 4. The total cost of electricity provision without private sector participation is US$18.07 billion,

and 1.0% of GDP, respectively, when there is private sector participation. The values are US$0.067 million, US$0.051 million,
and US$2.33 million, respectively, for budget allocations of 0.5%, 0.75%, and 1.0% of GDP when there is no private sector

would amount to US$10.84 billion. This is a decrease of US$1.44 over that under the 1.0% of GDP budget allocation and
shows the advantage that countries that are able to muster the needed ﬁnancing for meeting their electricity needs have
those who cannot.
Financial incentive analysis
One of the reasons for the slow progress of the Ghana REMP is lack of adequate incentives to attract private sector
investments [22]. One way to address this is for governments to device incentive packages to attract the private sector.
Such incentive when backed by a well justiﬁed reason receives public backing and survives even when there is a change in
government.
This section discusses such possible ﬁnancial incentive based on the cost savings derived from the values shown in Fig. 4.
We deﬁne the Value of Private Participation (VOPP) in energy transition as the annualized value of the difference between
the total cost of electricity provision when the government undertakes the energy transition initiative alone and when it
does so with the involvement of the private sector. Intuitively, VOPP is the cost savings the government makes from not
using its own money. If such cost savings exist, then it is a double win for the government as it gets the chance to utilize
its budget in other equally important sectors of the economy whiles getting its energy transition aspirations achieved at an
even cheaper cost. A portion of the VOPP can however be used to incentivize the private sector into investing in renewable
energy projects in line with a government’s energy transition goals such as the government of Ghana’s REMP.
The expected cost savings in the Ghana’s REMP with the participation of the private sector, translates into a VOPP of
about US$479.6 million, US$265.5 million, and US$61.9 million per year at budget allocations of 0.5%, 0.75%, and 1% of GDP,
respectively. This VOPP is as a result of many factors including a reduction in the amount of unmet demand, which is
penalized at US$225 /MWh in this analysis, and the chance to invest in more thermal generation capacity with the funds
which hitherto would have been spent on renewable energy generators to achieve the REMP target.
The VOPP of US$ 265.5 million per year at 0.75% of budget allocation is of interest as available data suggest Ghana tends
to commit on average about 0.75% of its GDP to new generation capacity each year [3]. A portion of this amount could be
used to provide ﬁnancial incentive to private investors. Such ﬁnancial incentive could be in the form of an increased feedin-tariff, or an amount paid in relation to size of installed capacity. A ﬁnancial incentive based on size of installed capacity
is particularly straight forward. For example, a 0.5% of the US$ 265.5 million amounts to roughly US$ 1.33 million. With an
estimated 1172.25 MW of renewable energy generation capacity to be provided by the private sector by 2030 at the current
feed-in-tariff, a capacity payment of roughly US$1132 /MW per year could be offered as an incentive for attracting private
investors.
Conclusion
Developing economies face genuine ﬁnancial challenge with regards to energy transition and need support from the
private sector. Unfortunately, these countries ﬁnd it diﬃcult attracting private investors to support their energy transition
aspirations. This paper examines the beneﬁt of private sector participation in energy transition investment in developing
economies that face ﬁnancial challenges and propound an argument for a ﬁnancial incentive for attracting private investors.
This is done through a comparison of the cost of electricity provision when a government undertakes a planned energy
transition target with and without private investors.
Using a generation expansion planning model formulated as a stochastic mixed integer linear program in a case study
involving Ghana’s Renewable Energy Master Plan (REMP), the result indicates that there is signiﬁcant beneﬁt (in terms
of cost savings and demand served) in involving the private sector in the energy transition drive in Ghana, even if the
government of Ghana has suﬃcient funds to do it alone. The beneﬁts are signiﬁcantly higher when the government is
ﬁnancially constrained, which is often the case for developing economies. Going by its annual expenses in new generation

capacity, cost of electricity provision could reduce by as much as US$265 million per year, whereas levels of unmet demand
could reduce by an average of 6.5% if Ghana could attract private investors at desired levels to support its REMP target slated
for 2020–2030. Part of the cost savings could potentially be used to incentivize private investors in an attempt to attract
them.
The contribution of this paper to literature is three –fold. In the ﬁrst, it illustrates the crucial role of private investors
in the energy transition aspirations of developing economies. In the second, it provides a means to determine the beneﬁt
of involving the private sector in the energy transition drive of developing economies. In the third, it provides a simple
mechanism for determining a ﬁnancial incentive to attract private investors in the energy transition drive based on the cost
savings derived from private sector participation.
A limitation of this research is that the various costs for the different types of electricity generators were assumed to
be certain. In practice, these costs will exhibit uncertain features over the planning period which could have an impact on
the result. Future research that takes into account the uncertainty in these costs is encouraged. A future study to determine
the ﬁnancial incentive using an optimal approached is also encouraged. Also, most of the plant costs were taken from
international reports such as the Energy Information Administration of the U.S. since local data was not available. In practice,
plant costs tend to be lower in the U.S. than in developing economies, and could lead to higher electricity cost.
Finally, it must be noted that the proposed tariff incentive cannot be a long-term solution to attracting private sector
investment in renewable energy projects. The root cause hampering private sector participation such as market liberation,
supporting regulation, stable government, and prompt payment if addressed would reduce the need for future incentive
programs.
