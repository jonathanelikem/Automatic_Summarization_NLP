A B S T R A C T 

While renewable energy is making inroads in certain jurisdictions, there appears to be little enthusiasm or effort by many countries in the Global South when examining overall patterns of investment, despite numerous countries announcing renewable energy adoption goals. Various studies attribute this apparent lack of interest to a lack of legislative and regulatory instruments, promotional measures, and unattractive market structures. While these may be relevant factors, this study demonstrates that lack of interest can also stem from concerns over levels of unmet energy demand, coupled with the cost of energy generation. The influence of these two factors on the decision to invest in renewable energy is discussed and demonstrated through a case study on electricity generation planning in Ghana. The case study indicates that, given the projected levels of Ghana's electricity demand and expenses for generation capacity addition, the level of unmet demand could be as high as 18.5% if Ghana were to follow through with its 10% renewable electricity target by 2030. Correspondingly, the cost of electricity provision could increase by over US$221 Million annually - a substantial amount for any country, but particularly for those in the Global South. Results from the case study indicate some of the key reasons why Ghana is nowhere near its 10% renewable electricity target originally set for 2020, and why it is likely the new target year of 2030 will suffer a similar fate. Having highlighted these concerns, the paper then offers suggestions for increasing renewable energy in the Global South.







1. Introduction

   There is a growing recognition that the way in which we produce, use and transport energy require fundamental shifts to sources that have less environmental, health and social impacts. Hence, there is increasing interest and investment in renewable energy worldwide. Renewable energy is not without criticisms however, such as greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions involved in their manufacturing processes [1]; social accep- tance issues when people feel their concerns about renewables have been dismissed [2,3]; and general issues of system reliability [4]. Nonetheless, the continuous declining costs of generating electricity from renewable sources [5], coupled with the alarming impact of emissions from fossil fuel power plants, have persuaded many countries to consider investing in renewable energy technologies.
   Renewable energy is now the world's fastest growing source of en- ergy, with consumption increasing by 3% per year [6]. It is estimated that renewables accounted for more than two-thirds of the world's total new power capacity in 2016, with almost 165 GW installed [6]. With

2. Renewable energy investment challenges in the Global South

   A number of studies have discussed ways to promote renewable energy in the Global South. Washburn & Pablo-Romero [23] have cited the setting up of renewable targets and promotion measures such as tax incentives, feed-in-tariffs, and net metering as measures for promoting renewable energy penetration. Sakah et al. [24] have also advocated for the prioritization of market driven support schemes for cost effective- ness, harmonization of renewable energy regulatory policies with do- mestic electricity pricing, and minimum government influence. In their study promoting renewable energy in Ghana, Gyamfi, Modjinou, & Djordjevic [25], advocated for the mandatory purchase of electricity generated from renewable sources, free access to distribution and transmission systems, and the creation of a renewable energy fund. Ajayi & Ajayi [26], also called for tax holidays, and low or interest free loans to utilities and individuals to encourage investment in renewable en- ergy. Alternatively, Obeng-Darko [27] calls for the enactment of legis- lative instruments to support renewable energy development, and the creation of independent institutions to oversee the regulation and enforcement of renewable energy laws and policies. Other studies also point to the important role that public authorities may have in spurring renewable energy development and deployment [28], including in emerging economies such as China [29,30].
It is interesting to note that a number of countries in the Global South
have taken steps to implement these recommendations, including setting up renewable energy targets [31]. Despite these developments, growth in renewable energy projects in the Global South has been minimal compared to other parts of the world. This paper argues that while these actions are necessary, two fundamental factors hindering the growth of renewable energy in the Global South - namely cost of energy generation and level of unmet demand - are largely glossed over. Although some purport that mechanisms like 'leapfrogging' (where, akin to mobile phones, people's first introduction to electricity is through smaller, distributed sources such as solar home systems and solar lanterns) [32] have led to renewable energy inroads [33], the pace and scale at which this is occurring is not enough to achieve a systemic energy transition at a timescale that addressing climate change requires. This paper demonstrates that until renewable energy performs better than conventional energy in relation to these fundamental factors (costs must be lower and must not exacerbate unmet demand), it will be difficult for many countries in the Global South to favour renewable energy over conventional energy. We begin by first discussing the challenges faced by countries in the Global South to transition their energy systems through the lens of the energy trilemma concept to better understand the fundamental factors discussed afterwards.

2.1. The "Energy Trilemma" concept

The apparent lack of investment in renewable energy in many

countries in the Global South could be understood from the point of view of the Energy Trilemma concept. Energy Trilemma, which stands for energy security, energy equity, and environmental sustainability is a term used to define energy sustainability by the World Energy Council (WEC).1 Energy security concerns the reliability of energy supply now and in the future. Energy equity concerns the accessibility and affordability of energy, while environmental sustainability ensures that in trying to achieve energy security and equity, we do not cause substantial damage to the environment. The environmental sustainability dimension therefore suggests the adoption of energy systems that have less of an environmental footprint such as solar and wind energy. These over- arching considerations are also echoed by the World Energy Outlook [14], pointing to the need for a comprehensive approach to energy policy. The energy trilemma is a major challenge to the world, but more so in the Global South. When analyzed carefully, any country facing financial difficulties and high levels of unmet energy demand - namely energy demanded but not supplied because of a lack of generation ca- pacity and/or interruption of power supply - would have to make a trade-off between the three dimensions [34].
   For a country facing financial difficulties, focusing on improving energy equity (i.e. increasing affordable energy options to broader swaths of the population) will mean investing in generation technologies that minimize the total cost of providing electricity. Although the cost of electricity generation from renewable energy generators is generally considered to be competitive with conventional electricity generators [35], their often higher initial and capital costs increase the cost of providing electricity in countries with limited financial resources [36]. This is because very little generation capacity can be procured with these limited funds, leading to a shortage of capacity and thus an increased level of unmet demand and its attendant economic loss. In view of this, a natural gas generator (with its relatively cheaper capital cost) might be favored over a renewable energy generator, such as solar, whose capital cost is currently more than double that of a typical natural gas generator [37]. In this case, the environmental sustainability dimension of the energy trilemma would suffer. On the other hand, energy equity would be negatively impacted if the key focus were to be on environmental sustainability, and renewable energy were favored. Given the intermittency of renewable energy sources, a focus on envi- ronmental sustainability could also affect the reliability of energy supply and thus the energy security dimension. In other words, these three pillars point to tradeoffs, which are particularly pronounced in the Global South - as one pillar is increasingly pursued, the ability to ach- ieve another is often negatively affected.
Having said this, in a context with more financial resources avail-
able, all three dimensions of the energy trilemma could be improved at the same time. It is thus not a surprise that countries with a higher in- come per capita are at the forefront of renewable energy penetration. Also, while some would dispute this [38], generally speaking, most of these high-income countries fare well in the two dimensions of energy equity and energy security in that people's minimum energy needs are met by the majority of the population, making it easier to focus on the third dimension of environmental sustainability. Compared to countries facing genuine financial constraints however, it is fair to say that these countries will prioritize the pillars required in energy sustainability, starting with their most pressing needs. It should not be a surprise therefore that many countries in the Global South are currently more concerned with improving energy equity than environmental sustain- ability. This remains true because while there have been major advances in electrification rates between 2000 and 2018 (e.g.in Asia, Indonesia went from 50% to 95% and Bangladesh from 20% to 80%; in Africa,
Kenya went from 8% to 73% and Ethiopia went from 5% to 45% [39]),

2.2. Principal factors hindering renewable energy investment in the Global South

   Having discussed the energy trilemma, we next take an in depth look at the principal factors hindering investment in renewable energy in the Global South and their connection to the dimensions of the energy tri- lemma. A practical analysis examining the magnitude and impact of these factors is illustrated in section 4.

2.2.1. Cost of energy generation
   While true that fossil fuels have and continue to receive generous subsidies, with a 2017 report estimating them to be US$425 billion globally [40], some studies purport that without generous government tax incentives and feed-in tariffs, the majority of renewable energy projects worldwide would not have been financially viable when they were initiated [41]. In fact, some studies show that the substantial contribution of renewable energy generation technologies to Germany's electricity production is primarily as a result of a subsidy policy that obliged utilities to pay renewable generators at prices far exceeding the cost of electricity generation from conventional sources [42-44]. This insight is important, particularly for countries in the Global South who find it difficult to balance their national budget, and depend on foreign aid to make up the shortfall. For instance, in Ghana, net aid was about 3.5% of Gross Domestic Product (GDP) from 2010 to 2017 [45]. Jacobsson et al. [46], examined the practicality of the European Union (EU) renewable energy policy and argued that, given the substantial financial commitment needed, and the implications on the cost of electricity to consumers, not all countries ought to invest in renewable

energy in such a manner. In some sense, this argument is akin to the ostensible lack of investment in renewable energy projects (except smaller renewable energy projects in remote areas which can often be the most economically viable option) by a number of countries in the Global South.

2.2.2. Increasing energy demand
   It is widely known that an increase in population and economic ac- tivities are two of the main causes of an increase in electricity demand. On record, the Global South has had high population growth [47]. Sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) for instance, had a population growth rate of 2.7% in 2018; the highest among all geographical regions [48]. Furthermore, although at present SSA is more rural, it is considered the world's fastest urbanizing region with about 4.1% annual GDP growth, compared to 2.0% globally [49], which suggests that electricity demand - often higher in cities - in the SSA region will increase in the near future. For example, the projected total electricity demand for West Africa (which is one of the regions within SSA) indicates that demand will more than double between 2020 and 2030 [50]. According to the World Bank, and as shown in Fig. 1, more than half of the population in SSA, or about 600 million people, did not have access to electricity in 2017. In Nigeria for instance, more than 80 million people were without electricity in 2017.
   For countries like Chad, Liberia, Burundi, the Democratic Republic of Congo, Uganda, Malawi, and Niger, fewer than 25% of the population had access to electricity in 2017. The situation is even worse for rural areas where the average electricity access rate was 22.6% in 2017 [39]. Even urban areas, which had an average electricity access rate of 79.0% in 2017, experience high outages throughout the year because electricity demand far outstrips supply [51]. Struggling to meet increasing elec- tricity demand, it is understandable that countries in the Global South - particularly those in SSA where electrification rates remain low, will focus on selecting generation technologies that minimize the cost of electricity provision to improve energy equity.

2.2.3. Capacity factor and intermittency of renewables
   A generation plant's capacity factor is the actual energy generated by the plant to the energy equivalent of its installed capacity over a spec- ified time period. In practice, the major obstacle to conventional energy generators attaining higher capacity factors is the availability of fuel and/or the plant, as well as economic reasons, particularly fuel cost. In most cases, the plant is available except when due for maintenance. Therefore, when the availability of the fuel and its cost is not an issue to the user, capacity factors in the region of 50% and above are quite achievable. On the other hand, the major obstacle to the attainment of higher capacity factors by renewable energy generators is their source of energy. A wind turbine can produce electricity only when the wind is blowing. Likewise, a solar photovoltaic system can produce electricity only when the sun is shining. As a result, renewable energy generators tend to have lower capacity factors compared to conventional genera- tors. The 2018 average capacity factors for solar photovoltaic and wind power in the United States (US) for instance were 26.1% and 37.4% respectively, compared to 92.5%, 55.0%, and 53.6% respectively for nuclear, natural gas combined cycle, and coal plants [52]. The average capacity factors of solar and wind are even less for some countries in the Global South (such as 19.7% and 17.4% in the West African Sub-region [50]).
In addition, as the intensity of wind and solar fluctuate during the
day, so does the electricity generated. This occurrence creates inter- mittency in the electricity grid leading to reliability issues. This is not to say that conventional energy generators do not encounter fluctuations in their source of energy. For example, volatility in fuel price could disrupt fuel supply and therefore electricity supply. However, such disruptions generally speaking do not occur as frequently as fluctuations in elec- tricity being generated by wind and solar. Although there have been some advances in energy storage such as smaller and utility-scale battery storage options and 'intelligent' electricity systems that provide enough 'real time' and granular data to help contend with the issue of inter- mittency, they are currently not cost competitive. Other options to tackle intermittency include relying on fossil fuel backup generators and electricity imports from neighboring countries [41,53]. However, in general, these options are not practical in the Global South due to a lack of financial resources and generation capacity shortages.

2.2.4. Financial constraints
   Another important reason that could explain the lack of investment in renewable energy projects in the Global South is financial constraint. For most countries in the Global South, securing the needed capital to finance new generation capacity is a big challenge, and is the major source of high levels of unmet demand [36,54-56]. Conventional eco- nomic theory would say that the high unmet demand would serve to attract private investors. However, private investors are often not motivated to invest in energy generation in the Global South due to the lack of a stable regulatory regime and too much political interference [27]. In fact, most countries in the Global South are forced to allocate a portion of their national budget for capacity expansion investment due to the inability to generate sufficient funds from the sale of electricity as a result of subsidized electricity prices [57]. In essence, if the limited budget available for electricity generation expansion is to be invested in renewable energy generators with high investment costs rather than conventional ones, significantly less generation capacity will be avail- able to meet growing electricity demand.

2.2.5. Cost of unmet energy demand
   Availability of electricity is crucial to the wellbeing of humanity as it promotes better health and sanitation, education, and business. A country's Gross Domestic Product (GDP) can be significantly affected when industrial producers, service providers, and institutions face increasing power outages. African countries are believed to be losing 2-4% of their annual GDP due to electricity shortages [58]. It is esti- mated that Ghana for instance, lost about US$2.1 million per day during

a 2014/2015 energy crisis [59], during which there was widespread load shedding due to low seasonal rainfalls and irregular gas supplies for thermal plants [60]. Thus, every unit demand of electricity not met is a cost to a country. Mindful of the cost of unmet electricity demand, it should not be a surprise if a country with limited financial resources favors generation technologies with low capital cost so that enough generation capacity will be available. Since the current capital cost of most renewable energy technologies more than double that of a natural gas plant, a renewable energy technology will likely not be the preferred option for most countries in the Global South.
   In sum, these factors suggest that it is currently not favorable for many countries in the Global South to invest in large scale grid- connected renewable energy projects from the energy trilemma point of view. Until the cost of renewable energy becomes cheaper compared to the cost of energy produced from fossil fuel generators, and energy access improves, countries like Ghana will keep missing their renewable energy targets.
   Having said this, it is important to acknowledge that renewable en- ergy costs are decreasing - including in the Global South - which coupled with a more favorable regulatory environment is in turn attracting investors in some countries from the Global South. For instance, in Vietnam, despite provisions that the electricity utility would only pay producers for the power it required, and a feed in tariff rate of 0.09/kWh for electricity produced, investors were keen to capitalize on the opportunity where 5 GW of solar was installed by the end of 2019 - compared to a situation where Vietnam had almost no solar power in 2017 [61]. Nevertheless, the issues highlighted above that policymakers face - particularly a situation of increasing unmet energy demand and budgetary constraints - suggest that meaningful renewable energy penetration is not guaranteed without further supports.
   The next section advances the debate on the challenges discussed above from a quantitative point of view to put into perspective the magnitude of the challenges policy makers face and how they influence the choice of electricity generation technologies in the Global South. In doing so, we demonstrate why, Ghana is nowhere close to even 1% of the 10% renewable electricity target due in 2020, and why it is likely it might not achieve its goal in the new targeted year of 2030 unless some major changes are made.

3. Case study: Examining the feasibility of Ghana's renewable electricity Target

   This section advances the debate in section 2 from a quantitative point of view in a case study involving Ghana. Since investing in renewable energy technologies is ultimately meant to increase capacity to meet future electricity demand, the debate is presented from the point of view of solving a Generation Expansion Planning (GEP) problem. A GEP model (which is typically used for finding cost effective generation plants to meet future electricity demand [62]) will be solved for two cases. The first case is when a 10% renewable electricity target is enforced. The second case is when no renewable electricity target is in place. Comparisons will then be made between the two cases when faced with specific budgets for financing new generation capacity on four important issues namely: (1) the size of new generation capacity ac- quired, (2) the proportion of demand met with renewable electricity, (3) the level of unmet electricity demand, and (4) the cost of providing electricity. We first present some contextual information about Ghana. Then, we discuss the GEP model and the model parameters related to Ghana as used in the case study.

3.1. Contextual profile of Ghana

   Ghana is a country in West Africa with a population of about 28.83 million and a GDP of US$58.997 Billion in 2017 [63]. With respect to electricity, the country had an installed generation capacity of 4533 MW in 2018 comprising of 2906 MW thermal, 1584 MW hydroelectric, and



42.5 MW solar. Ghana's total electricity generation in 2018 comprised of hydropower (39.6% from three dams), oil and natural gas-fired power plants (58.14%), electricity imports (2.26%), and renewable energy (0.2% from solar) [64]. Ghana also has one of the highest electricity access rates in SSA. The country's overall electricity access rate stood at 79% in 2017, which comprised of 90% urban and 65% rural [39]. Ghana also serves as a useful example in which the idea of unmet demand has become particularly acute at times, where as noted in section 2, a shortage in electricity supply in 2014/15 led to considerable economic losses. The case study is focused on Ghana because it has yet to achieve a 100% electricity access rate, and also because it had a 10% renewable electricity target which was set in 2006 and is due in 2020 [27]. How- ever, by the end of 2018, the country had achieved only 0.3% of the target (despite the existence of a generous feed-in-tariff [19,20]), thereby prompting a reschedule of the target to 2030. It is worth noting that Ghana's 10% renewable energy target excludes large-scale hydro- electric projects.2 According to the Ghana Renewable Energy Master Plan (REMP), the targeted renewable electricity is expected to be generated from a mix of biomass, wind, solar, and small and medium hydro plants of capacity not exceeding 100 MW [22]. Hydroelectric projects of capacity above 100 MW are excluded from Ghana's 10%

constraint for a renewable energy target which is needed in order to enforce the percentage of electricity generation expected from renew- able technologies by certain dates, as well as assess the cost and benefit resulting from favoring renewable energy generators over conventional ones.
   As in their case [36], this paper also takes the view of a central planner since this is the situation in most countries in the Global South, including Ghana as indicated above. Using the extended model, one is able to compare (1) the level of unmet energy demand, and (2) the cost of electricity provision under the two cases with and without an enforced renewable energy target.

3.2.2. Objective function
   The objective function is represented by Equation (1) and seeks to minimize the total cost of electricity provision over a desired planning period that start from period t 1/4 1 to period T. The total cost is the
present value of the sum of the capital cost, fixed operating cost, variable
operating cost, emissions cost, cost of electricity imported, and cost incurred due to unmet demand in each period of the planning horizon. The objective function value is also one of the major factors that could influence a country's decision to invest in renewable energy technology.

3.2.3. Model constraints
   The objective function of Equation (1) is determined subject to the constraints represented by Equations (2)-(9) which respectively relates to constraint on electricity demand and supply, production and gener- ator capacity, installed capacity of (new, and new and existing) gener- ators, electricity importation limit, renewable electricity target, and budget for new capacity. Except for the renewable electricity target constraint of Equation (7), readers are referred to section 3 of Afful- Dadzie et al. [36] for detail explanation of these constraints. For the renewable electricity target constraint, a target slated to be achieved by period T (i.e. the end of the planning period) is divided into sub-targets
to be achieved at selected periods k 1/4 1; 2; ...; K. This is done to mirror

approaches to renewable energy target attainment in practice (e.g. Argentina), but also to prevent the situation where the model waits till the last period to invest in renewable energy just for the sake of meeting the renewable energy target. The left-hand-side of Equation (7) ensures that the sum total of all electricity generation coming from renewables meets the target percentage for a selected period k.

Much of the low electrification rates in the Global South is due to the fact that many settlements (especially rural communities) are scattered far away from the national grid, making it too costly to connect them to the grid. This situation however presents an opportunity for the deployment of renewable energy technologies that are decentralized electricity options; often referred to as micro-grids. Providing electricity to these far away settlements through renewables will be far cheaper in the meantime as there will be no need for investment in transmission infrastructure. Solar PV, in parts of the world with high insolation rates in particular, will be ideal in the fact that this energy source can be sited near these settlements. In addition, a number of rural settlements yet to gain electricity access have little economic activities during night time when direct solar is unavailable, thus reducing the need for expensive batteries to provide large-scale electricity supply at night. Moreover, given that the electricity demands of these settlements are generally low, their economic activities would not be impacted as much by the inter- mittency of power from solar PV. Finally, while off grid electricity op- tions were often considered the 'poor cousin' to grid connected electricity (equated to 'modernity'), now there is a growing recognition by people that what is of more interest to them is the services and not whether or not the source providing them is connected to a larger electricity grid or a micro one [76].

5.2. Focus on businesses and households with attractive market instruments

   While it does not appear prudent for certain countries to invest in large scale renewable projects too quickly due to the potential for high levels of unmet demand and cost, investment in renewables by house- holds has the potential to be profitable if adequate relevant measures are put in place. Measures such as Net Metering to enable the selling of extra energy to the national grid have the potential to galvanize households to invest in solar PV. A number of households' negative experiences with solar PVs have centered on system maintenance and battery durability. To this effect, market mechanisms that can relieve households from system maintenance responsibilities and cost have the potential to in- crease interest in solar projects. The success of the Pay-As-You-Go solar in Kenya [18], attest to the need for innovative market mechanisms. It is interesting to note that almost all businesses in Ghana, from banks to educational institutions have power back-ups against electricity out- ages. The practice is also common among wealthier households. How- ever, many, if not all, of the back-up generators are diesel generators [77]. With no fuel cost, no noise, no air pollution and smell, as well as the ability to sell power to the grid, coupled with market mechanisms to relieve users from maintenance responsibilities, solar PV could replace

estimates for electricity generators, the extra cost incurred when
attempting to transition from fossil fuel-based electricity to renewable electricity is manageable if a country is relatively well off, like Germany and USA. However, the cost will likely be considered too high by elec- tricity planners if a country is financially limited.

5. The way forward and conclusion

   The analysis from section 4 illustrates that without significant ad- vancements, for countries constrained with funds, such as those in the Global South, the threat of increased levels of unmet demand will likely

diesel generators as the preferred back-up generator. The case for solar PV as the preferred back-up generator is even stronger given that most of the businesses operate during day time which will mean less need for battery storage that - although costs are decreasing - tends to be expensive.

5.3. Solar research, Training, and education initiative with concurrent efforts to spur demand

   The cost of renewable energy - particularly solar and wind - is cheaper in China, in particular because of the maturity of its renewable



industry [78]. This is as a result of China's continuous research into renewable energy technologies, along with policies that support their use domestically (such as policies seeking to reduce air pollution), which have had knock on effects on costs globally. This has enabled the country to ramp up its investments in renewable energy projects, becoming the world leader in installed capacity of renewable energy. Similar effort could be replicated in Ghana by the establishment of solar research centers to conduct research into solar PV adaptations, usage, and market that are in agreement with unique conditions of the country. It is also imperative that efforts are made to have qualified personnel in place who are capable of installing and maintaining renewable energy tech- nologies through the introduction of specialty programs in renewable energy installation and servicing, particularly solar PV systems, in the country's technical institutions. As noted above, many businesses and households in Ghana prefer diesel generators for power back-ups due mainly to maintainability concerns. Having a well-trained homegrown installation and service workforce for small scale renewable energy options and batteries would be key to adoption decisions by households and businesses. Finally, education must be intensified on the need to adopt renewable energy over energy from fossil fuels in the future. This, coupled with the provision of supporting policies such as the subsidy of solar panels for households by the government of Ghana [59] would be needed to encourage demand, and therefore investment in renewable energy.

5.4. Conclusion

   This paper discussed some of the key factors that have been discussed less within the literature that currently hinder countries in the Global South from embracing renewable energy on a large scale. The research showed that the level of unmet energy demand and cost of energy generation under budgetary constraints, are the two main factors that also influence a low income country's decision to invest in renewable energy. In particular, many countries from the Global South will be hard pressed to invest in large scale renewable electricity technologies if doing so impacts negatively on those two factors. Evidence of this argument in practice was demonstrated through a case study involving a model of electricity generation planning in Ghana. The case study shows that investing in renewable generation technologies to meet the coun- try's 10% renewable electricity target at current cost levels will be too costly and will also increase Ghana's level of unmet demand. This finding helps to support why Ghana (as of the end of 2018) had not been able to achieve even 0.3% of its targeted 10% renewable electricity production by 2020.
   Ghana's inability to meet its 10% renewable electricity target shows how difficult it is for countries in the Global South to transition from fossil fuel based energy to renewable energy. While investing in renewable energy will undoubtedly improve the environmental sus- tainability dimension of the energy trilemma concept, it will likely exacerbate the energy equity and energy security dimension for these countries, thereby affecting the government's ability to provide sus- tainable energy to its citizens.
   This therefore calls for rethinking in the manner at which renewable energy is promoted in the Global South. First, it can be inferred from the case study that state financed large-scale renewable projects could lead to an increase in already high levels of unmet electricity demand. In that sense, these countries could focus on leveraging the advantages of renewable energy, particularly solar, to provide electricity to commu- nities off the grid in the short term. Distributed solar projects supported by market schemes such as Pay-As-You-Go Solar in rural areas and by businesses and household in urban areas could also be encouraged.
   The foremost contribution of the paper to literature is the offering of rarely discussed views on barriers to renewable energy adoption in the Global South from the perspective of the energy trilemma. This paper also contributes to practice and research by providing a modified gen- eration expansion planning model for electricity systems that can serve

as a tool for analyzing the impact of proposed renewable energy targets on level of unmet demand and cost of electricity generation. To para- phrase Harjanne and Korhonen [79] that today's energy policy of transitioning to renewable energy must be carefully done such that it does not sentence the poor to perpetual poverty, this modified GEP model can serve to guide countries in the Global South to take renewable energy initiatives that balance the need for energy security, energy eq- uity, and environmental sustainability.
   A limitation of this research is that, as in most GEP models, fuel cost was assumed to be certain. Likewise, fuel was assumed to be available in abundant supply. However, since fuel cost and fuel availability often times are a problem for countries in the Global South, future research is encouraged to explicitly account for these in order to improve the GEP decision making process.
