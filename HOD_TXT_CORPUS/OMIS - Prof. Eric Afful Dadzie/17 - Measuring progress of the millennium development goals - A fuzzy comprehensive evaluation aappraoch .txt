 The United NationsβÄô target date to achieve its Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) to
free people from extreme poverty and multiple deprivations is 2015. As the date approaches,
several attempts have been made to evaluate the performance of the set goals, targets, and
indicators. Although the methodology used to measure the performance of member countries
has been questioned by many, the ο§¹ercest criticism so far has been the apparent lack of data
with which to measure progress. The subjectivity of the MDGs further increases the complexity
of the evaluation systems, but an effective measurability of each MDG is key to the overall
success. This study applies a fuzzy comprehensive evaluation method (FCEM) in evaluating
the performance of individual member countries with regard to the MDGs. The result shows
how, in the absence of data, the FCEM can be used to evaluate performance of member
countries involved in the MDG project.

INTRODUCTION
The United Nations Millennium Declaration, signed in September
2000 by 189 countries, brought into being an integrative set of
developmental objectives, the ο§¹rst of which aims to eradicate extreme
levels of poverty by the year 2015. To help assess the performance of
member countries toward these commitments, a set of 8 goals, 18 targets,
and 48 time-bound indicators were designed and called the Millennium
Development Goals (MDGs). Over the years, there have been debates
over the methodology used in tracking the performance of member
countries and concerning why all the MDGs are quantitatively evaluated.

For example, Clemens, Kenny, and Moss (2007) cast doubt over the
reliability of the MDGs as a measurement framework by pointing to the
βÄ€implausibilityβÄù of the MDGs for a greater number of countries involved.
Further on (Easterly 2007; Vandemoortele 2009), lament on how the
MDGs were poorly designed to measure performance and question its
effectiveness in assessing whether a member country is on track in
terms of meeting the set targets and goals. Fukuda-Parr, Greenstein, and
Stewart (2013) propose an alternative measurement methodology that,
interestingly, discovers that countries in Africa, for example, outperform
the global averages compared with what is often reported by the UN
and the World Bank. It is also argued (Atarran 2005) that although
quantitative goals help in observing poverty trends, the MDGs do not
deο§¹ne poverty, and poverty cannot be reduced to quantitative goals and
indicators. Atarran further states, for example, that the health goals for
2015 appear quantitative, but most of them have irretrievably ο§²awed
quantiο§¹cation.
Of all these, the most signiο§¹cant challenge to date has been the
apparent lack of data to measure the MDGs. On the UN MDG website,
there is little or no information on most of the indicators for most
of the developing countries, a problem that the United Nations (2008)
admits to. Because of this unavailable data, the research attempts to show
how the fuzzy comprehensive evaluation method (FCEM) and the Delphi
method can be combined to offer a solution for evaluating the MDGs
while also curing the subjectivity of the MDGs.
Currently, the standard methodology used by the UN to assess current
trends in the indicators in relation to the deο§¹ned targets compares past
levels of poverty in a country to recent levels. On the basis of the
assessment, performance is then evaluated linguistically at the country
level in ο§¹ve categories: achieved, on track, very likely to be achieved, possible
to achieve if some changes are made, and off track (UN MDG Monitor
2007). This study seeks, on the basis of this linguistic remark set and
the unavailability of data, to use the FCEM to effectively assess the
performance of member countries, particularly those for which data is
simply not available.

FUZZY COMPREHENSIVE EVALUATION MODEL (FCEM)
The FCEM employs fuzzy mathematics to thoroughly evaluate realworld systems that are not clearly deο§¹ned. The advantage and suitability
of FCEM is seen in its ability to evaluate results by analyzing and
comparing the membership degree with the index weight of factors,
which are often difο§¹cult to be quantiο§¹ed. The method has been
applied in areas such management, economics, policy, and engineering,
among others. Yunna and Zhijun (2008) applied FCEM in construction
businessesβÄô enterprise resource planning (ERP) project risk evaluations.
Yunna and Zhijun used the variable fuzzy set method and expert
experiences in calculating the membership degrees. FCEM is employed
by Zhao, Guo, and Cui (2009) to evaluate the selection of project

Assigning Weights by the Delphi Method
This article recommends the Delphi method (Linstone and Turoff
2002) for the UN Development experts involved in the MDG project
in various regions and countries in order to determine the weight of
each factor. Expert decision should be guided by the priority of the
Goal for each member country. This would be based on the prevailing
developmental challenges or conditions in a speciο§¹c country together
with MDG country historical data. For example, in determining the
degree of importance of the Goals, the Ivory Coast cannot be weighted
the same as Brazil, because the Ivory Coast is coming out of a war
situation in which basic infrastructures such as schools and hospitals have
been destroyed, whereas Brazil or Ghana has had stable political climate
for quite some time.

CONCLUSIONS
In the absence of data on the Millennium Development Goals for
most countries and regions and with the due date for the program
approaching, the proposed methodology explains how UN Development
ο§¹eld expertsβÄô opinions can be used to efο§¹ciently measure and track
progress. The methodology can be very useful for developing countries
where it is evidently clear there is difο§¹culty in gathering data on

the MDGs. The fuzzy comprehensive evaluation method shows how
to evaluate and track the performance of countries who have signed
onto the MDGs. Because the Delphi survey method wasnβÄôt carried out
experimentally to assign the weights, the weights were assigned arbitrarily
in order to demonstrate how the method works. The method provides
an efο§¹cient way to track the progress of member countries. It is also an
ideal method for tracking progress of individual goals and targets. This
approach at evaluating the performance of the MDGs would help the
UN and all other stakeholders to effectively track progress of countries
at any stage toward the set deadline target of 2015. The strength of this
methodology lies in its simplicity and ability to also evaluate any one of
the goals independently of all the goals. Future work would show how
the panel of experts would be formed and how analysis of the Delphi
survey method can effectively assign the weights.
