This paper aims to examine the relationship between sovereign credit ratings and funding costs of
banks and also the relationship between sovereign credit ratings. Using over 300 banks operating
in Africa from 2006 to 2012, the study investigates sovereign ratings’ impact on funding cost. The
long term domestic sovereign ratings announced by Fitch and Standard & Poor’s during the
period under study were used. The panel made use of Generalized Method of Moments estimation
strategy for funding cost. The ﬁndings of the study indicate that sovereign ratings upgrades have
an inverse and statistically signiﬁcant relationship with funding costs. The ﬁndings suggest that
sovereign rating upgrades makes it easier for banks to access funds from the capital and global
market at a cheaper cost compared to rating downgrades. The study recommends and encourages
emerging economies to use the services provided by credit rating agencies since these agencies
may help improve accessibility of funds in the international markets by banks. It is recommended
that sovereign rating should be considered as a supplement and not a substitute to our own
perceived judgement and research.

1. Introduction
There is no economy that can function properly without the existence of vital components like ﬁnancial institutions. Financial institutions
of which banks are the main ones in every economy because banks bring together lenders and borrowers of funds. Banks contribute to the
development of an economy by ensuring adequate degree of capital formation through mobilization of scattered and small savings of the
community and making the funds available for investing in productive sectors. Banks as well create value through provision of products and
services that improves the lives of the citizens and fuel economic growth. The growth of large corporations, entrepreneurship activities,
imports and exports will not have been possible without the intervention of banks providing them with the needed funds to carry out such
activities. Banks will have to incur some costs one way or the other before undertaking the activities aforementioned. In the case of capital
formation, banks mobilize funds from diﬀerent sources and lenders of funds charge interest rates on the borrowed funds. The interest rate
which is the funding cost of banks is mostly inﬂuenced by credit ratings. If the interest rate a bank has to pay for new funding increases
without a change in its lending rates, the proﬁts of the bank will decline (Beau et al., 2014). Sovereign credit rating over the years has been
given considerable attention in the global ﬁnancial markets since it has become the most common way of measuring a country’s risk.

3. Literature review
3.1. Sovereign credit ratings
Credit rating agencies provide diﬀerent services on credit rating but this study will be limited to long term sovereign credit rating.
In this ever globalized dispensation where governments and corporate institutions deal with multinational corporations across the
world, there is the need to assess how responsive governments could meet their ﬁnancial commitments. Assessment of sovereign
ratings is very paramount not merely for the sovereign country, but for other issuers in the country such as banks, corporations and
public sector entities and this is as a result of the sovereign ceiling above which is impossible for other borrowers situated in the
country to raise (Ratings, 2010). With developmental projects on the minds of governments to meet the needs of their citizenry as
well as score political points in the midst of limited access to internal funds, countries particularly developing ones of late resort to
international borrowing from international institutions. Such institutions should therefore have a reason of giving out credits and this
can eﬀectively be carried out with sovereign ratings. Up until the 1980’s, major sources of external credit for governments were made
up of small group of international banks. Sovereign credit rating was ﬁrst introduced by Standard and Poor’s in 1975 for this purpose
and have evolved to become an important credit rating agency. By far, two additionally recognized international rating agencies are
the Moody’s and Fitch. The Credit Rating Agencies have been under pressure especially during the recent ﬁnancial crises and they
have been accused of their inability to eﬀectively forecast rating downgrade in order to avoid the collapse (Kaminsky and Schmukler,
2002).
According to Bhatia (2002) the sovereign ratings by CRAs reveal the ability and readiness of rated governments to defray
commercial debt obligations in full and on time. Bhatia indicated that the deﬁnition has two important connotations:

• By obligor, the focus of the rating is extensively on government creditworthiness to provide an assessment with regard to the
sovereign risk.
• By obligation, the emphasis of the rating is exclusively on how credit worthy is the sovereign debt to private creditors (such as
bank loan, treasury bills and bonds) and no provision on credit risk assessment on sovereign debt to oﬃcial creditors.

A country’s credit rating is very crucial as it also aﬀects the domestic market which is made up of companies and their stock prices
even though Credit Rating Agencies are gradually shifting from a policy of not ever rating a ﬁrm above the sovereign against the
backdrop that sovereign ratings set a ceiling for the debt issues to domestic companies (Borensztein et al., 2007). Standard and Poor’s
(2012) reported that 113 private issuer ratings globally exceeded their sovereign foreign-currency rating and three out of them were
commercial banks. This implies that sovereign ratings continue to play key roles in domestic companies. A good rating tends to
reduce the costs of funding through the issuance of public debt either foreign or local currencies while a poor rating would bring an
increment in the interest diﬀerences over the suitable benchmark, normally the interest paid on Treasury bonds (Ratings, 2003).

3.2. Empirics on determinants of funding costs of banks
Babihuga and Spaltro (2014) investigated the determinants of bank funding costs using internationally active banks for the period
2001–2012. The study used the three-month Libor plus the ﬁve-year CDS premia as the marginal cost of funding for the dependent
variable. They highlighted that bank speciﬁc factors such as capital variables made up of total capital ratio, quality of capital, equity
returns and provision ratio. Macroeconomic variables used were, short term interest rate, real GDP growth, yield curve slope and a
dummy variable for ﬁnancial crisis. The results revealed that an increase in total bank capital in the short run lead to an increase bank
funding costs. Also, their ﬁndings suggest that higher capital quality is related to lower bank funding costs. The bank provision was
found to be positive and statistically signiﬁcant with bank funding costs while equity returns relate negatively with bank funding
costs. From the perspective of the macroeconomic variables, the real GDP growth result had a negative sign which suggest that an
improvement in growth would increase bank proﬁtability which decreases bank risk and ﬁnally result to lower cost of funding. The
yield curve spread also relate positively to bank funding cost. This suggest that short run bank funding costs increase with a steepening in the yield curve which stems from an increase in interest rates.
Hossain and Hossain (2015) used internal factors to examine the cost of Funds of Non-Bank Financial Institutions in Bangladesh.
The internal factors included in the study were age of business, size of business, equity contribution, proﬁtability and nature of assets.
The results indicated that age of business and proﬁtability were found to be negatively related to cost of funds at signiﬁcant level of
5%. This means higher earnings per share and old age non-ﬁnancial banks institutions can secure funds at lower costs while nature of
asset and equity contribution variables were not signiﬁcant but conform to the sign expected.
Jibrin et al. (2015) researched on Strategies for Lowering Banks’ Cost of Funds in Nigeria and investigated the determinants of
banks’ cost of funds from the period January 2012 to March 2014. The study employed panel data regression analysis and used bankspeciﬁc, macroeconomic and industry-speciﬁc variables to determine the cost of funds. The Generalized Method of Moment (GMM)
technique was used to solve problems of endogeneity as well as cross sectional heterogeneity. Bank speciﬁc variables were operating
Cost, Risk Premium and overhead Costs. Industry factors were liquidity ratio and cash reserve ratio, macroeconomic variables were
money supply and inﬂation rate. The result speciﬁed that overheads cost, risk premium, liquidity ratio, inﬂation rate and money
supply determines banks’ cost of funding. Sovereign credit rating in the literature is found to be one of the determinants of funding
costs and this has been discussed below.

3.3. Credit rating and funding cost
According to Beau et al. (2014) compensation that depositors and investors demand in exchange for funding a bank’s activities is
the cost of funding. The researcher further suggested that although lower costs of funding may be generally beneﬁcial from the
perspective of ﬁnancial stability nonetheless regulators must make sure that activities of banks are not funded in such a way that it
will lead to excessive risk-taking. Adelino and Ferreira (2014) in their study of bank ratings and lending supply conﬁrmed that the
shock to ratings aﬀects cost of funding of banks. They reported that ratings have directly aﬀected whether some institutional stakeholders such as insurance companies and banks are permitted to invest in a bank’s debt securities. Kisgen and Strahan (2010) show
that credit ratings aﬀect a ﬁrm’s cost of capital. It is certainly true that some customers are paid little or no interest on their
depositors. Those that normally receive a decent interest on their deposits are institutional or corporate depositors. Billett et al.,
(1998) evidenced that whenever there is an increase in risk, banks raise more funds from insured deposits for their operations and as
a result of that the market punish the banks by increasing the cost of funding for such banks. The researcher also ﬁnds that banks that
are downgraded rely more on insured deposits and the justiﬁcation was that bank managers perceive the increased in cost of insured
deposits to be lesser than the cost of uninsured non-deposits funds. An increased future funding cost is associated with rating
downgrades. Banks’ cost of funding is perceived to have an impact on banks proﬁtability and since sovereign credit rating aﬀect
funding cost it is possible to assume a connection between sovereign credit rating and bank proﬁtability.

3.4. Sovereign credit rating and funding cost of banks
The economic crisis that hit major economies in the world goes to suggest that investors are of the belief that banks are closely
connected with their governments since both the governments and banks rely on domestic activity which tends to aﬀect tax revenues
and the demand for ﬁnancial services (Correa et al., 2014). Many economically and ﬁnancially developed countries in the world have
suﬀered downgrade ratings. The United States saw a downgrade credit rating from AAA to AA+ in August 2011, AAA to AA+ in
January 2012 and then AA in November 2013 by Standard and Poor’s.
Correa et al. (2014) in analyzing the composite eﬀect of expected government support to banks in 37 countries asserted that
sovereign credit ratings have inﬂuence on bank funding within which it operates and that sovereign credit rating downgrades have a
huge adverse eﬀect on bank stock returns for those banks that are expected to receive stronger support from their government. A
study by Gennaioli et al. (2014) showed that sovereign defaults are likely to be preceded by falls in private credit in countries where
banks are expected to hold a major portion of their assets in government bonds and ﬁnancial institutions that are more developed. As
a result of a sovereign downgrade, there has been a signiﬁcant increase in wholesale debt and deposit funding costs because a portion
of the increment is as a result of investors demanding for high compensation for taking on the country’s risk (Panetta et al., 2011).
Sovereign credit risk stands as a major dare on the ﬁnancial stability of the world at large. The waning in sovereign credit worth
could have a negative impact on bank funding (Panetta et al., 2011). They further proposed that Public ﬁnances in advanced
countries have diminished as a result of ﬁnancial crisis and after a downturn which has ensued apprehensions about sovereign credit
risk. The Eurozone has seen three of its members receiving international ﬁnancial relief and a fall of credit rating for some other
countries in the region during 2009–2011 with a corresponding surge in funding cost. Most often than not the lenders are at the
disadvantage concerning asymmetric information and as a result of that they intend to limit borrowers’ access to their funds (Duﬃe
and Singleton, 2003). Adelino and Ferreira (2014) conducted a study on bank ratings and lending supply by comparing banks that
have ratings at the bound prior to a downgrade which they referred to as the treatment group and the control group was made up of
related banks whose ratings are lower than the sovereign bound. It was deduced that sovereign downgrades result in a greater
decrease in the ratings of banks at the sovereign bund than ratings below the bound. With inﬂuence on loan pricing, treated banks
tend to increase interest rates spread more than control bank as a result of a sovereign downgrade.
Panetta et al. (2011) share similar ﬁndings with Adelino and Ferreira (2014) as the former identify four main channels through
which a deterioration in sovereign credit worthiness could negatively aﬀect banks’ funding;

• In the ﬁrst place, losses on holdings of government debt deteriorate the balance sheets of the bank and this will expose the bank to
more risk and as a result funding becomes more costly and at times impossible to obtain.
• Also, a decline in sovereign ratings reduces the collateral value that banks may use for wholesale funding and also in obtaining

4. Methodology
The panel data framework was used to achieve the above objective. Panel data is constructed by putting together time series and
cross sectional elements. A panel study captures the variation of what arises through time or space as well as the variation of these
two dimensions simultaneously. This means with the panel study, a pooled regression model is tested for all the banks under the study
through time. The study made use of an econometric model with funding cost as the dependent variable with sovereign credit rating
and other bank speciﬁc and macroeconomic variables as explanatory variables.
4.1. Data sources
The study made use of bank level data which was obtained from bankscope. The frequency of the data is on annual basis and from
the period 2006–2012 for banks in Africa where by the countries in which these banks domiciled received sovereign rating from
either Fitch or S & P. The study used the long term local currency sovereign rating for countries in Africa that received sovereign
rating from either Fitch or S & P during the period of the study. The sovereign credit rating data was obtained from the public website
of Fitch and S & P. Macroeconomic data which include GDP, Inﬂation and money supply were obtained from the World Bank
Development Indicators for the same periods the study is undertaken.

4.3. Estimation strategy
The Arellano-Bover/Blundell-Bond Generalized method of moments (GMM) approach is used to estimate the variations in the
funding costs of bank which is made up of bank speciﬁc and macroeconomic variables. This estimation technique is an extension of
the Arellano-Bond where past values of the independent variable are used as instruments together with some other instrumental
variables. This estimator augments Arellano–Bond by adding another assumption that ﬁrst diﬀerences of instrument variables are not
correlated with the ﬁxed eﬀects and it allow more instruments to be introduced which in a way would improve eﬃciency. The GMM
technique is designed for situations where the data has a large number of individuals with each observe for a shorter period
(Roodman, 2006). This study has a shorter time period of seven years with over three hundred banks from seventeen countries in
Africa. The GMM approach allows the funding cost to be treated as a dynamic process, where there is a possibility that past funding
cost inﬂuence current funding cost. To ﬁnd out the consistency of the estimates, Sargan test of overidentiﬁcation restrictions was
employed to measure the validity of the instruments. This is suitable for the funding cost analysis because it is designed for situations
involving a linear functional relationship, independent variables that are correlated with previous and perhaps current realizations of
the error, autocorrelation and heteroscedasticity within individuals as well as ﬁxed individual eﬀects. The dynamic model is seen to
be more eﬃcient than the static model in estimation of the funding cost.
4.4. Variable deﬁnition and selection
4.4.1. Funding cost of banks
The dependent variable for the ﬁrst model is funding cost. The rate of interest that a bank will pay in order to obtain money
through deposits is known as the cost of fund. This study will follow the same way as deﬁned by (Dietrich and Wanzenried, 2011).
Funding cost is measured as interest expenses divided by the average of total deposits. Dietrich and Wanzenried (2011) further
argued that the funding cost is mostly determined by market competition, the bank’s credit rating, market interest rates and the
sources of funds composition.
4.4.2. Sovereign credit rating
In the funding cost model the sovereign rating is the main variable of interest and the others are control variables. The average
rating for each year were computed and converted into numerical scale for instance lower numbers correspond to lower ratings and
also higher numbers correspond to higher ratings. Example AAA = 20, AA+ = 19… CC = 1 in line with Cantor and Packer (1997)
and Li et al., (2008). The credit worthiness of a bank does aﬀect its cost of funding. Sovereign credit rating is a contemporaneous
variable which means the eﬀect is instantaneous and funding cost is very sensitive to its news. Babihuga and Spaltro (2014) argued
that the credit quality of individual bank is strongly linked with lower costs of funding. A study by Correa et al. (2014) asserted that
sovereign ratings inﬂuence the cost of funding of banks that operates within the sovereign and the impact is more with banks that

4.4.2.1. Bank size. This is a major determining factor of funding cost in the literature. It is measured as the log of total deposit. Larger
and systemic banks enjoy some advantages regarding their total funding costs as a result of their “too-big-to-fail” status (Babihuga
and Spaltro, 2014). Banks perceived to be “too-big-to-fail” have lower total funding costs due to funding cost subsidy they enjoy, that
is these banks are likely to be bailed-out if they are to fail (Acharya et al., 2015; Araten and Turner, 2012; Demirgü & -Kunt and
Huizinga, 2010). It is also possible that banks that are extremely large may solicit for more deposits to lend to their borrowers, which
will let banks pay more interest on the deposits and as a result it may increase the cost of fund of those banks. Thus, margins on
interest-bearing assets, or NIMs, impact more on larger banks than on smaller ones. Therefore the relationship between the size and
costs of funding could either be positive or negative.
4.4.2.2. Capital adequacy. This is measured as the ratio of equity to total assets. Banks that is well capitalized are perceived to be
safer and also less risky (Dietrich and Wanzenried, 2011). When a bank is seen to be less risky, it becomes easy for such banks to raise
capital at a cheaper cost. Babihuga and Spaltro (2014) argued that an improved capital buﬀers can cause a decline in the cost of
funding of a bank in the long run. Therefore an inverse relationship is expected.
4.4.2.3. Proﬁtability. In measuring funding cost, proﬁtability is considered as one of the determinants of cost of funding. Proﬁtability
is proxied with return on average asset (ROAA) of banks where ROAA is measured as ratio of net income to average total assets.
ROAA tells how eﬀective management is using the banks’ resources in achieving their objectives. An increase in proﬁtability should
cause the cost of funding to fall and vice versa. Investors will be willing to give additional funds to banks that utilized its resources
eﬀectively to earn a higher return and may also accept a lower rate of interest since they believed that those banks can easily pay their
borrowed funds in other words assurance of safety for their funds. Contrary to that, some investors who lend funds to banks that are
earning higher proﬁts may also expect a higher rate of returns on their funds. When investors demand that the banks pay high interest
rate on their funds, it will lead to a rise in the funding cost. Hence, a positive or negative relationship is expected between proﬁtability
and funding cost.
4.4.2.4. Overhead cost. This is measured as the log of personnel expenses plus other operating expenses of banks. These costs may not
necessarily have a direct eﬀect on the funding cost but indirectly it does impede the ﬂow of funds and ultimately the cost of fund.
Mostly fees and other legal costs incur by banks in securing funds also aﬀect the cost of funds. As these costs increases, funding cost
should also rise hence a positive relationship is expected. Jibrin et al. (2015) in their study mentioned overhead cost as one of the
determinants of funding cost.
4.4.2.5. Money supply (M2) growth. Money supply can be deﬁned as the money stock available in the economy. It is dependent on the
monetary policy carried out by the central bank. Typically, the central bank through the use of open-market operations and bank
reserve ratio can exert great impact on the volume of currency in the economy. Basically money supply is determined by the policy of
the central bank. The supply of money can also be aﬀected by actions of banks and households (Suﬁan and Habibullah, 2009).
Changes in money supply may bring about a change in the level of prices as well as the nominal GDP of a country. When there is an
increase in money supply, interest expense falls hence a decrease in funding cost. A negative relationship is expected.
4.4.2.6. GDP growth. The annual GDP per capita growth reﬂects current economic conditions of a country. An improvement in the
economic conditions of a nation should reduce the riskiness of bank and as a result a lower costs of funding (Babihuga and Spaltro,
2014). It is expected that the relationship between GDP growth and funding cost be negative.
4.4.2.7. Inﬂation. It impacts every aspect of the economy such as cost of living, the cost of doing business as well as borrowing
money. The consumer price index is used to measure inﬂation. If inﬂation becomes too high, it will aﬀect the economy in terms of
borrowing funds and high cost of living and this will result in an increase in cost of funding. On the other side, if inﬂation is controlled
at a certain level that is reasonable, it may beneﬁt the economy and all the sectors within. This will also help the banks to reduce their
cost of borrowing. The relationship is expected to be either positive or negative.

expectation and it suggests that banks that are well-capitalized can raise funds at a cheaper cost for their operations. Also, this will
mean that if the contribution from equity in the banks is higher than other external funds, the external funds providers will perceive
that the risk attached to their investment is less and hence will be willing to provide funds to banks at a lower rate of return. This is
consistent with the ﬁndings of Babihuga and Spaltro (2014).
The size of the bank is negatively correlated with funding cost and statistically signiﬁcant. The negative relationship is expected.

happened the banks will incur higher cost before they can access these funds and as a result the cost of funds would increase.
Overhead cost had a positive relationship with funding cost but it was not signiﬁcant.
The relationship between the GDP growth rate and funding cost is negative and signiﬁcant but at 5%. This implies that in those
periods in which the economy is booming, banks are able to raise funds at a lower cost since improvement in the economic conditions
of a country will reduce the risk attached to banks in paying its debts as they fall due.
Money supply was found to be positive and insigniﬁcantly related to funding cost of banks.
Inﬂation was found to be negative and signiﬁcantly related to funding cost. This may suggest that high inﬂation anticipated by
management and controlled at a certain reasonable level will reduce cost of funding by banks.
6. Summary and conclusion
The study examined the eﬀect of sovereign ratings announcement on funding costs of banks in Africa from 2006 to 2012.
The result shows an inverse relationship between sovereign rating and funding cost. Sovereign rating upgrades make it easy for
banks situated in that sovereign to raise funds at a lower cost. Banks whose sovereign receive a rating upgrade can also access funds

from the international market at a cheaper rate compared to banks situated in a country that has been downgraded. When banks are
able to access funds at a cheaper cost it reduces its cost of funding. Sovereign downgrades make it diﬃcult for banks to access funds
on the market. Lenders willing to give funds to banks from a country that has been downgraded do so at a higher cost which will
cause the cost of funding for the banks to rise.
The study controlled for some bank speciﬁc and macroeconomics variables. The result on capital adequacy is negatively related to
funding cost but not signiﬁcant. This suggests that well capitalized banks can raise funds for their operations at a cheaper cost. The
bank size is signiﬁcant and negatively related to funding cost. This may mean large banks can raise funds at a lower cost. The results
show a positive and signiﬁcant relationship between proﬁtability and funding cost. The reason may be that depositors and lenders
demand a high interest from banks as their proﬁt grows. GDP growth rate has negative and signiﬁcant relationship with funding cost.
This means banks are able to raise funds at a lower cost of funding when economic conditions are improved. The coeﬃcient of growth
in money supply was positive and not signiﬁcantly associated with funding cost. Inﬂation also shows a negative coeﬃcient and
signiﬁcantly related to funding cost.
The study concluded that sovereign rating upgrades will reduce the cost of funding of banks. This is because sovereign rating
upgrades lessen the burden in which the banks will have to go through in accessing funds on the capital market. The banks are able to
access these funds at a lower rate due to the rating upgrade and this reduces the funding cost of the banks. On the other hand,
sovereign downgrade makes it diﬃcult for these banks to access funds on the market and as a result the banks are compelled to pay a
higher cost on the ones they can get access to. Investors would only lend to borrowers at a cheaper rate when they know the default
rate of the borrower is less, than to a highly risky borrower who can easily default in terms of payment. In all, sovereign rating
upgrades (downgrades) lead to a fall (rise) in funding costs.
6.1. Policy recommendations
The ﬁndings have policy implications and direction for emerging markets especially when these outcomes are stemming from
emerging economies. The above result suggests that sovereign ratings have genuine economic consequences for banks. The study
recommends and encourages emerging economies to use the services provided by credit rating agencies since these agencies may help
improve accessibility of funds in the international markets by banks. Moreover, the study recommends that banks should pay particular attention to sovereign credit ratings announcement. This is because the Basel III accord advice banks to use solicited ratings
for both capital requirement and risk management purposes. Since many banks in Africa are not rated sovereign ratings can be an
option.
Also, there are no agreed upon or standard methods to measure the credit rating accuracy by the credit rating agencies. This is
partly because of the subjectivity nature of the ratings nonetheless the announcement of it causes market reaction. Therefore, in
making investment and funding decisions, it is recommended that sovereign rating should be considered as a supplement and not a
substitute to our own perceived judgement and research.
