

African Development Review, Vol. 28, No. 3, 2016, 332–343


Does Information Sharing Promote or Detract from Bank Returns: Evidence from Ghana

Baah Aye Kusi, Elikplimi Komla Agbloyor, Vera Ogeh Fiador and Kofi Achampong Osei*


  Abstract: This study examines the effect of information sharing through credit reference bureaus (CRBs) on the profitability of banks in Ghana. We adopt a Prais-Winsten panel regression for 25 banks across four years to examine the empirical relations. We establish that information sharing through CRBs is positively related to bank profitability. This implies that as banks use the services of CRBs (indicating information sharing) they are able to boost their profitability. This is because information sharing can lead to an increase in interest income by reducing incomplete and false information which in turn leads to a reduction in collateral constraints. Further, information sharing reduces information asymmetry by lowering the evaluation (adverse selection) and monitoring costs (moral hazard) associated with the lending proposition. The major implications of our findings are that sharing information across banks is important for the profitability of the banking system as a whole. In this regard, it would be useful to find more efficient and cost-effective ways to provide information sharing services to banks and other non-bank financial institutions. We recommend that the laws that mandate the gathering of credit information be expanded to include credit data from tax agencies, utility agencies and court rulings on financial matters.

1. Introduction
The continent of Africa in recent times has seen the emergence of credit information sharing through Credit Referencing Bureaus (CRBs), although information sharing through CRBs have long existed in most European and American countries. As at 2012, 8 out of 46 countries had established CRBs in sub-Saharan Africa, while 7 out of 19 countries had CRBs established in the middle, east and north of Africa. Also, only two countries (Ghana and Nigeria) in the West African sub-region had CRBs at the time (World Bank, 2012). CRBs are institutions that collect financial data, process the data, store it and at the request of lenders and other financial institutions, share or provide the credit worthiness status or report for lending decisions by the requesting institution. Theories and empirics suggest numerous effects of CRBs on the banks and economies as a whole. For instance, the information asymmetry theory (see Freimer and Gordon, 1965; Stiglitz and Weiss, 1981) argues that the lack of complete and accurate information leads to credit rationing and collateral requirement because banks are unable to distinguish between good and bad borrowers. That is, information asymmetry leads to low credit extension to credit worthy borrowers who cannot provide collateral. Again, credit information sharing theory (see Gehrig and Stenbacka, 2007) helps banks to reduce adverse selection and moral hazard in the credit market. This implies that banks are able to eliminate defaulters from the loan process, which increases their profitability through the interest paid by creditworthy clients.
  Empirical studies also provide similar arguments. For instance, Brown and Zehnder (2007), Barth et al. (2009) and Powell et al. (2004) find that CRBs can help improve loan repayment, reduce bank corruption and default rates respectively. Brown et al. (2009) and Djankov et al. (2007) have also shown that CRBs improve access to finance. However, we do not cite any empirical studies that examine the impact of information sharing through CRBs on bank profitability, especially in the African context. We argue the existence of this lacuna in a number of ways. First, Stiglitz and Weiss (1981) argue that credit rationing is adopted by banks because they are unable to differentiate between good and bad borrowers because of the cost of evaluating and monitoring borrowers. Hence, banks may charge a flat interest rate for all kinds of borrowers. In view of this, we argue that credit information shared through CRBs reduces the cost of evaluating, selecting and monitoring borrowers which improves bank profitability. This

2. Overview of Credit Referencing Bureaus in Ghana
CRBs started operations in Ghana in 2010. However, the Act (Credit Reporting Act 2007: Act 726) was enacted in the latter part of 2007. The Act permitted the existence of information sharing institutions in the banking sector and defined their roles. The Banking Survey (2010) reports that bank performance in terms of credit risk and profitability were worsening. Hence, CRBs came in when the performance of the banking sector was deteriorating. Given this, the introduction of CRBs was expected to salvage the worsening situation and also to strengthen the lending proposition of banks, which was clouded with many difficulties and challenges.
  Although the Act that permitted the existence and operations of CRBs was enacted in the later part of 2007, the first credit reference bureau company (XDS Data Credit Reference Bureau) was established in 2008 and given two years to gather and synchronize financial data from the banks so as to enhance their ability to deliver reliable, accurate and quality credit referencing services. Currently, three CRBs including XDS Data Credit Reference Bureau, Hudson Price Credit Bureau and Dun and Bradstreet Credit Bureau Limited operate in Ghana. XDS Data Credit Reference Bureau has its roots in South Africa, while the other two have a presence in America, Europe and some African countries. Until 2013, XDS Data Credit Reference Bureau enjoyed the monopoly of being the only credit referencing company in Ghana. Currently, only banks, savings and loans and microfinance institutions are mandated to submit their monthly financial statements to the three CRBs. Unlike the case of Ghana, Miller (2003) reports that in some European and American countries, CRBs source data from both the financial and non-financial institutions, including tax agencies, utility companies and from court rulings on financial matters, which makes their data comprehensive enough for accurate and reliable financial decisions.

3. Theoretical and Empirical Review
Information sharing in the credit market is a relatively new concept in most developing or emerging markets (Luoto et al., 2007). Numerous benefits are reported to be associated with information sharing in the credit market, and one important benefit of information sharing which we argue for and explore here, is its effect on bank profitability. The study briefly highlights some key empirical studies on information sharing.
  First, empirical studies suggest that information sharing reduces credit risk. Studies that examine the impact of information sharing on bank credit risk argue in two ways: screening and incentive effects (Brown et al., 2009; Luoto et al., 2007; Djankov et al., 2007). The screening effect suggests that information sharing enables banks to evaluate and identify clients’ ability to repay their loans, and hence to enhance the default predictive power of banks which helps to reduce adverse selection (Pagano and Jappelli, 1993). Also, the incentive effect proposes that bank clients are motivated to repay loans because of the fear of future denial of credit by lenders, because default is shared by all lenders. Hence, bank clients are pressured to perform and settle their loans because they will be denied access to credit in the future upon default (Padilla and Pagano, 2000). Empirical studies that provide evidence in support of a negative link between CRBs (information sharing) and credit risk includes Kallberg and Udell (2003), Bennardo et al. (2015), Brown and Zehnder (2007) and Luoto et al. (2007).
  Second, information sharing and access to credit has also been in the spotlight, especially in Africa, where obstacles to credit are most pressing. Studies on information sharing and access to credit argue that sharing information helps to extend credit to the poor, but creditworthy, borrowers who do not have collateral. This can be explained by the fact that the decision to advance credit is not solely based on the borrower’s ability to provide adequate collateral in the presence of information sharing. Information sharing therefore helps to reduce collateral constraints (see Love and Mylenko, 2003; Berger and Frame, 2006; Djankov et al., 2007; Brown et al., 2009; Behr and Sonnekalb, 2012).

4. Methodology
The study utilizes panel data techniques to establish the effect of CRB usage on bank profitability in the Ghanaian banking sector. Panel data estimation presents more convincing results than the traditional time series and cross-sectional analyses, since it exploits the advantages of the time series and the cross-sectional data. Another advantage of the panel is its ability to control for


omitted variables and bank specific effects and also allows for long- and short-run effects. This overcomes the shortcomings of the cross-sectional and time series estimations (Wooldridge, 2009).
The general form of a panel data model is
Y it = ai + gt + bXit + eit	(1)
where subscript i denotes the cross-sectional dimension (bank) i = 1 .. .N and t denotes the time series dimension (time), t =1... T; Yit is the dependent variable; ai is scalar and constant term for all periods (t) and specific to a bank fixed effect (i); gt is the time fixed effect t; b is a k × 1 vector of parameters to be estimated on the independent variables; Xit is a 1× k vector of observations on the independent variables comprising independent variables in the model which include controlled variables; and eit, which is iid as the error term.
Following the general panel data model above, the study contextualizes the bank profitability model to be estimated as:
ROAit = b1 Bank Specif ic Variablesit + b2 Industry Specif ic Variablet + b3 Macroeconomic variablest + eit	(2)


4.1 Empirical Estimation Approach
To establish the effect of CRBs on bank profitability, we employed the Prais–Winsten estimation after the homoscedasticity and the normality assumptions under ordinary least squares (OLS) approach were not met. The Prais–Winsten (1954) estimates linear regressions that are corrected for panel specific serially correlated residuals and heteroscedastic panels. The Prais–Winsten generalized least squares (GLS) estimation was employed ahead of other GLS estimations like Cochrane–Orcutt for a number of reasons. First, the Cochrane–Orcutt estimator does not effectively deal with the serial correlation when it is closer to one. Second, the variance of the first observations error term is important for relative efficiencies of the estimators which the Prais–Winsten preserves. Finally, the asymptotic results suggest that the feasible Prais–Winsten estimator is the best estimator in most applied situations (see Judge, Griffiths, Hill, Lutkepohl and Lee, 1985). Hence, we estimate our bank profitability models as:
ROAit = b1 CRBCRit + b2 CAPit + b3 SIZEit + b4 INEFFit + b5 HHILt + b6 GDPGROWHt + b7 INFLt + eit ···  (3)
  ROA is a dependent variable which varies across bank (i) and time (t). It is used to capture bank profitability. CRBCR is a measure for CRB service usage and usage cost ratio, CAP measures capital adequacy, SIZE measures bank size and INEFF measures management quality. These variables mentioned are bank specific variables and vary across time and bank. HHIL varies across time and is a measure for bank loan concentration. GDPGROWTH and INFL are macroeconomic variables and vary across time. They represent GDP growth and inflation respectively while eit is the error term and varies across bank and time.

4.2 Variable Selection and Description
Return on Asset (ROA)
The study employs return on assets (ROA) in the bank profitability model ahead of return on equity as a dependent variable, because ROE has a major shortcoming since it ignores financial leverage and the effect of regulation on financial leverage (Athanasoglou et al., 2008; Dietrich and Wanzenried, 2011). Given this challenge, the study adopts the use of ROA and this is measured as earnings before tax divided by total assets.

Credit Referencing Bureaus (CRBE and CRBCR)
CRBs which are mandated to share information are employed as a bank specific factor in our model. However, the variable of interest is employed using two different measures. First, it is measured as the natural log of the number of times each bank has used CRB services in a given year (represented as CRBE). Second, we divide the cost of using CRB services by the total loans


and advances by a given bank in a particular year (CRBCR). While CRBE measures whether the frequency of CRB services usage affect bank profitability, CRBCR measures whether the willingness and ability of a bank to use CRB services affect its profitability. These two variables measure credit information sharing from a bank’s perceptive. We develop and utilize these measures because we believe it captures more accurately a bank’s use of information from a CRB. These measures are employed since some earlier studies on CRBs (information sharing) have used dummies which do not best capture the effect (see Brooks, 2008; Wooldridge, 2009). Again, the study did not use the Public Credit Registries (PCR) and Private Credit Bureaus Coverage (PCB) in the World Development Indicators for three reasons. First, PCR and PCB measure the extent of coverage (number of adult population covered by credit information sharing institutions), not patronage or usage by banks, as this study set out to examine. Second, the PCR and PCB variables measure information sharing at the country level, while this study measures information sharing at the bank level. Third, the PCR and PCB variables do not capture the cost of usage or the patronage of credit information sharing cost which affect the profitabilities of banks. Given these challenges, the study employs CRBE and CRBCR. Following the information sharing theory, we anticipate a positive impact of CRB usage on bank profitability.

Capital Adequacy
Capital adequacy is measured as total equity divided by total assets. It is a measure of how much of a bank’s assets is financed with owner’s capital and forecasts the ability of a bank to soak up losses. In line with risk-return hypothesis (see Berger, 1995), we anticipate a positive relationship between bank profitability and capital.

Bank Size
Size is measured with total assets and normalized by logging the assets to ensure consistent, efficient and unbiased estimates. The relationship between bank size and profitability has mixed findings in bank profitability studies. First, due to the fact that governments are likely not to allow big banks to fail, a risk approach to size would expect that larger banks would require lower profits (Athanasoglou et al., 2008). On the contrary, current intermediation theory foretells there are efficiency gains that relate to bank size (economies of scale) (Athanasoglou et al., 2008), especially when bigger banks have a greater share of the domestic market, and operate in a less competitive environment, lending rates may stay high and as a result larger banks may enjoy higher profits.

Inefficiency
Inefficiency is employed in the profitability model and measured as operating expenses divided by total assets (see Athanasoglou et al., 2008). Following Athanasoglou et al. (2008) an improvement in the management of these expenses will increase efficiency and consequently raise profits. This suggests a negative relationship. Alternatively, where banks are able to pass on their cost operations to their clients, a positive relationship will exist between bank inefficiency and profitability (Iannotta et al., 2007; Naceur and Omran, 2011).

Concentration (HHIL)
The Herfindahl-Hirschman (HHI) loan concentration index is employed, and measured as the sum of squares of loans and advances. This study monitors how banking market structure (using loan concentration) affect bank profitability. The relationship between bank market structure and performance is explained by the competition stability and competition-fragility hypotheses (see Zhao and Murinde, 2011; Beck et al., 2006; Turk-Ariss, 2010; Schaeck and Cihak, 2010; Keeley, 1990). That is, a negative relationship between concentration and profitability indicates the competition stability hypothesis while a positive relationship indicates the competition fragility hypothesis. Hence, the anticipated relationship is unclear.

Gross Domestic Product Growth Rate
Bank profitability is responsive to economic conditions (GDP growth) (see Athanasoglou et al, 2005). A positive relationship implies that improvement in GDP growth leads to improvement in bank profitability because, borrowers are able and willing to


settle loan obligations when GDP growth improves (see Demirguc-Kunt and Huizinga, 1998; Athanasoglou et al., 2005; Bikker and Hu, 2002). However, the Pecking Order Theory and the loan growth analysis (Keeton, 1999) indicates a negative relationship could exist between the two. Hence, an improvement in economic conditions implies less loan demand which leads to low interest income and profitability (see Keeton, 1999).

In?ation Rate
Bank performance is responsive to macroeconomic sensitivities (Nguena and Nanfosso, 2014).The impact of the inflation rate on bank profitability depends on whether future fluctuations in inflation are completely anticipated, and in turn, it depends on the ability of banks’ management to accurately forecast future movements in the relevant control variables (Adom et al., 2015; Bourke, 1989; Demirguc-Kunt and Huizinga, 1998). Where bank management can fully anticipate changes in inflation and adequately adjust the increase rate, this will lead to higher bank profitability. However, if a bank management is sluggish to anticipate changes in inflation and accordingly to adjust the interest rate, this may also lead to lower bank profitability. Hence, the impact of inflation on bank profitability is ambiguous.

This indicates that risk averse shareholders require higher return on investment to compensate for their risk, and hence put pressure on bank management to perform in order to meet the high compensation demand of shareholders (see Berger, 1995; Athanasoglou et al., 2005; Iannota et al., 2007; Naceur and Omran, 2011). Bank size was positively significant in Model 3 in the absence of the CRB measures. We therefore argue that in the presence of CRB, bank size does not give any advantage to larger banks to make more profit than small banks.

The results suggests that banks in Ghana are able to pass on their cost of operation to depositors and lenders through the rates they charge, which increases bank profitability. Loan concentration, however, had a negative but significant effect on bank profitability in all three models. This indicates that as loans advancement concentrate in fewer banks, bank profitability decreases, implying that banks with market power (in terms of loan concentration) are unable to take advantage of the situation (see Pain, 2003; Turk-Ariss, 2010). This follows the competition- stability hypothesis which suggests that competition drives efficiency, resulting in bank stability and improves the soundness of the banking sector.

(2005), we suggest that this finding is consistent with the Pecking Order Theory and Keeton’s (1999) analysis on loan growth. They argue that banks lower or relax lending conditions and processes to attract clients in times of economic boom (depicting GDP growth) because individual and corporate entities have a disincentive to borrow during economic boom and prefer to rely on internal funds, since they would have enough funds (following pecking order theory). Hence, relaxation or lowering of lending standards leads to reduction of bank profits, since individuals and corporate entities will be attracted, but will not have any good use for the funds borrowed to pay back the loan. It is, however, interesting to know that inflation is negatively and significantly related to bank profitability in Model 3 (absence of CRBs) but is positively related to bank profitability in Models 1 and 2. We argue from Model 1 that banks that frequently use the services of CRBs are able to eliminate the negative impact of economic conditions on their profitability. This is because bank borrowers are pushed to service their loans regardless of the macroeconomic conditions due to the fear of future denial of credit or loan upon default (see Vercammen, 1995; Padilla and Pagano, 1997, 2000; Jappelli and Pagano, 2002; Djankov et al., 2007; Bennardo et al. 2015). However, both relationships between bank profitability and inflation are consistent with previous studies. While Athanasoglou et al. (2008) report a positive relationship, Boyd et al. (2001) also report a negative effect. We further establish that CRBs explain about 17.5 per cent of the total variation in bank profitability. This was established by deducting the R squared of Model 1 and 2 from that of Model 3.

5.2 Robustness Test
We first pursued our objective with the OLS approach. However, the assumption of homoscedasticity and normality of residuals were not met. Hence, we proceeded to estimate our model with the GLS approach. We then performed the Hausman test to choose between the fixed and random effects estimations. The Hausman test suggested a fixed effects estimation. However, the fixed effect regression was tested to be heteroskedastic (using the Modified Wald test for groupwise heteroscedasticity) but all other assumptions were met. To overcome the heteroscedastic problem, we employ the Prais–Winsten GLS estimation model to correct for heteroscedasticity (see Judge et al., 1985; Prais–Winsten, 1954). We further estimated the models without including the measures for CRBs (see Model 3 in both models), as additional robust tests. The results are largely consistent with the three models estimated.

6. Conclusion and Policy Implications
To improve banking stability, central banks and other institutions mandated for such duties have instituted many policies which enhance bank stability. One of such policies is the establishment of Credit Referencing Bureaus (CRBs). Ghana resorted to credit referencing in 2007 and it important to examine if the goals of the Central Bank have been achieved. In this paper, we test the effect of information sharing on bank profitability. Our empirical model indicates a positive relationship between bank profitability and CRB usage by banks. This helps banks to reduce their evaluation and monitoring cost. Further, the presence of CRBs puts pressure on the bank clients to service their loans. Our empirical investigations support the view that bank specific, macroeconomic and industry level factors together help explain variations in bank profitability in Ghana.
  The findings of this study have implication for CRB regulation, bank management and researchers. First, the patronage of CRB products by banks in Ghana helps banks boost their profitability. Hence, banks are advised to constantly patronize the services of CRBS so as to enable them to reduce the chances of adverse selection and moral hazard arising from incomplete or inaccurate financial data (see Brown and Zehnder, 2007; Doblas-Madrid and Minetti, 2013). It is expected that as banks continue to do this, they will be able to recover or retrieve more or all the loans and advances (principal plus interest). Second, though CRBs have a significant effect on bank profitability, there is more room for improvement since CRBs cover only about 10 per cent of the Ghanaian adult population. It is advised that policy-makers (parliament) enact laws that will increase the source of data to include insurance firms, tax agencies, utility companies and court rulings as is done in some European countries and the Americas (Barth et al., 2009; Kallberg and Udell, 2003). The study also recommends the publicity of CRBs’ presence and activities in Ghana since it forces bank borrowers to be disciplined in servicing their loans due to the potential of future denial of loans or credits upon default (see Doblas-Madrid and Minetti, 2013; Brown and Zehnder, 2007). For the purpose of future research directions, it is recommended that researchers take a look at the effect of CRBs on other performance indicators. Further, it would be important to examine factors that contribute to the efficiency and effectiveness of CRBs.
