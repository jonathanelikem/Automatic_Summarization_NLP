Abstract
Purpose – This study aims to examine the impact of economic freedom and corruption on bank stability in
sub-Saharan Africa (SSA).

Design/methodology/approach – This study uses 38 countries in SSA from 2008 to 2019 using system
GMM technique.

Findings – The authors found that greater economic freedom increases economic efﬁciency through

improving bank stability. Besides this, the authors also ﬁnd that banks in environments with greater business
freedom, ﬁnancial freedom, trade freedom and investment freedom are less prone to solvency. The results also
show that corruption improves bank stability, suggesting evidence of the “grease the wheels” hypothesis.
Practical implications – The results suggest to policymakers that a high economic freedom may be an
appropriate policy toward enhancing bank stability. Besides this, the results also suggest to policymakers to
prioritize addressing the core issues that encourage corruption to extort bribes.
Originality/value – This study provides insightful discussion on whether economic freedom and its
subcomponents and corruption have an effect on bank stability in SSA.

Keywords Economic freedom, Corruption, Business freedom, Financial freedom, Trade freedom,
Monetary freedom, Sub-Saharan Africa
Paper type Research paper

1. Introduction
Banks are considered one of the most signiﬁcant ﬁnancial intermediaries in a country.
Banks are important because they represent an economy’s primary source of savings and
credit allocation (Dell’Ariccia and Marquez, 2004). Banks are important in every economy
because they offer essential ﬁnancial services that drive development and economic growth
(Barth et al., 2005). Consequently, Levine (2004) posited that ﬁnancial institutions promote
economic growth by mobilizing and pooling savings, reducing information asymmetry,
monitoring investment and facilitating the ﬁnal exchange of goods and services.
According to Arun and Turner (2004), while the banking industry and ﬁnancial markets
work together to allocate credit in developed economies, the story is different in developing
economies because ﬁnancial markets are small or nonexistent in some cases. Therefore, it is
the responsibility of the banking sector to close the gap for both surplus and deﬁcit units
and to carry out all activities associated with the safe and proﬁtable allocation of funds.
Because of the signiﬁcance of banks, it is unsurprising that the ﬁnancial sector has received
far more attention. It explains why the banking industry has speciﬁcally sought special
protection and why there is so much focus on supervision and regulation (Barth et al., 2008).
There have been increased reforms across the globe, which have resulted in liberalization,
with developed countries having greater liberty than developing countries.

Extensive literature has examined the impact of economic freedom on economic growth
(Bergh and Bjørnskov, 2021; Bergh and Karlsson, 2009; Wiseman, 2016). Researchers have
recently been interested in the impact of economic freedom on the ﬁnancial sector (Suﬁan
and Habibullah, 2010; Suﬁan and Habibullah, 2014; Sarpong-Kumankoma et al., 2020;
Sarpong-Kumankoma et al., 2018). Most of this literature has focused on either advanced or
emerging economies (Asteriou et al., 2016; Asteriou et al., 2021; Chortareas et al., 2013). The
study contributes to the limited literature on economic freedom in the ﬁnancial sector and is
inspired by the latest developments in countries in sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) and the ﬁnancial
sector and contributes to the research on this topic. The study chose ﬁve subcomponents of
the economic freedom index (EF index) that are closely related to the ﬁnancial system, as well
as the overall EF index. These subcomponents of the indexes are monetary freedom (MF),
trade freedom (TF), investment freedom (IF), business freedom (BF) and ﬁnancial freedom
(FF). Researchers (Abbas, 2021; Chortareas et al., 2013; Harkati et al., 2019; Ghosh, 2016;
Suﬁan and Habibullah, 2010; Sarpong-Kumankoma et al., 2020) have examined these issues
separately and in various combinations in the banking sector. To the best of our knowledge,
this is the ﬁrst attempt to examine the relationship between the ﬁve subcomponents and bank
performance as proxied by stability.
Notwithstanding the body of literature on the relationship between corruption and the
economy (Ali and Sassi, 2016; Ali and Saha, 2016; Saha and Ali, 2017; Park, 2012), there is
limited research on the role of corruption in the performance of banks. Also, even though an
exceedingly overwhelming majority of literature points to the adverse impacts of corruption,
many studies suggest that it might have a positive relationship by improving capital
allocation efﬁciency (Acemoglu and Verdier, 2000; Dreher and Gassebner, 2011; Meon and
Sekkat, 2005). Furthermore, as far as we know, this study is the ﬁrst to provide empirical
evidence on the simultaneous impact of economic freedom and corruption on the
performance of banks in SSA. Studies by Asteriou et al. (2021) and Asteriou et al. (2016)
support the simultaneous examination of the role of economic freedom and corruption on
bank performance. The role of the banking industry in recent events ampliﬁes the
importance of understanding how curbing corruption, economic freedom and a sound
regulatory framework affect a bank’s ability to enhance stability.
In this regard, the study provides an investigation into the role of economic freedom and
corruption in the performance of banks in SSA. In doing so, the authors further seek to
expand the economic freedom using ﬁve subcomponents of economic freedom in the
Heritage Foundation Index to ascertain the impact of these subcomponents on bank stability
in SSA. The selection of these ﬁve subcomponents is based on their signiﬁcance to the
ﬁnancial system, as determined by earlier studies.
The rest of the paper is organized as follows: Section 2 reviews relevant empirical and
theoretical literature. Section 3 is the methodological framework used to investigate the
impact of the various variables on bank stability. Section 4 presents the empirical results
and a discussion of the results and Section 5 presents conclusion and policy implications.
2. Literature review
2.1 Corruption and bank stability
There are two opposing views on the role of corruption in bank performance. The pioneers
of the “grease the wheels” hypothesis (Leff, 1964; Huntington, 1968; Leys, 1965) believe that
endemic corruption can be advantageous in the “second-best world” due to disruptions
caused by poorly functioning organizations. Corruption can compensate for a variety
of aspects of the bureaucracy’s dysfunction. It suggests that in countries where other
elements and governance are overcentralized and ineffective, dishonesty is rampant and

bureaucracies are overly rigid, corruption may be favorable. Firms consider paying bribes to
state institutions or ofﬁcials when the payment beneﬁts their performance and growth, and
when not paid, ﬁrms will lose capital and resources (Boukou, 2017). As a result, the theory of
“grease the wheels” suggests that corruption enhances rather than harms ﬁrm performance.
On the contrary, the “sand the wheels” hypothesis is distinctive because it highlights the
adverse effects of corruption that may emerge in a weak environmental framework.
According to Myrdal (1968), the assertion that civil servants could perhaps speed up a slow
procedure highlights the perk of corruption in the lack of speed. However, corrupt
government ofﬁcials may cause slowdowns that would not be evident anyway to extort
bribes (Myrdal, 1968). Several studies have found evidence of corruption’s “sand in the
wheel” effect. La Porta et al. (2002) posit that economies with more banks owned by the
government have higher levels of corruption.

2.2 Economic freedom and bank stability
Theories on economic freedom are yet to be developed by researchers, but their impact on
the ﬁnancial sector has been established. Asteriou et al. (2016) found a negative effect of
economic freedom on the risk of bankruptcy; however, economic freedom is signiﬁcantly
positive on ﬁnancial stability when measured by the ﬁnancial system soundness index.
Again, Asteriou et al. (2021) found that the degree of economic freedom in the Eurozone has
a positive impact on bank stability. Harkati et al. (2019) found that the degree of economic
freedom, MF, BF and FF has a negative effect on the risk-taking behaviors of Malaysian
banks. However, Ghosh (2016) found that a higher degree of economic freedom and its two
subcomponents, namely, BF and MF, has a negative relationship with bank risk-taking in
Middle East and North Africa. Abbas (2021) found that TF and IF have a direct association
with the risk-taking of US banks. FF, however, reduces ﬁnancial risk-taking, exhibiting a
negative relationship. Similarly, Sarpong-Kumankoma et al. (2020) found that FF has a
negative impact on bank stability. Thus, advocating for government interference in the
ﬁnancial activities of banks in the region to ensure the stability of banks.
3. Methodology and data
3.1 Data and sample
The source of a large group of the data is the Global Financial Database, which reports
measure bank stability for the region of interest. The economic freedom and subcomponent
variables were sourced from the Heritage Foundation and retrieved from their webpage
(www.heritage.org). The corruption variable was sourced from Transparency International
and retrieved from their webpage (www.transparency.org). The macroeconomic indicators
were obtained from the World Development Indicators and retrieved from their webpage
(www.worldbank.org). The study originally comprised all countries in the SSA region from
2008 to 2019. The goal of this study was to use a lot of data as a way of generalizing the
results. Therefore, after excluding countries with missing or zero values due to data
limitations, the study settled on 38 countries in SSA from 2008 to 2019. The ﬁnal sample
after excluding missing or no variables for key variables is an unbalanced panel of 38
countries in the SSA region. The countries included in the study are Burkina Faso, Sierra
Leone, Angola, Chad, Ethiopia, Cabo Verde, Cameroon, Benin, Cote d’Ivoire, Togo,
Botswana, Uganda, Niger, Gabon, Burundi, Liberia, South Africa, Congo Democratic
Republic, Eswatini, Kenya, Ghana, Seychelles, Lesotho, Gambia, Mali, Nigeria, Namibia,
Mauritius, Mozambique, Guinea, Namibia, Rwanda, Senegal, Malawi, Tanzania, Zimbabwe,
Madagascar and Zambia. In accordance with the Economic Intelligence Unit, the countries
included in the ﬁnal sample encompassed all the largest banking industries in SSA.

3.2 Measuring bank stability indices
The Z-score is the most widely used measure to approximate bank stability in literature.
According to academic scholars Laeven and Levine (2009), the Z-score is the most widely
used metric of bank soundness. According to Boyd and Runkle (1993), the Z-score has an
inverse relationship with a bank’s solvency. The expression of the Z-score is given by:

volatility of the return on assets for country i at time t. To obtain the values for the volatility

According to Laeven and Levine (2009), our measure for bank stability measures how
distant a banking industry is from solvency. A banking system with a higher Z-score
translates to a highly stable banking sector and a lower probability of insolvency.
3.3 Economic freedom index
Following recent studies like Asteriou et al. (2021) and Sarpong-Kumankoma et al. (2020),
this study’s measure of economic freedom is proxied by the Heritage Foundation’s EF Index.
This index encompasses 12 subcomponents related to national policies on individual
freedom and each country’s interactions with other countries. It measures the overall
economic freedom based on the 12 subcomponents. The Heritage Foundation’s Index is
widely used in the literature and organized into four groups: government size, rule of law,
open markets and regulatory efﬁciency. The 12 subcomponents are labor freedom,
government integrity, MF, tax burden, FF, property rights, government spending, ﬁscal
health, judicial effectiveness, IF, TF and BF.
These 12 subcomponents are equally weighted to develop the overall EF index, with
values ranging from 0 to 100, with a higher value indicating greater economic freedom. The
overall score of the index is calculated by taking the equally weighted average of the 12
factors.
FF measures freedom from state control and interference in the banking system and
banking efﬁciency. The government holding more equity in banks and other ﬁnancial
intermediaries reduces the overall services available (Miller et al., 2022). A free banking
business environment fosters competition, resulting in an efﬁcient intermediation process
between deﬁcit and surplus units, particularly for investors and businesses. The stateimposed action that goes beyond the state’s goal of ensuring disclosure, integrity and
transparency will almost certainly reduce contestability and efﬁciency, while raising the
cost of ﬁnancing (Miller and Kim, 2016).
BF measures the rights of businesses and individuals to establish and operate businesses
without government interference. A higher score of BF means a lower regulatory barrier
that individuals and businesses must bear and a lower cost of output. The banking territory
is straightforward to forecast when there is greater BF as to productivity and proﬁtability.
However, in a free market, the state only interferes in price setting or other decision-making
(Miller and Kim, 2016).
MF involves market-determined pricing and the stability of the currency. There is no
universally accepted optimal monetary policy. However, modern monetary theories
advocate for central bank independence and a low inﬂation target. Also, there is a widely

held belief that price controls are useful in reducing market corruption, yet they result in
either surpluses or shortages (Miller and Kim, 2016). An expansionary monetary policy such
as low-interest rates may lead ﬁnancial institutions to engage in hazardous activities such as
seeking yield or boosting collateral value (Rajan, 2010; Borio and Zhu, 2012).
IF in a free and open society maximizes the potential of entrepreneurs and creates
incentives aimed at increasing employment, economic activity and productivity. A business
environment where individuals and businesses have the freedom to choose where to invest
and how to invest optimizes capital efﬁciency, allocation and ﬂow, resulting in the greatest
form of reward. The greater an environment’s investment limitations, the lower its degree of
entrepreneurship (Miller and Kim, 2016). A higher IF score means easier market access and,
as a result, cost efﬁciency. However, it may push banks to take unnecessary risks.
TF measures a country’s free exchange, the cross-border movement of goods and
services, as well as an individual’s and business’s ability to operate willingly in the global
marketplace as a buyer or seller. TF boosts the movement of investment and business
activity within and outside of a country, increasing competition between ﬁnancial
intermediaries. This, in turn, has an impact on ﬁnancial institutions’ risk-taking. Also, trade
restrictions keep advanced-technology products and services far away from local ﬁrms, thus
limiting their own productive development (Miller and Kim, 2016).

3.4 Corruption index
To assess corruption, this study used Transparency International’s Corruption Perception
Index. It is the primary proxy for corruption used in literature. Transparency International’s
Corruption Perception Index would be addressed as the TI index going forward. As in Chen
et al. (2015), the TI index is frequently used in literature. The index is a poll of polls and is
the mean of results assessed from business surveys and a country’s team of analysts’
assessments from various sources. If the errors in different surveys are independent,
averaging the results reduces the margin of error in the measurement.
A higher value of the Corruption Perception Index (CPI), scaled from 0 to 10, indicates
less corruption perceived in the country.
3.5 Control variables
Following relevant literature, the study controls for factors like diversiﬁcation (DIV),
expressed as noninterest income divided by total income, and the size of the banking sector.
Based on previous literature, researchers revealed that banks beneﬁt from diversiﬁcation by
reducing their unsystematic risk, which improves stability. Hence, diversiﬁcation is
expected to have a positive relationship with bank stability. There is also evidence to
support a negative relationship between diversiﬁcation and bank stability. The “dark side”
of diversiﬁcation argues that banks with too much diversiﬁcation tend to have higher
volatility, making them susceptible to insolvency (Stiroh and Rumble, 2006). According to
literature, larger banks have low fragility because they may have enough proﬁt to serve as
“capital buffers,” hence being less prone to insolvency. However, larger banks may have
inﬂated moral hazard problems due to being “too big to fail,” which makes them more
susceptible to insolvency.
In this study, the models also account for macroeconomic factors that control
business cycles and economic development within the 38 sub-Saharan countries.
Because global economies have experienced signiﬁcant volatility in the recent decade,
the study used two macroeconomic variables for each country’s macroeconomic
climate. These include economic development, proxied by the log of GDP per capita
(GDPit). The gross domestic product (GDP) is a measure of the volatility in economic

4.2 Eﬀect of economic freedom, corruption and bank stability in sub-Saharan Africa
Table 3 presents the regression results for the role of economic freedom and corruption on
bank stability. The table used the two-step GMM econometric methodology to model the
regression and reveals the outcome of the estimation of the impact of economic freedom and
corruption on bank stability. The estimated outcome for the lagged dependent variable
(Z-score) shows a highly signiﬁcant value of 1%, justifying this study’s use of a dynamic
panel model in the estimation. The outcome shows a direct association between the lagged
experimental variable and the experimental variable Z-score. The result indicates bank
stability is highly persistent because an increase in the previous values for stability has a
direct relationship with future stability, which is signiﬁcant. For our measure of overall
economic freedom, the variable shows a positive relationship with bank stability, which is
signiﬁcant at all levels. One explanation for this is greater economic freedom, which implies
an environment that is open and where businesses have the right to own and engage in
anything without government interference. This fuels competition, which may increase
economic efﬁciency and turn out to be less prone to insolvency. This ﬁnding agrees with
Asteriou et al. (2021), which revealed a direct association between economic freedom and
bank soundness.
Referring to the subcomponents, BF shows a positive relationship with bank soundness,
which is signiﬁcant. One explanation for this is that greater BF removes barriers to setting
up businesses that intend to increase competition, leading to economic efﬁciency and thus
being less prone to insolvency. The ﬁndings support Sarpong-Kumankoma et al. (2020), who
argue that competition enhances bank stability. MF indicates a positive relationship with
bank stability, which is signiﬁcant. It suggests that greater (or lower) monetary policy
independence increases (or reduces) the stability of banks in SSA countries’ banking sectors
during the period 2008–2019. This is contrary to Ghosh (2016), who reported a negative
association between MF and banking sector stability. TF shows a positive relationship with
bank stability, which is insigniﬁcant. This aligns with Abbas (2021), who revealed a direct
association between TF and bank stability. IF shows a direct effect on bank stability, which
is signiﬁcant. This means that a greater level of IF promotes bank stability. This supports
the results of Abbas (2021), who reported a positive association between IF and bank
stability. FF reports a positive relationship with bank stability. Thus, a higher level of FF
improves bank stability; however, this is insigniﬁcant. One explanation for this is that
greater FF leads to an economy of scope and scale for banks, which in turn decreases the
risk banks face and improves stability. This is contrary to the results of Abbas (2021), which
reported an indirect association with bank stability. Referring to our measure of corruption
using the Transparency International Index, corruption shows a negative relationship with
bank stability. This suggests that banks in SSA beneﬁt from corruption. One explanation

For the control variables, return on average assets (ROAA) shows a direct association
with bank stability, which is signiﬁcant. This means that increasing bank proﬁts tend to
reduce bank risk by improving stability. One explanation is that proﬁtable banks may
increase the lending rate to maintain their level of proﬁtability, which intends to reduce
their risk and improve stability over others. This aligns with recent studies by Ali et al.
(2019) and Harkati et al. (2019), who revealed a positive association between the margin
earned by banks and bank stability. However, the result is contrary to the “risk-return
tradeoff,” which posits that higher proﬁt is only realized when investors or businesses are
willing to accept more risk. Similarly, there is an inverse association between bank size
and bank stability; however, this is insigniﬁcant. As banks increase in size, their
systematic risk also increases as they take on more and more assets. The result is
supported by the agency theory postulation, which posits that an increase in bank size
increases bank risk, which dampens bank stability. This estimation outcome aligns with
a recent study by Abbas (2021), who established an inverse association between bank
size and bank stability. Similarly, diversiﬁcation shows a positive association with bank
stability. That is, highly diversiﬁed banks tend to be more stable with a lower probability
of insolvency. One explanation for this is that banks in SSA beneﬁt from diversiﬁcation
by reducing their risk, which improves bank stability. This ﬁnding is contrary to
Sarpong-Kumankoma et al. (2020), who reported an inverse relationship. However, this
estimation outcome aligns with Ghosh (2016), who predicted a direct association between
diversiﬁcation and bank soundness.
Economic development tends to have a direct association with bank stability. One
possible explanation for this is that higher GDP improves economic circumstances,
which may mean an increase in credit to private individuals and businesses, which may
increase the proﬁt of banks and serve as a capital buffer, which reduces risk improves
bank soundness. This outcome aligns with Harkati et al. (2019), who provided a direct
association between GDP and bank soundness. Inﬂation revealed an indirect relationship
between bank soundness and inﬂation. This means that a higher degree of inﬂation
harms the stability of banks. Ali et al. (2020) posit that when inﬂation rises, banks may
signiﬁcantly increase their lending rates above and beyond their expenses and costs,
which may involve banks in leery lending activities, increasing their risk-taking, and
resulting in increased banking fragility. The ﬁndings align with recent studies by
Asteriou et al. (2021) and Mavrakana and Psillaki (2019), who found a negative
association between inﬂation and bank stability.
5. Conclusion and policy implications
This study analyzes the effect of economic freedom and corruption on the stability of banks
in SSA. The data set uses a dynamic panel of 38 countries in SSA over a 12-year period from
2008 to 2019. Using the system GMM econometric technique, our results show that economic
freedom has a positive relationship with bank stability. This result denotes that a higher
degree of economic freedom enhances the banking sector’s soundness. For the
subcomponents that relate to the ﬁnancial sector, BF, MF, FF, IF and TF show a direct
association with bank stability, denoting that a higher score on these indexes reduces risk
and promotes bank stability. This suggests to policymakers that a higher degree of
economic freedom in the banking sector may be an appropriate policy toward ensuring the
stability of banks.

lower CPI score, which signiﬁes a high perception of corruption, improves bank stability.
This suggests that policymakers should prioritize the core issues, such as correcting an
overcentralized and ineffective, dishonest and bureaucratic environment that encourages
corruption to extort bribes.
For the control variables, return on assets reveals a direct relationship with bank
stability, denoting that a greater proﬁt boosts bank stability. Diversiﬁcation shows a
direct relationship with bank soundness, indicating that banks with a greater portion
of their funds diversiﬁed are more stable. Bank size revealed an inverse association
with bank soundness. Economic development measured by GDP also shows a direct
association with the stability of banks in SSA, indicating that countries with greater
GDP tend to have a more stable banking system. Inﬂation also shows a negative
relationship with bank stability. This means a higher level of inﬂation harms bank
stability.
