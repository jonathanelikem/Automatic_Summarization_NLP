Abstract
Purpose – The purpose of this paper is to explore the relevance of corporate governance in the quest
to attain organizational efficiency in the working capital management of listed firms. There is a
consensus that efficiency of working capital management is vital for firm’s growth and survival, yet
another consensus is the role of corporate governance in limiting managerial self-serving behavior and
ultimately improving firm’s efficiency. If the foregoing views hold, then the empirical question
“Is corporate governance important for firm-level working capital efficiency?” becomes important.
Design/methodology/approach – Panel data on 13 non-financial firms listed on the Ghana Stock
Exchange were employed in a pooled OLS regression.
Findings – The results of the study indicate mostly a negative effect of internal governance
mechanisms on the cash conversion cycle, the inventory, receivables’ periods and payables’ periods,
implying that governance structures do affect the efficiency of working capital management. Firm
characteristics like age, size and profitability also emerged as relevant influences on the efficiency of
working capital management.
Research limitations/implications – Data for the study cut across several sectors thus limiting the
specificity with which findings can be applied.
Originality/value – These findings have implications for board composition in the quest for
firm-level efficiency while raising the need for more industry-specific enquiries.

1. Introduction
The quest to provide answers to organizational performance issues is still ongoing, in
spite of the voluminous literature available that links firm performance to a host of
factors ranging from the macroeconomics (Zeitun et al., 2007; Bhattacharjee et al., 2002)
through industry level (Schmalensee, 1985, McGahan and Porter, 1997; Hawawini
et al., 2003) and to firm level (Abor, 2007b; Mahmoud, 2011; among others). At the firm
level, in particular, the value of organizational efficiency for improved firm
performance (Baik et al., 2013) is unequivocal, with competition over the years
making it even more important.
In response to this focus on organizational efficiency, quite a significant volume of
literature has emerged, documenting managerial efficiency in the use of resources as a
key ingredient in organizational success (Andreou et al., 2013; Carmeli and Tishler,
2004). One such area requiring managerial efficiency in the use of resources is the

working capital management. This is because working capital, according to
Mukhopadhyay (2004), is considered the life-giving force of any economic entity, an
assertion that is premised on the fact that every organization, whether profit making or
not or large or small and notwithstanding the nature of its business, needs a requisite
amount of working capital in order to operate. Furthermore, when investment projects
are being assessed, account is taken not only of investment in the fixed assets but also
in the current assets, because it is this capital that keeps an entity working. It is from
these views that the general belief is founded that the working capital management
setup of a firm has an important role in the success or failure of an organization.
The typical components of working capital are current asset items such as
cash, cash equivalents, short-term investments, accounts receivable, and inventory as
well as current liability items such as payables. When current assets are less
than current liabilities, then an entity has a working capital deficiency and may not be
in a position to meet its short-term liabilities with its current asset base. Working
capital management is thus considered pertinent because of its effect on profitability as
well as liquidity (Ukaegbu, 2014). Mehrotra (2013), and Guimaraes and Nossa (2010)
also support this assertion by arguing that efficient working capital management
is a relevant factor in the discourse on organizational survival, liquidity, solvency
and profitability. Block and Hirt (1992) further affirm the importance of working capital
by stating that “while long-term decisions involving plant and equipment or
marketing strategy may well determine the eventual success of the firm, effective and
efficient short-term decisions on working capital determine whether the firm gets to
the long term.”
In view of the value of efficiency in general for the corporate organization’s financial
success, there has been a quest to identify drivers of managerial or resource efficiency.
In the process, the concept of corporate governance emerged. Proponents of the agency
theory of governance ( Jensen and Meckling, 1976) posit that managers are
opportunistic shirkers who must be controlled if their actions are to be aligned with
the interests of shareholders. They go on further to offer corporate governance
mechanisms for controlling this opportunistic behavior. One such mechanism is the
corporate board. It is argued that the strength of the board in executing its most
important role is dependent on its characteristics, namely, size, composition and
leadership structure (CEO duality).
Over the years, it has also been quite well established that the propensity of a firm to
make it to the long term is predicated on the appropriateness or else of its governance
mechanisms (Waddock et al., 2002), among other factors. This suggests that a firm
without appropriate governance structures is likely to fail. Dittmar and Mahrt-Smith
(2007) subsequently argue that good corporate governance is the shareholders’ defense
against the inefficient use of corporate assets by managers. This view from the agency
theory perspective, that corporate governance is able to control the opportunistic
behavior of management, has kindled significant research interests. Numerous studies
have sought, in one way or the other, to document the relationship between corporate
governance and the efficiency of the corporate finance decision-making processes.
A number of these studies have amply supported efficiency effects of corporate
governance on corporate finance decisions like investment (Datta et al., 2001; Ayogu,
2001; Hutchinson and Gul, 2004), financing (Wen et al., 2002; Kyereboah-Coleman and
Biekpe, 2006; Abor, 2007a) and dividend payout (Short et al., 2002; Gugler, 2003;
Gugler and Yurtoglu, 2003; Farinha, 2003; Knyazeva, 2007; Abor and Fiador, 2012).
There is a relative dearth, however, of studies linking corporate governance to the

efficiency of working capital management. This link is particularly relevant because

be realized. Though a few studies have investigated corporate governance and
aspects of working capital, like cash holdings and liquidity (Kusnadi, 2011; Dittmar

Notwithstanding these findings and the fact that the literature on corporate
governance and efficiency of working capital management has been growing, the
evidence in the literature is mostly skewed toward developed countries. This skewness
constrains the applicability of the findings in the context of developing countries, Ghana
inclusive. For instance, Ghana as a country is in its incipient stages of corporate
governance (World Bank, 2005), with no standard governance code by which firms are
governed. The World Bank (2010) also indicates that beyond the legal and regulatory
governance requirements, enforcement and implementation in Ghana are plagued with
challenges. Okike (2007) sums up this situation and thus calls for context-specific
governance solutions with the argument that different socio-economic, political, cultural
and legal systems existing in each country exert influences on corporate governance,
hence what works for one country may not necessarily work for another. It is in the light
of such barriers to the general applicability of the available study findings to other
settings that some pertinent questions ultimately come to the fore: for example, how does
board governance impact the efficiency of working capital management in a context
where the broader governance structures like shareholder rights, shareholder redress,
legal structures and enforcement are weak? Does the corporate board exert any significant
influences on the efficiency of inventory and receivables and payables management
decisions of the firm so as to align with shareholder wealth maximization and thus
compensate for the weaker external governance environment? Answers to these
questions, for Ghana, will prove relevant in the quest to deploy corporate governance
structures that harness managerial efficiency toward shareholder wealth maximization.
To answer these questions, the study sets the objective of determining the influence
of the board structure on the efficiency of working capital management as a whole and
separately on its component parts: inventory, receivables and payables.
The rest of the paper is structured as follows: the next section presents an overview
of governance in Ghana, followed by sections on the method employed, the results of
the study, the conclusion and implications for management.
2. Overview of corporate governance in Ghana
In Ghana, the Companies Code (1963), which governs companies listed on the stock
exchange, is based on UK legislation and is supported by the Securities Industries Law
1963 (PNDCL 333) as amended by the Securities Industry (Amendment) Act 2000
(Act 590). In fact, Franks and Meyer (1994) describe the Ghanaian system of governance
as essentially an “outside control system,” having been inherited from its colonial days.
With regard to standard best practice governance code for companies, there is none,
and though there is a corporate governance guideline promulgated by the Securities
and Exchange Commission for public companies, compliance with this guideline

is purely voluntary. This implies an almost complete reliance on internal governance
mechanisms, such as the corporate board, to deliver on shareholder value. In terms of
investor characteristics, institutional investors in the Ghanaian financial market are
somewhat passive and focused on short-term profits, rather than corporate governance
issues. Awareness of corporate governance is in incipient stages. Ownership
concentration is on the high side and is primarily institutional with foreign and state
ownership but rarely family ownership. In terms of shareholder rights, these are
generally well observed. However, enforcement, especially on material facts disclosure,
monitoring for content, related party transactions and ownership disclosures, is lacking
(World Bank ROSC, 2005). Also, the companies’ code, which provides the main
regulatory framework for companies, does not provide for the balance between executive
and non-executive board members. In the 2010 update of the report on the observance of
codes (World Bank ROSC, 2010) on governance, it is still the case that governance
provisions remain weak, and there is not much in the area of shareholder redress,
disclosure on third party, or related transactions among others. So while governance has
somewhat improved over the years, investor protection is still just above average.

3. The data and method
The study uses all non-financial companies listed on the Ghana Stock Exchange, with at
least nine years of data from 2001 to 2012. The final sample of 13 firms is thus employed
for the study. An unbalanced panel is generated from the data collated from the published
financial statements and annual reports on the listed companies. The data were mainly
collated from the Ghana Stock Exchange fact book (various editions) and updated where
necessary from the individual company websites. Multiple regressions were used to test
the relationship between the corporate board and the efficiency of working capital and its
components while controlling for other firm-level influences like size, profits and age. The
use of panel data regressions allowed for the control of omitted variable bias that may
result from the exclusion of unobservable variables. The increase in the number of
observations, from using panel data, also provides more information and hence precision
while allowing for the analysis of economic questions that cannot be addressed using time
series or cross-sectional data (Hsiao, 2003). To test the efficiency effect of the corporate
board on working capital, the following model is estimated:

4.2 Corporate governance practices
The internal governance practices in Ghana seem to have undergone some significant
changes during the period from 2001 to 2012. Board leadership consolidation
(CEO duality) has seen a tremendous drop from around 75 percent of firms in the
market to being almost non-existent by the year 2010. The first decline in CEO duality
became evident by 2006, dropping further by 2007 and fizzling out by 2010 (Figure 5).
In view of best practice governance codes advocating for the separation of board
leadership and firm leadership, it appears that the market has attained the best practice
status for this particular mechanism. This ultimately suggests that too much power
does not lie with one person.
The proportion of non-executive members on corporate boards, on the other hand,
stayed stable at about 78 percent from 2001 to 2006. It then spiked to 82.5 percent,
plateauing there for 2007, before once more declining to its previous levels. On
the average though, it seems board independence is a common feature across the
market. This is not surprising considering it is the preferred board feature for listed
firms (Figure 6).
The relatively high proportion of non-executive board members may also suggest
that firms on the market are controlled more from the outside than from the inside. On
the one hand, this may indicate more objectivity in the decision-making process. On the
other hand, it could also pose a problem to tailored strategies since the outside directors
may not have the benefit of insider information, sometimes a very valuable factor for
making decisions that fit.

4.3 Correlation analysis
To avoid the possibility of spurious regression coefficients from the presence of
multicollinearity in the regressors, a pairwise correlation was run. None of the variables
exhibited any high correlations, so the possibility of multicollinearity was absent.
The CCC exhibited a positive and significant correlation with the inventory period and
the debtors’ period. It seems, however, that receivables management has more influence
on the efficiency of working capital (proxied as CCC) compared to the inventory
management. Payable management, as expected, exhibits a significant and negative
correlation with the CCC. The correlation value is, however, not large enough at −0.2215.
This may suggest a weak influence on CCC from the management of payables. From these
correlations, it appears the greatest influence on the CCC emanates from debtors’
management with relatively small impacts from inventory and payables management.
Board size, composition, profits and age were negatively correlated with working
capital efficiency (CCC). Age was, however, not significant. These correlations may

correlations between CEO duality and size also seem to suggest that large firms are more
likely to have the same person as CEO and board chair. The correlation values also seemed
to indicate that larger firms are likely to be associated with smaller boards or less
independent boards. Older firms, on the other hand, are likely to have smaller boards.
4.4 Regression analysis
The coefficient for board size is significant and negative for inventory holding period
and payables period but not significant for CCC and receivables. This seems to suggest
that as boards get larger, payables and inventory periods decline. The negative effect of
board size on inventory holding period and payables goes against the argument by
Yermack (1996) and Lipton and Lorsch (1992) that smaller boards are more effective in
decision making and are thus able to enhance performance. In fact, the finding seems to
suggest that the relatively larger pool of expertise associated with large boards is what
results in the efficiency of inventory and payables management. The decline in
inventory period may stem from the board advocating leaner inventories, while the
negative effect of board size on payables may also be indicative of the board pursuing
swift payment for supplies for reasons of reputation or reduction in the use of trade
credit for cost reasons. The negative relationship for payables is consistent with Karani
(2013) but contradicts the positive relationship found by Gill et al. (2014). The statistical
significance of the board size in relation to inventory efficiency, however, does not find
support in the literature, as most of the available studies (see Karani, 2013; Gill and
Biger, 2013) report an insignificant relationship. The insignificant effect of board size
on the cash conversion cycle finds support in studies by Achchuthan and Kajananthan
(2013) and Kamau and Baweti (2013) (Table IV).
The coefficient of the proportion of non-executive board members is negative and
significant for CCC, the receivables period, and the payable period but not for the inventory
period. It would appear, therefore, that as the board becomes more independent, the

(2013) also report a similar relationship between board independence and efficiency
of the CCC. The notion of efficiency of receivables management in the presence of an
independent board does not find support, as the available literature (Gill et al., 2014) finds
the relationship to be insignificant. Gill et al. (2014), however, contradict the negative
relationship between payables and board independence found in this study. They found
that board independence increases the payables period. In relation to efficiency of
inventory management, however, the independence of the board proves inconsequential.
CEO duality is insignificant in explaining any of the working capital measures. This
seems to support the trend in the literature: Achchuthan and Kajananthan (2013) and
Gill and Shah (2012) also find the relation is insignificant for the CCC. Gill and Biger
(2013) also find CEO duality has no significant impact on inventory efficiency and
payables management efficiency. This could be because the drawback of ineffective
monitoring from CEO duality is exactly offset by the cost-saving benefit of leadership
consolidation and information transfer, hence the neutral effect (Brickley et al., 1997).
In terms of the effect of firm characteristics, the results show that profit is weakly
significant in explaining the payables period. It appears that profitable firms are likely to
enjoy more trade credit and longer credit periods, possibly based on the perception of less
risk of default. The other variables such as inventory period, receivables period and the
CCC exhibit insignificant associations, which are consistent with the finding by Gill and
Biger (2013). The profit-payables relationship does not seem to converge with or
contradict any of the available literature. The coefficient of age is negative and significant
for CCC and receivables but positive and insignificant for inventory and payables periods.
It may appear that as firms get older, their CCC and receivable period improve but their
age has no impact on the inventory and payable management characteristics. It can be
inferred that the older firms may be exhibiting greater efficiency in these areas of working
capital management due to experience gained over the years. The efficiency effect of age
on the conversion cycle is consistent with the findings of Wasiuzzaman and Arumugam
(2013) but contradicts those by Banos-Caballero et al. (2010b). The coefficient of firm size is
positive and significant for CCC, inventory management, and receivables management in

It could also be that large firms are in a position to stock large inventories (hence the

from their suppliers because of their cash flow position. This study’s findings, however,
contradict the findings of Attari and Raza (2012), Wasiuzzaman and Arumugam (2013)
and Zariyawati et al. (2010), all of whom report negative relations between size and
working capital efficiency.

5. Conclusion and implications
The study set out to examine the influence that internal governance characteristics such
as board size, board independence and CEO duality exert influences on the efficacy of
working capital management and its components. The study found some board
characteristics as significant in explaining working capital efficiency albeit with
differential impacts on the various components. The study also found board
characteristics to be mostly negatively influential in relation to working capital
management. It also seems that the working capital unit of the corporate finance function
is driven by other factors and not necessarily internal governance structures. This may
also reflect the general posture of the responsibility of the board for strategic vs tactical
decisions. It is quite well acknowledged that strategic decisions rest with the board while
day-to-day decisions like working capital rest with management, and the study’s findings
seem to be confirming that ideology. It appears, however, that, where important, the board
may extend its influence on the working capital management of listed firms.
In terms of policy implications, it would appear that firms can still influence the
efficiency in the management of their inventory practices through an increase in their board
size so as to benefit from the diverse expertise that comes from a large board. It would also
be appropriate for firms seeking to minimize the CCC to have more external members on
the board. It is also recommended for firms seeking more efficient debtors’ management to
constitute more externally dominated boards while those seeking reduction in payables
deferral periods more likely to achieve that with larger or non-executive board members.
Consolidating the leadership position has no impact on working capital and its components.
On the whole, it seems that some characteristics of the corporate board are
important in explaining the efficiency of working capital in firms albeit with differential
impacts on the various components constituting working capital management. Also, it
appears that efficient working capital management can become an outcome of effective
governance structures. It must, however, be noted that since the study pooled a sample
comprising firms across different industries, the findings may be more general than
specific in terms of applicability. Future studies may look at industry-level data and
derive industry-specific influences of governance on working capital.
