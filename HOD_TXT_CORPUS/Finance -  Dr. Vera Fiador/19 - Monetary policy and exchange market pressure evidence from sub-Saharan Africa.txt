The purpose of this study was to assess the impact of monetary policy on foreign exchange market pressure (EMP) in developing country contexts for some selected countries in sub-Saharan Africa (SSA) and to measure the ability of monetary policy to significantly address currency pressures that arise from trading on the global market. This study was motivated by the fact that most of the SSA countries are developing economies that have negative net export positions and stand to lose significantly from consis- tently deteriorating foreign exchange position. The study, therefore, employs a dynamic panel model to test the hypothesis that a tighter monetary policy stance lends strength to a currency and vice versa, using 20 SSA economies for the period from 1991 to 2010. This study finds a negative and significant relationship between monetary policy and EMP, implying an easing of EMP in the face of contractionary monetary policy. The findings also point to significant relations between aggregate output, levels of public debt, the current account balance, terms of trade and EMP. Findings of this study have important implications as regards the policy direction on exchange rate and currency management.
Keywords: exchange market pressure (EMP); exchange rates; monetary policy; sub-Saharan Africa
JEL Classification: E4; E52; E61



I. Introduction
In the wake of the globalization euphoria, the foreign exchange market has taken on a very important role in the growth quest of many countries around the globe. Countries have had to seriously manage their domestic currencies in relation to major trading currencies to attain and sustain steady rates of

long-term economic growth. This renewed attention is premised on the assertion that an appreciation or depreciation of domestic currencies plays a key role in influencing a country’s trade balance (Stucka, 2004; Aziz, 2008) and ultimately its growth (McPherson and Rakovski, 2000).
  In this regard, countries have over the decades sought  to  stimulate  either  an  appreciation  or

depreciation in their domestic currencies in order to achieve some developmental target. From conven- tional economic theory, currency depreciation which makes a county’s exports cheaper relative to foreign imports is expected to promote economic growth by directing the flow of capital into the domestic econ- omy. This assertion, however, is dependent on the country having a positive net export position or, as Guitian (1976) and Dornbusch (1988) illustrated, on the ability of the country to switch demand in the proper direction and magnitude.
  However, other theoretical arguments (see Alejandro, 1963; Cooper, 1971; Krugman and Taylor, 1978; Barbone and Rivera-Batiz, 1987) are of the view that currency depreciation is more of contractionary, as opposed to being an expansionary, move. From their perspective, consistent currency depreciations may not necessarily lead to growth. It can, instead, lead to a currency crisis due to poorly managed exchange rate set-up. Excessive pressures in the exchange market (García and Malet, 2007) can therefore be disastrous for a country’s growth agenda. A typical currency crisis results in loss of trust in the ability of a country’s currency to act as a store of value and therefore directs flow of capital away from the domestic economy and negatively affects the currency.
  Given the seemingly important role of a country’s currency in the pursuit of growth objectives, a num- ber of studies have sought to unravel the determi- nants of exchange market pressure (EMP) and ultimately currency crisis. The motive has been for appropriate policy formulation. Parlaktuna (2005) in a Turkish study spanning between 1993 and 2004 reported that domestic credit is an important factor in explaining the occurrence of EMP. Ucer et al. (1998) employ the signals approach for Turkish data for the period from 1986 to 1999 to find that the short-term foreign debt and weak exports relative to imports increased the economy’s EMP and ultimate vulner- ability to currency crises. Studies by Girton and Roper (1977) and Connolly and Da Silviera (1979) in Canada and Brazil, respectively, suggest that growth in real GDP has an appreciative effect on local currencies. Tanner (2001) in a cross-country study reported that contractionary monetary policy helps to alleviate currency pressure. Gochoco- Bautista and Bautista (2005), however, are of the view that contractionary monetary policy only alle- viates currency pressure during tranquil periods, but

during crisis periods, the effect is in the opposite direction. This suggests that EMP deepens in the face of contractionary monetary policy when there is a prevailing currency crisis.
  Studying EU member states, Van Poeck et al. (2007) find current account deficits and growth in domestic credit to be consistently significant in explaining EMP. Hegerty (2010) later confirms part of Van Poeck et al. (2007) conclusions by also report- ing that a deterioration in current account deficits worsens EMP. In Turkey, Kibritcioglu et al. (1998) employ the leading indicators approach for the period from 1986 to 1998 for Turkey and identify the terms of trade (TOT) as a leading indicator of currency crisis.
  In general, the empirical results from these studies seem to point in the direction of macroeconomic fundamentals as determinants of EMP. Jayaraman and Choong (2008) have also argued that for devel- oping countries, the determinants of EMP are macro- economic in nature. This assertion notwithstanding, the studies on the determinants of EMP are far and in between and, where available, is skewed in the direc- tion of developed economies, making generaliza- tions difficult. Also, the available literature on EMP also offers no common ground with regard to sample selection, methodology and variable choice. As a result, the findings vary along with the different approaches in the literature. Feridun (2009) further documents the disagreement on the causes of EMP. According to Feridun (2009), the heterogeneity of crises in each country makes the results of available studies cannot be used reliably to draw specific con- clusions regarding the crises in other countries.
  A cursory look at the literature on EMP also points to a clear dearth of literature to aid policy design for sub-Saharan Africa (SSA). For this region, a question that has not been adequately addressed is whether the monetary policy tools employed in the quest for growth and development have had the posited effect on the target macroeconomic variables. Another one is whether these macroeconomic variables exert some feedbacks that affect the conduct of policy. This reality is especially intriguing, as most of the countries in SSA in their quest to achieve and main- tain sustainable levels of economic performance have often alternated between the use of monetary or fiscal policy variables.
  This study is one such attempt to provide some empirical answers and help fill the gap for SSA. The

main objective of the study was to explore the mone- tary policy transmission mechanism in SSA from the perspective of the foreign exchange market. The study mainly seeks to test the hypothesis that con- tractionary (expansionary) monetary policy strength- ens (weakens) a currency. The secondary objective was to identify which macroeconomic fundamentals help to explain EMP in SSA.
  The rest of the article addresses the methodology, the empirical findings, conclusions and implications of the study findings respectively.

II. Methodology
This section details the sample, data sources and model for the study. It also discusses the conceptual framework on which the econometric model is built.
Sample and source of data
This study uses data on 20 SSA countries1 over a 20-year period from 1991 to 2010 (see Appendix A for summary statistics and data sources). The coun- tries used in the sample are based on the SSA emer- ging economies identification carried out by Kehl (2007) and Radelet (2010). These analyses employed macropolitical and macroeconomic indicators in identifying emerging and upcoming emerging (threshold/frontier) economies. Performance above the international average on a significant portion of the elements for the past decade or more was used as the selection criteria. The following parameters were the metrics for the classification by Kehl (2007) – culled from the Africa Foreign Investment Survey 2003 – United Nations Industrial Development Organization: political stability; economic stability; local market access; transparency; legal framework; skilled labour availability infrastructure; and govern- ment agency support. Radelet (2010) used policy changes, the rise of democracy and economic perfor- mance as the selection criteria. Merging the output of the two studies produced a set of emerging and upcoming emerging/frontier economies. Availability of data, however, reduced the sample to 20 countries. The data for the study were captured from the International Financial Statistics (IFS) and are annual data series. The data series include central

bank policy/discount rate (as proxy for monetary policy), international reserves, the domestic currency to US dollar exchange rate, the real GDP growth, the current account balance (CABA, as a percentage of GDP), the TOT (captured as the price of exports to imports), private capital flows (PKFL) and public debt.
Model motivation and estimation
Exchange rate management regimes fall into two broad categories, fixed or freely floating. A third category, referred to as the managed float, is a hybrid of the two. Under the floating regime, disequilibria within the money market of an open economy are usually offset via an adjustment in the exchange rate of that currency, while under a fixed regime the adjustment is via foreign reserves. The managed float entails either an adjustment of the exchange rate or a change in foreign reserves or a combination of the two, a phenomenon generally captured as EMP. According to Girton and Roper (1977), EMP can be defined as the sum of exchange rate deprecia- tion and change in foreign reserves that is required to restore equilibrium to the domestic foreign exchange market. Large, increasing or positive EMP is gener- ally defined as depreciation pressure while small, decreasing or negative values indicate pressure for the domestic currency to appreciate.
  Given that EMP is a composite measure of adjust- ments to restore equilibrium to the money market, macroeconomic characteristics that exert influences on the equilibrium of the exchange rate market can be expected to also influence EMP. Following the ‘exchange rate fundamentals’ intuition, EMP is mod- elled as being a function of the central bank policy rate, the rate of real GDP growth, the CABA, the level of capital flows, the level of public debt and the TOT. This is on the assumption that ceteris paribus changes in these macroeconomic variables will affect the pressure that is exerted on a country’s currency relative to major trading currencies. Conceptual framework, variable measurement and justification
According to Connolly and Da Silviera (1979), the basic theoretical proposition is that any excess supply of money can be relieved by exchange rate depreciation in freely floating regime, a loss in foreign reserves in a fixed exchange rate regime or in the case of a managed float, some combina- tion of the two. Capturing currency pressure in the foreign exchange market using EMP therefore significantly reduces the potential error that may arise from using only changes in either the exchange rate or foreign reserves alone. The fact that 82% of the world’s currencies have some sort of peg or managed float (IMF, 2009) makes EMP a more appropriate measure of currency pressure. For the purposes of this study, EMPit, a depen- dent variable, is measured as the sum of the percentage change in the domestic price of the US dollar (it is the currency most countries trade in) plus the changes in international reserves. Positive (increasing) EMP value reflects deprecia- tion pressure, while negative (decreasing) value suggests appreciation pressure.

Monetary policy rate (MPR). We employ the central bank policy rate, MPRit, a de facto indicator for a country’s policy stance (see Kamaly and Erbil, 2000; Tanner, 2001; Gochoco-Bautista and Bautista, 2005) as a proxy for monetary policy in this study. Increases in interest rates, relative to trading partners, make investment in the higher interest rate economy more attractive and direct the flow of capital into the economy. This inflow results in excess supply of foreign reserves, thereby reducing EMP. This sug- gests that contractionary monetary policy stance (high interest rates) results in reduction in EMP; Goldfajn and Baig (1998) and Gochoco-Bautista and Bautista (2005) are among the studies that have sought to test this relationship empirically. They all find

evidence to support the posited inverse relationship between the monetary policy rate and EMP.
  An alternate view, however, holds that increases in domestic interest rates may actually have a perverse effect, thereby resulting in currency depreciation (García and Malet, 2007). This view argues that an increase in the monetary policy rate will trigger a rise in the cost of capital, ultimately reducing output growth. The fall in returns to investment and domes- tic output reduces demand for the domestic currency and causes the currency to depreciate. This argument is further buttressed by the notion that an increase in interest rates can cause panic about expected and actual bankruptcies among investors who may view the monetary policy stance as an indication of the monetary authorities’ devaluation expectations. These arguments make for an ambiguous impact of the monetary policy stance on EMP.

Output growth (RGDPG). Real GDP growth is used as a proxy for output growth. As per the intui- tion, lower GDP growth generally implies smaller demand for money (due to drop in demand), leading to expectations of currency devaluation and ulti- mately mounting pressure on the domestic currency. This suggests that a country with strong economic growth will attract investment capital (for domestic assets) to earn higher returns. This results in higher demand for the domestic currency relative to foreign currency and appreciation pressure (Balassa, 1964).2 Conversely, nations with weak growth rates are likely to experience capital flight and weaker curren- cies, resulting from a demand for foreign-denomi- nated assets earning relatively higher returns. A number of studies have included real GDP growth in studying EMP. In Brazil, Connolly and Da Silveira (1979) reported that GDP has a negative and signifi- cant effect on EMP. All things being equal, real GDP growth is expected to ease EMP in an economy.

Private capital flows (PKFL). Flowing from the argument above linking capital flows to EMP, private foreign capital flows are also included as an explana- tory variable. The investment of private capital inflows into infrastructural developments results in increases in productivity and aggregate demand. This translates into increases in the demand for the domes- tic currency and currency appreciation. On the other hand, where the capital inflows end up in the

consumption basket, it only serves to increase the relative price of domestic goods. This increases the demand to imported goods and spurring depreciation pressure (Combes et al., 2010) A number of other studies have strengthened this argument by docu- menting that the impact of PKFL on the currency position and related adjustments depends on whether the flows are procyclical or countercyclical. According to Lueth and Ruiz-Arranz (2007) and Chami et al. (2008), countercyclical flows of private capital only serve as buffers to the economy and, therefore, have no influence on the exchange rate set-up. They proceeded to indicate that procyclical flows have a tendency to overheat the economy and cause currencies to appreciate.
  Despite the seemingly well-defined route through which PKFL may affect the currency management set-up of any economy, the empirical evidence is still ambiguous. Lartey (2007), for example, reports that aggregate capital flows are inconsequential in influ- encing the exchange rate set-up using data on econo- mies from SSA. In an earlier study on six Central American Economies, Rajan and Subramanian (2005), using six Central American Economies, reported that aggregate capital flows are insignificant in explaining the EMP of an economy (see also Izquierdo and Montiel, 2006). Saborowski (2009), on the other hand, contradicts these findings with results that suggest that private capital inflows to developing countries are significant in explaining appreciation in the domestic currency. These argu- ments make for an ambiguous role for PKFL.
Current account balance (CABA). Most empiri- cal studies on EMP suggest an inverse relationship between CABA and EMP (see Hegerty, 2010; Khan, 2010). According to Rosenberg (1996), trade flows drive currency demand. A positive or surplus trade balance on the current account implies an increase in the demand for the domestic currency relative to the foreign currency. This increases foreign reserves and reduces pressure in the foreign exchange market.3 Hegerty (2010) also finds that the CABA exerts the most significant influence on EMP as predicted by theory (see also Van Poeck et al., 2007), while Khan (2010) also reports that the CABA is significant in

explaining not only the current level of EMP, but also that of two subsequent future periods.
Terms of trade (TOT).  According to Bagchi et al. (2004) and Bergvall (2004), a general increase in TOT, resulting in more imports being exchanged for one unit of exports, ceteris paribus, implies an appreciation of a local currency The appreciation in the domestic currency is, therefore, driven by the increase in demand for the domestic currency as a consequence of the rising price of the country’s exports. On the other hand, deteriorating TOT implies a need for more foreign currency to fill the gap between export revenue and import expense. Since TOT also captures the elasticities of foreign demand for domestic goods, as well as domestic demand for foreign goods, a significant deterioration in the TOT, from either declining export values or increasing import values, is likely to precipitate a depreciation of the local currency either to directly stimulate export demand or to indirectly switch domestic demand to domestic products so as to mini- mize pressure on the domestic currency. Based on the views above, TOT is expected to alleviate EMP.

Public debt. Public debt is employed in the model, to capture fiscal effects. According to the Ricardian equivalence principle, increases in the levels of debt have the same implications as government financing via tax increases as it only causes rational economic agents to increase savings today in order to maintain equivalence in the future. This neutralizes the effects of government deficit financing via debt. Opponents to this theorem, however, indicate that accumulation of public debt could undermine investor confidence and, therefore, lead to capital flight resulting in depreciation pressure on the domestic currency (Mandilaras and Bird, 2008). Another argument is that the use to which the accumulated debt is put is a key to its effect on the foreign EMP. In general, it seems that the impact of debt on EMP flows more from the increase in perceived vulnerability to shocks such as reversals in capital inflows and reduction in investor confidence due to perceived unsustainability of the public debt levels (Eichengreen and Arteta, 2000). Estimation technique
Panel data regressions were used to determine the relationship between EMP, the central bank mone- tary policy rate, the CABA, output growth, TOT, PKFL and debt. The use of panel data regressions allows for the control of omitted variable bias that may result in the exclusion of unobservable vari- ables. The increase in the number of observations, as a result of the panel data technique, also provides more information and hence precision while allow- ing for the analysis of economic questions that can- not be addressed using time series or cross-sectional data (Hsiao, 2003).
  The model for the study is based on the EMP framework of Girton and Roper (1977) for capturing exchange market dynamics and modelled as a func- tion of the macroeconomic fundamentals. Given the possibility of persistence flowing into the determina- tion of EMP, dynamic panel data estimation is used. The system is, therefore, estimated as

EMPit = ?i + ?1EMPit—1 + ?2MPRit + ?3GDPit
+ ?4PDit + ?5PKFLit + ?6TOTit
+ ?7CABAit + ?it

where EMPit–1 is the lag of the dependent variable along with the policy rate, real GDP growth, debt, PKFL and the TOT.
  The Arellano and Bond (1991) approach to dynamic panel estimation, using the difference gen- eralised method of moments (GMMs) is used. This is on account of the fact that the right-hand-side vari- ables cannot be said to be strictly exogenously coupled  with  the  possibility  of  unobserved

heterogeneity and the fact that the panel is unba- lanced and the sample selection is not random.

Regression results
The regression results are presented in Table 2. The coefficient of the lag of the dependent variable, EMP (?1), is negative and significant in explaining current levels of EMP. This indicates that past (higher) levels of EMP reduces current EMP and suggests the absence of EMP persistence. This could be an indica- tion that high periods of EMP are usually expected to tapper out and therefore end up doing so.
  The central bank policy rate, MPR, has a signifi- cant negative impact on EMP, which is consistent with Tanner (2001) and Kamaly and Erbil (2000). This confirms the position of the conventional wis- dom which posits an appreciation effect for increas- ing domestic interest rates. This implies that contractionary monetary policy stance, which trans- lates into higher interest rates, has a tendency to ease depreciation pressure on the domestic currency. Given that the countries under study are yet to experi- ence currency crises, the findings of Gochoco- Bautista and Bautista (2005) may prove more rele- vant to the SSA situation. Hence, our findings can only be expected to hold during tranquil periods, but not in crisis periods.
  The stock of public debt has a significant negative effect on EMP. This indicates that rising levels of public debt is characterised by lowering levels of EMP. This contradicts the generally accepted notion which supports a depreciation effect for high levels of debt due to perceived unsustainability (see Eichengreen and Arteta, 2000). The appreciating effect seems to indicate that accumulated debt finds its way into the production function of the sample

countries to stimulate output growth. This translates into high demand for the local currency (as a result of a high demand for goods and investments from the domestic economy).
  Output growth, captured as real GDP growth, shows a significant positive relationship with EMP. This indicates that increasing real GDP is associated with depreciating domestic currencies, which is con- trary to theoretical and empirical literature which posits an appreciation effect (see Balassa, 1964; Samuelson, 1964; Hegerty, 2010). This is similar to the findings of Gunsel et al. (2010) from panel of Latin American and Asian Economies. Our results suggest that currency depreciation in the face of increasing GDP is usually underpinned by excessive local demand for foreign goods. This creates an excess demand for foreign currency over and above the domestic currency and triggers depreciation. Alternatively, the finding is also supported by the inflation-induced depreciation. According to this intuition, increases in aggregate spending and the resulting increases in GDP, which are not matched by the productive capacity of the economy in the tradable sector, will result in inflation and a subse- quent depreciation in the currency (see also Combes et al. (2010)).
  The coefficient of TOT, as expected, has a signifi- cant negative effect on EMP. This implies that as TOT improves (export earnings flow in excess of import expenses), the supply of foreign currency exceeds that of the local currency, creating apprecia- tion pressure on the domestic currency. Sensitivity analysis and robustness checks
To test for the possibility of biases in the results, they were subjected to robustness checks. The first test was to exclude South Africa (being the oldest and largest emerging economy in the sample) so as to rule out any form of domination by South Africa. From Table 3, there is no indication of significant differ- ences from the previous regression including South Africa in the sample. On the whole, the variables retained their signs and significance, giving no rea- son to infer any biases in the coefficient estimates as a result of including South Africa in the panel.
  Further tests for sensitivity to specification also included the addition of lags of GDP to the main model (results in Appendix B). Upon the addition of one lag, both the current level and lag of GDP were insignificant but maintained their signs.
  PKFL, however, were significant and with the expected negative sign. This implies that an increase in PKFL causes an appreciation in the domestic currency. This finding is in consonance with the findings of Hegerty (2010). In essence, it appears that when countries are able to direct the flow of foreign capital into the economy, the local currency will appreciate. Given that EMP captures changes in the exchange rate and international reserve positions, inflow of foreign capital ultimately translates into accumulated reserves, thus causing currency appre- ciation. It can thus be expected that countries that pursue policies to direct the flow of foreign PKFL into the domestic economy are likely to experience

currency appreciation. This finding, notwithstanding the implication, must be taken with caution since it only emerges after the inclusion of the lag of GDP.
Tests for model suitability. All the regression models were subjected to the Arellano–Bond test for autocorrelation and Sargan’s test for overidentifi- cation, respectively. The implausibly perfect scores for the Sargan’s tests are not considered as indicative of misspecifications given that Abiad et al. (2010) report similar p-values. On the whole, since both tests failed to reject the null hypotheses, there is no reason to doubt the regression results generated.


IV. Conclusion and Policy Implications
The study sought to test the hypothesis that contrac- tionary monetary policy strengthens currency positions (by reducing EMP) while controlling for other known macroeconomic determinants of EMP. From the study, it emerged that the conventional wisdom of defending a currency via increasing interest rates holds for the study sample. A rise in interest rates is, therefore, more likely to trigger an appreciation of domestic currencies by redirecting foreign inflows into the economy. Despite this finding, the position of Feridun (2009) that fragile financial systems are prone to perverse reactions in the face of rising interest rates must not be ignored completely within such economies.
  Contrary to the literature, debt is associated with appreciation, while economic growth is associated with currency depreciation. Currency pressure, it seems, is also not persistent across the region, with periods of high pressure being followed by periods of low EMP and vice versa. TOT and the CABA are highly significant in explaining the currency pressure and have the posited appreciation effects. On the whole, it can be said that while monetary policy, along with other macroeconomic fundamentals, is relevant in explaining currency positions, the effects of the fundamentals, for SSA, are not consistent with trends in the existing literature.
  In terms of policy implications, it would appear that within the SSA region, attempts at addressing depreciation pressure on their domestic currencies can be achieved by increasing interest rates. However, while these economies need lower interest rates to stimulate appropriate levels of investment, this policy must be implemented with caution. Improving the TOT position and the CABA will

also provide a way of defending speculative attacks on the domestic currency.
  Another policy implication from the finding is with regard to the elasticity of domestic demand for foreign goods. Policy moves aimed at stimulat- ing demand in the direction of domestically pro- duced goods may hold the key to reversing the growth currency depreciation cycle. This could include export promotion policies in general through government investment in the appropriate infrastructure. Also, support could be provided to target local industries to produce goods that can compete favourably on both the domestic and inter- national markets. An alternative, but more stringent, approach may include the imposition of significant tariffs and controls with regard to imported goods especially consumables that are produced domestically.
