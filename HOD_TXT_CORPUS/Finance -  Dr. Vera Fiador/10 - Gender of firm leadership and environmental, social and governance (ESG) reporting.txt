Abstract
Purpose – This study aims to examine how woman corporate leadership indicators and environmental,
social and governance (ESG) disclosure in listed banks on Ghana Stock Exchange are related.

Design/methodology/approach – Data was obtained from the audited annual reports of the banks for
the period 2006–2020. Empirical result estimation was achieved using Panel Corrected Standard Errors.

Findings – The result revealed that female chief executive ofﬁcer (CEO), female board chairperson and
board gender diversity are associated with higher disclosure of ESG issues in listed banks in Ghana in overall
terms. However, in terms of individual disclosures, female board chairperson positively impacts social
disclosure, whereas both female CEO and female board chairperson affect governance disclosure positively.
Research limitations/implications – In this era of business where there is much emphasis on green
business and investment by various stakeholders for purposes of ensuring business legitimacy, the result

1. Introduction
In the current business terrain, the subject matter of board composition and woman
corporate leadership has received wide recognition and attention as being a key feature of
boards (Ahn et al., 2020), and ultimately has implications for business outcomes (Chen et al.,
2021). The discussion has largely been centred on the inclusion of women on boards to
ensure diversity for purposes of ﬁrm value creation (Chen and Hassan, 2022; Arora, 2022).
The argument is that a board that is gender diversiﬁed improves the intellectual capacity of
the board, as ideas, skills, experiences, innovations and qualiﬁcations are brought on board,
which tend to ensure quality board deliberations leading to quality organizational decisions
and outcomes (Luh and Kusi, 2023). Thus, the presence of both males and females on boards
is acknowledged in literature as being one of the panacea for ensuring corporate success
(Gomez and Bernet, 2019; Hossain et al., 2021). In all these discussions regarding board
gender diversity and woman corporate leadership, it is worthy of note that as compared to
men, women still represent a lower proportion on boards and in top executive positions
globally (Hamdan et al., 2022; Martínez et al., 2022).
On the back of the supporting arguments regarding board gender diversity and the
business case for gender diversity on boards and woman corporate leadership, a number of
reforms have taken place around the globe. Essentially, a number of countries have legislated
required quota for female representation on boards, key among them being France, Norway,
Sweden and Spain (Sousa and Santos, 2022; Yu and Madison, 2021). More so, there is a global
effort to ensure gender diversity and equality in all facets of life. This is reﬂected in the
United Nations Sustainable Development Goal 5, which is intended and aims to achieve
gender equality and empower all women and girls. These legislative, regulatory and global
efforts are not ends in themselves but a means to an end. Although such legislative,
regulatory and global efforts are gradually gathering momentum across the globe and may
ultimately achieve some results, happenings and signals in various corporate and economic
systems around the globe suggest that it may take quite a lengthy period of time to achieve
such gender equality and diversity as far as business and economic life is concerned.
Following the narrative above, there have been extensive empirical research to provide
insight on how women in leadership and gender diversity on boards is linked with issues
such as cost of capital (Sarang et al., 2022), earnings management (Anh and Khuong, 2022;
Mensah and Onumah, 2023), ﬁrm proﬁtability (Luh and Kusi, 2023), intellectual capital
efﬁciency (Sanyaolu et al., 2022), audit fee (Garcia-Blandon et al., 2023; Luh, 2024), audit
quality (Saidu and Aifuwa, 2020; Koh et al., 2022) and ﬁrm stability/risk (Sattar et al., 2022;
Menicucci and Paolucci, 2022a). In overall terms, what these studies have revealed is that
gender diversity on boards and top executive management has implications for various
organizational outcomes, which includes both ﬁnancial and non-ﬁnancial outcomes. In spite

of affairs in organizations affects an important issue such as environmental, social and
governance (ESG) disclosure, particularly within the context of Africa and by extension
Ghana, where such concept is still at a nascent stage and being embraced. Thus, the aim of

empirically take stock of how gender of ﬁrm leadership inﬂuences ESG disclosure which is a
relatively new area in the corporate terrain in Africa and now being embraced.
The UN Global Compact (2004) and the UNEP Finance Initiative (2005) explain the
concept of ESG to be that mechanism “that ties corporate social performance and corporate
ﬁnancial performance together” (Lo and Kwan, 2017, p. 607). The overall argument is that
businesses should not only be concerned about disclosures pertaining to their economic
wellbeing but must more importantly incorporate issues pertaining to how they are
addressing social, environmental and governance issues in their investment decisions
(Cucari et al., 2018). That is, ESG disclosures is acknowledged as a corporate strategy which
is believed to be a means by which ﬁrms are able to maintain good relations with its
different stakeholders (Friede et al., 2015). Given the arguments that have been made in
relation to ESG disclosure, the subject matter of ESG has attracted the attention of investors,
leading to consistent scrutiny of annual reports of ﬁrms for such ESG issues (London Stock
Exchange Group, 2018). The present study thus focuses on the issue of sustainability
reporting given that these are set of information on the activities of a ﬁrm on society,
environment and corporate governance that are revered and welcomed by investors in the
current business environment for investment and economic decision-making.
Ghana is a country located in the west part of Africa. Like many countries, it has its own
set of culture, institutional and governance systems and beliefs. However, when it comes
to issues of gender, it has certain commonalities with countries globally. In the period of
colonialism, women were largely marginalized, and their labour was deemed as less valuable
to that of men (Bosak et al., 2018). In addition, women who held key public positions during the
colonial period were largely sidelined (Steady, 2007). Fast forward, Ghana has gone through
several transformations post-independence in 1957. These reforms relate to social, economic
and political issues. These transformations include increased gender equality which have been
made possible through varied interventions. Notwithstanding the progress made, there still
exist gender disparities in all different facets of life as far as Ghana is concerned (Bosak et al.,
2018). In spite of women being marginalized in the pre-independence era in Ghana, in the postcolonial period, women are acknowledged as being key to nation-building and seen as persons
who are endowed with features of beauty, grace and gentleness. Overall, just like in many
countries around the globe, women in Ghana are acknowledged as persons who because of the
nurturing and the social training they receive tend to adopt ethical and communal approach to
doing things as supported by the gender socialization theory. In Ghana, the social system is
such that women end up performing most domestic chores such as cleaning, cooking and
childcare due to their upbringing. Overall, due to the type of training they go through, they
often appear to exhibit tendencies of compassion and concern for others, ethics and being less
corrupt. To buttress the aforementioned point, in a recent speech by the Attorney General of
Ghana at the 10th Session of the Conference of States Parties to the United Nations Convention
against Corruption (COSP 10) in Atlanta, USA, it was revealed that research done on about
15,000 respondents highlighted that Ghanaian women were less corrupt in comparison with
men. More so, Asomah et al. (2023) with speciﬁc reference to Ghana, empirically conﬁrmed

that compared to men, women are less likely to offer bribes. Clearly, from these studies, one
could argue that women in Ghana are on average more likely to exhibit ethical behaviour as
compared to their male counterparts. Essentially, the general notion regarding women in
Ghana is that they are socially inclined, communal and prioritize the welfare of stakeholders
other than pursuing just their personal interests (Kuada, 2009).
The underlying assumption of the study is that given that women exhibit traits of
compassion, ethics and communalism and tend to be concerned about stakeholders and
pursue larger stakeholder interest other than personal interest only, they are likely to be
largely concerned about sustainability issues which may have positive implications on
sustainability reporting represented by ESG disclosures.
Consequently, this manuscript contributes to the extant literature on gender diversity and
ESG disclosure by showing how female board chairperson, female chief executive ofﬁcer
(CEO) and board gender diversity contribute to ESG disclosure from a developing country
context using data of banks listed on the Ghana Stock Exchange (GSE) for the period 2006–
2020. More so, with the recent launch of the ESG manual guidelines by the GSE, the study
contributes to the extant literature on ESG, by providing evidence of gender of ﬁrm
leadership (i.e. woman corporate leadership) as a determinant of ESG disclosure in ﬁrms.

2. Literature review – theories, empirics and hypothesis development
2.1 Theoretical underpinnings of the study
The study is premised on the gender social role theory and the gender socialization theory.
The general argument pertaining to the aforementioned theories is that social roles and the
varied models of socialization differ among males and females (Lomazzi and Seddig, 2020;
Schneider and Bos, 2019). Overall, the indication is that when it comes to leadership, there are
certain differences among males and females hence differences in what each gender may be
concerned about or get attached to. From the perspective of the gender social role theorists,
the argument is that women tend to exhibit communal qualities (i.e. generous, socially
oriented and concern-centred) as compared to their male counterparts who are more agentic
in nature (e.g. more ambitious and self-directed). Thus, on the basis of the communal nature
of women, it is advanced that women are likely to engage in voluntary behaviours, hence the
higher probability of them engaging in environmental protection and social issues than their
male counterparts (Ahmad et al., 2021). In sum, women are acknowledged by the theorists as
being persons who cherish long-term relationships and care more about others’ needs, and
are kind and compassionate, which ultimately makes them more stakeholder-oriented, hence
positive implications for sustainable accounting reporting under their watch. Drawing on the
gender social role theory, Khatri (2023) conﬁrmed that women directors have positive effect
on sustainability performance, and Nadeem et al. (2020) reported a positive signiﬁcant effect
of board gender diversity (i.e. women directorship) on environmental innovation. These prior
studies overall ascribe better sustainability performance to the values and concerns of
women directors to stakeholders and the society as a whole.
The gender socialization theory is similar to the social role theory. For the gender
socialization theory, the argument is that women are raised to be nurturing and
compassionate to others (Burkhard, 2019). Based on the aforementioned argument, it is
advanced that women directors tend to be highly concerned and conservative in making
decisions that pertain to sustainability issues. Furthermore, the position of the theory is that

2.2 Empirics and hypothesis development
Despite the area of sustainability performance/reporting and women directorship being a
relatively grey area as far as empirical research is concerned, it is worthy of note that
empirically, the body of work on women directorship and sustainability performance/
disclosures has seen growth in the past ten years. In overall terms, the issue of sustainability
performance/reportage/disclosure has been represented by ESG disclosures of ﬁrms. Largely,
the limited but growing empirical literature on the subject matter under consideration
provides that sustainability performance/reporting/disclosures is improved in the presence of
women directorship. In this regard, Arayssi et al. (2020) used data of Gulf Cooperation
Council (GCC) countries and empirically conﬁrmed that higher female board representation
results in improved social responsibility. In addition, Bhatia and Marwaha (2022) empirically
conﬁrmed that board gender diversity has positive inﬂuence on ESG disclosures of 327 ﬁrms
listed in India. More so, Buallay and Alhalwachi (2022) studied the environmental disclosure
impact of board gender diversity and found that boards with woman representation between
21% and 50% were associated with higher levels of environmental disclosure. Further
conﬁrming the positive effect of board gender diversity on ESG performance is the study by
Yadav and Prashar (2023), who empirically established that relatively smaller percentage of
women on boards has little impact on ESG performance. Nonetheless, in the presence of at
least three women, the positive effect of board gender diversity on ESG performance turned
out to be more favourable. Shakil et al. (2021) using data of banks in the USA found that there
is signiﬁcant positive effect of board gender diversity on ESG performance of banks. In the
European context, Chouaibi et al. (2022) conﬁrmed that there is a positive effect of diversity
on integrated reporting quality represented by ESG disclosures. Alkhawaja et al. (2023) used
data of ﬁrms from 48 countries globally and reported that there is signiﬁcant positive
relationship between gender diverse boards and ESG disclosure.
Contrasting the ﬁndings of the earlier studies is that of Manita et al. (2018), who
empirically demonstrated that there is no signiﬁcant relationship between board gender
diversity and ESG disclosure in US ﬁrms. However, the study added that concept of critical
mass is partially supported, as it turned out that below three women directors, no statistical
signiﬁcance was found between board gender diversity and ESG disclosure.
In the African and Ghanaian context, few studies have considered gender diversity and
sustainability or ESG performance. Largely, the focus has been on gender diversity and
ﬁnancial performance indicators. In this regard, Toerien et al. (2023) studied board gender
diversity and ESG disclosure of companies in South Africa and conﬁrmed a positive
relationship between ESG at the aggregated level and at the social disclosure level. In
addition, Gerged et al. (2023) studied environmental disclosure and gender diversity and
empirically conﬁrmed using quantile regression that presence of women directors on board
results in higher corporate environmental disclosures. From the reviews, it is obvious that

3. Study methods, data, estimation technique and variable deﬁnition and
measurement
The study is guided by the co-relational and non-experimental design of research which
form the centrality of majority of quantitative research (Gill and Biger, 2013). These designs
are deemed appropriate for the study given that the intention of the study is to investigate
the causality between indicators of female corporate leadership/directorship and ESG
performance in listed banks on GSE. Data used for empirical analysis is obtained from the
audited annual reports of banks listed on the GSE. The audited annual reports were
obtained from the database of the GSE. The inclusion of bank data for empirical analysis
was solely based on data availability. In effect, banks that had data for ﬁve or more years
had their data being used for empirical analysis. Using this criterion, data of nine banks
listed on the GSE for the period 2006–2020 was used.
For purposes of result estimation, panel data estimation technique was used. Using the
panel data estimation technique approaches among other things comes with the advantage
of producing more robust, convincing, reliable and accurate econometric estimates
(Wooldridge, 2009). Thus, the application of the panel data estimation technique helps to
counter the weaknesses that comes with using either the cross-sectional or time-series
approaches where issues of homogeneity, simultaneity and heterogeneity tend to be a
problem. The general panel data framework is given as follows:

3.1 Variable measurement
3.1.1 Response variable – environmental, social and governance disclosure index. To proxy
ESG disclosure/reporting, ESG index was developed. This ESG disclosure index is obtained
by summing scores of the three acknowledged aspects of overall ﬁrm information disclosure
which consists of environmental disclosure, social disclosure and governance disclosure
(Naveed et al., 2021). In arriving at the disclosure, the guideline of Global Reporting Initiative
standards on sustainable reporting was followed (Naveed et al., 2021), where the score for
environmental disclosure was obtained by focusing on issues such as energy disclosure,
biodiversity disclosure, water, emission, environmental reporting, waste management,
whereas the score for social disclosure was obtained by focusing on disclosure pertaining to
issues such as health and safety disclosures, customer and complaints disclosure, donations
to external communities, human rights, product quality and safety, community
development and philanthropy. Lastly, the governance score was obtained by focusing on
issues such as board remuneration, risk disclosures, CEO roles, leadership ethics, board
composition, executive composition, transparency and reporting and stakeholder treatment
disclosures. For purposes of this study, the overall ESG disclosure index and the three
different pillars serve as the dependent variables in the model. The usage of these measures
as sustainable reporting indicators is acknowledged in literature as valid and reliable (Luo
et al., 2017). Consistent with prior literature (Yadav and Prashar, 2023; Naveed et al., 2021;
Aabo and Giorici, 2022), ESG disclosure index and its three pillars are used as response
variables (for measurement and description of variables, see Table 1).
3.1.2 Predictor variables. Literature advances that as far as leadership is concerned,
the traits of those in leadership tend to matter enormously when it comes to decisionmaking and ultimate organizational outcomes. Thus, literature argues that differences in
behaviour and traits between men and women account for what they do. The predictor
variable in the empirical model is gender of ﬁrm leadership represented by female CEO,
female board chairperson and percentage of females on board (board gender diversity).
Female CEO is a dichotomous variable where 1 represents a situation a ﬁrm’s CEO for a
particular year is a woman otherwise 0. In addition, female board chairperson is a binary
variable where 1 represents a situation a ﬁrm’s board chairperson for a speciﬁc year is a
woman, otherwise 0. More so, board gender diversity is measured as the percentage of
female directors on boards for a particular year. In using these variables as independent
variables, inspiration was drawn from prior literature (Luh and Kusi, 2023; Aabo and
Giorici, 2022).

their leadership (Ahmad et al., 2021; Toerien et al., 2023).
In addition, the result shows no effect of woman leadership on environmental disclosure.
This suggests that for the purpose of this study, woman corporate leadership is not a
determinant of environmental disclosure. On the basis that the study used data of banks for
estimation, the non-signiﬁcant impact of all woman leadership indicators on environmental
disclosure is not surprising. This is because banks are largely not engaged in activities that
have implications for the environment as a whole. That is, the presence of women on boards
and in leadership positions does not contribute to environmental disclosure as far as this
study is concerned. This result is consistent with the ﬁndings by Toerien et al. (2023), who
used data of ﬁrms in South Africa and reported no signiﬁcant effect of woman corporate
leadership on environmental disclosure.
Finally, woman CEO and woman board chairperson have positive effect on governance
disclosure. The clear understanding from this result is that banks where the CEO and the
board chairperson are women, are associated with higher levels of governance disclosures
which are largely internal governance-related issues. This result points to the fact that aside
being concerned about external sustainability issues, women in leadership tend to be
concerned about transparency pertaining to internal stakeholders, whose activities are
ultimately responsible for the success or break up of the ﬁrm. That is, on the basis that
woman corporate leadership has been identiﬁed as a key factor for enhancing ﬁrm
governance practices (Adams and Ferreira, 2009), it would be valid to argue that improved
disclosure of ﬁrm internal governance issues would be observed under their leadership. This
result is congruent with that of Wasiuzzaman and Wan Mohammad (2020), who found that
woman corporate leadership represented by presence of women on boards has positive
effect on governance disclosure in Malaysian ﬁrms.

5. Study conclusion and recommendations
The paper investigated how woman corporate leadership indicators impact ESG disclosure
in banks listed on the GSE for the period 2006–2020. To estimate the empirical results, PCSE
estimator was used. The results revealed that female CEO, female board chairperson and
presence of women on boards (board gender diversity) have signiﬁcant positive effect on
sustainable accounting reporting represented by ESG disclosure. This implies that banks
where the CEO and board chairperson are female and where there are women on board
(board gender diversity), there tends to be an improvement in ESG disclosure. However, at
the individual ESG level, only female board chairperson has signiﬁcant positive effect on
social disclosure. This suggests that banks with female board chairperson are associated
with an improvement in social disclosure. More so, female board chairperson and female
CEO have a signiﬁcant positive effect on governance disclosure, suggesting that banks
where the CEO and board chairperson are women tend to engage in higher levels of
sustainable accounting represented by ESG disclosure.
The result has conﬁrmed that gender of ﬁrm leadership has implications for ESG
disclosure, which is acknowledged as a mechanism through which businesses can operate
more competitively globally. It therefore means that organizations that intend to ensure
more sustainability reporting have to ensure that more opportunities are created for women
to be placed in strategic decision-making levels of organizations where they can positively
affect decisions such as ESG disclosure. Overall, the results provide empirical evidence from
a developing economy perspective that gender of ﬁrm leadership (i.e. woman corporate
leadership) has implications for ESG disclosure in banks.
In an everchanging and complex business environment, the practical implication of this
result is that ﬁrms must ensure females are represented in top level management where they
can positively affect decision-making such as those related to ESG disclosure. This is key
because in the modern business environment, ESG disclosure is considered as a mechanism
through which corporate brand building and competitive advantage can be obtained. For
this reason, it is crucial that organizations particularly banks ensure and pay attention to the
gender of bank leadership (i.e. woman corporate leadership) since it has implications for
sustainability reporting as represented by ESG disclosure. In terms of social implications,
the ﬁndings of the study go a long way to minimize the stereotyping issues against women
as the economic and business case for women is reinforced.
There are a number of limitations with the study. Firstly, the data used for empirical
analysis only focused on banks listed on the GSE. It means that the results must be applied
with some level of caution. Essentially, on the basis that it is not only banks that are listed on
the GSE, it is recommended that further research consider data of both non-ﬁnancial and
ﬁnancial entities and also undertake a comparative analysis between these ﬁrms to ﬁnd out
whether any insight will be provided. Also, the study focused on only the gender of ﬁrm
leadership in banks as determinants of ESG disclosure. It is possible that in this nexus, a
number of moderating factors can affect how gender of ﬁrm leadership impacts ESG
disclosure, hence the need for further study to go beyond just the gender of the ﬁrm leadership.
