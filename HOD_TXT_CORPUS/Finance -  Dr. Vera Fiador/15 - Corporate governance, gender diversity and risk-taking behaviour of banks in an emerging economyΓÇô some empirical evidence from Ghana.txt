


Corporate governance, gender diversity and risk-taking behaviour of banks in an emerging economy û some empirical evidence from Ghana
Vera Fiador
Department of Finance, University of Ghana Business School, Legon, Ghana

Abstract
Purpose û This study aims to examine the relationship between corporate governance, including board gender diversity and bank risk-taking behaviour in Ghana.
Design/methodology/approach û This research uses panel corrected standard errors estimation on 26 selected banks over an 11-year period from 2006 to 2016.
Findings û Using three proxies for bank risk-taking and two proxies for gender diversity for the purposes of checking robustness, this study finds ample evidence to support the important inRuence of corporate governance and board gender diversity on bank risk-taking behaviour. The findings suggest that independence, gender diversity, size and leadership consolidation can have significant effects on the risk profile of banking firms. The initial finding of the study suggests the possibility that female board gender diversity on Ghanaian banking boards follows the tokenism theory. Subsequent estimations seem to provide evidence to suggest that attaining a critical mass of female board members imposes a significant control on risk-taking behaviour by banks.
Originality/value û This study has important implications for gender diversity in board construction within the banking sector and the discourse on bank risk-taking in an emerging market context.
Keywords Corporate governance, Board gender diversity, Banks, Risk-taking, Financial risk
Paper type Research paper

1. Introduction
Following the global financial crisis and economic meltdown of 2007/2008 and the corporate failures of the Enrons and WorldComs, the concept of corporate governance has attracted far reaching attention, both from academia and industry. At the core of this corporate governance discussion is the role of the corporate board, whose most important job is to limit managersÆ self-serving behaviour should conRicting goals arise between managers and owners (Eisenhardt, 1989). In the case of banking, where business models revolve around the taking of risks in pursuit of profits, self-serving behaviour can emerge in the form of excessive risk-taking on the part of management for the purposes of bonuses and perks, and when excessive, this excessive risk-taking typically manifests in the form of bank failures and financial crises as was evident with the 2007/2008 financial crisis. Bank failures and financial crises that are triggered by excessive bank risk-taking behaviour also have the potential to negatively affect bank market confidence and derail economic pursuits within affected economies. Given the importance of the banking sector in the pursuit of economic growth, numerous studies have sought to document the bank-specific factors

   Notwithstanding the consensus on the significance of the governing board with regards to shareholder wealth maximisation, the line is not so clear cut when it comes to the characteristics of the board and although the board is to ensure shareholdersÆ interest û indeed there is evidence that boards do not always protect shareholdersÆ interests (Mace, 1971; Patton and Baker, 1987), thus birthing a discourse on what constitutes an effective board. Many studies have attempted to document some of the characteristics that an effective board of directors should exhibit û from board independence to separation of powers between the board chair and chief executive officer (CEO) and board gender diversity among many others.
   In recent years, the literature on board and gender diversity has gained growing interest, with several studies in advanced economies exploring the nexus between board gender diversity and corporate performance, including risk-taking behaviour. A number of the studies provide ample empirical evidence on how female representation on corporate boards inRuence risk-taking in advanced countries (Jensen and Meckling, 1976; Berger et al., 2014; Faccio et al., 2012 among others). Other studies have also done extensive work relating to the involvement of women in firm-level decision-making and find that women are less likely to take on certain levels of risk when compared to men (Burgess and Tharenou, 2002; Carter et al., 2003; Farrell and Hersch, 2005; Dwyer et al., 2002; Faccio et al., 2012). Similarly, Ertac and Gurdal (2012) concluded that men are generally more confident and tolerant towards risk and more likely to undertake uncertain exploits. Indeed, both empirical and experimental evidence have shown that women directors tend to be less overconfident (Croson and Gneezy, 2009), more diligent in monitoring (Adams and Ferreira, 2009) and can bring different experiences and perspectives (Cox and Blake, 1991) into the board.
   Despite the increased research in board gender diversity, it appears most of these studies have mostly been in the context of developed nations and focused broadly on financial performance effects with very little attention being given to developing or emerging economies. In emerging economies in particular, the issue of corporate governance and agent behaviour takes on added importance due to the existing weaknesses in their legal environments. This makes a study that explores the implications of internal board mechanisms, including gender diversity, in such contexts a valuable research agenda. In terms of the setting, Ghana, an emerging economy in the Sub-Saharan African region presents an opportune contextual setting for such a study. The Ghanaian economy is characterised by institutional weaknesses and has its broader corporate governance environment at nascent stages, which makes the role of internal governance mechanisms even much more crucial. In Ghana, shareholder rights, shareholder redress, legal structures and enforcements continue to be weak thus making internal governance mechanisms such as the board and its performance effects worthy of some research attention.
   Drawing on the backdrop of the numerous studies that link corporate governance and performance effects and corporate governance and risk, this study seeks to ascertain:
? Whether the presence of females on the board holds any implications for risk-taking behaviour at the bank level?
? Whether the size, independence and leadership structure (CEO duality) at the board level inRuences bank risk appetite? and
? Whether the economic setting in which banks operate exerts any risk-taking inRuence?



Answers to these questions will contribute to knowledge in a number of ways. First and foremost, it will enrich the gender diversity literature and also the discourse on the risk- taking behaviour of banks. It will also contribute to the understanding of the role of corporate governance on the behaviour of economic agents and thus prove relevant for board structuring to minimise agency problems and costs. The focus on banks is particularly pertinent as it allows for the study of financial risks as opposed to the numerous board gender studies that have focussed mostly on corporate performance measures. The study is also important on account of the fact that like Kinateder et al. (2021), we argue that peculiarities of the financial sector, such as complex regulations, economic sensitivity and the quest for better risk management in the sector, merit further enquiries into the performance implications of board gender diversity.
   The rest of the paper is organised as follows: Section 2 reviews both theoretical and empirical arguments on corporate risk appetite, risk-taking behaviour and board and gender effects on risk behaviour; Section 3 specifies the measurement and construction of the key variables, data and econometric specifications; Section 4 discusses and presents the empirical results, and finally, Section 5 concludes.

2. Literature review
This Section reviews first the agency theory as the foundation of the need for corporate governance, the various studies conducted in the field of governance, risk and board gender diversity, providing the theoretical concepts and models and empirical evidence that support this study.

2.1 Agency theory and board gender diversity
Globally, the numerous instances of bank failures have been attributed to poor governance practices that failed to manage bank risk-taking. The agency theory suggests that strong corporate governance systems have a controlling role over director behaviour. The agency theory emphasises the role of the board of directors in monitoring and controlling managers (Fama and Jensen, 1983; Jensen and Meckling, 1976) and suggests that gender-diverse boards may help reduce agency problems between managers and shareholders. Agency theory further suggests that a more diverse board can ensure better monitoring of managers, because diversity of the board increases board independence. Adams and Ferreira (2009), in extending the arguments for the agency theory, also posit the view that the presence of female directors can improve board effectiveness through better monitoring of managers of the firm. This study thus rides on the agency theory on corporate governance to test the performance implications of board gender diversity.

2.2 Gender diversity and corporate risk taking
The discourse on risk-taking behaviour of firms has continued to receive some attention in recent years on account of the corporate failures and banking crises being linked to excessive risk-taking behaviours. In interrogating the role of the governing board vis-a-vis risk-taking behaviour, the discourse has revolved around issues such as the effectiveness of female directorship in curtailing excessive risk-taking behaviour, with an ultimate call being made for gender-diverse and balanced boards to enhance decision-making of firms (Liao et al., 2019). In the area of gender diversity and corporate risk-taking behaviour, a review of the limited literature provides contradictory and inconclusive evidence with respect to female board presence and corporate risk appetite. Prior studies on gender and risk-taking behaviour assumed risk aversion on the part of women, which subsequent studies such as Berger et al. (2014) and Farag and Mallin (2017), have indicated is not fully supported in the

This inconclusive nature of the literature thus provides limited empirical evidence to use as a basis for female board representation in the quest to manage corporate risk levels. The inconclusiveness of the literature notwithstanding, the general consensus on studies comparing men and women in terms of risk preferences seem to suggest that women are less likely to take risks compared to men (Charness and Gneezy, 2012), and the studies go on to link these differences to consequences of biological factors and characteristics such as information processing, diligence, conservatism, overconfidence and risk tolerance.
On the empirical front, Cobey et al. (2013), using three observational studies, tested sex
differences in risk-taking in situations of financial risk. Their results corroborated previous findings by showing that across domains, men are more inclined to risks than women. An Austrian bank did research on the risk behaviour of women when it comes to financial decisions and found out that women usually take less risk than men would in the same situation (Mitterbacher, 2007). Slovic (1966) earlier reported that females showed lower risk preference than males when subjects repeated a previously undertaken task. He found that female entrepreneurs are less willing than male entrepreneurs to become involved in situations with uncertain outcomes, supporting the behavioural finance studies that have reported that females as being more risk averse compared to their male counterparts when making financial and investment decisions (Olsen and Cox, 2001).
Many have argued that a greater presence of women in the financial market could have
prevented a number of the financial crises experienced globally (Slovic, 1966; Wagner, 2001). ôIf Lehman Brothers had been Lehman Sisters, todayÆs economic crisis clearly would look quite differentö (Lagarde, 2011). Adams and Funk, (2012) suggest things would have been different if more women were running corporations in the USA and the world around. Thus, the question of interest is whether the risk appetite of the female is brought to bear as a member of a board of directors?

2.3 Gender diversity and corporate performance
Much scholarly attention has been paid to the presence or absence of female board directors over the last decade (Terjesen et al., 2009; Davidson and Burke, 2011). The main themes of these studies have centred on why females are, in general, absent from the corporate boardroom (Hillman et al., 2007; Singh and Vinnicombe, 2004; Thomson et al., 2005). Particularly controversial has been the decision by Norway and other governments to lobby and legislate for increased female presence in the boardroom (Grosvold et al., 2007). The research concern with gender diversity has focussed on performance imperatives as a justification for an increase in female board participation. Peni (2014), using a sample from Standard & Poor 500 firms, reports a positive relationship between female CEOs and corporate performance, thus suggesting that gender-based differences could affect a firmÆs success.



   Measuring corporate performance by return on assets, Krishnan and Park (2005) also noted a direct and positive impact of female directors on firm performance. Consistent with these findings, Davis et al. (2010) find female managers outperform male counterparts. Relying on a sample of 25,000 Danish firms, Smith et al. (2006) observe an average positive effect of female managers on corporate performance. A frequently cited study, Catalyst (2004) documents a strong positive link between female directors and financial performance. They used 352 Fortune 500 firms over a period extending from 1996 to 2000. Financial performance in the study was measured by stock return and return on equity. In a subsequent study by the same organisation, Joy et al. (2007) confirmed their previous findings, but this time with a bigger sample size. Carter et al. (2003) found out that the inclusion of females at the board level benefits firms financially, resulting in increased shareholder returns and higher returns on equity. With respect to governance, studies have shown that female board members contribute to better corporate governance and enhance firm performance (Grosvold et al., 2007; Adler, 2001; Coffey and Wang, 1998; Webb, 2004; Williams, 2003). Thomson et al. (2005) further argue that gender-diverse boards are less inclined to rubber stamp the CEOÆs decisions and that the presence of women can limit the power of male ôgroupthinkö to nod through ostensibly value-creating proposals or policies that are ethically questionable. Adams and Ferreira (2004) also report that female directors have better attendance records than male directors and are more likely to join monitoring committees. Female board directors are more diligent monitors and demand more audit efforts than male directors (Adams and Ferreira, 2004; Gul et al., 2011). There is evidence as well that women are more robust and thorough in their analyses before agreeing to corporate proposals (Singh and Vinnicombe, 2004; Walt and Ingley, 2003). In addition, female directors bring different perspectives and experiences into the boardroom, which help improve the quality of board decisions and enhance the legitimacy of firm practices (Hillman et al., 2007). Gender-diverse boards could also partially offset weak corporate governance (Gul et al., 2011).
The findings of other studies have, however, contradicted the above claims. B°hren
and Str°m (2010), for instance, find a negative impact of female board members on corporate performance. Per this construct, a gender-diverse board can result in increased conRicts and delayed decision-making (Erhardt et al., 2003; Knight et al., 1999). Consensus may also be harder to achieve, which in turn may delay decision- making and devolve personal responsibility. Rose (2007), using Danish firms concludes that there is a negative relationship between boardroom gender diversity and firm performance. Adams and Ferreira (2007) report that excessive monitoring has a negative effect on firm value and female directors are found to be over-engaging in monitoring activities in well-governed firms. Investigations made into Norwegian firms after the imposition of the 40% female director quota found a declining financial performance. For instance, Ahern and Dittmar (2012) make this observation and attribute it to the fact that the rush to implement the legislation resulted in the recruitment of inexperienced female directors.
Finally, other researchers did not find any significant relationship between boardÆs
gender diversity and corporate performance. Rand°y et al. (2006), using a sample of 500 firms from Sweden, Norway and Denmark, find no significant link between female board presence and corporate performance. Carter et al. (2007) and Rose (2007) both measuring financial performance by TobinÆs Q and return on asset find no significant relationship between boardÆs gender diversity and corporate performance. Huse et al. (2009) do not find any particular association between the presence of females on the board and monitoring tasks, such as strategic and budget control.

2.4 
Corporate board characteristics and risk
A substantial and growing literature links the composition of the board of directors to observable firm outcomes. Evidence from existing literature suggests that boards matter for firmsÆ risk appetite (Sila et al., 2016). Boards are pivotal in defining a firmÆs risk appetite, as one of the key roles of the board of directors is to establish a risk appetite framework for the corporation.
   Thus, risk appetite needs to be paramount on any boardÆs agenda and is a core consideration of any enterprise risk management practice. Various research works have been conducted into aspects of the boardsÆ features and how they impact on the overall risk- taking of firms. Cheng (2008), for instance, argues that firms with smaller boards have higher performance inconsistencies while Nakano and Nguyen (2012) conclude that firms with larger boards exhibit lower performance variability as well as lower bankruptcy risk. Pathan (2009) goes on to report a negative relationship between the size of the board and stock return volatility. Taillard et al. (2010) highlight the fact that larger and more independent boards are associated with a lower appetite for risk. However, these studies treated the board members as a homogenous group. Gender, age, ethnicity and other personal traits were not controlled for.
   In the case of banks, evidence from Chan et al. (2016) also shows that banks are better off with boards that have a higher average age and government affiliation because they enhance problem-solving, market insights and facilitate adherence to government or regulatory policies resulting in a reduction of the banksÆ risks. Sila et al. (2016) in turn opine that variations in characteristics such as gender, in particular, may be able to explain the difference in risk-taking choices among firms. A key but under-researched characteristic of the board which may inRuence the risk appetite at the firm level is the gender diversity of the board. While psychological and economic studies have found women to have a lower risk appetite than men (Barber and Odean, 2001; Byrnes et al., 1999; Hinz et al., 1997), it is not conclusive whether greater female presence at the boardroom would mean firms would engage in less risk-taking.

3. Methodology
To explore the effect of corporate governance on the risk-taking behaviour of Ghanaian banks, the study uses annual data series on a panel of 26 banks [1] in Ghana over an 11 year period from 2006 to 2016. According to Hsiao (2005), the use of panel data offers several advantages such as providing more accurate inference on model parameters, providing greater capacity for capturing the complexity of human behaviour, in comparison to single cross-section or times series data and combining both cross-sectional and time dimensions of a phenomenon under study. The panel is also unbalanced and allows for banks to enter and exit, thus eliminating survivor bias from the studies. Panel data regressions usually allow for the control of omitted variable bias that may result from the exclusion of unobservable variables. To reduce survivor bias in the data set, cross-sectional units without complete data for the entire period were included. The data for the study is collected from the annual reports of the banks and the macroeconomic data from World Development Indicators. The explanatory variables used to explain risk-taking behaviour follow the broad literature on the determinants of risk-taking by banks (Khan et al., 2017; Haque and Shahid, 2016; Boadi et al., 2016; Chen et al., 2015), which posits that risk-taking behaviour is a function of both bank-specific variables and macroeconomic setting. The effect of corporate governance on risk profile is, therefore, modelled as a function of bank-specific governance variables and estimated using panel-corrected errors because of its finite sample advantages. The model to be used is specified as follows:



RISKit = b0 + b1BSIZit + b2CEODit + b3NONEXECit + b4GENDit + b5GDPt + eit
where RISKit is the bank risk profile, and following Noth and Tonzer (2017) and Ashraf et al. (2016), the study uses three variables: Z-SCORE, non-performing loans ratio and loan loss provisions ratio to proxy bank risk-taking behaviour. Z-SCORE, the bankÆs stability measure and an inverse measure of risk taking, is captured as return on assets (RoA) plus capital ratio scaled by the standard deviation of RoA; NPL, the bankÆs non-performing loans, is measured as non-performing loans to gross loans; and LLR, the bankÆs loan loss provisions, is captured as provision for bad and doubtful debts to gross loans; the governance variables, based on data availability, are limited to board size, board independence, CEO duality and board gender diversity. BSIZ represents board size, the number of board members; CEOD represents CEO duality, which is measured as a dummy taking on the value of 1 when the CEO also doubles as board chair and zero otherwise; NONEXEC means proportion of non-executive board members; GEND is gender diversity, proxied by the proportion of females to the size of the board and the BlauÆs index for gender diversity. GDP is gross domestic product, and ½ is the error term; i is firm and t is time. To check for the possibility of threshold effects and to test the critical mass theory on gender diversity, the gender variable is included as a square in alternate specifications.

4.2 Regression results
Table 3 presents the regression outputs of panel corrected standard errors (PCSE) estimations. The table reports on the effect of gender diversity on the three risk proxies: the z-score, non-performing loans and loan loss provision. The 2-stage least squares (2SLS) estimations to tackle endogeneity are presented in the Appendix. In summary, the main results discussed are based on estimations from the PCSE methodology. More critically, the results from the 2SLS estimations are generally statistically insignificant except for the gender variables which mostly maintained their signs and significance. From the regression output presented in Table 3, the size of the board (BSIZ) appears to have an ambiguous effect on risk-taking behaviour of banks. Per the output, the effect of board size on bank risk- taking behaviour is sensitive to the risk measure used. In relation to the z-score, which measures stability and therefore signifies less risk-taking when large, the relationship is positively significant, suggesting that a large board leads to less risky bank behaviour. This risk reducing effect is again reinforced when the dependent variable is provision for loan losses. The story is, however, different when non-performing loans are used as the risk measure. In all three regressions, notwithstanding the ambiguities, there appears to be considerable economic impact emanating from the standard deviation movements in the board size versus risk behaviour.
   This finding ultimately indicates that as bank boards get larger, risk-taking behaviour becomes less excessive. This finding seems to support the argument by Dalton et al. (1998), for instance, which holds that a larger board of directors has a beneficial impact by way of improved performance because it boosts the collection of expertise and resources available to a firm. Intrinsically, this finding also supports Adams and Mehran (2003), who find that banks usually are characterised by larger boards due to their complex organisational setup, so that a larger board in a bank setting is beneficial. In the case of the non-performing loan proxy, it appears that large boards are associated with high-risk profiles.

   Independence of the bankÆs board (NONEXEC) emerges as being detrimental to the insolvency risk of banks (z-score) and bank provisioning for loan losses. Per the results, it appears that as non-executive directors on the board increase, risk-taking also increases. This finding is at variance with Kutubi et al. (2018), Lee et al. (2020) who document a risk- reducing effect from non-executive directors on the board. Per the findings, it appears other characteristics of the non-executive board members, beyond their independence, are at play here. In other words, the findings may be suggestive of other board composition effects, structural effects and director specific characteristics beyond just being independent may be at play. Indeed a number of studies have reported personal director characteristics also hold implications for board effects. The coefficient of non-executive members is not only statistically significant but also economically significant. This finding may be suggestive of a non-existent monitoring and control role of non-executive directors due to limited access to information and lack of requisite skills, which transforms the non-executive directors into a less confrontational, rather than a more critical monitoring role (Agrawal and Knoeber, 1996; Dalton et al., 1998; and Hermalin and Weisbach, 2003).
   Board gender diversity (GEND), in contrast to the literature, exhibits an increasing effect on the risk-taking behaviour of banks. From the findings, board gender diversity increases bank risk-taking behaviour in a statistically significant manner. In other words, having women on the board detracts from good performance. There is also economic significance of this result. This finding, however, may be indicative of tokenism, where women are placed on boards for the sake of being women but not necessarily because of expertise. When this happens, the obvious effect is failure to deliver on the appropriate roles. The study findings thus go against the standpoint of Lu and Boateng (2018) and Gulamhussen and Santa (2015) who argue that gender diversity is associated with improved risk management or moderate risk-taking. The increasing demand for gender diversity in the boardroom and other high levels of decision-making could be leading some institutions to appoint women who may not have the relevant experience and commitment required for effective monitoring. These findings are robust to alternate estimations using the Blau index.
   Following Liu et al. (2014), who provide evidence that a critical mass may be needed to have the desired gender impact, this study tested the hypothesis. The findings, both statistically and economically significant, suggest that for gender diversity to improve performance as argued in the literature, a critical mass must indeed be attained. In this instance, the study found that boards with more women have a greater impact on firm risk- taking behaviour as opposed to those who had fewer women. A number of studies have advocated for a minimum of three females on a board for effects to emerge.
   In terms of how a macroeconomic variable like GDP affects risk-taking in the Ghanaian banking sector, this study fails to find unequivocal evidence to support a positive or negative effect. Across the three estimations, the signs and statistical significance differ.

In relation to risk-taking proxied by the Z-score, GDP has a potentially risk reducing effect, albeit insignificant. For non-performing loans, GDP appears to significantly reduce the risk profile of banks. This particular finding supports Babou?cek and Jan?car (2005), who indicated that economic booms decelerate non-performing loans. In essence, when the economy is performing well, the risk-taking behaviour is tempered by virtue of banks being able to book safer loan portfolios which reduces their risk profiles. With regards to risk- taking proxied by the provisions for bad and doubtful debt, GDP emerges as positively and statistically significantly related. This could point to aggressive or excessive lending in times of booms which translates into increased risk and provisioning by the banks.

4.3 Robustness checks and endogeneity
To test for robustness of the study findings and account for potential endogeneity of the gender variables within the model, tests of endogeneity and subsequent 2SLS estimations were run (see Appendix). The results do show the gender variables as being endogenous. The 2SLS results are not discussed on account the coefficients not being statistically significant. The cross-sectional dependence test is also run as a robustness check using the Pesaran test for cross sectional (see Appendix), and the PCSEs estimation resolves the cross- sectional dependence identified.

5. Conclusion and policy implications
The standard literature is replete with the assertion that boards of directors are responsible for the identification, assessment and management of all types of risk. It is also documented that boards that fail to meet these requirements leave their companies open to significant risk management failures, as evidenced by the global financial crisis of 2007/2008. Indeed, the assumption of appropriate risk levels by banks is key to the continued functioning of financial intermediation and ultimately growth. A number of studies have sought to explain the drivers of firm risk-taking behaviour, especially after the global financial crisis of 2007/ 2008. What has clearly emerged is that bank risk-taking is strongly driven by factors such as size, shareholder structure, managerial characteristics, appetite for profits and governance, among others, both of both bank-specific nature and macroeconomic in nature. In this paper, the quest was to examine the role of bank-level governance characteristics and economic growth on risk-taking behaviour in an emerging economy - Ghana. The study thus examined board-level characteristics like size, independence, gender diversity and CEO duality together with GDP growth as drivers of risk taking, which was proxied by bank z- score, provision for bad and doubtful loans and non-performing loans, the latter two scaled by the gross loan portfolio.
   The findings of the study make a clear case for the relevance of governance in the discourse on risk-taking behaviour of banks, despite the argument that bank-level governance may be irrelevant in the face of the stringent regulatory framework that governs the conduct of banks. The findings expand the debate on the implications of governance for performance and risk-taking in particular. In essence, the size of the board has to reRect the needs of the specific bank while pooling the right expertise for the purposes of adding value to the bank. It is also important that external membership to the board have the requisite characteristics beyond just being external to the organisation so as to deliver on the critical monitoring role expected. The emergence of potential tokenism in the space of gender diversity and bank performance requires further interrogation to truly map out the role of gender within the banking sector of Ghana. On the issue of needing a critical mass of women on boards to garner impact, there is the need for regulatory agencies to pursue programmes to generate the population of qualified female board members to meet the increasing

demand for gender diversity in the boardroom. So, while the available literature may make a business case for gender diversity, it is clear in the case of an emerging economy that significant efforts must be made to reRect or experience the value-additive effect of gender diversity. On another note, future research may be worthwhile to investigate what constitutes a critical mass for gender diversity in emerging country contexts.
