Abstract
Purpose – The purpose of this study is to assess the impact of corporate governance variables on the
quality of bank loan portfolios.

Design/methodology/approach – The study used a panel-corrected standard errors estimation model
with the most recent 11-year data from 2006 to 2016 on selected Ghanaian banks.

Findings – The ﬁndings indicate that corporate governance is relevant within the banking sector and plays
a key role in improving loan quality. Having a large board with the attendant pool of expertize, boards with
mostly non-executive members and duality of the CEO-board chair can be harnessed to improve bank loan
quality. Female participation on boards seems to detract from good performance, creating the impression of
tokenism in the Ghanaian banking sector.
Originality/value – The study has important implications for board construction within the banking
sector and the discourse on bank asset quality.

1. Introduction
In recent times, non-performing loans (NPLs) have been the bane of many banks and were
essentially the major trigger in the 2007 ﬁnancial crisis. Post the 2007 crisis as well, bank
insolvency has been a signiﬁcant problem in many countries all over the world. One of the
main reasons for the insolvency of banks is asset quality deterioration.
Banks, in general, are known to be in the business of managing risks in the pursuit of
proﬁts, and key among these risks is credit risk. An increase in the non-performing portion
of a bank’s loan portfolio is ultimately an increase in the bank’s exposure to credit risk, and
this exposure has, among other effects, the potential to cause a shrink in the bank’s
willingness and capacity to generate loans and also affect the soundness of the industry.
The ﬁnance-growth debate (Levine, 2003) makes a case for a sound banking system. In
essence, when bank intermediation is efﬁcient, costs of capital drop within an economy with
the ultimate effects rippling through capital accumulation to economic growth. Factors
identiﬁed as inﬂuencing the capacity of banks to conduct efﬁcient intermediation have,
therefore, received signiﬁcant research attention in recent decades. In the wake of the 2007
ﬁnancial crisis particularly, corporate governance emerged to the forefront as one of the
contributing factors to the ﬁnancial crisis. Corporate governance has, over the years, been
touted as one very important mechanism for facilitating efﬁcient and value-maximizing

According to Levine (2003), however, the ﬁnancial opacity and tight governmental
regulation of banks render many of the traditional mechanisms of governance weak. This
raises the empirical question of whether the internal corporate governance structures among
banks have any relation whatsoever with a bank’s NPL portfolio or they are just plain
irrelevant? This question motivates the study, which seeks to establish, among other
determinants of bank loan quality, the role of corporate governance. In other words, the
study seeks to examine the role of corporate governance characteristics such as board size
(BS), leadership consolidation, gender diversity (GEND) and board independence (BC) on the
asset quality of banks. The underlying assumption of this argument is that better-governed
banks are more likely to conduct a rigorous review of their investments, and thus, maintain
sound loan proﬁles. It is also extremely important that banks in a developing economy
context like Ghana are cognizant of the plausible drivers of NPLs so as to effectively manage
the loan portfolio and to reduce the incidence of loan defaults. This is especially important
because the Ghanaian economy is mostly bank-dependent such that any instability in the
banking sector could prove catastrophic for the economy.
The rest of the work is structured as follows: Section 2 reviews literature on corporate
governance and loan portfolio management among banks along with determinants of bank
loan quality. Section 3 addresses the methodological issues while the ﬁndings and
discussions are presented in Section 4. Finally, Section 5 looks at the conclusions arrived at
and the recommendations for the advancement of bank loan administration.
2. The literature: non-performing loans, corporate governance and related
issues
The literature on the factors that drive NPLs in the books of banks has received widespread
research interest in recent years. Many such studies have characterized NPL determinants
into internal or bank-speciﬁc factors and external or macroeconomic factors (Dash and
Kabra, 2010; Espinoza and Prasad, 2010). On the one hand, the bank-speciﬁc indicators are
variables such as the return on assets, solvency, interest rates on deposit or loans among
others, which are directly under the control of the bank management. On the macroeconomic and structural (systemic) side, indicators such as the growth rate, real interest rate,
real exchange rate, inﬂation rate, unemployment rate, political climate, instances of global
ﬁnancial depression, banks level of maturity, money supply and economic policy, also
inﬂuence the incidence of NPLs.
The empirical literature has also been largely supportive of certain standardized
associations between the documented determinants of NPLs and their effects. In the area of
macroeconomic determinants, the conventional wisdom posits that an improvement in the
macroeconomic environment has positive implications for NPLs. Studies such as Salas and
Saurina (2002), Fofack (2005), Jimenez and Saurina (2006) and Dash and Kabra (2010) ﬁnd
that an improvement in economic growth and employment results in higher income, which
ultimately reﬂects in a reduced incidence of bad debts. The interest rate effect on NPLs
seems to be more visible under ﬂoating rate regimes, thus, an increase in interest rates
increases interest obligations and increases the likelihood of default. Fofack (2005), with a
focus on sub-Saharan Africa, further argues that economic growth and the real rate of
interest are very signiﬁcant in explaining NPLs in the region and this is mainly because of
the undiversiﬁed environment of some of the economies and their high exposure to external
shocks. Turning to the Spanish banking sector, Jimenez and Saurina (2006) also show that
NPLs are explained by gross domestic product (GDP) growth, real interest rates and credit

conditions while Khemraj and Pasha (2009), using evidence from Guyana, ﬁnd that with an
appreciation of the local currency the NPL portfolios of commercial banks are likely to be
higher.
On the side of the banking sector speciﬁcs, Louzis et al. (2010), using dynamic panel data,
show that aside from the macroeconomic determinants, quality of bank management is also
a signiﬁcant determinant of NPLs. Other bank-speciﬁc determinants of NPLs are bank size,
efﬁciency, credit terms, bank market power and risk proﬁles. Hu et al. (2004) for instance
studied the impact of size and ownership structure on NPLs. They report that government
ownership reduces NPLs and so does bank size. Similarly, Tarchouna et al. (2017) combine
principal component analysis and GMM estimation to determine the effects of the corporate
governance system on NPLs of US commercial banks. They report that small banks have a
sound corporate governance system that reduces their NPLs. On the other hand, the
corporate governance system of medium and large US commercial banks are not sufﬁciently
robust to protect them from excessive risk-taking that damages their loan quality, especially
during the ﬁnancial crisis.
The role of corporate governance as a major tool used to align corporate management
interests to that of shareholders has been studied extensively (pioneering studies such as
Fama and Jensen, 1983a). Corporate governance, per Mensah et al. (2003), is the set of
arrangements through which organizations account for shareholders and the general public.
They go further to indicate that sound corporate governance facilitates development by
ensuring efﬁcient allocation of economic resources. Corporate governance is regarded as a
means of addressing the agency conﬂicts between management and shareholders of the
ﬁrm. Corporate governance issues have recently gained prominence among regulators and
corporate society at large. At the core of the corporate governance, discussion is the
corporate board, whose most important job is to limit managers’ self-serving behaviour in
the event that the company’s managers and owners have conﬂicting goals (Eisenhardt,
1989). The corporate board, therefore, serves as the link between the providers of capital and
the users and is seen as the primary means for shareholders to exercise control on top
management (John and Senbet, 1998). The board has the obligation to determine the ﬁrm’s
overall strategy and to ensure that adequate controls are in place to protect shareholder
value (Keenan, 2004).
2.1 Corporate governance, bank loan quality and bank risky behaviour
The effectiveness of the corporate board’s monitoring role on loan portfolio quality in banks
has diverse beneﬁts for a number of interest groups in the particular case of banks. The
boards of banks, like all other boards, are responsible for the management of the banks and
being up to date on the speciﬁc types of risks associated with the banks’ decisions and
operations.
The importance of a responsible and participative board, as far as bank management is
concerned, also emerges from the framework of the prudential supervision regulations
(Basel 2, Pillar II), which qualiﬁes the board as “the apex body of the internal governance
system”. This implies that the board may be given the task of supervising all the risks of the
banking business. Moreover, besides the governance aspects, bank boards also have the role
of credit risk control committees, with the task of expressing opinions on any signiﬁcant
credit risks in the bank portfolios.
Given the recent trends in the behaviour of operators and the current credit market
environment, the relationship between bank governance and risk-taking has assumed
crucial importance. Given the renewed attention on the board of governance in banks with
the global ﬁnancial crisis, the study investigates the relevance of board structure (size,

2.2 Board characteristics and performance
2.2.1 Size. There are mixed views on how BS impacts ﬁrm performance, according to
agency theory. On one hand is the argument that a larger board of directors is beneﬁcial to
improved performance because it boosts the collection of expertise and resources accessible
to a ﬁrm (Dalton et al., 1999). On the other hand, Hermalin and Weisbach (2003) argue that
the consensus in the economic literature is that a larger board will impair ﬁrm performance,
and as Jensen (1993) argues, this is because as BS increases, the boards’ ability to monitor
management decreases due to a greater propensity for shirking and an increase in decisionmaking time. Yermack (1996) reports that a larger board is composed of experts from
various ﬁelds; however, an excessively large board will drag down the efﬁciency of the
board and also the effectiveness of corporate governance mechanisms. Given the unique
operating environment of banks, bank boards tend to be larger than boards in other
industries. Adams and Mehran (2003, 2005) ﬁnd that banks have larger boards because of
their complex organizational structure and presence of more committees such as lending
and credit risk committees. Furthermore, the expectation is a positive linear relationship
between bank BS and bank performance. It is argued that loan quality increases with BS,
especially as a larger board might bring more expertize and increase the potential of
establishing contacts with diverse customers and depositors. However, the relationship
becomes negative when the board becomes too large, which might impair bank performance
because of a lack of efﬁcient monitoring by the board and increased agency conﬂicts.
2.2.2 Board composition. One of the central themes regarding the board’s governance
role revolves around the independence of directors. Within the banking space, BC plays the
role of counterbalancing the executives and management, promoting the proper functioning
of the board and stimulating internal dialogue while reducing the areas of greater conﬂict of
interest in the bank. Many empirical studies conﬁrm that an independent board of directors
has fewer conﬂicts of interest when monitoring managers. Insider representation, in contrast
to BC, is the presence of a majority of non-independent or afﬁliated directors and such
directors are less likely to be independent of the CEO or other top executives. In the agency
framework, the decision-making of non-independent directors is likely to be affected by
managers, which might increase managerial entrenchment. Concerning a majority of
insiders on the board of directors, Baysinger and Butler (1985) found that companies
perform worse if their boards have more insider representations, and Klein (2002) found a
lower presence of abnormal accruals when the board had more than a majority of outside
directors. Executive members on the board, however, can also enhance performance because
they bring internal and ﬁrm-speciﬁc knowledge to board decision-making, thus allowing for
more tailored decisions. In fact, some past studies have documented a negative relationship
between BC and ﬁrm performance in emerging markets, for example, in Bangladesh (Rashid
et al., 2010) and in Pakistan (Ahmad et al., 2016). These studies typically refer to the fact that
while independent directors increase the quality of monitoring, they may lack sufﬁcient
knowledge of ﬁrm-speciﬁc information, leading to sub-optimal decisions. Duchin et al. (2010)
examine whether the effectiveness of outside directors is inﬂuenced by how difﬁcult it is for
outsiders to acquire expertize about the company. They ﬁnd that if the cost of acquiring
information is low, performance increases when outside directors are added to the board. If
on the other hand, the cost is high, performance deteriorates. The conclusion is, thus, as
follows: the effectiveness of outside directors depends on the “information gap”. However,

some studies have also found that BC has no impact on NPLs (Saha and Ghosh, 2019; Nyor
and Mejabi, 2013).
2.2.3 CEO duality. Agency theory argues that separating the roles of CEO and chairman
of the board can mitigate agency costs. As a leader of the board, the chairman of the board
has the responsibility for monitoring the CEO’s decision-making and overseeing the process
of CEO hiring, ﬁring, evaluation and compensation. The combination of the two leadership
roles of CEO and board chair would, therefore, constrain the effectiveness and objectivity of
this monitoring role, thus promoting CEO entrenchment and intensifying agency conﬂicts.
Beasley et al. (1999) ﬁnd that CEO duality (CEODUAL) was involved in 72 per cent of the
frauds examined by the SEC, and Yermack (1996) ﬁnds that CEODUAL also reduces BC.
The opacity of banks and the lack of market for control can further weaken the CEO
discipline, which makes it more important to separate these leadership roles at banking
ﬁrms. Duality is, thus, considered as a key driver of agency conﬂicts because of reduced
monitoring and allowing the CEO to act in his or her own interests. The business press
provides anecdotal evidence that the risky lending decisions were possible because of loose
oversight and lax boards of directors. For instance, the dual role of CEO and chairman is a
key characteristic of insider power and is considered an indicator of weak corporate
governance (Larcker et al., 2007; Yermack, 1996). In their work on the impact of corporate
governance, Grove et al. (2011) noted that CEODUAL exhibits a negative association with
loan quality in the long-run.
2.3 Macro-economic factors
In the majority of studies that investigate the determinants of NPLs, the aggregate level of
NPLs is considered and either macroeconomic or bank-speciﬁc determinants are used as
explanatory variables. Exceptions include Salas and Saurina (2002), who combine
macroeconomic and microeconomic variables to explain aggregate NPLs of Spanish
commercial and savings banks for the period 1985-1997. They focus on the NPLs
determinants for commercial and savings banks and ﬁnd that bank-speciﬁc determinants
can serve as early warning indicators for future changes in NPLs. Other similar studies
include Clair (1992) and Gonzalez-Hermosillo et al. (1997). Most empirical studies examine
the inﬂuence of the macroeconomic environment on NPLs. Rinaldi and Sanchis-Arellano
(2006) analyse household NPLs for a panel of European countries and provide empirical
evidence that disposable income, unemployment and monetary conditions have a strong
impact on NPLs. Berge and Boye (2007) ﬁnd that problem loans are highly sensitive to the
real interest rates and unemployment for the Nordic banking system over the period 19932005. Boss et al. (2009) examine the coupling of the credit risk of the main Austrian corporate
sectors with the business cycle.
2.3.1 Gross domestic product. GDP is one of the key macro-economic factors that have
the potential to impact every ﬁrm that operates within the larger economy. Lenders, in
particular, stand to feel the effects of changes in GDP growth faster and seriously because
they create a bigger risk exposure by lending to various customers across the entire
economy. The European Central Bank (ECB) (2011) established that GDP growth has been
the main driver of nonperforming loans during the past decade. One of the earliest empirical
studies on the causes of loan losses by Keeton and Morris (1987) showed that a substantial
part of the variation in asset quality was because of differences in economic conditions and
unusually poor performance in sectors such as agriculture and energy. Baboucek and Jancar
(2005) found that GDP decelerates NPLs.

3. Methodology
To explore the effect of corporate governance on the asset quality of the banking sector, the
study uses annual data series on a panel of 26 banks[1] in Ghana over 11-year period from
2006 to 2016. The panel is unbalanced and allows for banks to enter and exit, thus
eliminating survivor bias from the studies. Panel data regressions were used to ascertain the
relationship between corporate governance and bank asset quality. Panel data regressions
usually allow for the control of omitted variable bias that may result from the exclusion of
unobservable variables. The increased observations also provide more information, and
hence, precision while allowing for the analysis of economic questions that cannot be
addressed using time-series or cross-sectional data (Hsiao, 2005). To reduce survivor bias in
the data set, cross-sectional units without complete data for the entire period were included.
The data for the study is collected from the annual reports of the banks and the
macroeconomic data from world development indicators. The explanatory variables used to
explain asset quality follow the broad literature on the determinants of NPLs (Flamini et al.,
2009; Kumar et al., 2018; Brownbridge, 1998; Ranjan and Dhal, 2003; Swamy, 2012; and
others), which posits that NPLs are a function of both bank-speciﬁc and macroeconomic
variables. The effect of corporate governance on loan quality is, therefore, modelled as a
function of bank-speciﬁc governance variables and estimated using panel-corrected errors
because of its ﬁnite sample advantages. The model to be used is speciﬁed as follows:

4.1 Summary statistics and correlation
From the descriptive statistics, banks in Ghana provision an average of 4 per cent of the loan
portfolio to cater for the possibility of bad and doubtful debt. In some cases, however, this
can go as high as crossing the entire loan portfolio and more but with others over the period
making recoveries, and thus, reporting negative provisions. This wide gap within the
provision statistics obviously speak to very different credit risk exposures within the
Ghanaian banking sector. In regard to NPLs, the same set of peculiarities emerge.
While some Ghanaian banks recorded very negligible NPLs, others had more than half
their loan portfolios becoming non-performing. In terms of BS, the Ghanaian banking
landscape is characterized by BS’s of 10 members on average with some having as many as
23 members. Of these board members, approximately 70 per cent are non-executive
members. In essence, it appears that the banking sector uses a signiﬁcant number of nonexecutive members on its boards. Leadership consolidation, that is, CEO duality, is not a
very prevalent practice in the banking industry in Ghana. In fact, approximately only 10 per
cent of industry players have the same person in the positions of board chair and CEO.
GEND within the banking sector is relatively non-existent. In total, an average of 12.3 per
cent of bank boards is made up of women. The maximum representation of women on
banking boards in Ghana is capped at 37.5 per cent. In other words, no board has more than
40 per cent of its membership being women. In breaking the GEND variable further, it
emerges that approximately 29 per cent of banking boards are completely devoid of women
with approximately 90 per cent having a quarter or fewer women representation on the
board.

BC captured as the proportion of non-executive members on the bank’s board emerges as
a countervailing factor that improves the loan portfolio quality of the banks. Per the results,
it is beneﬁcial for banks if the board increases its proportion of non-executive membership.
The coefﬁcient of non-executive members is not only statistically signiﬁcant but also
economically signiﬁcant. A standard deviation increase in the number of non-executive
board members (0.24) is associated with a 0.23 and 24.15 points reduction in provision for
bad loans and NPLs, respectively. This ﬁnding obviously conforms to the standard
literature (Beasley, 1996; Dechow et al., 1996; Peasnell et al., 2000; Klein, 2002; Davidson
et al., 2005) on the beneﬁts of having external members on the board. As Peasnell et al. (2000)
succinctly put it, as outside members do not play a direct role in the management of the
company, their existence may provide an effective monitoring tool to the board, and thus,
produce higher quality output.
Contrary to the conventional wisdom about the positive implications of having a
gender diverse board, the case of GEND in the banking sector seems to have
detrimental effects on its loan portfolio quality. From the ﬁndings, it appears that as the
board becomes more gender diverse, asset quality, whether in terms of provisions for
bad loans or NPLs begins to deteriorate. In other words, having women on the board
detracts from good performance. The economic signiﬁcance of this result is also
important. For instance, a standard deviation increase in female boardroom
representation (0.11) would lead to 0.18 and 20.52 points increase in provision for bad
loans and NPLs, respectively. This ﬁnding, however, may be indicative of tokenism,
where women are placed on boards for the sake of being women but not necessarily
because of expertize. When this happens, the obvious effect is detraction from good
performance. The study, thus, goes against the standpoint of Lu and Boateng (2018)
and Gulamhussen and Santa (2015) that GEND is associated with improved credit risk
management or moderate risk-taking. The increasing demand for GEND in the
boardroom and other high levels of decision-making may lead some institutions to
appoint women who may not have the relevant experience and commitment required
for effective monitoring. It appears that the pool of talents from which to select
potential female board members is rather small. Besides, as noted by Medland (2004),
the informal social network linking directors consists primarily of men, thereby
depriving even qualiﬁed women of accessing the corporate boardroom. Also, Liu et al.
(2014) provide evidence that a critical mass may be needed to have the desired impact.
For instance, they found that boards with three or more women have a greater impact
on ﬁrm performance as opposed to those who had two or fewer women.
In terms of how a macroeconomic variable like GDP affects asset quality in the Ghanaian
banking sector, this study fails to ﬁnd unequivocal evidence to support the position. Across
the two estimations, for both provisions for bad and doubtful debt and NPLs, GDP is not
statistically signiﬁcant. This ﬁnding goes against studies such as Baboucak and Jancar
(2005), who found that GDP decelerates NPLs.

5. Conclusion and policy implications
Asset quality has always been a very crucial ingredient in the discourse on bank
intermediation and the stability of the ﬁnancial services sector. Indeed, the minimization of
poor asset quality is key to the continued functioning of ﬁnancial intermediation and
ultimately growth. A number of studies have sought to explain the drivers of deteriorating
asset quality, especially after the global ﬁnancial crisis of 2007/2008. What has clearly
emerged is that bank asset quality is a function of both bank-speciﬁc and macroeconomic
variables. In this paper, the quest was to examine the role of bank-level governance
characteristics and economic growth on bank asset quality in Ghana. The study, thus,
examined board-level characteristics such as size, independence, GEND and CEODUAL
together with GDP growth as determinants of asset quality, which were proxied by
provision for bad and doubtful loans and NPLs, both scaled by the gross loan portfolio.
The ﬁndings of the study make a clear case for the relevance of governance in the bank
asset quality discourse, despite the proposition that bank-level governance may be
irrelevant in the face of the stringent regulatory framework that governs the conduct of
banks. The ﬁndings expand the debate on the implications of governance for performance.
In essence, large boards may be beneﬁcial for banks if it pools the right expertize for the
purposes of adding value to the bank. It is also important that external membership to
the board is encouraged to ensure the monitoring role expected of such boards is executed to
the fullest. The emergence of potential tokenism in the space of GEND and bank
performance requires further interrogation to truly map out the role of gender within the
banking sector of Ghana. The increasing demand for GEND in the boardroom may have led
some institutions to appoint women who may not have the relevant expertize, experience
and commitment required for effective monitoring. Also, a critical mass of women directors
may be needed to inﬂuence decision-making in the boardroom. So, whilst the available
literature may make a business case for GEND, it is unclear in this case whether certain
factors must prevail to experience the value-additive effect of GEND.
