Corporate governance and value relevance
of financial information: evidence from the
Ghana Stock Exchange
Vera Ogeh Fiador

Abstract

Vera Ogeh Fiador is based
at the Business School,
University of Ghana, Legon,
Ghana.

Purpose – This study aims to look at how corporate governance, specifically internal mechanisms of
corporate governance, affects the value relevance of reported accounting earnings of listed firms on the
Ghana Stock Exchange.
Design/methodology/approach – The study used the Ohlson valuation model with a panel dataset,
employing pooled regression analysis with random effects.
Findings – The findings indicate that net asset value per share is value relevant on the Ghanaian
market, and even more so when the board size is small or the CEO also doubles as the board chair.
Board independence as captured by the percentage of non-executive directors on the board is
relatively irrelevant in the market valuation of shares, and when relevant has a negative effect.
Originality/value – The value relevance of accounting information on the Ghanaian financial market, by
implication, requires that the necessary rules and supervision regarding financial reporting must be
provided to ensure that the information that reaches the investing public is therefore a true reflection of
the underlying economic value of firms, so that capital allocation decisions based on these sources will
be efficient.
Keywords Value relevance, Boards of directors, Corporate governance, Accounting information, Ghana
Paper type Research paper

1. Introduction
Agency theory posits that the separation of ownership and management as enshrined in the
act of incorporation usually comes along with a myriad of agency problems. This conflict of
interests between management and shareholders has given rise to many tools in a bid to
control the opportunistic behaviour of corporate managers. Corporate governance has, over
time, become one of the major tools used in aligning corporate management interests with
those of shareholders (Fama and Jensen, 1983a).
Corporate governance, according to Mensah et al. (2003), is the set of arrangements
through which organisations account to shareholders and the general public. Mensah et al.
(2003) go on to indicate that sound corporate governance facilitates development by
ensuring the efficient allocation of economic resources. One of the pillars that supports
corporate governance in the efficient allocation of investable funds is adequate information,
both financial and non-financial. Financial information from the accounting statements of
listed firms has for decades been the main mode of communication for firms seeking to
attract investors in search of investment avenues. Financial reports usually provide data on a
firm’s liquidity, solvency, profitability, efficiency and wealth creation positions.
Corporate governance awareness in Ghana can be described as being in its incipient
stages (World Bank, 2005). Regulations relating to disclosure are adequate. Enforcement,
however, especially with respect to related-party transactions, is non-existent (World Bank,
2005). In terms of internal governance characteristics, corporate boards are usually

requirement leans towards more non-executive members. On the whole, Ghana’s rating in

The rules governing financial reporting mainly stem from the Companies Code, Act 179. The
Act provides for the preparation and publication of financial statements, disclosures and
auditing, among other provisions. Nonetheless it fails to stipulate the standards for the
preparation of such reports such as the International Accounting Standards or the Ghana
National Accounting Standards. In 2007, however, the country has officially adopted the
International Financial Reporting Standards for all listed companies, with full compliance
expected of small and medium-sized enterprises (SMEs) by 2009.
Financial statements across the globe, Ghana included, are usually generated from a
multitude of inputs, namely legal, regulatory, industry and firm-specific elements (Ragab
and Omran, 2006). Given the importance of financial statements in communicating firm
details to shareholders and the public at large, the issue of value-relevance becomes an
important factor that is worth researching. The value-relevance of financial information has
generally been captured as the ability of financial data to summarise firm value or to reflect
information that affects stock market measures, i.e. stock returns and stock turnover.
Pursuant to one of the tenets of good corporate governance, which states that there must be
full disclosure of all material facts to all stakeholders, is the question of whether corporate
governance has any influence whatsoever on the value-relevance of a firm’s financial
reports.
The study is motivated from the point-of-view that alternative sources of information such as
earnings forecasts or firm performance ratings for investor analysis are scanty if not
completely non-existent on the Ghanaian financial market. The significance of the study
therefore lies in the fact that the value-relevance or otherwise of financial statements of listed
firms as well as the influence of corporate governance on such value-relevance will help in
the necessary policies to ensure that accounting information, if it has predictive power, can
be improved upon and can be relied upon as an accurate reflection of firm value and an aid
to the efficient allocation of economic resources.
This study seeks to determine the relationship between corporate governance and the
value-relevance of accounting information. Specifically, the study seeks to determine the
value-relevance of accounting information of Ghanaian listed companies and also to assess
the influence that corporate governance lends to the degree of value-relevance of
accounting information in Ghana.
The rest of the paper is structured as follows: the next section reviews literature relevant to
the study followed by the method employed, the results of the study, the conclusion and
policy implications.

2. Literature review
2.1 Corporate governance
This section reviews literature on the concepts relevant to the study, i.e. corporate
governance, financial reporting standards and value-relevance. In terms of definition, there
is no universally accepted definition for corporate governance, although it has been defined
broadly as the set of private and public institutions, including laws, regulations and
accepted business practices, that, in a market economy, govern the relationship between
corporate managers (‘‘corporate insiders’’) on one hand, and those who invest resources in
corporations on the other (Oman, 2001). Other writers define corporate governance as ‘‘an
umbrella term that includes specific issues arising from interactions among senior

management, shareholders, boards of directors, and other corporate stakeholders’’ (see
Cochran and Wartick, 1988). Agency theory (Jensen and Meckling, 1976) posits that
managers are opportunistic shirkers who must be controlled if their actions are to be aligned
with the interests of shareholders. As per the agency paradigm, corporate governance
through an independent corporate board is a key way of controlling opportunistic
managerial behaviour. The operation of corporate governance generally encompasses both
internal and external mechanisms. Internal corporate mechanisms include such measures
as the board of directors, internal auditors, and executive compensation. The most dominant
of these measures of internal corporate governance is the corporate board, which is usually
charged with overseeing the strategic management of the company (Keenan, 2004). Its
most important role is to ensure that shareholders’ interests are upheld in all situations. The
strength of the board in executing its most important role is dependent on its characteristics,
namely size, composition and CEO duality.
The influence of the size of the board on its ability to deliver has received significant research
interest over the years. The consensus is that board size is significant in influencing the
effectiveness of the board, although the relationship is assumed to be non-linear (see
Agoraki et al., 2009; Karamanou and Vafeas, 2005; Pye, 2000; John and Senbet, 1998;
Yermack, 1996; Jensen, 1993). According to Boone et al. (2007), Raheja (2004) and Harris
and Raviv (2006), the size of the board usually reflects the trade-off between the
firm-specific benefits of monitoring and the costs associated with such monitoring. The
driving force behind the size of the board is usually economic, specifically the firm’s
competitive environment and its managerial team.
Board composition, usually used as a measure of board independence, captures the ratio of
non-executive directors on the board. It is generally accepted that a board is more
independent and efficient the higher the ratio of external directors (Yermack, 1996). In other
words, the more external directors there are on a corporate board, the more effective it is in
performing its wealth maximisation duties to shareholders through its capacity to monitor
and control inefficient managerial behaviour. Others (see Hermalin and Weisbach, 1991; Pi
and Timme, 1993; Adams and Mehran, 2008; Belkhir, 2006) are of the view, however, that
board composition has no bearing whatsoever on wealth maximisation. This implies that a
haphazard selection of executive and non-executive board members is of no consequence
in the quest for value creation.
CEO duality, where the CEO of the firm also doubles as the board chair, has received
significant research attention in recent times due to the pursuit of higher codes of
governance. In general, CEO duality is perceived as a constraint on the independence of the
board. Put simply, when the CEO also doubles as the board chair, the performance of the
board in terms of effectiveness and efficiency in maximizing shareholders wealth is
compromised (Bliss, 2010). This is based on the reasoning that too much power in the hands
of one person results in non-value maximizing and inefficient behaviour. It is on this
reasoning that the South African King Report, one of the codes for best practices in
corporate governance, clearly delineates the two powers and requires that they be vested in
two persons. The goals of corporate governance include, among other things:

The goal of ensuring the efficient allocation of resources is dependent on the available
financial information, usually reported via accounting statements.

information seems to indicate a higher value relevance of accounting information for firms
with strong governance structures, although this finding is not consistent across studies (see

governance matters for the value relevance of accounting information, the specific
governance variables that can be manipulated to improve information quality in different
settings call for individualized country studies. This study seeks to make such an attempt.

2.2 Financial reporting standards, opacity and value-relevance
The vital role played by published financial information in the capital market in the face of
globalisation cannot be over-emphasised. Published financial data serve the purpose of
providing information to facilitate capital flow from international investors as well as showing
management’s stewardship of the resources entrusted to it (Van Greuning, 2009). It is
precisely this function of published financial data that has resulted in most countries
adopting financial reporting standards that provide meaningful information to local and
foreign investors alike. Compliance with the International Financial Reporting Standards
ensures that the data are relevant, have predictive value, constitute a faithful representation
of material facts, are free from bias, and are verifiable (Van Greuning, 2009).
Corporate governance, together with the requirements of the financial reporting standards,
seeks to create a safe environment for stakeholders through a culture of transparency.
Transparency results from making information on existing conditions, decisions, and actions
accessible, visible and understandable to all market participants. Earnings opacity may be
easily defined as a measure that reflects how little information there is in a firm’s earnings
number about its true, but unobservable, economic performance (Bhattacharya et al.,
2002). Financial opacity therefore stands to reduce the predictive power of financial
reporting while constraining the efficient allocation of resources within the economy.
Bhattacharya et al. (2002) identify three channels by which earnings opacity, which
ultimately results in lack of value relevance, may affect the cost of equity. According to them,
better financial accounting information, through reducing estimation risk, distinguishing
between good and bad managers and thereby reducing agency costs, and reducing the
level of information asymmetry, invariably helps investors to distinguish between good and
bad investments and ultimately reduces the cost of equity. Whilst Bhattacharya et al. (2002),
suggest that earnings opacity is not necessarily good for the cost of equity capital and the
financial market, Fan and Wong (2002), believe that in a competitive market environment,
earnings opacity may be a good strategy as it may prevent the leakage of proprietary
information to competitors.
One way or the other, corporate governance is inexorably linked with the quality and value of
accounting information. In general, corporate governance seeks to improve the value of
accounting information, reveal managerial efficiencies and reduce manipulation of earnings
by management through the enforcement of appropriate standards (de Almeida et al.,
2009).

The study uses all non-financial companies listed on the Ghana Stock Exchange from 1997
to 2006, given that financial data after this period was reported using the International
Financial Reporting Standards, which differ from the reporting standards used prior to 2007.
The data was collated from the published financial statements and other reports on the listed
companies.
3.2 Model estimation
Panel data regressions were used to ascertain the relationship between stock prices,
accounting values and corporate governance. Panel data regressions allow for the control of
omitted variable bias that may result from the exclusion of unobservable variables. The
increased observations also provide more information and hence precision while allowing for
the analysis of economic questions that cannot be addressed using time series or
cross-sectional data (Hsiao, 2003). The model for the study is based on a modified Ohlson
(1995) earnings model of stock valuation. As per this concept, the earnings reported by a firm
and the net asset value (book value) are a priori indicators of firm value. This is on the
assumption that, ceteris paribus, accounting records of value should ultimately be linked to
the market valuation of shares. In line with this proposition, the model for the study is given as:

where Pit, the dependent variable, is the end-of-year share price of firm i in period t. The
explanatory variables include earnings per share (EPSit), which is defined as end-of-year
earnings after tax divided by shares outstanding (capturing the income statement) and net
asset value per share (NAVit), which is defined as total assets minus total liabilities of firm i in
period t divided by the number of shares outstanding (capturing the status of the balance
sheet). There are three internal governance measures in this model:
1. board composition (BCit), defined as the percentage of external directors on the board;
2. board size (BSit), which refers to the number of members constituting the board and is
also squared to capture its posited non-linear behaviour; and
3. CEOit, for CEO duality, which is a dummy taking a value of 1 if the CEO doubles as the
board chair and 0 otherwise.
The interaction terms are included to capture the influence that corporate governance lends
to the value relevance of these accounting measures of value.
Price and net asset value per share were logged for standardisation. Breusch-Pagan LM
tests returned significant values, and thus the models were estimated using the random
effects approach.

The value-relevance of net asset value is not surprising given that other sources of
information in relation to securities on the Ghanaian financial markets such as analyst
forecasts are non-existent, thus making the accounting earnings report the only source of
information available to the investing public. What is surprising, though, is the irrelevance of
earnings per share in investor valuation of traded shares. It may be inferred that market
participants consider earnings as unreliable sources on which to base a valuation of shares.
This finding is in support of the findings by Collins et al. (1999), who document that book
value played two roles in equity valuation, i.e. it provided information about both expected
future normal earnings and about abandonment/liquidation value, thus making the book
value of equity especially value-relevant in the presence of negative earnings.
The square of the board size, the proportion of outsiders on the board and CEO duality were
included on the premise that measures of good corporate governance ultimately influence the
market valuation of shares through the perception of efficient monitoring and control of
managers in favour of shareholders (Bai et al., 2004). The square of the board size was
included to capture its posited non-linear effect. The coefficient for the square of the board size
was positively and significantly related at 1 per cent to share price across all three regressions.
This finding provides evidence for larger boards, as suggested by Dalton et al. (1999) based
on the proposition that larger boards bring diverse expertise and efficient decisions to the
boardroom, thus resulting in favourable performance and ultimately a favourable market
valuation. This finding, in favour of larger board sizes, is a contradiction of the evidence in
favour of smaller boards (Yermack, 1996), which is predicated on the argument that fewer
board members are able to share ideas more easily due to their reduced numbers, thus leading
to faster decision-making and ultimately higher performance and market valuation.
The composition of the board is insignificant in regressions 1 and 3 and only marginally
negatively related at 10 per cent to share prices in regression 2. The relative insignificance of
board composition within this study is interesting, given that listed firms in Ghana, as per the
regulations for listing, are encouraged to have more non-executive directors on the board. It
would appear that the compliance of listed firms with this requirement (approximately 77 per

CEO duality is also insignificant in the first two regressions but turns highly significant, albeit
with a negative sign, in the regression 3. It would appear that the Ghanaian market does not

finding is in consonance with expectations as regards agency theory, which requires the
separation of power between the CEO and the board chair, since such a situation vests too
much authority in one person and does not augur well for firm performance. Bugshan (2005)
and Kyereboah-Coleman (2007) support this finding by documenting negative effects of
CEO duality on value relevance of accounting information for Australia and Tobin’s Q for
Kenya, respectively.
The regression output from the second equation as per Table III incorporates the interaction
terms between corporate governance variables and the accounting measures of value.
From the output, earnings per share become value relevant in the presence of corporate
governance, albeit with a negative sign as regards the board size. The negative effect of the
interactive term between board size and earnings per share seems to imply that as the
board gets larger earnings per share loses its value relevance. On the other hand, earnings
per share relate positively to share price when the board is composed of more independent
directors and has the CEO as the chair. This finding seems to imply that, in Ghana, the
market has a higher valuation of shares when the earnings per share reported also tie in with
smaller corporate boards composed of mostly independent directors with the CEO acting as
the board chair.
The third regression also incorporates interactive terms between the governance variables
and net asset value per share. The findings seem to indicate that net asset value is
negatively related to share prices as the board gets larger, providing more evidence in
favour of smaller boards. Board composition, however, does not help the value relevance of
the book value of equity in any way, as it is insignificant. CEO duality, on the other hand,
improves the value-relevance of net asset value per share, suggesting that when the CEO
also doubles as the board chair, the investing public considers net asset value per share to
be relevant in the valuation of shares.

5. Conclusion and implications for policy
From the findings of the study, it can be posited that accounting measures of value,
specifically net asset values per share, are important in explaining share prices. In essence,
increases in net asset values translate to a higher market valuation. This is not surprising,
given that all things being equal, an increase in net asset values implies an increase in the
size of the pie that goes to the shareholder. The value irrelevance of earnings per share in its
own right, inferred from its relative insignificance in market valuation of shares, may be
inferred as implying a perception by the investing public of opportunistic managerial
earnings manipulation with the goal of achieving higher market valuations for their own
purposes. Policy makers must therefore seek to ensure that the main sources of information
to the investing public, and also those that seem to influence the security valuation process,
should reflect the true underlying values and not be ‘‘cooked’’ for the purposes of deceiving
or misleading the market, as this would result in the allocation of scarce resource on the
financial market to inefficient businesses.
