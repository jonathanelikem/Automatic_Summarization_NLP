
Does corporate governance explain dividend policy in Sub-Saharan AfricaK
Joshua Abor and Vera Fiador
Department of Finance, University of Ghana Business School, Legon, Ghana
Abstract
Purpose ľ This study aims to examine the effect of corporate governance on firmsĺ dividend payout policy in sub-Saharan Africa. The study also aims to examine how dividend payout influences corporate governance.
Design/methodology/approach ľ Using a sample made up 27 Ghanaian firms, 177 Nigerian firms, 51 Kenyan firms, and 270 South African firms covering the period 1997-2006, the paper employs a simultaneous panel regression model in its estimation.
Findings ľ The results show that board composition and board size exhibit significantly positive relationship with dividend payout in Kenya and Ghana, respectively. Institutional ownership positively influences dividend payout among South African and Kenyan firms. In the case of Nigeria, all the corporate governance measures show significantly negative effects on dividend payout. The findings clearly suggest that, with respect to South Africa, Kenya and Ghana, good corporate governance structures lead to high-dividend payout, probably due to easy access to and low cost of external finance. However, in Nigeria, improving the governance structures may be associated with high-earnings retention or low-dividend payment in order to reduce cost of external finance. We found in the case of Ghana that, dividend payout positively affects board composition, suggesting that Ghanaian firms with high-payout tend to adopt good corporate governance structures in order to ensure protection of shareholder interest. The findings of this study certainly have important policy implications.
Originality/value ľ This present study contributes to the corporate governance literature by looking at the importance of corporate governance in influencing firmsĺ dividend behaviour in selected African countries.
Keywords Corporate governance, Dividend payout, Dividend policy, Sub Saharan Africa, Dividends

1. Introduction
Agency theory suggests that the existence of different types of corporate governance mechanisms depends on agency costs. Consequently, a system of corporate governance is introduced, which discourages managers from pursuing objectives that fail to maximize shareholder wealth (Hutchinson and Gul, 2004). One important mechanism of the corporate governance system is the role of the board of directors. This is because the board of directors is seen as the primary means for shareholders to exercise control over top management ( John and Senbet, 1998). The board has the obligation to determine the firmĺs overall strategy, and to ensure that adequate controls are in place to protect shareholder value (Keenan, 2004).   The few empirical studies on corporate governance and dividend policy have focused on non-African firms (Short et al., 2002; Gugler, 2003; Gugler and Yurtoglu, 2003; Farinha, 2003; Knyazeva, 2007), making it important to also understand the issue from an African perspective, considering that corporate governance structures, ownership structures and institutional frameworks in most countries and continents function differently. For example, in most Anglo-Saxon countries, such as the USA or UK, where investor protection is high, stock ownership is often dispersed and it is claimed that each individual shareholder has only limited incentives and ability to monitor the management. The major conflict in the governance of companies, accordingly, appears to be between powerful managers and small outside shareholders (Gugler and Yurtoglu, 2003). In Japan and most of the South East Asian countries, common governance devices include business groups with their pyramidal and cross-ownership structures. In these countries, legal requirements for management are rather weak (Claessens et al., 2000). In Continental Europe, the main feature of the governance system is that firms often have a concentrated ownership structure, but their corporate law tends to play a minor role. Corporate governance systems are also characterized by large shareholders, and according to Gugler and Yurtoglu (2003), such majority control gives the largest shareholder considerable power and discretion over key decisions, like dividend payouts. In sub-Saharan Africa countries, firms are mostly held byinstitutional blockholders and the corporate platform is characterized by weak and sometimes fragmented regulatory systems as well as low levels of investor protection. In this regard, sub-Saharan Africa provides an interesting opportunity to investigate the relationship between corporate governance and dividend policy considering the variations in the degree of investor protection, and the prevalence of high-institutional ownership.
  Following from the above, the importance of the study thus lies in its contribution to the literature by providing a perspective on governance in Africa in general, considering that corporate governance structures, ownership structures and institutional frameworks in most countries and continents function differently. Second, this present study also contributes to the corporate governance literature by looking at the importance of corporate governance, proxied by corporate boards and institutional shareholding, in influencing firmsĺ dividend behaviour in the selected African countries. Last but not least, it also sheds some insight on the complementarity versus substitutability between corporate governance and dividend policy for the Sub-Saharan African countries under study.
  The current paper is motivated from an agency perspective and examines the extent to which the corporate governance structures affect dividend payout decisions. We focus specifically on how corporate board characteristics and institutional ownership structure affect dividend payout policy in South Africa, Nigeria, Kenya and Ghana. We selected listed firms in these countries because they have the most active and functioning stock exchanges in sub-Saharan Africa. We also examine how dividend payout influences corporate governance. The rest of the paper is structured as follows: Section 2 provides an overview of the corporate governance terrain in the selected countries with Section 3 discussing the literature. Section 4 explains the methodology employed in this study. Section 5 includes a discussion of the empirical results. Section 6 concludes the study.



2. Overview of corporate governance and dividend behaviour in the selected countries
In terms of maturity, corporate governance is said to be in its budding stage in Africa. However, it is recognised by investors in Africa as being equally important as and sometimes more important than financial issues. Nganga et al. (2003) suggest that investor protection in Africa is comparable to other developing countries but like all emerging markets, the inefficiency in the legal system makes enforcement of the law slow and patchy. Over the years, however, significant improvements have been made to address corporate governance weaknesses through changes in stock market regulations and the introduction of governance codes. In general, some of the countries on the African continent have adopted a code of corporate governance best practices based on international standards like the South African King report, the UK Combined code, OECD Code and the Commonwealth Secretariat Code. The individual countries within the sample, however, exhibit slightly differing corporate governance characteristics worth discussing.
  South Africa is considered one of the best emerging markets in terms of corporate governance. The law that governs listed firms in South Africa, the Companies Act (1973) is inspired by the British common law. The legal framework for its implementation, especially in relation to corporate governance, is self-regulatory and fragmented between three institutions: the Financial Services Board, the Department of Trade and Industry and the Companies and Intellectual Properties Registration Office. This, according to the 2003 Reports on Observance of Standards and Codes (ROSC, 2003) on South Africa, leads to regulatory duplications on one hand, undesirable loopholes on the other and finally gives way to a weak enforcement culture. In terms of shareholder rights and minority protection, these are quite well-observed. There are some deficiencies, however, in the opportunities provided for shareholders to participate and vote in meetings and be privy to relevant company issues. According to a study by La Porta et al. (2000), South Africa, in general, scores 5 on a scale of 1-7 with 1 representing worst and 7 representing best. Disclosure and issues of transparency are, however, only partially observed, with ownership being concentrated. In 1994, South Africa also adopted the King Report to regulate corporate governance practices. The report was updated in 2002 (King Report II) and again in 2009 (King Report III) to incorporate new developments in international corporate governance. The King Report II applied to listed companies, financial institutions and public institutions, while the King Report III focuses on all entities, irrespective of their nature, size or form of business (Els et al., 2011).
  In Nigeria, the legal framework is derived from British Common Law. There are similar commercial codes that deal with shareholder rights and minority protection. The main corporate governance code is the Companies and Allied Matters Act of 1990 which requires, among other things, that directors of every company prepare and present financial statements (e.g. five-year summaries, balance sheets, profit and loss accounts) on an annual basis. The Development Policy Centre (1999) in a survey of enterprises revealed that a majority of businesses, 87 percent, operate outside the legislation governing the capital market. Institutional ownership is significant in Nigeria (approximately, 48 percent), followed by individual holdings and by government holdings. Despite the large institutional holdings, a significant portion of this is in foreign hands. Shareholdersĺ rights are not that well-observed. In fact, while the countryĺs regulations require fair conduct of shareholderĺs meetings, publication

Minority shareholders rights are lacking in some respects, according to the report on corporate governance in Africa by Nganga et al. (2003).
  In Ghana, the Companies Code 1963, which governs companies listed on the stock exchange is based on UK legislation and supported by the Securities Industries Law 1963 (PNDCL 333) as amended by the Securities Industry (Amendment) Act 2000 (Act 590). Institutional investors on the Ghanaian financial market are somewhat passive and focused on short-term profits, rather than corporate governance issues and awareness of corporate governance is in incipient stages. Ownership concentration is on the high side, primarily institutional, with foreign and state ownership but rarely family ownership. In terms of shareholder rights, these are generally well- observed, but enforcement, especially on material facts disclosure, monitoring for content, related party transactions and ownership disclosures, is lacking (World Bank, 2005). Also, the companiesĺ code, which provides the main regulatory framework for companies, does not provide for the balance between executives and non-executive board members. According to a report by the World Economic Forum, the effectiveness of Ghanaĺs legal system with respect to governance issues scores 2.96 on a scale of 1-6, with 1 representing most effective. In Ghana, the most frequently occurring board size is 8 and usually composed of internal or executive board members as opposed to external or non-executive members.
  Comparing dividend behaviour across the sample reveals interesting dynamics. South Africa, in general, exhibits a rising trend in terms of payout ratio over the years, with some dips in between years of increasing payout ratio. Kenya, on the other hand, seems to have a relatively erratic payout trend, rising and falling with the same momentum, while Nigeria dips significantly in the year 2000 and exhibits a relatively stable behaviour in subsequent years. Ghana, however, seems to have a pattern of its own. Aside from being the country with the lowest payout ratio overtime, the trend has also not been consistent. Surprisingly, however, increases and decreases in payout ratio, where occurring, are not significant enough to signal shareholders or the

  In terms of tax policies in relation to investments, all the countries in the sample over the study period, with the exception of South Africa, operated a withholding tax system with regard to dividends. Ghana and Nigeria had a 10 percent withholding tax on dividends, while Kenya had a 5 percent withholding tax. South Africa, on the other hand, did not have withholding tax on dividend, thus favoring dividend payments over capital gains on which it operated a 15 percent tax. Ghana and Nigeria had a 10 percent capital gains tax. Capital gains were, however, not taxable in Kenya. This system obviously favoured capital gains over dividend payments, a reverse of the South African case.
3. Theoretical overview
The essence of the agency problem is the separation of management (control) and finance (ownership) (Shleifer and Vishny, 1997). This is mainly due to the fact that management has an incentive to pursue policies to their advantage and to the detriment of shareholder value. Fama and Jensen (1983) argue that agency conflicts can be circumvented by large dividend payments to shareholders. Easterbrook (1984) affirms this view by indicating that dividend payments control the agency problems by facilitating capital market monitoring of the firmsĺ activities and performance.
  Another perspective on governance and dividend payments is proposed by Zeckhauser and Pound (1990). They suggest that institutional shareholders may act as a substitute monitoring device, hence reducing the need for external monitoring by the capital markets. Short et al. (2002) on the other hand, however, indicate that given the armĺs length view of investment held by many institutional investors, coupled with the incentives to free ride with respect to monitoring activities, institutional shareholders are unlikely to provide direct monitoring themselves. and Pound (1990) affirm this argument by suggesting that institutional shareholders usually tend to impress on firms to increase their dividends such that they are later compelled to raise capital from the external capital market. Yet another argument, advanced by Myers (2000), suggests that managers may actually be willing to pay dividends in order to avoid disciplining action by shareholders.
  The literature on signaling hypothesis builds upon the pioneering work of Bhattacharya (1979). The signaling theory suggests that dividends are used to signal managementĺs private information regarding the future earnings of the firm (Bhattacharya, 1980). As per Healy and Palepu (1988), investors interpret announcements of dividend initiations and omissions as managerĺs forecast of future earnings changes. Dividends are generally used in signaling a firmĺs future prospects, and dividends are paid even if there is profitable investment opportunity (Baker et al., 1985).
  The flip side of dividend policy is that dividends may be subject to tax penalty, and this depends on the tax regime in a particular country. In countries such as the USA that operate a classical tax system, companies are taxed separately from their shareholders. Firms pay differential rate of corporate tax on their profits and shareholders pay income tax on the dividend income received (Black, 1976). However, in countries like the UK that operate a partial imputation company tax system, company tax is charged on firm profits but part of the company tax paid is taken into account when assessing shareholdersĺ liability to income tax (Short et al., 2002).
  Another negative aspect of dividend is that dividends are unwelcome by debt holders, since they potentially deplete the collateral base available to the debt holders, and potentially increase the cost of debt. Debt is believed to play an important role in monitoring managers. In particular, financial leverage has been argued by Jensen and Meckling (1976) and Jensen (1986), among others, to play a role in reducing agency costs arising from the shareholder-manager conflict. Farinha (2003) argues that debt is likely to cause less dividend payments because of debt covenants and related restrictions that may be imposed by debt holders. Therefore, firms with high-financial leverage and implied financial risk may be restricted from paying more dividends.
  Agency problems could be restricted through internal (the role of board of directors and executive compensation contracts) and external corporate governance mechanisms (markets for corporate control and direct action/shareholder activism) ( John and Senbet, 1998). We focus specifically on board governance and institutional ownership. Internal governance occurs as the board of directors actively seeks to protect the interests of shareholders. Important board characteristics include the size of the board, the composition of the board, and the role of the CEO. A large board for instance has the tendency of subjecting the management and operation of the firms to greater scrutiny and monitoring ( John and Senbet, 1998). With respect to board composition, Yermack (1996) argues that having a high proportion of non-executive directors is likely to increase the independence of the companyĺs board. High proportion of outside directors provides a better forum for making quality corporate decisions (Fama and Jensen, 1983; Jensen, 1993; Kyereboah-Coleman and Amidu, 2008). Another significant aspect of the board system is the role of the CEO. Fama and Jensen (1983) argue that the concentration of decision making and decision control in one individual reduces the boardĺs effectiveness in monitoring top management. A weak board is unlikely to advance shareholdersĺ cause.
  In terms of external governance systems, institutional blockholders may also act as a monitoring device on the firmĺs managers. This is because agency problems are



said to be minimal in firms with large block shareholders that are able to monitor management activities (Shleifer and Vishny, 1986), thus dampening in principle the need for high-dividend payouts. Blockholder monitoring is expected to be more intense in the presence of institutional blockholders with large holdings in the firm. It is likely for institutional shareholders to influence higher dividend payouts by a company to enhance managerial monitoring by external capital markets, namely if they believe their own direct monitoring efforts to be insufficient or too costly. On the other hand, Jensen and Meckling (1976) argue that when shareholders are too dispersed to take action against non-value maximisation behaviour, insiders may use the firmĺs assets for their own personal benefit, such as shirking responsibilities and consuming perquisites.

3.1 Empirical literature on corporate governance and dividend policy
Empirical studies on corporate governance and firmsĺ dividend policy have yielded interesting results. In a study by Fenn and Liang (2001), investigating how managerial stock incentives affect dividend payout policy, they found that managerial stock incentives mitigate the agency costs for firms with excess cash flow problems. Their results revealed a negative association between management stock options and firmsĺ dividend payout policy. Using a UK panel data set, Short et al. (2002) also found a negative association between dividend payout policy and managerial ownership. Using a sample of over 600 UK firms, Farinha (2003) found strong evidence that after a critical entrenchment level of insider ownership estimated in the region of 30 percent, the coefficient on insider ownership changes from negative to positive. He also found that shareholder dispersion has a significantly positive impact on dividend policy.
Another strand in the empirical literature is the relationship between institutional
ownership and dividend policies. Han et al. (1999) and Short et al. (2002), studied institutional shareholders and dividends and found dividend to be positively related to institutional ownership. Gugler and Yurtoglu (2003) also investigated the dividend, ownership and control structure of firms in Germany. Analysing 736 dividend change announcements in Germany over the period 1992-1998, they found significantly larger negative wealth effects in the order of 2 percent points for companies where the ownership and control structure makes the expropriation of minority shareholders more likely than for other firms. They found that large shareholding of the largest owner reduces the dividend payout, while shareholding of the second largest owner increases the dividend payout ratio. Their results provide evidence that dividends signal the severity of the conflict between the large, controlling owner and small outside shareholders.
In their study, La Porta et al. (2000) also looked at the agency problems and dividends
policies around the world. Using a sample of firms from 33 countries around the world, they distinguished two alternative agency models of dividends. They found that firms operating in countries with better protection of minority shareholders pay higher dividends. They also showed that, fast growth firms in countries with strong legal protection tend to pay lower dividends than slow growth firms, consistent with the idea that legally protected shareholders are willing to wait for their dividends when investment opportunities are good. However, poorly protected shareholders seem to take whatever dividends they can get, regardless of investment opportunities.
  Gugler (2003) examined the relationship between dividends and the ownership and control structure of Austrian firms. Using a panel of firms over the 1991/1999 period, he found that state controlled firms engage in dividend smoothing, while family-

  Knyazeva (2007) examined the effects of corporate governance on dynamic dividend behaviour of US firms. He also found that in the absence of strong corporate governance, dividends can limit inefficient managerial investment. He established that weakly governed managers face more shareholder pressure to sustain the dividend commitment, and that weakly governed managers make fewer dividend cuts and engage in more dividend smoothing. His results showed that weak governance has a positive effect on dividend changes, mainly in response to large cash-flow increases, and also total payout adjustments made by weakly governed managers support the dividend commitment.
  In a recent study, Adjaoud and Ben-Amar (2010) investigated the relationship between corporate governance quality and dividend policy in Canada. Using a sample of 714 firm-years listed on the Toronto Stock Exchange over the period 2002-2005, they found that firms with stronger corporate governance have higher dividend payouts. Their results indicate that board composition and shareholder rightsĺ policy are positively related to payout ratios.
  In summary, the literature leans towards the argument that agency conflicts can be circumvented by large dividend payment to shareholders. Dividend policy is seen as an outcome of an optimizing policy based on the trade-off between the positive side (signaling information and reduction of managerial agency) and the negative side (tax penalty and increase in debt agency). Agency problems could be restricted through internal and external corporate governance mechanisms. Therefore, the link between corporate governance and dividend policy is based on the notion that good corporate governance motivates decision-makers to follow an optimal dividend policy that maximizes shareholder wealth. When good corporate governance is presumed to motivate management to pay higher dividends (considering the ôgood sideö theory of dividend), then we will expect corporate governance to have a positive effect on dividends. However, dividends can also serve as a substitute disciplining mechanism, leading to the substitution effect, and a negative relationship between dividends and corporate governance. In this study we use board size, board composition, CEO duality and institutional ownership as measures of corporate governance.



4. Methodology
This study focused on listed firms on the Johannesburg stock exchange, the Nigerian stock exchange, the Nairobi stock exchange, and the Ghana stock exchange. Firms on these stock exchanges were chosen because they are the most active and functioning stock exchanges in sub-Saharan Africa for which we were able to get information easily. The financial data and corporate governance indicators were obtained from the annual reports of the listed companies and the fact books of the respective stock exchanges. South Africa for instance is included as a ôbest practiceö country. In all, we used 270 firms listed on the Johannesburg stock exchange, 177 firms listed on the Nigerian stock exchange, 51 firms listed on the Nairobi stock exchange and 27 firms listed on the Ghana stock exchange. The study covers a ten-year period, 1997-2006. The panel is based on annual time series data. We estimate a simultaneous panel regression model to examine the relationship between corporate governance and dividend payout. The estimation technique adopted in this study is the instrumental variable (IV) panel regression. This method is preferred to OLS estimation of the individual equations in the system because the OLS estimation introduces ôsimultaneous equations biasö and therefore the estimators are inconsistent. This therefore requires instrumental variable (IV) methods like two stage least squares (2SLS) to obtain consistent parameter estimates (Baltagi, 2005). We use number of years the firm has been listed as an instrument for board composition. The rationale for using this instrument is that we expect firms that have been listed for long to have a greater percentage of external board members. We expect that the stock market will put pressure on such firms to have more independent members on their corporate board. But this will not in any way affect dividend payout. We also use profitability as an instrument for dividend payout. The rationale for this is that, the level of profitability is likely to affect dividend payout but may not affect board composition.



5. Empirical results
5.1 Descriptive statistics
Table I gives an overview of the descriptive statistics of the sample under study. In terms of dividend payout, South Africa records the highest mean value of 58.26 percent with Ghana recording the lowest mean value of 30.99 percent. The second highest is Kenya having a mean dividend payout of 57.44 percent, followed by Nigeria with a mean dividend payout of 54.27 percent. The countries seem to have board sizes that range between 8 and 10. Nigeria records the highest mean value for board composition (87.63 percent) followed by Kenya (84.76 percent), then Ghana (73.84 percent). South Africa records the lowest board composition of 61.95 percent. CEO duality is almost non-existent in South Africa with approximately 4 percent only, but just about the norm in Ghana, where approximately 64 percent of CEOs also act in the capacity of board chairs. Ghana records the highest mean with regards to institutional ownership (75.57 percent) with Kenya having the lowest mean institutional ownership of 38.09 percent. South Africa and Nigeria have mean institutional ownership of 42.43 percent and 53.02 percent, respectively. Evidently, there are differences in the corporate governance structures in the four countries and this may be attributed to differences in industrial composition, level of financial development and regulatory and legal environment in these countries.

5.2 Correlation analysis
In order to examine the possible degree of multi-collinearity among the regressors, a correlation matrix of the variables for the South African, Nigerian, Kenyan and Ghanaian samples are included in Table II. The high magnitude of the correlation coefficients between profitability and growth in respect of the Nigerian sample indicates the presence of multi-collinearity. We subsequently dropped the growth variable in the estimation of the effect of corporate governance on dividend payout. CEO duality, growth and firm size were dropped in the estimation results with respect to the Kenyan sample due to collinearity. In the case of Ghana, we dropped firm size from the estimation on the effect of corporate governance on dividend payout due to multi-collinearity.

5.3 Regression results
Table III presents the regression results on the effect of corporate governance on dividend payout. Corporate governance measures include board composition, board size, CEO duality and institutional ownership. We observe a significantly negative relationship between board composition and dividend payout in the case of Nigeria. This indicates that, Nigerian firms with greater percentage of external board members may pursue low-dividend payout policy. This could mean that such firms may be interested in retaining more profits to invest in future projects. The presence of external board members plays an important role in influencing dividend payout decision in Nigeria.   Taxation turned out to be significant among Nigerian and Kenyan firms. Consistent with theoretical stances and empirical studies, the results from the Nigerian sample reveal a statistically significant and negative association between taxation and dividend payout. Certainly, firms with high-tax rate may have difficulty paying dividend because the tax rate would affect the distributable profit and subsequently the amount of dividend to be paid. Nigeria operates a classical tax system where companies are charged corporate tax on their profit and shareholders are also charged income tax on the dividends received. This means that dividends are taxed twice, first in the form of corporate tax and second in form of income tax on shareholders dividend income. Under such circumstances, we expect shareholders to be interested in the firm paying low dividend and retaining more profit for investment, since this is expected to translate into increase in their share price. Such firms would therefore, pursue low-dividend payout policy in favour of high retention. This finding is consistent with the position of Short et al.   We observe that debt ratio has a significantly negative effect on dividend payout with respect to Nigerian and Kenyan firms. We note that firms with high-debt ratio may shy away from paying high dividend, considering the monitoring role of debt. Debt is believed to play an important role in reducing agency costs emanating from shareholder-manager conflict. Debt holders may introduce certain protective covenants to prevent firm managers from paying more dividends in order to fulfill their debt obligations as and when they fall due. This finding provides support for previous studies. Jensen (1986), Stulz (1988) and Farinha (2003) maintain that debt is likely to cause less dividend payments because of debt covenants and related restrictions that may be imposed by debt holders. Therefore, firms with high-debt ratio and implied financial risk may be restricted from paying more dividends. In the case of South Africa, the results show a positive relationship between debt ratio and dividend payout.
  Market-to-book value ratio is used as a proxy for the firmĺs growth. One would have expected a negative relationship between growth and dividend payout ratio to suggest that firms with growth or investment opportunities would pay less dividends and retain more funds in order to finance their future growth. However, the results from the South African sample reveal a positive relationship between growth and dividend. We argue that firms that exhibit high growth are very capable of maintaining high-dividend payout policy. In other words, high-growth firms in South Africa would be in a better position to pay high dividend to their shareholders than those with low growth. This finding contradicts the results of previous studies by Rozeff (1982), Lloyd et al. (1985) and Collins et al. (1996).
  Profitability appears to be an important determinant of dividend payout policy in Nigeria and Ghana. The results of this study indicate a positively significant association between profitability and dividend payout ratio among Nigerian and Ghanaian firms. This suggests that highly profitable firms are capable of declaring and paying more dividends. Profitability is considered as the primary indicator of the firmĺs capacity to

Others like Baker (1989) and DeAngelo and DeAngelo (1990) showed that, an important reason cited by firms for not paying dividends is ôpoor earningsö. They found that, a significant proportion of firms with losses over a five-year period, tend to omit their dividends entirely.
  We find in the case of only Nigeria that firm size is significantly and positively related with dividend payout. This may be indicative of the fact that, larger firms often have stable cash flow to make dividend payments. Larger firms in Nigeria might have taken advantage of all positive Net Present Value projects and as such would be in the position to pay out more dividends with free cash flows. Where large firms have exhausted future growth opportunities, it would not be prudent to retain profits for future investments. In such a situation, more dividend payments would be a better option. Also, given that shareholders would not expect increases in their share price as a result of future investments, they would then require more dividends. This finding is in line with those of prior studies.

6. Conclusion and implications
This study examined the effect of corporate governance on firmsĺ dividend payout policy in sub-Saharan Africa. We also looked at how dividend payout influences corporate governance. The regression results from model 1 revealed that a high percentage of external board members influence high-dividend payout in Kenya. We found that increasing board size is associated with high-dividend payout among Ghanaian firms. The results also revealed that institutional shareholders in South Africa and Kenya influence high-dividend payouts. With respect to Nigeria, all the corporate governance variables showed negative effects on dividends payout. In terms of the results of model 2, we found dividend payout to have a positive effect on board composition among Ghanaian firms, implying that firms with high-payout exhibit good corporate governance structures as a way of protecting the interest of their shareholders.
  Corporate governance mechanisms have been identified as important determinants of dividend payout. The essence of corporate governance is to ensure that investors receive returns on their investment. Employing more external board members by Kenyan firms is important in protecting the interest of shareholders as such external board members will push for more dividend payment to shareholders. Strengthening shareholder activism for instance in South Africa and Kenya may be useful in ensuring that equity investors are rewarded in the form of dividends. Ghanaian shareholders could insist on firms adopting larger boards up to a certain point. This means having a stronger board will check the opportunistic behaviour of management and put pressure on them to pay high dividends to shareholders. Generally with respect to South Africa, Kenya and Ghana, we note that good corporate governance structures lead to high-dividend payout, probably due to easy access to low cost of external finance. However, in the Nigerian case, the findings lead us to suggest that improving the governance structures is associated with managers investing profits in positive NPV projects in order to increase shareholder value. This means Nigerian firms may be compelled to retain more earnings to reduce cost because of the difficulty in accessing low cost of external finance. In addition to strengthening the corporate governance
