Abstract
Purpose – The purpose of this paper is to explore financial governance as practiced by NGOs in
Ghana and further examines the determinants of the financial governance structures of the NGOs. The
study specifically investigates which organizational-level characteristics exhibit any link whatsoever
with governance as it relates to budget preparation, budget execution and internal controls and budget
monitoring.
Design/methodology/approach – Using a questionnaire to conduct the survey, a cross-sectional
regression analysis was executed.
Findings – The findings of the study indicate that the most positively influential factor in explaining
an NGO’s adoption of a governance framework is its size. The other variables, organizational age and
independence, are not significant across all three financial governance proxies and when they prove
significant, the effect is negative.
Originality/value – The findings hold important policy implications and especially so for countries
that attract significant funding via relatively small-sized NGOs.
Keywords Financial governance, NGO, Organizational demographics, Size,
Non-governmental organizations, Ghana
Paper type Research paper

1. Introduction
The role of civil society organizations, also referred to as non-governmental
organizations (NGOs) or non-profits, in the quest for growth and development has
expanded significantly in recent years. This expanded role was adequately captured by
Lewis and Kanji (2009), who indicated that, NGOs have over the years come to be
recognized as important actors on the landscape of development. According to Easterly
and Rebelo (1993), growth and development discussions would be incomplete without
the mention of fiscal management, which, encapsulated on the macro level as the
sourcing for and uses of financial resources in line with development agendas, is
captured as the financial management function on the micro level. Efficient financial
management is the backbone of any entity’s growth drive. When well-executed, the
financial management function forms the bedrock for growth, development and
sustainability (Aldaba, 2002).
The importance and sensitivity of financial management to an entity’s quest for
growth has in recent times brought the issue of financial governance and accountability

governance and accountability as per financial management are not new to them. In fact,
apart from the core issues that may elicit funding from such donor entities, another very
important framework required by donors is a system of good governance and
accountability of donor funds (Kihato and Rapoo, 1999) which is generally embodied in a
comprehensive financial management system.
According to Naidoo (2003), in the case of NGOs, the need for a system of financial
governance is a very pressing issue. This need, as per literature, is predicated on the
apparent protection of NGOs from potent market-disciplining devices such as hostile
takeovers, proxy fights among others (Steinberg, 1990; Bolton and Mehran, 2006).
Another reason is the apparent lack of multiple monitoring platforms with regards to
financial resources which provides the stage for an unstructured and unorganised
access to and disbursement of donor funds which will in the long-run affect the financial
health of the NGO and its capacity to execute intended projects (Oxford Analytica, 2005).
Notwithstanding the apparent fact that financial governance is a prerequisite for the
sustained growth and financial stability of any organization, its implementation is
usually dictated by the size and political setting of the organization in question
(Goodhart, 2004). In other words, the peculiar characteristics of an organization may
dictate to a large extent how its financial governance via financial management
systems will be implemented and operationalized.
This organization-specific feature of financial management system implementation
raises a few but pertinent research questions in a developing country like Ghana that
attracts a lot of donor funds through NGOs (Hearn, 2001). These are: what is the nature
of financial governance as is practiced by NGOs in Ghana? Is the financial governance
system of NGOs influenced by any set of organizational demographics? In other words,
to what extent do characteristics like size, independence and age influence the financial
management system that may exist within an entity? To answer these questions the
study set the following objective: to explore the framework of NGO financial
management systems, as a proxy for financial governance, and identify any relevant
relationships.

2. The literature
2.1 Defining financial governance and components
Financial governance, essential to all successful organizations, can be defined as the
legitimate use of power and authority in the management of an entity’s financial
resources. Good financial governance, while being seen as a significant contributor to
sound fiscal management, effective and efficient resource use, is also seen as
underpinning transparency and accountability (African Financial Governance Status
Report, 2011). Effective financial governance systems are therefore required in the
quest to maximize the efficient use of resources, create the highest level of
transparency and accountability in an organization’s finances and to ensure long-term
economic success. Recent literature has also highlighted the importance of financial
governance via sound financial management systems to service delivery, poverty
reduction and the achievement of the millennium development goals (Pretorius and
Pretorius, 2008).

With regards to best practices in finance and governance, the UK Department for
Social Development (2005) intimates that governance is not about doing but it is about
ensuring that things are done. By inference, the deployment of an efficient system that
is expected to ensure that things are done can be implied as governance. An AfDB
Group (2006) guideline for financial management and financial analysis of projects
supports this assertion by also indicating that efficient deployment of appropriate
modern financial management systems can be construed as representing sound
financial management and good governance.
In pursuance of such measures of governance via financial management systems,
some schools of thought have identified several criteria that the financial management
systems of NGOs and other donor funded projects must meet in pursuance of financial
governance. One such major classification is proposed by Shizhen (2005) which includes
financial reporting, accounting records and source documentation, internal control,
budget control, cost allowance and cash management and compliance frameworks.
On the same issue of components of a governance system, the African Financial
Governance Status Report (2011) indicates that effective financial governance can also
be predicated on effective systems and structures (Wixley and Everingham, 2005), key
among these: budget preparation, budget execution, internal control and accounting,
reporting, auditing and monitoring systems (Ott and Bajo, 2001). According to the
report, without relevant information on these systems, users of public funds cannot be
held to account, thereby opening the door for wasteful spending and possible corrupt
practices. In a similar vein, a number of funding agencies have also developed financial
governance assessment frameworks along the areas of the mode of budget planning,
execution, internal control and monitoring required of funded projects (AfDB Group,
2006) and thereby inferring the level of governance as practised by an institution based
on the presence of such predefined systems.
2.1.1 Budget preparation. Defined generally as the process of quantitatively
expressing an organization’s strategy or the financial perspective of what an
organization seeks to achieve over a specific period of time (Horngren et al., 2006), the
budget preparation process is at the heart of the financial management function. Budgets
or financial plans, which result from the budget preparation process, typically form the
basis on which subsequent performance may be assessed. Gaist (2009) indicates that by
budgeting and producing periodic financial reports, NGOs are able to constantly
monitor their revenue and costs to ensure that funding streams sustain operations.
In order to align financial planning and budget preparation to strategic intent of
organizations, there is usually a need for certain features to be present with regards to
budget preparation. Key among these is comprehensiveness (Umapathy, 1987).
Put simply, the budget must include sources and uses of funds for all aspects of
operations and maintain a true and holistic reflection of plans for the budget period.
Moreover, budgeting requires significant input from fiscal staff as well as clearly
defined duties, responsibilities, lines of supervision and limits of authority for all
(Brownell, 1982). These features, according to Ahmad et al. (2003), Joshi et al. (2003)
and Joshi and Com (1997), in turn make the budget the performance monitoring tool for
use by the organization. On the issue of budget transparency, which also make for
successful budgeting, Poterba and von Hagen (1999) stipulate that budgets can only be
considered transparent if they are easily accessible to participants involved in the
decision-making process and represent consolidated information.

In terms of empirics, a number of studies have sought to find a link between the
planning phase and organizational performance. Thune and House (1970), for example,
found that formal long-range planning actually does pay off in the way of improved
performance. Phillips (1996) and Hansen and Van der Stede (2004) also corroborate this
finding by documenting that the formality, thoroughness, sophistication and
participation that characterizes long-range planning exhibit a positive relationship
with organizational performance. In general and from these studies, it can be inferred
that governance in the use of organizational resources, which ultimately flows from the
planning process contributes to the positive improvement in performance.
2.1.2 Budget execution and internal control. Budget execution is the phase where the
organisation’s resources are used in implementing its activities. In general, the main
focus of budget execution is compliance with budgetary authorizations and this is
usually governed by an internal control system. According to Mensah et al. (2003), weak
internal control systems as per an organization’s execution framework create incentives
for company buyers to make side deals with vendors and or make side-payments to
influence contracts. In a bid to ensure tight internal control systems and ultimately
governance, certain provisions are usually provided for. These provisions usually
revolve around roles of the board of directors, payments and financial accounting as it
relates to budget execution.
Efficient budget execution, according to Tommasi (2007), is budgets implemented in
line with the pre-set rules and authorizations of the implementing entity, with changes
or adaptations being made by the appropriate authority in the event of changes in
baseline assumptions or conditions. This setup ensures that procurement and
management of procured goods and services are done efficiently and also prevents risk
and abuse to the implementing organization and its resources. Lambert (1990) further
augments this list by indicating other factors that are key to successful budget
execution: written authorization and evidence in the giving and receipt of funds as well
as timeliness and accuracy of financial reports.
On the whole, it seems there is empirical evidence in the literature to support the
assertion that effective internal control systems in the budget execution process have
strong links with governance. One study that documents such a relationship is by
Elbannan (2009) using US firms. He finds that internal control quality is positively
related to good governance. Another study that supports that relationship is by Jensen
(1993) who reports that the effectiveness of internal control is positively related to the
efficiency of board governance. In general, it seems safe to infer that the strength of
internal control with regard to project execution framework is positively related to
overall good governance.
2.1.3 Monitoring and evaluation. Monitoring and evaluation is the feedback
mechanism within a management framework. It has as its main objective the
improvement in the management of programmes, projects and supporting activities and
ensuring the optimal use of funds and other resources while providing a platform to
learn from experience so as to improve the relevance, methods and outcomes of
cooperative programmes (Khawaja, 2011). According to Tirole (2006), monitoring is
very critical to the governance framework. So critical is it that general talk on
governance ultimately seems to imply the monitoring of management to deliver on the
interests and desires of relevant project to stakeholders. Gillan et al. (2003) further this
argument by opining that optimal monitoring implies strong governance. In fact

Mackay (2007) refers to monitoring as the centre-piece of the governance system.
He goes on to indicate that monitoring and evaluation contribute to sound governance

The components of monitoring as per Shapiro (undated) include the establishment
of indicators of efficiency, effectiveness and impact; setting up of systems to collect
information relating to these indicators; collecting and recording the information;
analysing the information; and last but not least using the information to inform
day-to-day management. In an empirical study of the determinants of the level of
governance using firm-level data for 624 US firms, Khanchel (2007), reports that firms
with effective monitoring systems score best on governance. It can be said that the
general literature gives reason to classify effective monitoring as a core requisite for
effective governance.
2.2 Organizational demographics and governance structures
While there is evidence to support the assertion that adoption of good governance
can positively affect an organization’s performance (Abor and Biekpe, 2007; Abor
and Adjasi, 2007 among others), other researchers also indicate that the extent
of adoption of the tenets of good governance as prescribed by the codes of best
practices may be influenced by organizational demographics, key being size.
Gallery et al. (2011), for example, alerted that given that costs of adoption of certain
governance principles for smaller firms far outweigh the benefits derived, there was a
need to reassess most governance frameworks with a view to customizing them to
smaller entities.
This alert followed a study by Gallery et al. (2011) to assess the adoption of
governance codes among small firms. They found that the size of the organization and
cost of adoption played key roles in influencing the rates of adoption of governance
codes. In another finding by Moorhead (2007) on internal controls and governance, he
also finds evidence to support the notion that larger NGOs compared to smaller NGOs
have more resources to put in place stronger internal control and governance
frameworks. In general, it seems that organizational size, which seems to have a
significant bearing on capacity, both human and financial, is a key influence in the
level of governance within an organization.
Another organizational characteristic that has received some attention in the
governance literature is age. According to Loderer and Waelchli (2011), as organizations
age, their governance structures deteriorate because the complexity of expansion makes
for weaker monitoring and ultimately poorer governance. Gillian et al. (2007) also hold
the view that older firms are likely to have in place anti-takeover mechanisms which
ultimately make for lower governance. Another school of thought is of the view that as a
firm grows it gets better, implying that governance improves as firms age. Kyereboah
and Biekpe (2007) found negative relationships between firm age and the size of
the board and the proportion of outside directors. By inference from best practice, the
negative relationship with outside directors imply poor governance while the negative
relationship between firm age and board size can be construed as ambiguous since the
literature has not yet reached a consensus on which is best for good governance in terms
of large or small board. Given the two conflicting views, the relationship between NGO
age and level of governance is left open.

3. Methodology
3.1 Research design and sampling
The study adopted a mostly descriptive research design in assessing the status of
financial governance in the sample under study and a regression analysis to test the
organizational drivers of the financial governance setup. This approach was
considered most appropriate since the purpose of the study was to document the
systems as is and also model possibility of cause and effect.
The population for the study is the universe of NGOs in Ghana. For reasons of
access, however, the study was limited to NGOs based in the Greater Accra Region.
Due to the fact that it is not feasible to sample the entire network, a total of 60 NGOs
were sampled for the study. For the exploratory purpose of this study, a survey
approach was chosen, using face-to-face contact in filling the data collection
instrument. The major drawback envisaged with the inability to draw a larger sample
is the fact that observed behaviour from the sample group may not necessarily reflect
that of the entire population but may still offer useful constructs from the exploratory
perspective.

3.2 Data type, sources and collection
The data was collected mainly using the survey approach and the type of data
collected was cross-sectional. The instrument for data collection, a structured
questionnaire, was developed from reviewing the literature on financial governance.
The questionnaire was modelled along the basic framework of best practices for
financial governance as it applies to NGOs/funded projects. This framework was
chosen because it provides a reference point from which deviations from or otherwise
as regards financial governance systems could be compared.
Categorization of observance with regards to the required frameworks ranged from
“not observed” through to “fully observed”. The questions were fashioned to elicit
governance information on budget planning, budget implementation and internal
controls and budget evaluation and control. The study sought to assess the compliance
of the NGOs as regards best practices of governance in financial management. For the
purposes of the study, the questionnaires were administered interview-style and filled
out by the interviewer. This was to reduce the incidence of inappropriate responses or
non-response due to non-clarity. Senior management and officials responsible for the
financial decisions of the NGOs were the respondent group used. The response
categorization followed that applied in the Report on Observance of Codes by the
World Bank, where a requirement being fully observed means that all essential criteria
are met without significant deficiencies. Largely observed means only minor
shortcomings are observed, which do not raise questions about the organization’s
ability and intent to achieve full observance in the short term. Partially observed meant
that while the main framework complies with the principle, practices and enforcement
diverge. Materially not observed meant that, despite progress, shortcomings were
sufficient to raise doubts about the organization’s ability to achieve observance. Not
observed means no substantive progress toward observance had been achieved.

4. Discussion
This section presents the analysis and discussion of study findings. This section deals
with the summary statistics of the variables under study, the correlation matrix
between the variables and the regression output.
4.1 Nature of NGO characteristics and financial governance
On the whole, 42 out the targeted 60 NGOs approached over the study period,
July-October, 2011, were willing to participate in the survey. This represents
approximately a 70 per cent response rate and forms the basis of reported findings. From
the summary statistics, the sampled NGOs were aged between 2 and 25 years with the
average age being 9.8 years and in terms of size, employ about 10 permanent staff with
the maximum employment strength being 20 and a minimum of 3. On the question of
full-time or permanent staff managing the finance function, the statistics indicate an
average of 2.6 employees with some NGOs having no full-time worker on the finance
function while some had as many as 4. In terms of independence, which sought to
measure whether or not the sampled NGOs were offshoots of much larger unilateral
organisations or networks and therefore a proxy for the amount of influence that may
flow from being associated with larger networks, the responses indicate that the NGOs
sampled were more of independent entities, not necessarily branches of larger/global
organizations.
In the area of financial governance, the budgetary framework, which sought to
gauge the robustness of systems that guide the budget preparation process, the
average score was 3 out of 5 with a minimum of 0.9091 and a maximum of 4.55.
By implication the average non-profit organization in Ghana works with a budget
preparation framework but enforcing the budget is not as expected. The minimum
score of 0.9091 out of 5 indicated that a portion of the sample have no framework
whatsoever to work with. The maximum score of 4.5 which implies full observance,
however, also indicates that there are NGOs within the system that have budgetary
frameworks which serve as their planning and monitoring tool thereby implying that

Regression analysis. To answer the main problem of the research, the study sought to
investigate the possible drivers. In this regard, the budget preparation; execution and
internal control and monitoring scores were regressed on NGO age, size (employee base,
see Dalbor et al. (2004)) and independence, where independence sought to measure
external influences and takes on the value of 1 for NGOs that are stand-alone entities
with own-defined mission and 2 when NGOs that are offshoots larger organization and
therefore bound by the rules of the parent NGO. For the purposes of standardization,
NGO age and employee base were logged for the regression analysis. The results, see
Table III, indicate that independence is negatively significant in explaining the internal
control framework of NGOs but does not, however, affect the budget preparation and
monitoring frameworks. In other words, an NGO’s financial governance framework is
not necessarily influenced by being affiliated to or mission-dependent on a larger
over-arching body and when that relationship proves significant, it is only important in
explaining its execution and internal control framework and the effect is negative.
Size is the only variable that is significantly and positively related to the budget
preparation, execution and internal control and monitoring frameworks, confirming
the finding by Gallery et al. (2011) and Moorhead (2007) that larger organizations have
more resources at their disposal to implement governance systems compared to
smaller ones and also because the benefits derived from implementing governance
systems far outweigh the costs of implementing such. It is therefore expected that as an
NGO gets larger in size, their financial governance systems will improve. In other
words, scores that seem to indicate seemingly low observance levels with regards to
codes of financial governance may only be reflecting a lack of resources to implement
governance structures as opposed to refusal to comply with codes of best practices.

4.3 Conclusion and policy implications
As per the findings of the study, it can be posited that NGOs only partially observe
best practices as they relate to financial governance and this is mostly explained by the
size of the NGO. This is not surprising, as the literature seems to point to the fact that,
all else equal, larger organizations are more likely to implement full-scale governance
systems because they benefit from increased resources and higher benefit-to-cost
ratios. Age on the other hand seems to be negatively related to financial governance
measures. The implication may be that once an NGO has carved a reputation for itself
overtime, it is likely to get lax with its governance structures. In terms of policy
making, it presents the need to review governance codes with a view to making
provisions for size and thereby accurately capture and differentiate inability from
refusal to comply. Also, there is the need to ensure that older organizations do not
necessarily enjoy the glories of past achievements but instead must be constantly
scrutinized to ensure compliance with codes of best practices. A lack of proper systems
may ultimately lead to the early death of otherwise very viable projects while efficient
financial management and governance, on the other hand, will ensure that the funds
are put to the right use and benefits accrue to all stakeholders. Policy moves such as
those indicated above will ensure that only capable and accountable NGOs play their
role within the third sector. While the study helps to fill the research gap on
governance issues as regards NGOs, the literature could be further enriched with
studies probing further into the governance frameworks of NGOs, issues of financial
sustainability among others.
