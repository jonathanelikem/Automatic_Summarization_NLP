Purpose – As a result of the lack of reliable data, systematic research that informs public policy
and practice from Ghana seems to be lacking. Although the World Bank’s survey concerning
Ghana’s manufacturing sub-sector has been available for some time now, yet little is known as regards
firm level research and so policy formulation has been affected exceedingly. The purpose of this
paper is to identify the strategic factors which influence firm performance from Ghana (an emerging
economy).
Design/methodology/approach – This study employs the World Bank data set relating to a panel
of firms within the Ghanaian manufacturing sector from 1991-2002.
Findings – By pooling the data and setting OLS regression, the results of the study indicate that joint
venture (JVs) ownership predicts significant performance compared with similar counterparts that are
wholly indigenously owned. Besides, firm size, workforce productivity and the location where a firm
operates are strategic drivers of firm performance in a significant way.
Originality/value – The paper shows that alliances with foreign businesses by indigenous firms
from Ghana boosts the capacity of the local firms with regard to human and financial capital
resources, as well as linking them to international supply and distribution networks that greatly
enhance their performance.
Keywords Ghana, Manufacturing industries, Organizational performance, Emerging economy,

Introduction
Economies of emerging markets (especially those in sub-Saharan Africa) have lagged
behind their counterparts in the developed countries (e.g. the USA, the UK, and
Europe). While, it has recently been argued in the literature that the problem is partly
caused by low skill levels and lack of technological capabilities among firms from
emerging economies, it is also contended that firm level research, which would
facilitate efficient and effective resource mobilisation, allocation and utilisation, as well
as encourage firm growth and socio-economic transformation, is lacking among these
economies (Graner and Isaksson, 2007; Sousa et al., 2003).
Firm level research that links public policy to practice is argued to be one of the
preconditions for successful socio-economic development of every economy (Grunberg,
2004; Cocca and Marco, 2010). Yet there is a controversy about access to credible data

strategic factors concerns any issues with regard gaining competitive advantage in the
market place. Following Pearce and Robinson, strategic factors are usually determined
and/or identified by the top management because they differ from operational issues
which are directed by middle level and/or first line managers and because they focus
on the day-to-day function of a business at shop floor. However, the concept is
stretched to encompass several themes. For example, Finlay (2000) argues that what
constitutes strategic factors depends on what is important to top management at a
given time, based on the kind of the objective a firm aims to achieve. Following Finlay,
there can be: strategic goal; strategic assets; strategic business unit; and strategic
thinking. Among these themes, Finlay maintained that strategic factors, be they assets
and/or resources, possess distinctive characteristics from other assets and so are used
as the cornerstone for market-place competitive advantage over competitors.
Systematically identifying the strategic factors that significantly influence firm
performance from Ghana offers many benefits. The reason for this is because by such
activity firms are placed in a better position to manage the key strategic factors for their
survival. By unravelling the strategic factors, firms are in the position to know the kind
of resources to mobilise for success because not all resources will be considered as
strategic. Through identifying the strategic performance factors, a firm is in the position
to know where to channel its skill training activity in order to compete effectively and
meet customer expectations. The identification of the strategic performance factors
means these factors can be used as core competencies to exploit a critical success factor
for industry advantage over rivals. Overall, given the fact that firms from an emerging
economy such as Ghana are disadvantaged compared with their counterparts from the
developed countries (e.g. the USA, the UK and Europe), undertaking such exercise will
strengthen the local firm to compete effective both home and abroad.
In the light of the argument above, the main objective therefore, of this study is to
examine the strategic factors that determine the performance of firms from Ghana. The
reason for this is that while this line of research has served as a good foundation for
public policy and practice in the developed economies, less is known about the Ghanaian
context. While it seems therefore, reasonable to commend extant empirical studies in this
line of research, the question that still requires an answer is: how do the extant findings
from developed economies compare and/or contrast with evidence from Ghana?
To address this question and in order to offer a plausible pursuit of the objective of the
paper, the remainder of the paper is structured as follows. Second section presents
conceptual background, empirical literature and hypotheses development. Third section
presents description of the data, estimating procedure and presentation of results.
Finally, the discussion, conclusion and implications of the study with regard to public
policy, practice and theory are presented.
Conceptual background and hypotheses development
Firm age and firm performance
It is plausible to argue that older firms will perform better compared with younger
firms. A number of explanations have been put forward to advance this argument.
A popular theme is the liability of newness argument originally advanced by
Stinchcombe (1965). Stinchcombe (1965) argued that new and/or younger firms face
greater difficulty and risk of failure. Among the reasons Stinchcombe offered to
support the claim is that creation of new firms accompanies new roles that need to be

Downloaded by University of Ghana At 05:34 02 February 2016 (PT)

learnt, but the young firms lack the standard routine to address challenges and also
lack the reliable network ties. Following, Stinchcombe, all this is because young firms
are constrained by the disadvantage of being new. For instance, the lack of reliable
network ties on the part of new firms implies that young firms have difficulties
accessing business services (e.g. loan finance and supplier credit) because they lack of
credible track record. Another argument is that, even where some financers will be
prepared to meet the needs of young firms, their liability of newness will be more likely
to cause them to encounter the problem of information asymmetry. From a finance
viewpoint, asymmetric information means that whilst a service for a particular need
may be available for all (e.g. the provision of a loan finance and supplier credit), a firm’s
inability to obtain a reliable information about the provider of the service in question
because it is new may cause small firms to meet disappointment from the borrowing
and the lending markets (Berger and Udell, 1995).
Some empirical evidence supports the liability of newness theory; for instance,
Acquaah and Eshun (2010) failed to support the hypothesis that the positive impact of
managerial networking relationships with political leaders, bureaucratic officials and
community leaders on performance will be stronger for younger firms than for older
firms. Besides, Leonidou (2000) contends that a newly-established firm usually
experiences much difficulty in dealing with industry barriers, which limits its
performance in the short-term. Carroll (1983), using 52 different data sets, found that
organisational death rate declines with increasing age. Moreover, applying data on US
firms, Nucci (1999) found that firm dissolution declines with increasing age, regardless
of industry, size grouping or region. It has also been established that long-established
firms have better access to debt finance and social capital resources, due to their long
track record, compared to newly-established and young firms without such a lengthy
track record, regardless of how forward looking they might appear in the eyes of
stakeholders (Hite and Hesterly, 2001; Bitler et al., 2001; Nguyen and Rose, 2009). As a
result, it is conceivable to argue that younger firms will incur liabilities of newness,
consisting of both internal processes of coordination, role definition, developing trust
and loyalty among employees, as well as external obstacles such as resource
acquisition and stabilisation of relationships with customers and suppliers. These
problems will significantly affect new and younger firms’ performance relative to their
older and long-established counterparts.
Whilst the liability of newness theory coupled with the empirical evidence appears
sound, a rival argument has emerged in recent times. According to McDougall et al.
(1994) and Balabanis and Katsikea (2003), it is argued that because of the influence of
globalisation phenomenon through improvement in international transportation and
information technology, the argument behind the liability of newness theory appears
difficult to constrain the performance of younger firms compared with older firms.
Some of the reasons to the argument are that with easy access to knowledge, for
example via the internet, there cannot be much difference between younger and older
firms with regard to their stock of knowledge. Implicitly, what younger firms should
take years to learn can be shortened because of advancement in transportation,
information and communication technology based on the globalisation process. This
argument supports the line of research which contends that older firms are tempted to
relax because of inertia, as well the bureaucracies that accompany age. Acquaah and
Eshun (2010) found that the positive impact of managerial networking relationships

Firm size and firm performance
Firm size affects firm performance in many ways because large firms are said to have
different skills and capabilities compared with their counterparts smaller firms. Given
the composition of large firms, because they consist of different skills and capabilities
they are better able to utilize economies of scale and scope which offer them
performance advantages compared to small firms. Unlike smaller firms, large firms are
more likely to invest in state-of-the technologies and other essential equipment because
they have the resources to acquire them. Besides, because of their huge resource
capacity compared with small firms, large firms are able to execute plans in the most
efficient and effective way, better than smaller firms. It is further argued that large
firms, because of their high resource capacity, are more likely to be risk tolerant and so
they are better able to absorb competitive pressure and combat the threat of demise in
relation to the responses from managers of smaller firms.
Therefore, lack of adequate and appropriate size will constitute negative
consequences for a firm because small-sized firms, for example, may have difficulties
with regard to competing for skilled labour with large organisations that offer better
wages and more attractive benefits to employees. So because small firms are less likely
to attract better qualified workforce following their inability to remunerate a qualified
workforce, their performance levels are likely to be affected relative to their counterparts’
large firms. Large-sized firms are seen as wielding more power in bargaining with
suppliers and having the necessary resources to maintain good customer relationships.
Empirically, Abdurahman et al. (2003) confirmed the claim of a positive association
between the size of a firm and its performance. Acquaah (2005) also confirmed that the
number of employees is significant and positively related to the emphasis a firm places
on efficiency and cost reduction. Overall, large firms will tend to perform better than
smaller firms.
However, definition of firm size with regard to academic sources (Calof, 1993; Hall
and Cook, 2009) and official sources (e.g. the USA – Hatten, 1997; the UK – Bolton,
1971; Storey, 1994; European Commission – European Commission, 1996) varies. The
main groupings adopted in such definitions are: number of full-time employees; the
money value of sales volume; and money value of total assets. Consistent with Obben
and Magagula (2003) and Hall and Cook (2009), the total number of full-time employees
is applied in this study because this appears to be common practice among academic
sources. Following these lines of argument based on the wider array of skills at the
disposal of a large firm, its performance will be better than that of a smaller concern.

Productivity and firm performance
Productivity is one of the crucial variables in the management of firm performance.
The reason for this is that productivity impacts positively on cost and eventually on a
firm’s performance. Productivity advantages secure a competitive advantage for a firm
amongst its competitors. So, the overall efficiency of a firm cannot be improved if
improvement of workforce productivity is relegated to the background. A useful
explanation is that a high level of workforce productivity, demonstrated, for example,
in low unit cost of production, coupled with production of greater quantities of better
quality products, will lead to higher output and on performance. Consistent with this
assumption, using a sample of firms from Australia (from 1995 to 2005), Stierwald
(2009) found a positive association between productivity and the performance of a firm.
Just as there is considerable variability in definitions of firm size, the definition of
what constitutes productivity also varies. For instance, Fisher (1990) views
productivity as the value of total income as a ratio of cost. Along the lines of Fisher
(1990), a common and, indeed, one of the most widely applied definitions is the ratio of
output over inputs (Hill, 1993; Bernolak, 1996). As a result, in this study, productivity is
operationalised as the ratio of output over the total number of employees: that is,
output per employee. Therefore, in line with the assumption that improvement in
productivity improves performance, the relationship between workforce productivity
and firm performance is hypothesised as follows:
H3. There will be a positive relationship between workforce productivity and firm
performance.
Firm ownership structure and firm performance
One of the strategic variables that drives performance of a firm is its ownership
structure. A firm’s ownership structure sets the path in terms of its product line, the
choice of competitive arena, as well as the overall strategy for achieving its mission
and purpose. In addition, a firm’s ownership structure determines the size of its assets
(tangible and intangible), as well as its level of efficiency and effectiveness. Besides,
ownership structure sets the directions and the governance mechanisms within which
a firm operates. Researchers have explored different dimensions of ownership
structure and their impact on the behaviour of firms, including family ownership
versus corporate ownership and their effect on firm performance (Fernández and Nieto,
2006). Others have examined the effects of independent ownership (e.g. managed by
the owner(s)), subsidiary ownership (e.g. corporate ownership) (Hall and Tu, 2003) and
public ownership (public limited liability) versus private ownership (private limited
liability) on firm performance ( Javalgi et al., 2000).
In addition, there has been examination of the different institutional investors and
their influence on a firm’s scope of activities (Fernández and Nieto, 2006). However, in
recent times, one of the elements of ownership structure that has increasingly attracted
researchers’ attention is the joint venture (JVs) ownership (i.e. partly foreign and partly

The motivation to extend this line of research is because a key feature of the

Unlike a wholly domestic firm, it is argued that JVs build the capacity of local firms
in the areas of new technology, physical capital, management expertise, international
market knowledge and global distribution network opportunities, which exerts
significant influence on the performance of a firm (Zhang and Felmingham, 2001). For
instance, drawing from Acquaah and Eshun (2010), it is argued that foreign-domestic
JVs benefit from access to resources and capabilities owned by their more resource-rich
foreign partners consisting of advanced managerial skills, technical know-how, and
marketing capabilities. Again Acquaah and Eshun contend that unlike wholly
domestic owned firms, foreign-domestic JVs possess established networking
relationships with other organisations and government politicians and bureaucrats
which influence their performance in a positive way.
Using an Italian sample, Cerrato and Piva (2007) found that association of SMEs in
Italy with foreign partners (JVs) positively impacted on their behaviour and
performance. Greenaway et al. (2010), using data from China, confirmed the positive
significant relationship between foreign owned firms ( JVs) compared to the
performance of the local firms. Chari et al. (2009) conducted a similar study based
on associations between US firms and emerging market firms. In this study, the
researchers confirmed the hypothesis that the alliance of the US firms with JVs from
emerging markets impacted positively on profitability. Furthermore, using an African
sample, Ramachandran and Shah (1998) also supported the hypothesis under
discussion. From the foregoing argument, unlike the wholly owned companies,
foreign-domestic JVs can be argued to hold an advantageous strategic position, given
the advantages they enjoy. In the light of this the following hypothesis is posed:
H4. JVs between foreign and locally owned firms will perform better than those
that are wholly locally-owned.
Location and firm performance
Drawing from the agglomeration literature (Appold, 1995), the effect of business
location will affect the performance of a firm. Among business environment’s factors,
it is argued that the location of a firm appears crucial, but it has been given less
research attention (Kala and Guanghua, 2010; Minai and Lucky, 2011). Kala and

Downloaded by University of Ghana At 05:34 02 February 2016 (PT)

Guanghua (2010) defined location of a firm as the choice of place where a firm is
located. According to these authors, firm location could be urban, rural centre,
metropolis and/or a municipality. This definition concurs with Esteban et al. (2010)
who also maintained that the location of a firm is either the rural and/or the urban
area where the firm operates. However, an added dimension to the foregone
conceptualizations is that the location of a firm could also be either a capital city of a
country or any of its regional and/or provincial capitals (Ramadan, 2011). Some of the
theoretical arguments about the influence of firm location on its performance are as
follows. It is argued that firms derived various opportunities from a location where
other firms cluster; here regardless of whether or not the location is a large city, the
cluster will confer positive externalities on other firms.
The reason for the above is because clustered firms are likely to share high levels of
mutual proximity, which makes it relatively easy for the firms to acquire, process, and
exploit information to their advantage, thereby impacting positively on their
performance. Firm cluster promotes the formation of beneficial business alliances,
which attracts other subsidiary firms and/or key service providers to the location and
as a result the productivity and the performance of the clusters will be impacted in a
positive way. Firm concentration promotes the growth and the development of
idiosyncratic business cultures, thereby enhancing inter-firm interaction and mutual
understanding. However, in recent times, a theme within the agglomeration literature
that is attracting research attention is the effect of metropolitan location and its effects
on firm performance (Ramadan, 2011). Implicitly, metropolitan areas and/or national
capitals are crucial to politicians and businesses alike because of the strategic position
of such locations. Metropolitan areas and/or national capitals are associated with
transaction cost savings which benefit firms that are located in such area. Samples of
such transaction cost savings include, but are not limited to: information search cost;
supply-chain cost; and labour pooling cost savings. Fan and Scott (2003) argue that
national capitals and/or metropolitans areas represent dense localization firms and
create large concentration of job seekers and job vacancies. According to the authors,
metropolitan centres attract key business service providers such as consultancy
companies, law firms and financial institutions. As a result, firms that find themselves
in these locations are more likely to be efficient and effective and will tend to perform
more than their counterparts in the provincial areas. Besides, firms located at national
capitals are likely to enjoy better infrastructure facilities, better network with policy
makers coupled with powerful competition among neighbouring industrial actors;
these firms will constantly undergo renewal and innovation and perform better. They
are also likely to enjoy large customer bases because most national capitals and
metropolitan areas are densely populated.
Empirically, Zahra et al. (1997) found significant positive association between
location at a metropolitan centre and the performance of a firm. Using an Australian
sample, Freeman et al. (2010) supported the hypothesis between metropolitan location
and firm performance. Mariotti and Piscitello (2001) confirmed that metropolitan
infrastructure and resource availability were strong determinants of firm performance.
Furthermore, In Ghana, Accra is the capital city of the nation, but because Ghana is a
relatively small emerging economy, key activities are concentrated in Accra. The major
activities that cluster in Accra, include, but not limited to, better communication
network, good infrastructure, intercity transportation system, different cluster of firms

Industry type and firm performance
Drawing on the industrial economics literature, it can be argued that industry
membership influence firm performance (Bain, 1956; Porter, 1980). Bain (1956)
contends that industry’s characteristics such as entry barriers, firm concentration,
product differentiation, market growth rate and/or customer base drive firm behaviour
which in turn determine firm performance. Researchers (Sutcliffe and Huber, 1998;
Slevin and Covin, 1997) share the positive influence from the nature of an industry on
the performance of a firm. For profit seeking organisations, performance measures
(e.g. profit before interest and tax, return on capital employed (ROE), return on asset
(ROA)) are pertinent to each of such firms and so the decision on which competitive
arena to enter is linked strongly to these performance indicators. As a result, industry
entry choice remains one of the major strategic decisions for new and potential
businesses, including firms that are confronted with a need for diversification
(e.g. diversification into unrelated industries). It was therefore, not surprising that
Gomes-Casseres (1997) submitted that the success of firms, especially SMEs, depended
partly on their capacity to choose their competitive arena (industry) “strategically”.
Entry into a particular industry involves strategic decisions and choices that have far
reaching implications for the performance, growth and survival of a business. Whilst it
was argued that despite the importance of industry and firm performance, little
research evidence is known in the strategic management research field (McGahan and
Porter, 1997); yet, Short et al. (2007) maintained that in recent times, a key development
within the strategic management field concerns the inclusion of an open system
approach that studies strategic processes outside the influence of a single firm’s
internal capacity allowing for stronger empirical examinations and generations
(Hoskisson et al., 1999).
In line with this new development in the strategy field, Short et al. (2007) have
established a significant relationship between firm industry and firm performance.
Houthoofd et al. (2010), using a Belgian sample, supported the hypothesis that firm
performance varies significantly according to the nature of the industry. Further,
Short et al. (2009), using 7,256 young Swedish firms over a period of five years
maintained that though the main effect of firm performance relates to firm effect, the
industry effect cannot be disregarded. Sidhu et al. (2000), analysing the performance of
56 firms in heterogeneous industries, found that firm performance was industry specific.
Type of industry in this study concerns whether a firm operates in one industry and
not the other (e.g. operating either in the wood sub-sector or otherwise) and the
influence from a specific industry on firms performance. The reason for this is because
the type of industry determines its characteristic (e.g. entry barriers, competition, and
innovation). In Ghana, firms are found in different industries and/or sectors
(e.g. garment and textiles, bakery, metal, machine) therefore drawing from the
motivation regarding industry effect and firm performance, the study examines the

Measurement of variables
Depended variable – firm performance. The theoretical and empirical position with
regard to the key determinants of organisational performance is one of the top lines of
debate in strategic management. In the strategic management field, different researchers
operationalise firm performance differently. For instance, Chew (1988) views
performance in terms of units of outputs and the amount of inputs used to produce
them. McGuire et al. (1988) use stock market price of a firm to define firm performance.
Notwithstanding, in the strategic management field, accounting measures (e.g. the
money value of operating profit, ROE, and ROA) are widely used (Qian and Li, 2002;
Aras et al., 2010). The reason for this is because accounting measures are more sensitive
to the capital structure of a firm. In most emerging economies (e.g. Ghana) listed firms
are few and so applying accounting measures to measure performance appears more
representative than, for example, using stock price as a surrogate. In this study,
accounting measure (the value of operating profit) acts as the proxy for performance.
As accounting measures are widely applied in the field, using accounting measure in this
study will enable the study to be compared to a wide array of extant studies.

Discussion
As discussed in the empirical literature, one would have expected that firms without
liability of newness because of their extensive industry experience would perform
better than those with less experience. The reason for this is that intuitively, experience
can be best associated with higher firm performance. Based on the regression results in
Table III, H1, that concerns the effect of firm age on performance, is not supported. The
relationship was not significant. Although in this study the association is not
statistically significant, nevertheless, it supports a growing line of research which
contends that, as with living organisms (e.g. human beings), as firms grow older they
lose their edge to compete strategically (Agarwal and Gort, 1996, 2002; Chun et al.,
2008). Some of the arguments presented to support this claim are that older firms tend
to be more associated with structure inertia and process rigidities than younger firms
(Hannan and Freeman, 1984; Leonard-Barton, 1992). According to these research
findings, firms’ rigidities and inertias weaken their capacity to foresee key signals from
the operating arena and to respond by developing either offensive or defensive
strategy. The reason is that as firms grow they lose the will to remain flexible or to
continue to evolve. The unwillingness to change and lessening in flexibility, along with
a consequent limitation of scope for improvement, may lead to a decrease in productive
efficiency, decline in market share and in margins among older firms compared to new
and younger ones, as was found by Agarwal and Gort (2002) and Chun et al. (2008).

H2 of the study is fully supported, implying that as firms grow in workforce size
from micro firms into small and medium-sized concerns they perform better. Implicitly,
compared with the micro firm, the small and the medium-sized firm will perform better.
The statistically significant relationship between size and performance implies that a
large firm, because of the diversity of skills sets within its workforce, will perform
better than a firm with a very small capacity. A related explanation is that larger firms
will find it easier to raise capital finance, mobilise other resources to perform better
than smaller companies. It is also suggested that larger firms can compete better for
skilled workforce, manage risk and maintain competitive bargaining power of
customers and suppliers better compared to small businesses, and will therefore
perform better. As indicated in the empirical literature, large firms usually possess

In addition, H3 is fully supported. Implicitly, productivity improvement that is linked
to low operating cost advantages enables firms to outperform their competitors. This
confirms similar study in Australia (Stierwald, 2009). H4 is also supported. Extant
literature argues that foreign partners are better endowed with human capital resources,
capital finance, physical capital and managerial acumen. In addition, foreign partners
possess better international networking links (e.g. networking with international
distributors and suppliers). As a result, the association of foreign firms with emerging
market businesses will augment the capacity of the indigenous firms, and those firms
will perform better than others that are wholly locally owned. The findings are in line
with Acquaah and Eshun (2010) who maintained that foreign-domestic JVs posses many
advantages because of access to resources and capabilities owned by their more
resource-rich foreign partners consisting of advanced managerial skills, technical
know-how, and marketing capabilities. This result again supports an Italian study by
Cerrato and Piva (2007) who found evidence to suggest that association of SMEs in Italy
with foreign partners (JVs) positively impacts on their behaviour and performance. It also
confirms a study from China (Greenaway et al., 2010), the US study (Chari et al., 2009)
as well as Ramachandran and Shah (1998) who used an African sample and supported
the hypothesis under discussion.
H5 is also supported. The results indicate that firms located in Accra (the capital of
Ghana) will perform significantly positive compared to firms located in Kumasi (one of
the province in Ghana). The finding supports the argument that firms sited in capital
cities will be advantaged due to their access to benefits such as financers, services of
government ministries, departments and agencies, and competition that sparks positive
changes and innovation. Taken in tandem, enterprises operating in capital cities perform
better than similar firms operating in the rural and regional centres, especially
manufacturing concerns. Theoretically, the finding supports the agglomeration literature
(Appold, 1995). It also confirms an Australian study (Freeman et al., 2010). Other studies
which the finding supports include but are not limited to: Zahra et al. (1997), Mariotti and
Piscitello (2001).
In line with the industrial economics literature (Bain, 1956), H6 is supported. Using
the textile sub-sector as the reference group, the result indicates that firms in the textile
industry will perform significantly higher than similar manufacturing firms from the
bakery, metal, machinery and chemical industries. Implicitly, while location of a
business positively influences its performance (H5), the operating arena a firm chooses
is also crucial because in this study there is evidence to suggest that operating in the
textile industry will greatly enhance a firm’s performance. The finding that industry
differentials determine significant performance variations among firms may be due to
factors such as: nature of competition in the industry, demand and supply issues with
regard to the product of the industry, and government assistance to that sub-sector. For
instance, the textile industry in Ghana is one of the sectors that has benefited
particularly from government support (Quartey, 2006; Kuffour, 2008). Examples of
recent government assistance to the industry are: $2 million (USD) for the refurbishment
and restart of operations of the redundant Juapong Textile Company under the new

must be taken into account in any detailed examination of the factors that determine firm
performance. Consequently, from the findings of this study, both industry and firm
effect are crucial in understanding firm performance and must be borne in mind by
researchers inclined to focus on firm effect when analysing performance of firms,
especially those from emerging markets.
Conclusion
Strategic factors that explain firm performance in an emerging economy can consist of
both firm level and external factors. It is plausible therefore to argue that a misfit between
the external moderating factors and the firm level factors could affect the degree of firm
performance. In this study, in addition to the firm level factors (firm size and workforce
productivity), type of industry and where a firm is located were significant. With regard to
firm experience, the results imply that new and younger firms, in recognition of the
challenges posed by their newness, could still position themselves with extra aggression
from the start of trading, in order to compete side-by-side with their more established
counterparts. This is accounted for by the fact that in today’s globalised world, because of
easy access to national and international business information following the
advancements in communication and transportation technologies, difference in firm age
cannot disadvantage new and younger firms to a larger extent.
Implication of the study
With regard to practice, the results mean that because of influence from the
globalisation phenomenon based on advancements in information and communication
technologies, differences in firm age cannot disadvantage new and younger firms
against their counterparts’ large multinational enterprises to a greater extent.
Consequently, new and younger firms should not be intimidated by their more
experienced large-scale competitors, but must take advantage of the globalisation
processes and compete side-by-side with large enterprises. The importance of large
firm size cannot be overemphasised with regard to impacting positively on firm
performance. Implicitly large firm size defined as the total number of full-time
employees implies a wide array of difference in skill sets at the disposal of a firm.
Therefore, micro and small businesses from Ghana that desire to improve their
performance in order to be sustainable must not fritter away their profits on family and
personal luxuries (e.g. building luxurious house, buying expensive cars, spending on
funerals and marrying many women), but must ensure that a proportion of the profit is
ploughed back into attracting, training and retaining an adequate amount of skilled

must not be taken for granted.

private sector organisations (Association of Ghana Industries (AGA), Ghana Association
of Women Entrepreneurs (GAWE), etc.), to sponsor and/or host industrial fairs (both at
home and abroad). Such platforms showcase the potential of Ghanaian businesses to
foreign businesses and offer the Ghanaian businesses an opportunity to interact with
foreign businesses and forge diverse forms of JVs alliances.
Limitations and opportunity for future research
The data set consists of panel of firms, but the model was based on the pooled OLS
estimation, therefore, an advanced panel models such as the fixed effect and the
random effect may be applied to confirm the consistency or otherwise of the results.
Besides, the data set was unbalanced compared with the balance data set that contains
more robust properties and so future research may focus on a more balanced data set to
comment on the findings. The use of a sample from Ghana’s manufacturing sub-sector
limits the generalizability of the results. Consequently, the generalisation could not be
extended to cover other sub-sectors in Ghana (e.g. the service and the extractive
sub-sectors) and so this offers opportunity for future research. Furthermore, a lack of
insights from the institutional theory and sociology viewpoints on the phenomenon,
shows some limitations. The reason for this is because the current thinking concerning
these topics maintains that institutions and social-cultural values (e.g. taboos, customs
and traditions) of a nation are critical to firm performance and development of a
country. This appears more crucial to countries from Africa, where weak institutions
(e.g. laws, regulations, political atmosphere, and property rights) and cultural values
(taboos) militate against firm performance and the welfare of the people. So it is
suggested that future study must shed light on the topic, from the institutional and
sociological perspective, in order yield more robust results and implications.
