Abstract
It is argued that the present pace of small and medium enterprises (SME) research on Africa remains slow, despite being one of
the growing fields. Consequently, there have been calls to advance the field from the perspective of Africa. This study partly
responds to the call and aims to systematically review and organize the different SME research contributions from Ghana to help
us appreciate and understand how research on SMEs in Ghana has advanced over the years. Combining major online databases
such as Web of Science, Business Source Complete (EBSCO), ABI/INFORM, and Scopus databases, this paper reviews and
analyzes 231 SME studies between 2000 and 2018. The paper structures SME research on Ghana into salient themes to offer the
current state of knowledge and, consequently, aids scholars to scope the field and discover value-adding avenues to advance our
understanding about SME research in the context of Ghana. Based on the findings, implications on theory, policy, and practice
are discussed.

alongside. While SMEs research, in general, remains attractive to researchers, a growing amount of researchers (e.g.,
Ariyo et al., 2015; Jones et al., 2018) have argued for the need

Although SME research on Africa remains a growing field,
authors (Igwe et al., 2019; Igwe & Icha-Ituma, 2020; Jones
et al., 2018; Meagher, 2018) argue that the current pace of
research in the field remains slow thereby calling for more
studies to advance SME research in the context of Africa.
For example, in the context of Ghana, previous studies (e.g.,
Abor & Quartey, 2010; Aryeetey, 2001) have discussed
SMEs and their contribution to the economy of Ghana. Yet,
there exists a lacuna in research that systematically reviews
and organizes the different SME research contributions to
Ghana to help us appreciate and understand how research on
SMEs in Ghana has developed and advanced over the years.
Precisely, to our knowledge, there are limited studies dedicated to carrying out a systematic literature review of SME research on Ghana. Consequently, this paper reviews the current
state of SME research on Ghana to consolidate what has been
done so far, identify gaps, and map-up productive future research directions.
Specifically, the motivation of this paper is to situate the
findings in the context prior synthesis of global SME literature
which contains limited literature on Africa, especially subSaharan Africa. To contribute to this research gap, three basic
questions inform our study. That is, from the Ghanaian context, what are the key thematic research areas explored in
SME research? Which methods drive existing SME studies
on Ghana? Which sectors have received the most of SME
research attention? A systematic review of SME research
within the context of Ghana is very important because there
is enough evidence pointing to the profound contributions of
SMEs to the Ghanaian economy — SMEs provide about 85%
of manufacturing jobs in Ghana, contribute about 70% to
Ghana’s GDP, and account for about 92% of businesses in
Ghana (Abor & Quartey, 2010; Aryeetey, 2001; Asare, 2014a,
2014b; International Trade Centre, 2016). Consequently,
SMEs have become the focus of public policy and, as a result,
a review summarizing what is known and not known about
SME research on Ghana becomes an important input in policy
formulation.
Our paper makes significant contributions to the SME research field. First, the study develops a comprehensive review
of the specific body of literature on SMEs in Ghana and situates our analysis within the broader SME and management
literature which has limited studies on Africa (see Igwe
et al., 2019; Igwe & Icha-Ituma, 2020; Jones et al., 2018;
Meagher, 2018) especially sub-Saharan Africa. Second, based
on this systematic review, the study identifies crucial themes
and insights about how SME research has advanced in Ghana
in terms of key thematic research areas, methods, key findings, and sectors which have received research attention to
offer an avenue for scholars and practitioners to reflect on
questions which appear critical yet have not received research
attention to inform public policy, education, and practice.
Third, in the final section, we organize and consolidate the

main findings, identify lingering gaps, and suggest important
guidance for future research avenue. To achieve our goal, we
structure the rest of the paper as follows. In what follows, we
give a brief overview of SME research on Ghana and section 2
discusses the method employed for the study, while sections 3
reports and discusses the findings and finally, section 4 concludes the study.

1.1 Overview of SME research on Ghana
The catalytic role of SMEs to the socio-economic development of economies is recognized worldwide (Belás et al.,
2020). The economy of Ghana is not without SME activities;
in fact, SMEs are known for dominating the Ghanaian economy (Abor & Quartey, 2010). As a result, several dimensions
of activities of SMEs in Ghana have been studied by various
researchers. Among the themes which have driven SME research on Ghana are as follows: studies that address SME
challenges in Ghana include Mabe et al. (2013), constraints
facing new and existing small and medium-scale enterprises
(SMEs) in Greater Accra Region of Ghana; Charles et al.
(2015), a qualitative approach to examining the challenges
of Ghanaian small and medium-scale enterprises; and
Prempeh (2015), problems of financing SMEs in Ghana.
Papers that explore regulatory influences on SMEs in Ghana
also include Aryeetey and Ahene (2005a, 2005b), the changing regulatory environment for Ghanaian SMEs; Kayanula
and Quartey (2000), the SME policy environment; and Abor
and Quartey, SME development in Ghana. Samples of studies
that address accounting and financial management of SMEs in
Ghana also include Biekpe (2004), SME finance in subSaharan Africa; Abor and Biekpe (2007), SME finance in
Ghana; and Musah and Ibrahim (2014), SME recordkeeping and bottom line and Mahmoud (2011) market orientation and business performance among SMEs in Ghana.
Overall, a critical analysis of the SME research on Ghana
indicates that the studies have been done in isolation. This
calls for a methodical review and classification of the diverse
contributions of SME research on Ghana to help us comprehend how research on SMEs in Ghana has progressed over the
years.

3 Results, discussion, and future research
directions

3.2 Sectors of SME research focus

This section presents and discusses the results of our systematic literature review as they emerged from the analysis. First,
we present and discuss the main thematic issues addressed in
SME literature on Ghana. Second, we zoom in to discuss the
sectors in Ghana in which SME studies have been focused.
Third, we highlight the methodological approach adopted for
SME research on Ghana. Fourth, we demonstrate the level of
authors’ collaboration and their affiliated institutions. Then,
we focus on examining the level of analysis employed in
SME research on Ghana. Finally, we suggest important research areas for future research directions.

3.1 Issues and thematic areas explored in SME
research in Ghana
Our analysis categorized various issues and thematic areas
explored in SME research on Ghana into 13 broad themes,
namely, (1) challenges, barriers, problems, and constraints of
SMEs; (2) tax, accounting practices, record keeping, risk management, financing, and credit of SMEs; (3) SME promotion,
market strategies, and their economic implications; (4) alternative model for financing SMEs; (5) contribution of SMEs to
economic growth, and development and analysis of SME performance; (6) innovation and information technology of
SMEs; (7) entrepreneurship and labor; (8) local content of
SME activities; (9) government regulations, policies, and
how they affect SME growth; (10) health and safety management of SMEs in construction and technology industries; (11)
total quality management and service management of SMEs;
(12) women and gender-related issues in SME; and (13)
others. The results show that a significant number of SME
research on Ghana have addressed financial management
challenges (i.e., accounting records and taxation). Ideally,
there were twelve broad themes and all the articles that could
not fit into any of the twelve themes were included in a distinctive theme which was named “other.” Table 1 gives a
detailed presentation of these issues and thematic areas of
SME research on Ghana. In entirety, out of the 231 publications, 49 of the articles representing 21.21% belonged to the
theme named “other” and it was the highest; 43 of the articles
(18.61%) also belonged to tax, accounting principles, and record keeping theme; challenges or constraints of SMEs also

The analysis of the reviewed studies indicates that SME research on Ghana has been conducted in 13 different sectors, of
which one of the sectors was classified as multisector. Any
article that does not focus on the specific sector but cuts across
is referred to as a multisector. Out of the 231 publications on
the SMEs, 177 (76.66%) of them covered a collection of several sectors; 16 (6.9%) of the articles published within the
period are within the financial or the banking sector. This is
not surprising because the financial sector of Ghana has
witnessed a significant expansion over the period as compared
to other sectors (Ackah & Asiamah, 2016). Several
microfinance, micro credits and savings and loans companies
have sprung up across the length and breadth of the country.
This has become a talking point among the populace, and
researchers try to figure out what the major drivers of this
expansion are. Recently, the financial sector in Ghana has
been hit by several crises which resulted in the collapse of
some indigenous banks and this has become a major concern
to the industry players as well as the Bank of Ghana and other
stakeholders.2 This probably may be a reason why most of the
publications in SMEs have focused more on the financial or
the banking sector. The manufacturing and construction sectors are the two other sectors that have received some considerable number of publications in SME research on Ghana,
accounting for 8 (3.46%) and 7 (3.0%) of total publications
in SMEs respectively, over the period. These are two other
sectors that are rapidly expanding to meet the demands for
housing and job creation and therefore may spark some interest among researchers. Agriculture and Tourism sectors have
also received some little attention in terms of publications on
SMEs with a total of 5 publications each over the period
accounting for 2.34% of the total publications. Even though
these are two major traditional sectors in Ghana, they do not
attract many investors in SMEs as one will expect.
Surprisingly, the furniture and craft sector did not receive
any publication on SMEs in Ghana over the period. This
may be because most of the people involved in such activities
do not properly register their businesses, and therefore,

3.3 Methodological approaches
Our analysis of the methodological approaches used in SME
studies on Ghana shows that various methods have been
employed to explore issues of SMEs in Ghana. We grouped
these papers into four methodological dimensions, namely conceptual, empirical qualitative and quantitative and mixed
methods. Out of the 231 reviewed publications, 88 of them
representing 38.10% used empirical quantitative; 80 (34.63%)
used empirical mixed-method approach in their study; 33
(14.29%) used empirical qualitative; 30 (12.99%) employed
conceptual. We observed that quantitative method (mostly
through survey instrument to collect primary data) was the most
widely used in SME publications on Ghana. Because most
SMEs do not keep proper records of daily activities, secondary
information is unavailable and, hence, scholars employ survey
method to gather data for their quantitative analysis. The empirical mixed method is the second most widely used method in
SME publications on Ghana. The mixed-method is mostly seen
as the best since it involves the combination of both qualitative
and quantitative approaches. It is mostly used because it helps
the authors to get a clearer picture of what is happening in

SMEs for a wholistic analysis to be made. The least used methodologies are conceptual. Again, data constraints may account
for the relatively lower usage of these two methods. Details of
these methodological dimensions and their proportions are presented in Table 2 and Table 3.
The study further examined the specific type(s) of methodology that was employed to analyze each paper. Under the
conceptual approach, three different methodologies were
employed, namely, context-bound based review, inductive review based on theory, and thematic review. Out of the 30
publications of the conceptual approach (representing 11%
of the total number of SMEs research on Ghana), 10
(3.67%) were a context-bound based review, 16 (5.87%) were
inductive review based on theory, and 4 (1.48%) were thematic review. Table 3 presents the details of these specific
methods adopted to analyze SME research on Ghana and their
respective proportions.
In the empirical qualitative, 7 (2.56%) studies employed
exploratory analysis. Several quantitative or econometric
models were used in the empirical quantitative type of papers.
Some of these include regressions, discriminant, factorial, and
principal component analyses, and many more. However, majority of them used the descriptive, correlation, and the ordinary least square approach. The same observation was made
for that of empirical mixed approach (see Table 3).

3.4 Nature of author collaboration
The nature of collaboration that exists among the authors of
the reviewed papers is summarized in Table 4. We found out
that regarding the nature of collaboration existing between the
authors in the reviewed papers, publications authored by two
people formed the majority. Seventy-eight (78) out of the 231
publications reviewed representing 33.76% were written by
two individuals. A collaboration between two is most likely
to be effective because the two can bring diverse opinions that
will enrich the content of the paper as well as limit delays that
may result from too many conflicting opinions when the authors are more than two. Additionally, 63 (27.72%) were
single-authored; 64 (27.71%) was authored by three people,
while 18 (7.79%) and 6 (2.60%) were authored by four and
five authors respectively.

3.6 Level of analysis
Here, we analyze the level of analysis for SME research on
Ghana. Following prior studies (e.g., Aguinis & Glavas,
2012), we classified the level of analysis into three (3), namely, institutional, organizational, and individual variables.
According to Scott (1995) cited in Aguinis and Glavas
(2012), the institutional level analysis covers themes and constructs external to the organization. The PESTLE (Political,
Economic, Social-cultural, Technological, Legal and
Ecological) model which most researchers (e.g., Song et al.,
2017; Thompson & Martin, 2006) employ fit as a good framework that explains the constructs for institutional level of analysis. Following the PESTLE model, variables such as the
external regulations and standards, interest rates, including
topics shaped by civil society and other external stakeholders
are all categorized as the institutional level of analysis. The
PESTLE model presents external variables outside the control
of organizations that influence their activities. The

organizational level analysis includes themes that focus on
the organization itself. Among the examples are constructs
such as top management teams, products, processes, services,
policies, procedures, and board behavior. Individual level of
analysis looks at topics such as individuals in the organization
(e.g., a person, staff, manager, worker) and group behavior in
organizations (Nicholls-Nixon et al., 2011).
Out of the 231 articles reviewed, 76 (32.92%) were conducted
at the institutional level; 153 (66.23%) were also conducted at the
organizational level and only 2 (0.87%) were conducted at the
individual level. The study further examined the type of variables
these studies focused on. Out of the number that was conducted
at the organizational level, 75.16% of them were on predictor
level and the remaining 24.84% at the outcome level. In the case
of the institutional level, 75% of them were on predictor level and
the remaining 25% on the outcome level. Details of this analysis
are presented in Table 6.

3.7 Future research directions
Altogether, while we acknowledge that extant SME literature has
significantly contributed to our understanding of SMEs and their
development in Ghana, we observed that significant amount of
research is still warranted to better shape our understanding on
the topic from the Ghanaian perspective. In light of the analysis
above, the following are some underexplored SME research
areas on Ghana. Internationalization of Ghanaian SMEs (antecedents, actors, processes, and practices) needs future attention.
The next one concerns innovation in SMEs; it is argued that
because SMEs by their nature are small and revolve around
flexibility and quick decision making of the owner-managers,
innovation thrives among SMEs. Also, the issues of innovation
are linked to intellectual property rights in SMEs. In the recent
study by Igwe and Icha-Ituma (2020), the authors contended that
more research on SMEs in Africa is needed especially in areas,
namely, gender and SMEs, family-oriented SMEs, ethnic SME
activities, artisans SME activities, entrepreneurship, and SME
education in Africa. Igwe and Icha-Ituma (2020) stressed the
importance of these areas following the dominance of youth
unemployment and the informal aspects of African economies
to inform policy and practice. The issue of E-business in SMEs,
social entrepreneurship, and total quality management in SMEs
are also called for. New emerging areas in the field such as green
business in SMEs, scenario planning, big data analytics, and
artificial intelligence are seen as critical.
Networks — the interconnectedness of firms to create several opportunities for learning, problem-solving, decisionmaking, and personal interaction (Rainie & Wellman, 2012)
— have been recognized as an important channel to manage
SMEs in a resource-constrained business environment as in
the case of Ghana (Acheampong et al., 2017; Acheampong
et al., 2018; Acheampong & Hinson, 2019). As SMEs encounter myriad of challenges (as shown in this study), they

these networks will help them to compensate for the scarcity
of internal resources and competences (Colombo et al., 2012).
This strand of the literature was not fully covered by this
present study, and therefore, we call on future studies to devote attention to this important research agenda.
Concerning the sectoral focus, as can be seen from Fig. 2,
the results show that the sectors media, transportation, food
and beverage, hospitality, and small-scale miming have received the least attention, but these are critical sub-sectors of
the Ghanaian economy given its informal dominance and,
therefore, need research attention. In terms of themes, we consider these as important research avenues and therefore recommend that future research should be directed to such areas.
In terms of methodological approaches, the major research
designs (i.e., survey, qualitative, and mixed methods) are present
in the studies (see Table 2), but given the informal nature of the
SME subsector of Ghana, a more emphasis on qualitative studies
depending on constraints available is recommended. Again, as
can be seen from Table 3, in terms of analytical methods, extant
studies seem a bit comprehensive in our view. However, in the
light current trends, popular qualitative methods such as ethnography, phenomenology, and grounded theories are currently underemployed in the extant SME literature on Ghana. Another
analytical technique that warrants application is structural equation modeling (SEM). SEM is seen as offering robust parameter
estimates compared to ordinary least squares regression (OLS)
and, therefore, drives most high impact quantitative journals.
Regarding the nature of author collaboration, the results appeared
interesting in our view because it ranges from single to five authors (see Table 4). It is also interesting to see most foreign
Universities focus their studies on SMEs in Ghana (see
Table 5). About the level of analysis, we saw that SME studies
on Ghana are based on multi-level (i.e., institutional, organizational, and individual) (Table 6) but because most SMEs are
dominated by the behaviors of the owner-managers, a focus on
individual (owner-manager) level analysis in SME research will
warrant future research direction. Finally, even though we relied
on a comprehensive approach to search and locate the relevant
papers for our review, it is also possible that some important
studies (with significant contributions) may have been missed.
Therefore, we encourage future studies to employ other means (in
addition to our approach) to locate and retrieve all relevant papers.

publications are based on the finance and or banking sector.
The furniture and craft sector did not receive any publications
on SMEs in Ghana over the period under study though they
play an important role within the informal economy of Ghana.
The qualitative, quantitative, and mixed methods are the most
commonly used in studies on SMEs in Ghana. The study
revealed a collaboration among authors in publications relating to SMEs in Ghana. Majority of the publications were
authored by one, two, or three persons with two-authored
papers being the most dominant. Looking at publications by
institutions, the University of Ghana has the highest; Kumasi
Technical University and University of Development Studies
followed in that order. Finally, the study releveled that most of
the publications were at the institutional and organizational
levels with a small percentage at the individual level.
Consequently, proposed research directions for the future are
suggested under the sub-heading “Future research directions.”
The paper contributes to SME reviewed works in extant
reviews on Africa. Even though our present review cannot
be said to be completely inclusive in terms of the databases
consulted and the timing horizon employed, our understanding is enhanced regarding adding up to existing reviews on
SME research on Africa. As a result, our review responds to
calls by researchers (e.g., Jones et al., 2018; Meagher, 2018;
Igwe et al., 2019; Igwe & Icha-Ituma, 2020) regarding the
need for more SMEs and entrepreneurship research to be carried out on Africa. Again, given the context of Ghana, we also
think that this paper is one of the few, if not the only paper,
that have synthesized SME work on Ghana, especially given
the fact that SMEs dominate the economy of Ghana and presently drive most socio-economic policies of the government.
Following the least contributions of the publications by
both public and private universities, central government policy must aim to resource universities (Business Schools) in
Ghana to create a level playing field for faculty members in
these institutions to be research active as well as
complementing the efforts of those who are making initiatives. Moreover, the central government must make deliberate
attempt to award part of its research consultancy assignments
to the least endowed universities (e.g., most of the private
ones) to enhance the research capacity of the faculty members.
Finally, research is one of the core duties of every academic
and, therefore, the management of private universities in
Ghana, in particular, must find ways to balance teaching and
faculty research. In this case, within the Ghanaian universities,
faculty members from different Business Schools (i.e., both
public and private) must be encouraged to do much local
research collaborations thereby enhancing research efforts.
