In most Sub Saharan African countries renewable energy has been part of the energy mix balance-sheet for quite a long time. However, the share of total primary energy from renewable sources have declined over time despite signi?cant investment into the sector in the last decade. This paper pierce the veil of the energy sector to unearth the underlying factors driving renewable energy output in the region using a panel data of 32 SSA countries from 1990 to 2015. The study rely on the Driscoll-Kraay estimator to correct the problem of cross sectional dependence and serial correlation in the model. Findings from the study point to the underlying factors of renewable energy output in Co2 emissions per capita, income per capita, oil prices, trade openness, natural resource rents, urbanization, population growth rate, and cli- matic stress. The study proposes policies that incorporates environmental awareness in the national development plans of countries; increasing renewable energy consumption among the middle class; encourage regional renewable energy grid sharing; implementing and expanding the feed-in-tariff system; and granting tax incentives to companies that seek to invest and develop the renewable en- ergy sector in the region.

1. Introduction

   In the last three decades, the share of renewable energy output from total primary energy in Sub Saharan Africa (SSA) has declined over time, meanwhile there have been consistent increase in in- vestment in the sector over the last couple of decades. We ascribe a higher share of off-grid renewable energy infrastructure consumed by the poor rather than the middle class as one of the primary reasons for the decline in investment. Ultimately, there is an increasing interest in renewable resources around the world given the incontestable evidence of anthropogenic climate change. As a result, both Annex I and II countries1 have all committed to voluntary reduction in carbon emission under the Paris agreement which has provided political momentum to the adoption of renewable energy in most countries. It is therefore innocuous to argue that, the resurging of interest in renewable energy sector is partly driven by climate change or environmental consideration. It is against the Paris agreement and the United Nations Framework

3.2. Estimation procedure

   In estimating panel data models, certain assumptions needs be underlined. First, the variables in the model should be stationary. Next, the covariates should not be correlated with each other [31]. An optimal lag should be determined to avoid the curse of dimensionality. Another important requirement is, there should be no cross sectional dependence in the model as it could bias the results downwards [32]. Considering the similarity in regional dy- namics, it is anticipated that a phenomenon in one country could have a cascading effect on another country. One such effect could be internationalization of commodity price in the world market (oil price) and other exogenous factors such as climate related shocks. For this reason, we implement the Driscoll and Kraay [33] estimator that is robust to cross sectional dependence, serial correlation and heteroscedasticity.  Considering  the  structure  of  the  data (T < N and N < T ) for the total number of countries and group spe- ci?c countries permit the estimation of the model with this estimator.

3.3. Testing procedure

   The validity of the results is underlined by performing the following test. This follows the presence of time effects and serial dependence in the model. We perform the cross sectional depen- dence test to ascertain the temporal and spatial dependence of the variables in the model. Before implementing these tests, we describe the characteristics of the variables in the model and the correlation among the variables in the model.

Our coef?cient of regression reveal a decline in renewable energy output by 0.12% as a result of trade openness. The pressure of urbanization has forced countries to source energy from alter- native sources to cope with the rising demand of energy. Our result reveal a positive and statistically signi?cant impact on the share of renewable energy output for both groups of countries. We show that urban population growth rate for countries that generate a lower share of energy from renewable sources resulted in a 0.14% increase in renewable energy output. 4.3. Robustness using other related variables

   We now turn our attention to the robustness of our results. To start with, we ?rst estimate the model to see whether the result apply to other control variables. This follows a classi?cation based on the level of average tariffs

rates, the presence of black market exchange rate, non-tariff bar- riers, a socialist economy and state control over major exports. This allows us to explore the policy component of trade rather than relying on a generic quanti?cation of trade openness which is in- direct. Next, we also include an economic development indicator as a measure for economic progress. As an empirical matter, human development index is a proxy for economic development. Because most countries depend on gas for energy generation in the region, we control for gas price effect on renewable energy output for both groups. The general growth rate of Africa's population is alarming and could pose signi?cant threat to energy generation and consuming, as such, we control for population growth in this model. The results reported in Table 5 con?rm a positive relation- ship between economic progress (HDI) and renewable energy output. This result is consistent across columns 1 to 7. Column 8 reports a negative outcome due to climatic shocks. Hinging our results on columns 1 to 7, our argument presented in Table 4 is further strengthened that economic progress or growth is impor- tant for renewable energy development in the region.
   To capture the price effect of competing alternatives to renew- able energy growth, we control for gas price in this model. Again, the results reported in Table 5 con?rm a negative relationship be- tween gas price and renewable energy output. This con?rm the complementary nature of natural gas price and renewable energy output. This results also highlights the need to improve the energy mix in the region and more importantly, increase the share of en- ergy from renewable sources. Our policy variable for trade liber- alization retained a positive relationship on renewable energy output and thus provides evidence that an open economy is on the average good for renewable source of energy in SSA.
   The relationship between population growth and renewable energy output is an interesting one. We show that population growth negatively affects renewable energy output across the re- gion. We argue that because a greater share of renewable energy sources (solar) is off-grid and consumed by those in the lower in- come bracket, this result is not surprising. On the contrary, we see a positive relationship between urbanization and renewable energy output thus con?rming how urban population growth pushes policy makers to look for alternative forms of energy production. As a last step, we try to examine if climatic policy variable are related to renewable energy output. One could also argue a lack of technical capacity to exploit these abundant resources. Also, technological progress in this area is expensive. Extreme temperature event show an encouraging effect on renewable energy output and points the importance of solar energy development. Extreme rainfall on the other hand is nega- tively related to renewable energy output.

5. Conclusion and recommendation

   In this study, we relied on cross-country panel data to analyse the drivers of renewable energy in SSA. We employed a novel estimator that is robust to cross sectional dependence, serial cor- relation and heteroscedasticity of the explanatory variables. Our empirical results show that Co2 emissions per capita has a negative and statistically signi?cant effect on the share of total primary energy generated from renewable sources. Which leads us to conclude the lack of concern for environmental issues in these countries. This effect is re?ected in the positive relationship be- tween energy inef?ciency and the share of renewable energy output for both groups of countries. We also see a negative rela- tionship for the effect of the rati?cation of the Kyoto protocol on renewable energy output in the region. Our results show a negative relationship between extreme rainfall events and renewable en- ergy output. Perhaps, this outcome illustrates the level of under- development of the hydroelectric source of energy generation in the region.
   On the contrary, economic progress, trade liberalization, natural resource endowment, and Urbanization positively affect renewable energy output. We show the positive causal relationship between economic progress (GDP per capita) and renewable energy output when a different indicator for economic development is used in the sensitivity section of the study. We found a strong relationship between human capital development and renewable energy output. Using a more direct measure of trade liberalization in the sensitivity analysis our results con?rmed the importance of trade on renewable energy output in the region. However, in the preferred model, we ?nd no such evidence when we use the trade openness indicator and this outcome is only applicable to countries that generate a higher share of energy from renewable sources. A potential explanation to this relationship is due to the recent dis- coveries of oil and gas in these countries. Employing two different proxies for natural resource endowment (forest rents and total natural resource rents) for the selected countries, our results con?rmed the importance of natural resources to renewable energy output in the region. Interestingly, our results reveal the signi?- cance of urbanization to renewable energy output. We argue that the growing pressure for energy demand coerce policy makers to seek alternative forms of energy generation which, in this case, is energy from renewable sources.
From a policy point of view, some recommendations can be
derived from the empirical results. Importantly, our results points to the urgency to environmental protection. Environmental con- cerns and awareness should remain a vital component of national development plans of countries in SSA. A deliberate policy towards the generation of energy from renewable sources could foster the urgent need to decouple fossil fuel powered economic growth. From the consumption side of the economy which is re?ected in

urbanization, a policy direction should be aimed at increasing en- ergy mix in urban areas of the region. Most importantly resolving the shortfall in energy demands with renewable sources of energy. As a means to attract investors into the untapped humongous and diverse renewable energy potential, governments and policy makers should encourage regional grid sharing as well as grant tax exemptions to institutions that a keen on exploiting the renewable energy in the region. Through international trade, countries in Af- rica can bene?t from the exports of renewable energy to neigh- bouring European countries that have increasing demand for energy. A promising way in this instance, is a strategic partnership with technologically endowed countries for renewable energy output. Also, policy makers could as an incentive to consumers, introduce rebates for the consumers who purchase and use renewable source of electricity.
