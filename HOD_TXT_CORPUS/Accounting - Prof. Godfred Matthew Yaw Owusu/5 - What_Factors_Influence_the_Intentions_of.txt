Evidence from Ghana

Accounting, University of Ghana Business School, Accra, Ghana
ABSTRACT

KEYWORDS

This study examines the views of students on the ethics of tax evasion, their intentions to evade
tax, and investigates the factors that predict such intentions. Relying on the extended version of
the Theory of Planned Behaviour, the study investigated whether attitudes, subjective norms,
perceived behavioural control, and moral obligation may be good predictors of the intention to
evade tax. Data were gathered from 662 students of University of Ghana Business School using
questionnaires. The hypotheses were tested using the structural equation modelling technique.
Results indicate that the intention to evade tax among the students is low and most respondents
generally perceive tax evasion as an unethical practice. The results also demonstrate that attitude,
subjective norms and moral obligations have important implications on the intention to evade
tax. The evidence we provide should be useful to governments and tax agencies interested in
promoting responsible tax compliance behaviour among individual taxpayers.

Introduction
One major challenge that governments across the globe
have been confronted with for decades is the inability to
meet their annual tax revenue targets. Being one of the
most important and reliable sources of revenue to every
government, the gap in tax revenue—which is the absolute
value of non-received tax revenue in a fiscal year—remains
one of the critical challenges of most economies (Choo,
Fonseca, & Myles, 2016). A major source of this problem
has been attributed to the widespread evasion of tax by
both individuals and corporations. Defined as the use of
illegal means to pay less taxes or no taxes at all (Slemrod &
Weber, 2012), tax evasion has become a very topical issue
in contemporary times due to its debilitating effect on the
capacity of many countries to raise sufficient revenue to
finance public expenditure. Notwithstanding the efforts by
most governments especially in enacting laws that seek to
punish tax offenders as a deterrent measure (Casagrande,
Di Cagno, Pandimiglio, & Spallone, 2015), such efforts
unfortunately have not yielded the desired outcome of
convincing every citizen to fully fulfill their tax obligations
to the state (McGee, Ho, & Li, 2008; Pui Yee, Moorthy, &
Choo Keng, 2017).
Consequently, governments especially from developing countries, have over years depended so much on
foreign assistance and on the continuous support of

Bretton Wood institutions including the World Bank
and International Monetary Fund (IMF) to support
their budgets. Reliance on these external sources of
funding, however, have not been without problems.
While donor support which traditionally provided financing for a large share of public investment in developing
countries has been dwindling for years (Asiedu, 2006;
Hernández-Catá, 2000, p. 7), dependence on IMF and
World Bank support has also been criticized for its
unfair conditionalities and negative consequences. For
instance, the World Bank and IMF funding programmes
have been criticized for widening income inequalities
(Forster, Kentikelenis, Reinsberg, Stubbs, & King,
2019), affecting public health-care delivery negatively
because of restricted government spending on social
services (Benton & Dionne, 2015), infringing on labour
rights (Blanton, Blanton, & Peksen, 2015) etc. These
problems have brought to the fore the need for developing countries to reduce their over-dependence on external funding sources and rather concentrate on financing
their developmental agenda using domestic tax revenues.
Like most developing countries, Ghana has had its
fair share of difficulties in her quest to maximize domestic revenue mobilization through taxes. Notwithstanding
the fact that taxes constitute the largest proportion of
total revenue to the state, accounting for approximately

many circles as the ‘difficult to tax group’. About 86%

While not discounting the contribution of the informal
sector to the tax evasion menace, individual tax evasion
decision is believed to be influenced by a multiplicity of
factors (Ali & Ahmad, 2014; Machogu & Amayi, 2016).
An understanding of these factors is therefore critical to
developing appropriate strategies to promote responsible
tax behaviour among citizens so as to help reduce the
occurrence of tax evasion. Relying on the extended version of the Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB) as previously used by Alleyne and Harris (2017), the current
study investigates the intention of people to engage in tax
evasion and the key predictors of such intentions. Unlike
the empirical work of Alleyne and Harris (2017), however, this study additionally investigates the ethical perception of individuals on tax evasion and ascertains the
influence of this perception on the intention to engage in
tax evasion. We contribute to the tax evasion literature by
highlighting the dominant factors that predict the intention of people to engage in tax evasion applying the
extended version of the TPB framework in a developing
country setting.

Various reasons have been adduced to explain why
people evade taxes. For instance, a huge tax burden,
dishonesty, and corruption have been cited as reasons
for tax evasion (Torgler, 2005). Also, McGee (1999)
reports inadequate tax collection mechanisms and peoples’ belief that they have no tax payment responsibility
towards government since government does nothing
for them as reasons for tax evasion. Other reasons
including unfair treatment by tax authorities, lack of
trust in tax officials and poor customer service have
been cited in some studies (Alm & Martinez-Vazquez,
2007; Tanzi & Zee, 2000; Torgler, 2005). It has also
been argued that the existence of an opportunity to
evade taxes can also influence tax evasion behaviour
(Forest & Kirchler, 2010; Hashimzade, Myles, Page, &
Rablen, 2014) while social norms and the ethical orientation of individuals have also been associated with tax
evasion (Bazart & Bonein, 2014; Pickhardt & Prinz,
2014). Thus, a multiplicity of factors can be responsible
for the tax payment compliance or noncompliance
behaviour of people.
Researching into actual unethical behaviours can be
daunting in reality for reasons such as difficulties in
locating the actual wrongdoers, difficulty in gaining
access to information and even where access is granted,
information may be censored (Alleyne, 2016; Alleyne,
Hudaib, & Pike, 2013; Chiu, 2003). Consequently,
researchers have resorted to using intentions to engage
in an unethical behaviour as a useful proxy for actual
unethical behaviour (Alleyne & Harris, 2017; Alleyne,
Hudaib, & Haniffa, 2015).

Literature review
The concept of tax evasion

Intentions to engage in tax evasion

Tax evasion is a phenomenon that poses a serious threat to
society. It denies a country substantial amounts of money
that would otherwise have been available to undertake
developmental activities such as provision of healthcare,
education, infrastructure and other social services
(Korndörfer, Krumpal, & Schmukle, 2014). Alleyne and
Harris (2017) assert that it has become an albatross around
the neck of governments worldwide due to perpetrators’
adoption of innovative and changing schemes making its
regulation quite difficult. Korndörfer, Krumpal &
Schmukle (2014, p. 19) define tax evasion as “an illegal act
that violates the law and deviates from social norms that
prescribe that taxes should be paid”. The definition brings
to bear the issue of illegality and an unethical practice once
an individual engages in it. It is thus, a phenomenon that

The motivational factors that influence an individual’s
behaviour are captured within the individual’s intentions.
Intentions give an indication of people’s determination
and willingness to pursue a behaviour as well as the
amount of planned effort to be exerted to perform the
behaviour (Ajzen, 1991). Ajzen (1991) posits that an
individual’s intention to perform a particular behaviour
is influenced by attitude towards the behaviour (the
degree to which an individual has a favourable or unfavourable evaluation of the behaviour), subjective norm
(the perceived social pressure from significant others to
either perform or not to perform a behaviour) and perceived behavioural control (the perceived ease or difficulty in the performance of a behaviour). Prior studies
have used these constructs to predict an individual’s

intention to engage in a tax behaviour (Alleyne & Harris,
2017; Bobek, Hatfield, & Wentzel, 2007).
The theoretical framework: extended version of the
theory of planned behaviour
The Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB) is an extension of the Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA) predominantly used to explain and predict human behaviour.
The TPB posits that attitude, subjective norm and perceived behavioural control are the factors that influence
the behavioural intentions of individuals with the behavioural intentions influencing the performance of
actual behaviour (Ajzen, 1991).
Ajzen (1991) explains that attitude is the extent to
which an individual assesses a behaviour to be favourable or unfavourable. Subjective norm describes the
perceptions that an individual has on the opinions of
other important people whose views can influence the
decisions of the decision maker. Perceived behavioural
control on the other hand describes the degree of control that an individual has about his/her own ability to
engage in a particular behaviour. From the tenets of the
TPB, the attitude of an individual towards a behaviour,
the influence of other prominent community members
on the behaviour of an individual and the perception of
an individual’s ability to perform the behaviour influence the performance of the actual behaviour.
Beck and Ajzen (1991) later extended the TPB framework with an additional variable ‘moral obligation’ which
captures the perception that an individual has about the
moral uprightness or otherwise of engaging in a certain
behaviour. Due to its comprehensive nature in explaining
and predicting the behaviour of individuals, this study
draws on the extended version of the TPB to examine the
factors that influence the intentions of individuals to
engage in tax evasion. The study specifically investigates
whether students’ attitude towards tax evasion (attitude),
their perception of tax evasion approval by prominent
stakeholders (subjective norm), the perception of their
capacity to engage in tax evasion (perceived behavioural
control), and their perception of the moral correctness or
otherwise of engaging in tax evasion (moral obligation) are
significant predictors of intentions to engage in tax evasion.
Attitude and intentions to engage in tax evasion
Attitude towards a particular behaviour describes the
degree to which an individual either favourably or
unfavourably evaluates the behaviour (Ajzen, 1991).
The attitude of an individual towards tax will have an
effect on compliance. Extant literature has documented
some tax attitudes to include taxpayers’ perception of

3

trust in authorities (Torgler, 2005), perceived fairness
of the tax system (Zainan, Noor, Omar, Aziz, & Sanusi,
2017) and taxpayers’ expectation of positive potential
for gains (Baldry, 1987). Empirically, some studies have
found a link between taxpayers’ attitudes and tax compliance behaviour of taxpayers (Abdul–Razak &
Adafula, 2013; Alabede, Ariffin, & Idris, 2011). Other
studies have also found attitude to be associated with
tax evasion (Kirchler, Hoelzl, & Wahl, 2008; Trivedi,
Shehata, & Mestelman, 2004). Similarly, Alleyne and
Harris (2017) found attitudes towards a behaviour to
be a significant predictor of an individual’s intention to
engage in tax evasion.
The key argument by these studies is that individuals
who are more likely to engage in tax evasion are those
who have favourable attitudes towards tax evasion. On
the other hand, individuals who have unfavourable
attitudes towards tax evasion are less likely to engage
in tax evasion. Based on the above, we hypothesize that:
H1: An individual’s attitude towards tax evasion is
positively related to intention to engage in tax evasion.

Subjective norm and intentions to engage in tax
evasion
Subjective norm indicates the influence that the opinions of other important people in an individual’s life
have on the decisions made by that individual. Beck
and Ajzen (1991) argue that the decision-making process of an individual can be influenced greatly by the
opinions of other important people in one’s life such as
family members, friends, co-workers and peers. The
perceived endorsement or otherwise that an important
person will give to a particular behaviour will influence
an individual to either conform or not to conform.
The perceived support or otherwise given by significant others on a particular behaviour can influence an
individual’s intention to engage in that behaviour
(Fishbein & Ajzen, 1975). For instance, the opinion of
significant others is reported to be the most important
factor that influences individuals to comply with taxes
(Scholz, McGraw, & Steenbergen, 1992). Jimenez and
Iyer (2016) note that tax compliance intentions are influenced by social norms which in turn increases the personal norms of individual tax compliance. Kirchler et al.
(2008) also assert that taxpayers are more likely to be
non-compliant if they believe that tax non-compliance is
quite widespread in the society and significant others do
not frown on it. Also, Hasan, Mohammad, and Alam
(2017) report that subjective norm influences significantly the tax compliance behaviour of taxpayers.

Specifically, on tax evasion, extant literature has
found a relationship between subjective norm and
intentions to engage in tax evasion (Alleyne & Harris,
2017; Bobek et al., 2007; Richardson, 2006; Tsakumis,
Curatola, & Porcano, 2007). Generally, individuals are
less likely to engage in tax evasion if they perceive that
significant others do not approve of that behaviour and
vice versa. Therefore, the study hypothesizes that:
H2: An individual’s subjective norms are positively
related to intention to engage in tax evasion.

Perceived behavioural control and intentions to
engage in tax evasion
Perceived behavioural control which is the perceived ease
or difficulty of engaging in a particular behaviour influences the intention to perform that behaviour (Ajzen, 1991;
Beck & Ajzen, 1991). The perceived ease or difficulty is
reflected in the belief that the individual has with respect to
the availability of resources and opportunities as well as the
obstacles and hindrances in engaging in a behaviour.
Basically, where perceived behavioural control is quite
high (more resources and opportunities), it is more likely
an individual will have greater intentions to engage in tax
evasion. For instance, extant literature documents that tax
non-compliance and tax evasion become quite prevalent in
the presence of an opportunity (Antonides & Robben,
1995; Carnes & Englebrecht, 1995). On the other hand,
where perceived behavioural control is very low (more
difficulties and hindrances), it is less likely an individual
will have greater intentions to engage in tax evasion. As
surmised by Carnes and Englebrecht (1995) an important
factor that influences the tax compliance decisions of an
individual is the perception of detection risk. Alleyne and
Harris (2017) conclude that individuals with low perceived
behavioural control have less motivation to engage in tax
evasion. As a result, this study hypothesizes that:
H3: An individual’s level of perceived behavioural control is positively related to intention to engage in tax
evasion.

Moral obligation and intentions to engage in tax
evasion
Molero and Pujol (2012) assert that it is quite difficult
to understand the behaviour of humans without considering ethical factors. Ajzen (1991) notes that in dealing with ethically sensitive situation, consideration
needs to be given to how an individual feel with regards

to the moral obligation to either perform or not to
perform a particular behaviour. Beck and Ajzen
(1991) thus, added moral obligation as an antecedent
which is influential in increasing the predictive power
of the theory of planned behaviour.
Shaw, Shiu & Clarke (2000, p. 882) define moral
obligation as “an individual’s internalized ethical rules,
which reflect their personal beliefs about right and
wrong”. It is basically about how personally responsible
an individual feels towards others. Bobek and Hatfield
(2003) note that individuals who personally have
a sense of responsibility towards others will respond
to ethical issues in a different manner from those who
do not have any sense of responsibility towards others.
Some studies have found moral obligation to significantly influence an individual’s intention to engage in
certain behaviours (Beck & Ajzen, 1991; Cronan & AlRafee, 2008; Yoon, 2011). Tax compliance behaviours
have been found to be influenced by moral obligations
(Bobek & Hatfield, 2003; Riahi-Belkaoui, 2004). With
respect to tax evasion, Reckers, Sanders, and Roark
(1994) find moral obligation to be a predictor of tax evasion
intentions. Torgler (2003) also finds that individuals who
view tax evasion as immoral are more likely to comply.
Also, Culiberg (2018) finds support for moral obligation
influencing an individual’s intention to engage in tax evasion. Again, Alleyne and Harris (2017) find that individuals
with high moral obligation are less likely to engage in tax
evasion. Based on these, we hypothesize that:
H4: An individual’s level of moral obligation is negatively related to intention to engage in tax evasion.

Ethics of tax evasion
The concept of ethics can be explained as the normative
systems of rules of conduct that guides the social interactions and interpersonal relationships of individuals (Hogan,
1973). Ethics defines the right or wrong conduct of an
individual in a particular situation. Surbhi (2015) asserts
that ethics functions as a guiding principle that helps to
determine what it good or bad. Arens, Elder, and Mark
(2012) see it as something that helps society to function
orderly. On the ethics of tax evasion, McGee (2006b) presents three views that exist in extant literature.
The first view which is described as the absolutist view
states that tax evasion is always or almost always unethical. Three basic reasons underpin this view of tax evasion.
The first reason is that individuals must submit to an
authority and that they owe a duty to God to pay taxes
or it is a command from God to pay taxes. Proponents of
this view normally use the Holy Bible by quoting Mathew

INTERNATIONAL JOURNAL OF PUBLIC ADMINISTRATION

22:17–21 and Romans 13:1–2 to buttress their point.
The second reason is that for a state to function properly,
individuals have a duty to pay taxes. That last reason is
that individuals have an ethical duty to contribute to the
welfare of other people of the community by paying taxes.
The second view which is described as the anarchist
view states that tax evasion is never or almost never
unethical. The proponents of this view assert that individuals do not owe any duty to pay taxes to any government
because all governments are illegitimate and unworthy to
receive tax payments. They further describe government
as a thief which confiscates assets and percentages of
paychecks without the consent of the owners of the property (McGee, 2006b; McGee & Guo, 2007).
The third view holds the assertion that tax evasion is
ethical in some circumstances but unethical other circumstances (McGee & Guo, 2007). Normally, individuals who
subscribe to this view often have reason (s) to buttress their
stance on tax evasion, whether it is ethical or unethical.
These reasons could emanate from their religious and
moral inclinations. This view towards tax evasion is the
most prevalent view. We thus hypothesize that:
H5: An individual’s ethical perception when taxes are
being used for good purposes is negatively related to
intention to engage in tax evasion.
H6: An individual’s ethical perception when taxes are
being used for bad purposes is positively related to
intention to engage in tax evasion.

Methodology
Research design and instrument development
The study used the survey method of research with questionnaires as the means of data collection. The questionnaires consisted of three different sections. The first section
requested the background information about the respondents including age, gender, course major, level of study,
religion and monthly income. The second section sought
respondents’ views on the ethics of tax evasion. The respondents were asked to indicate their response category on the
ethics of tax evasion instrument developed by McGee et al.
(2008). The third section focused on the specific questions
used to measure respondents’ intentions to evade tax and
the factors that influence their intentions. Using the theoretical lens of the extended version of the TPB, four constructs: Attitude, Subjective Norm, Perceived Behavioural
Control and Moral Obligation are considered to be important predictors of intention to engage in tax evasion. The
indicators used to measure these constructs were adapted

Participants and data collection
The questionnaires were self-administered to the targeted
students from the University of Ghana Business School
(UGBS) after explaining to them the purpose of the study.
To get honest responses and minimize the possibility of
getting socially desirable answers, the respondents were
made aware that participation in the survey was voluntary
and they were assured that their responses will be kept
confidential and solely used for this study. Out of the 750
questionnaires distributed 667 were returned representing a response rate of 88.93% and 662 were deemed usable
as five questionnaires were not filled fully by the respondents and therefore, were excluded from the analysis.

Conclusion
The study examined the views of some university students
on the ethics of tax evasion, their intentions to engage in
tax evasion and the factors that influence such intentions.
Using the extended version of TPB as the theoretical frame,
the study investigated the influence of attitude, subjective
norm, perceived behavioural control and moral obligation
on the intention to evade tax and also ascertained whether
the intention to evade tax could be explained by reference
to respondent’s stance on the ethics of tax evasion.

The findings from the study suggest the intention to
evade tax among the respondents is quite low and that
most of the sampled students view tax evasion as an
unethical practice even when tax revenues are perceived
not to be properly used by the state. From our descriptive
analysis, it appears the sampled students generally subscribe to the absolutist view which argues that tax evasion
is always or almost always unethical and therefore have
lower motivations to engage in tax evasion. The results
from the structural model suggest that students’ attitudes
and subjective norms have a positive influence on their
intentions to engage in tax evasion whilst an inverse
relationship exists between moral obligation and intentions to engage in tax evasion. Also, it was found that
students’ ethical views on taxes have important implications on their intentions to evade.
Findings of this study offer some useful insights into
the tax payment behaviour of the youthful population
who arguably constitute the backbone of every state. An
understanding of the intentions of students towards tax
evasion and the predictors of such intentions may be
valuable to policy makers interested in promoting good
tax compliance behaviour among would-be tax payers.
Given that the ethical orientation and moral issues
affect the intention to engage in tax evasion, an important way of promoting responsible tax compliance
behaviour among would be employees is to emphasize
the teaching of ethics and the promotion of ethical
behaviour at the university and other training institutions aimed at inducing in individuals a sense of moral
obligation towards the payment of taxes.
Again, given that individuals with positive attitudes
towards taxes are less likely to engage in tax evasion,
governments and tax authorities could fashion out programmes aimed at sensitizing would-be taxpayers on
the need to pay taxes and the consequences for noncompliance to shape their attitudes favourably with
respect to the payment of taxes. This can be achieved
by deploying effective communication programmes
such as seminars, symposiums and media education
to sensitize the public on their civic responsibility to
the state through the payment of taxes.
Despite the contributions of this study, the findings
are limited in some regards. Findings of this study only
reflect the intentions of people to engage in tax evasion
and the factors that predict such intentions and not the
actual tax evasion behaviour. Moreover, the study relied
on the views of students from a single public university in
Ghana most of whom are yet to be employed as workers.
Whilst their present view may be useful indicators for
predicting actual tax compliance behaviour in future, this
view can change in the future. Care should therefore be
taken in the interpretation of the findings.
