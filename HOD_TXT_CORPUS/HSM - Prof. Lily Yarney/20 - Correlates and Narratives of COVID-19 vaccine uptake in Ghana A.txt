Objectives: The COVID-19 pandemic significantly impacted public health and disrupted socioeconomic activities
globally. Although vaccination efforts have intensified, overall community uptake of COVID-19 vaccines remain
suboptimal. This study explored the facilitators and barriers of vaccine uptake in the New Juaben South Municipality (NJSM) in Ghana.
Study design: The Exploratory Case Study Design was employed for the research.
Methods: Using concurrent mixed-method approach, quantitative data were collected from 418 randomly
selected respondents from eight communities. The qualitative data consisted of responses from 23 participants.
Quantitative data were analyzed with SPSS version 28, descriptive statistics, regression analysis, and p-values
were used to interpret the findings, whilst participant interviews were recorded, transcribed, and analyzed
thematically.
Results: The level of COVID-19 vaccine uptake was 53.1 %. Various factors significantly influenced uptake,
including age (p = 0.053), occupational category (p = 0.001), education (p = 0.003), income (p = 0.001),
awareness of effectiveness of vaccines (p = 0.002), awareness of ongoing vaccination (p = 0.011), infection with
coronavirus (p = 0.005), close family member infected with coronavirus (p = 0.039), death of someone due to
COVID-19 (p = 0.022) and overall perception towards COVID-19 disease and vaccine (p = 0.001). Additionally,
respondents above 47 years of age [AOR = 4.99, CI= (1.32–18.91), p = 0.018)], health workers [AOR = 2.65, CI
= (1.03–6.18), p = 0.043)], those with basic education [AOR = 0.50, CI = (0.27–0.92), p = 0.025)], and good
perception towards COVID-19 disease and vaccination [AOR = 2.30, CI = (1.37–3.85), p = 0.002)], all had
higher odds of COVID-19 vaccine uptake. However, deeply rooted religious and cultural beliefs were major
barriers to vaccination uptake.
Conclusion: Community-based health communication strategies are essential to counter vaccine misconceptions
during pandemics and foster enduring behavioural change.

1. Introduction
Africa recorded over nine million cases of COVID-19 and almost
176,000 deaths [1]. In Ghana, over 171,735 cases and 1,462 cumulative
deaths were reported since March 2020 [1]. Due to the high transmissibility of COVID-19, vaccines were rapidly developed and distributed globally to counter it [2–5]. Despite this, by October 2023, only
36% of Ghana’s total eligible population was fully vaccinated; well

below the projected 70% target [1]. Vaccine hesitancy was among the
main causes of the low COVID-19 vaccination coverage in Ghana [6].
Studies have shown that many Ghanaians were unprepared to accept the
COVID-19 vaccines [7].
Vaccine hesitancy arises from many causes, particularly mistruct in
heath systems (Confidence), barriers to access (Convenience), and low
risk perception of diseases (Complacency) [8–10]. A cross-sectional
study by Malesza and Wittman [11], suggested a significant

2.2. Study design and population
We employed the exploratory case study design, with concurrent
mixed method approach. Quantitative surveys were used to measure the
predictors of COVID-19 vaccination, and in-depth interviews used to
explore underlying motivations, barriers, and contextual influences. The
results were integrated to identify areas of divergence and complementarity [20].
2.3. Sample size, sampling techniques, and instruments
The target population included adult residents of NJSM. A sample
size of 424 was calculated using Cochran’s formular (1977) (95 % CI,
10% nonresponse) from eight randomly selected communities (Old Estate, Social Welfare, Two Streams, Zongo Community C, Residential
Area, School Town, Nsukwao Abotanso and Klu Town-from) from NJSM.
A structured questionnaire, adapted from previous literature [21,22]
and covering respondents’ sociodemographic characteristics, vaccine
uptake, knowledge, awareness, experiences, and perceptions of
COVID-19 was used. Trained researchers assisted respondents. Each
respondent took about 10 minutes to complete the questionnaire.
Overall, 418 residents (98.6%) completed the questionnaire.
Further, participants were purposively sampled from the questionnaire completers and interviewed. The in-depth interviews were conducted using an interview guide designed along the research objectives
and the COM-B model. Each interview lasted approximately 45 minutes,
in the English or Twi languages, and progressed until data saturation
was achieved with 23 participants.

4.1. Limitation

As the breadwinner and the one who was always going out for food and
other essentials at the peak of the pandemic and lockdown, I needed to
save my life and protect my family. (P7)

The case study design employed for the research limits the generalizability of our findings to other parts of Ghana. However, in areas with
similar contextual issues, our findings may be applicable. Future studies
should have broader scope, covering multiple regions in the country to
examine the complex socio-cultural and economic predictors of vaccine
uptake nationally.

4. Discussion
The study assessed the predictors of COVID-19 vaccination in NJSM.
Our analyses found old age, educational level, health worker status and
positive COVID-19 perception to be significant uptake predictors
(Table 2). This mirrors previous studies in Nigeria and Ghana where
lower vaccination rates were recorded among non-health workers [24,
25], likely due to lower virus-related knowledge. Surprisingly, and
contrary to other studies in LMICs [26], participants with basic education in this research were 50% less likely to vaccinate compared with
those with no formal education (AOR = 0.50, CI = 0.27–0.92, p =
0.025).
Although vaccine hesitancy was low (32 %) compared to 80.9 % in
Nigeria [24] and 62.7 % in other parts of Ghana [27], uptake to full
dosage reached only 53.1%, showing lack of trust. Concerns about side
effects and efficacy hinder acceptance [26]. These were evidenced by
participants reporting fever (76.8 %), headache (73.6 %), muscle pain
(68.5 %), pain at the site of injection (58.9 %) and muscle weakness
(52.5 %) as side effects. Similarly, in a study of Ghanaian university
students by Darko et al. [28], low perception of severity of infection, fear
and doubt were strong predictors of vaccine hesitancy. Rzymski et al.
[29] suggested that even with 68% vaccination acceptance, people
would be hindered by side effects, low efficacy and testing mistrust [30,
31].
Further, vaccine misconception and misinformation, facilitated by
religious and cultural beliefs was a major barrier [32]. For instance,
similar to findings of this research, in Malawi, individuals mentioned
that church officials preached that immunization was associated with
devil worship [33,34]. In Uganda, officials propagated misinformation,
fueling skepticism about COVID-19 vaccines [33].
Vaccine uptake in the NJSM was also influenced by motivation from
leaders in society. Political leaders, religious leaders, and traditional
leaders had significant influences on participants’ decision to vaccinate.
This is consistent with findings of Lazarus et al. (2021) who noted that
social and frontline workers, as well as trust in governments, promote
vaccine acceptability and contribute to public vaccination compliance.

4.2. Conclusion
COVID-19 vaccination uptake in Ghana is linked to older age, health
worker status, educational level and having good perception about
vaccines. Key facilitators of vaccine uptake include motivation from
people who have taken the vaccine, protection of significant others,
workplace/official requirements, exposure to COVID-19 vaccine information and the influence of leaders in society. In addition to efforts to
